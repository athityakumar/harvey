Hoechst Pharmaceuticals Limited and Another Etc v State of Bihar and Others

Supreme Court of India

06 May 1983

Case Analysis

Bench

A.P. Sen, E.S. Venkataramiah, R.B. Misra

Where Reported

1983 Indlaw SC 273; (1983) 4 SCC 45; (1983) SCC (Tax) 248; AIR 1983 SC 1019; [1985] 154 ITR 64; 1983(1) SCALE 723; [1983] 3 S.C.R. 130; 1984 (55) STC 1; 1983 UJ 617

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Sales Tax

Keywords: Ultra Vires, Prohibition, Dealers, Laboratories, Essential Commodity, State Law, Disproportionate, Constitutionality, Finance Act, 1981, Label, Levy of surcharge, Disproportionate burden, Class of dealers, Collection of surcharge

Summary: Sales Tax - Constitution - Bihar Finance Act, 1981, ss. 2(j), 3(I), 5(I) - Essential Commodities Act, 1955, ss. 3(I), 6 - Drugs (Price Control) order, 1979, para 21 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 246, 286 - Levy of surcharge - Disproportionate burden - Class of dealers - Collection of surcharge - Prohibition - Constitutionality - Appellants (companies) engaged in manufacture and sale of various medicines and lifesaving drugs throughout India including respondent (State) - Appellants filed their annual returns for A/ys 1980-81, 1981-82 showing gross turnover of their sales in respondent (State) and paid tax along with return inclusive of surcharge at 10% of tax - Appellants filed petitions before HC challenging constitutional validity of s. 5(I) of 1981 Act, which provided for levy of a surcharge of 10% of total amount of tax, on every dealer whose gross turnover during a year exceed Rs.5.00 Lakhs, in additional to tax payable and s. 5(3) of 1981 Act, which prohibited such dealer from collecting amount of surcharge payable from purchasers - HC by impugned judgment upheld the constitutional validity of ss. 5(I), (3) of 1981 Act - Aggrieved appellants filed instant appeals - Appellants contended that field of price fixation of essential commodities in general, and drugs and formulations in particular, was an occupied field by virtue of various control orders issued by Central Govt. u/s. 3(I) of 1955 Act, which allows manufacturer of producer of goods to pass on tax liability to consumer and respondent had no legislative competence to enact s. 5(3) of 1981 Act and that there would be repugnancy between 1981 Act and 1955 Act - Further contended that transactions were covered by art. 286 of Constitution and were outside purview of 1981 Act and thus they could not be taken into consideration for computation of gross turnover as defined in s. 2(j) of 1981 Act - Respondent contended that there was no conflict in 1981 Act and 1955 Act - Whether there was repugnancy between s. 5(3) of 1981 Act and para 21 of the Order - Held, there was no doubt that Central and State legislations operate in 2 different and distinct fields - 1955 Act provided for regulation, production, a supply distribution and pricing of essential commodities and was relatable to Entry 33 of List III of Seventh Schedule of Constitution - It could not be doubted that a surcharge partakes of the nature of sales tax and thus it was within competence of State Legislature to enact s. 5(I) of 1981 Act for the purpose of levying surcharge on certain class of dealers in addition to tax payable by appellants - When State Legislature had competence to levy tax on sale or purchase of goods under Entry 54, it was equally competent to select the class of dealers on whom charge would fall - Where fixation of price of an essential commodity was necessary to protect the interests of consumers in view of scarcity of supply, such restriction could not be challenged as unreasonable on the ground that it would result in elimination of middleman for whom it would be unprofitable to carry on business at fixed rate or that it did not ensure a reasonable return to manufacturer or producer on capital employed in the business of manufacturing or producing such an essential commodity - Contention that in field of fixation of price by the control Order issued u/s. 3(I) of 1955 Act Central Govt. should have due regard to securing of a reasonable return on capital employed in business of manufacturing or producing an essential commodity was entirely misconceived - Predominant object of issuing the Order u/s. 3(I) of 1955 Act was to secure equitable distribution and availability of essential commodities at fair prices to consumers, and mere circumstance that some of those engaged in field of industry, trade and commerce might suffer a loss was no ground for treating such a regulatory law to be unreasonable, unless the basis adopted for price fixation was so unreasonable as to be in excess of power to fix price, or there was a statutory obligation to ensure a fair return to industry - It was obvious that art. 246 of Constitution imposed limitations on the legislative powers of Union and State Legislatures and its ultimate analysis would reveal essentials - Entry 54 of List II was a tax entry and thus there was no question of repugnancy between s. 5(3) of 1981 Act which was a law made by State Legislature for imposition of tax on sale or purchase of goods relatable to Entry 54 and para 21 of the order issued by Central Govt., which was a law made by Parliament relatable to Entry 33 of List III - Question of 'repugnancy' could only arise in connection with subjects enumerated in Concurrent List as regards which both Union and State Legislatures have concurrent powers so that question of conflict between laws made by both Legislatures relating to same subject might arise - There was no ground whatever for holding that s. 5(3) of 1981 Act was arbitrary or irrational or that it treats 'unequals as equals', or that it imposes a disproportionate burden on a certain class of dealers - Liability to pay a surcharge was not on gross turnover including transactions covered by art. 286 of Constitution but was only on inside sales and surcharge was sought to be levied on dealers who have a position of economic superiority - A. V. Fernandez v. State of Kerala, 1957 Indlaw SC 18, relied on - Appeals dismissed.
