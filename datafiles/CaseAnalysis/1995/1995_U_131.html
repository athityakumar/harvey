B.C. Chaturvedi v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

01 November 1995

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Ramaswamy, B.P. Jeevan Reddy, B.L. Hansaria

Where Reported

1995 Indlaw SC 676; (1995) 6 SCC 749; (1996) SCC (L&S) 80; AIR 1996 SC 484; AIR 1995 SCW 4374; 1996 (32) ATC 44; 1996 (1) CLR 389; 1996 (72) FLR 316; JT 1995 (8) SC 65; 1996 LabIC 462; 1996 (1) LLJ 1231; 1995(6) SCALE 188; 1995 (5) SLR 778; [1995] Supp4 S.C.R. 644; 1996 (1) UPLBEC 680

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Summary: (Majority Order)

Ramaswami J, for himself and Jeevan Reddy, J.

Service - Constitution - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1947, s. 5(1)(2) - Constitution of India, 1950, arts 311(2), 142 and 21 - Administrative Tribunals Act, 1985, s.19 - Imposition of punishment by disciplinary and appellate authority - Interference by Tribunal - Challenged - An investigation was conducted against the appellant (Income Tax Officer) by the CBI which disclosed that the appellant possessed assets disproportionate to his known source of income - A departmental enquiry was initiated against the appellant and Inquiry Officer found the charges against the appellant as having been proved - Thereafter, appellant was dismissed from service after consultation with the UPSC - On appeal, Tribunal upheld the recording of the Inquiry Officer that the charges had been proved but converted the order of dismissal from service of appellant into the compulsory retirement keeping in view the length of service of the appellant - Respondent (Union of India) filed appeal against order of the tribunal contending that tribunal had erred in changing the punishment awarded to the appellant from dismissal into compulsory retirement - Whether tribunal/HC can converted the order of dismissal of appellant from service into the compulsory retirement? - Held, the disciplinary authority, and on an appeal the appellate authority, being fact-finding authorities have exclusive power to consider the evidence with a view to maintain discipline - They were invested with the discretion to impose appropriate punishment keeping in view the magnitude or gravity of the misconduct - The HC/Tribunal, while exercising the power of judicial review, cannot normally substitute its own conclusion or penalty and impose some other penalty - If the punishment imposed by the disciplinary authority or the appellate authority shocks the conscience of the HC/Tribunal, it would appropriately mould the relief, either directing the disciplinary authority/appellate authority to reconsider the penalty imposed, or to shorten the litigation, it may itself, in exceptional and rare cases, impose appropriate punishment with cogent reasons in support thereof - In the instant case, appellant having been found to be in possession of assets disproportionate to the known source of his income, the interference with the imposition of punishment was wholly unwarranted - Hence, the order of the Tribunal modifying the punishment was set aside and order of the disciplinary authority was maintained - Appeal allowed.

(Minority Order)

Hansaria, J.

Service - Constitution - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1947, s. 5(1)(2) - Constitution of India, 1950, arts 311(2), 142 and 21 - Administrative Tribunals Act, 1985, s.19 - Imposition of punishment by disciplinary and appellate authority - Interference by Tribunal - Challenged - Whether tribunal/HC can converted the order of dismissal of appellant from service into the compulsory retirement? - Held, although art. 142 of the Constitution of India had specifically conferred the power of doing complete justice on the SC, to achieve that result it may pass such decree or order as deemed necessary; it would be wrong to think that other Courts were not to do complete justice between the parties - If the power of modification of punishment/penalty were to be available to the SC only u/art. 142 of the Constitution, a very large percentage of litigants would be denied this small relief merely because they were not in a position to approach the SC, which may inter alia, because of the poverty of the concerned persons - The mere fact that there was no provision parallel to art. 142 relating to the HC, can be no ground to think that they have not to do complete justice, and if the moulding of relief would do complete justice between the parties, the same cannot be ordered - A HC would be within its jurisdiction to modify the punishment/penalty by moulding the relief in cases when the punishment/penalty award shocks the judicial conscience - Appeal dismissed.
