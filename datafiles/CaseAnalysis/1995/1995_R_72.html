Rama Narang v Ramesh Narang and Others

Supreme Court of India

19 January 1995

Case Analysis

Bench

A.M. Ahmadi, R.M. Sahai, K. Jayachandra Reddy

Where Reported

1995 Indlaw SC 2249; (1995) 2 SCC 513; 1995 (1) CCR 108; [1995] 83 Comp Cas 194; 1996 CRLJ 3495; JT 1995 (1) SC 515; 1995(1) SCALE 276; [1995] 1 S.C.R. 456

Case Digest

Subject: Corporate; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Prohibition Act

Summary: Corporate - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 389(1), 374, 482, Companies Act, 1956, s. 267 - Appeal arose from the order of DB of the Bombay HC against an order passed by the Single Judge making absolute the notice of motion taken out by appellant and respondent Nos. 4 and 5 - Two reliefs granted by Single Judge were to restrain respondent Nos. 1 and 2 from (a) acting upon, implementing, circulating, or taking any steps in furtherance of any decision purported to have been taken at the Board meeting alleged to have been held on 13th July, 1992 and from (b) obstructing or interfering with the petitioner's functioning as Chairman and Managing Director of the respondent-company - By the impugned judgment, the DB partly allowed the appeal by setting aside the order of the Single Judge in respect of grant of prayer (b) of the motion while keeping the relief in terms of prayer (a) of the notice of motion undisturbed - Hence instant appeal - Whether the scope of s. 389(1) of the Code extends to conferring power on the Appellate Court to stay the operation of the order of conviction -

Held, if the order of conviction is to result in some-disqualification of the type mentioned in s. 267 of the Act there is no reason why it should be given a narrow meaning to s. 389(1) of the Code to debar the court from granting an order to that effect in a fit case. The appeal u/s. 374 is essentially against the order of conviction because the order of sentence is merely consequential thereto; albeit even the order of sentence can be independently challenged if it is harsh and disproportionate to the established guilt. Therefore, when an appeal is preferred u/s. 374 of the Code the appeal is against both the conviction and sentence and therefore, we see no reason to place a narrow interpretation on s. 389(1) of the Code not to extend it to an order of conviction. Although that issue in the instant case recedes in the background because HC's can exercise inherent jurisdiction u/s. 482 of the Code if the power was not to be found in s. 389(1) of the Code. SC therefore, of the opinion that the DB of Bombay was not right in holding that the Delhi HC could not have exercised jurisdiction u/s. 482 of the Code if it was confronted with a situation of there being no other provision in the (lode for staying the operation of the order of conviction. Since the interim order of stay did not specifically extend to the stay of conviction for the purpose of avoiding the disqualification u/s. 267 of the Act, there is no substance in the appeal and the appeal. Appeal dismissed.
