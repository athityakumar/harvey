 T.N. Seshan, Chief Election Commissioner of India v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

14 July 1995

Case Analysis

Bench

A.M. Ahmadi, M.K. Mukherjee, N.P. Singh, S.P. Bharucha, J.S. Verma

Where Reported

1995 Indlaw SC 1694; (1995) 4 SCC 611; AIR 1995 SCW 3341; JT 1995 (5) SC 337; 1995(4) SCALE 285; [1995] Supp2 S.C.R. 106; 1995 (3) UPLBEC 1805

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Election

Keywords: Bombay Prohibition Act 1949, Chief Election Commissioner And Other Election Commissioners (Conditions Of Service) Act, 1991

Summary: Election - Constitution - Chief Election Commissioner and Other Election Commissioners (Conditions of Service) Act, 1991 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Constitutional validity - Public interest - Malice - CEC insisted on strict compliance regarding code of conduct of all political parties - With a view to freeze powers of CEC, ruling party chose to amend and issued notification regarding the fixing of the number of EC's at two - 1993 Ordinance was promulgated to amend the 1991 Act that laid down the conditions of service of the Chief Election Commissioner (CEC) and Election Commissioners (EC) appointed u/art. 324 of the Constitution of India - CEC challenged the constitutional validity of the Ordinance - Hence, instant W.P.s - Whether, the 1193 Ordinance (1994 Act)and notifications regarding the appointment of EC's, were liable to be upheld - Held, that during the pendency of the W.P.s, the Ordinance became an Act in 1994 - The Ordinance was issued in public interest and the two EC's were appointed to associate with the CEC - It was a bonafide exercise with no intention to sideline the CEC nor erode his authority - No malafides could be attributed in the exercise of legislative powers and it was appropriate to expand the Election Commission by making the required changes in the extant law - Demand for a multi-member Commission was being voiced for several years - Amendment in the statute through an Ordinance could not infer that the decision was actuated by malice - Art. 324 of the Constitution did not stipulate that before the EC's were appointed, the CEC would have to be consulted and so their appointment could not held as vitiated - Though some of the conditions of service of the CEC were similar to those of the Judges of the SC, the CEC could not legitimately claim to be equated with SC Judges - Impugned notifications and the Ordinance in its entirety were liable to be upheld - Petitions dismissed.
