T. S. Balaram, Income Tax Officer,Company Circle Iv, Bombay v M/S. Volkart Brothers, Bombay

Supreme Court of India

05 August 1971

Case Analysis

Bench

K.S. Hegde, A.N. Grover

Where Reported

1971 Indlaw SC 190; (1971) 2 SCC 526; AIR 1971 SC 2204; [1971] 82 ITR 50; [1972] 1 S.C.R. 30

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Income Tax Act, 1922, ss. 2(9) and 17(1) - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Income Tax Act, 1961, s.154 - A/ys 1958-59, 1960-61, 1961-62 and 1962-63 - Rectification of assessment - Respondent firm was duly registered under IT Act, 1922 as well as IT Act, 1961 - For the a/ys 1958-59, 1960-61, 1961-62 and 1962-63 assessments of the respondent's firm were made on the slab rates prescribed under respective Finance Acts - Subsequently, respondent firm was served with notices dt. 29.01.1965 intimating to it that in its assessments for the 4 years in question there were mistakes apparent form the record in as much as the firm had not been charged at the maximum rates of tax - Department proposed to rectify those assessments u/s. 154 of IT Act, 1961 - Respondent challenged the validity of said orders in a writ petition - HC held that there was no obvious and patent mistake in the original assessment orders, therefore, Income Tax Officer was not competent to pass the impugned order - Hence, present appeal - Whether Income-tax Officer was justified in opining that there was an apparent mistake in the original orders of assessment,? - Held, a mistake apparent on the record must be an obvious and patent mistake and not something which can be established by a long drawn process of reasoning on points on which there may conceivably be two opinions - In present case, the applicability of s. 17(1) of IT Act, 1922 to the respondent would depend upon the decision of the question whether a firm can be considered as a 'person' within the meaning of that section - The term 'person' was defined in the 1922 Act as including a Hindu Undivided Family and a local authority - In the 1961 Act the definition has been expanded and includes firm - It is a matter for consideration whether the new definition contained in s. 2(31) of the IT Act, 1961 is an amendment of the law or is merely declaratory of the law that was in force earlier - Hence, the Income-tax Officer was not justified in thinking that there could be no two opinions about the applicability of s. 17(1) of IT Act, 1922 - Income Tax Officer was therefore wholly wrong in holding that there was a mistake apparent from the record of the assessments of the respondent - Appeal dismissed.
