 District Council of United Khasi and Jaintia v Miss Sitimon Sawian

Supreme Court of India

25 August 1971

Case Analysis

Bench

I.D. Dua, S.M. Sikri, G.K. Mitter, C.A. Vaidyialingam, P. Jaganmohan Reddy

Where Reported

1971 Indlaw SC 238; (1971) 3 SCC 708; AIR 1972 SC 787; [1972] 1 S.C.R. 398; 1971 UJ 848

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: United Khasi Jainta Hills District (Transfer of Land) Act (No IV of 1953)

Summary: Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14 and 132(1) - United Khasi-Jaintia Hills District (Transfer- of Land) Act (No. IV of 1953), s. 3 - Transfer of land Act - Constitutional validity - Certificate of fitness granted by the HC u/art. 132(1) of the Constitution related to the validity of s. 3 of the impugned Act, passed by the District Council - HC struck down said section as beyond the competence of the District Council and also as offending art. 14 of the Constitution - Question of 'the Constitutional validity of s. 3 of 'the impugned Act falls for determination solely on the interpretation 1 of the relevant provisions of the Constitution without any reference to the facts - Hence instant appeal - Whether para 3 (1) (a) of the Sixth Schedule confers on the District Council power to make laws with respect to transfer of land; or whether the subject of transfer of land was covered by the expression 'allotment, occupation or use or the setting apart of land' - Held, it was clear from the provision, r/w para 3 (1) (a) that the District Councils unlike the Parliament and the State Legislatures were not intended to be clothed with plenary power of legislation - Their power to make laws was expressly limited by the provisions of the Sixth Schedule which has created them and they could do nothing beyond the limits which circumscribe their power - It was beyond the domain of the courts to enlarge constructively their power to make laws - Proviso to para 3 (1) (a) merely serve to ensure that no, law made by the Regional and District Councils with respect to, allotment, occupation or use or setting apart, of land, as mentioned in that clause, should have the effect of preventing compulsory acquisition of land for public purposes, by the State Govt. in accordance with the law in force authorising such acquisition - Proviso might undoubtedly be sometimes inserted to, allay fears considered by some to be unfounded - But the question should ultimately come back to the point whether or not power to make laws conferred by cl. (a) includes the power to do so, with respect to transfer of land and this must turn upon the exact language and its primary meaning - Simple words used in cl. (a) were incapable of bearing the construction suggested and the provision found in the proviso did not in any way alter the operative effect of this clause - Preamble of impugned Act no doubt did speak of necessity to make provisions with respect to 'transfer, allotment, occupation or use of land for any purpose likely to promote the interests of the inhabitants thereof' but the subject of transfer was clearly beyond the scope of the law-making power conferred on the District Council by the Constitution and to that extent, therefore, the impugned Act which means s. 3 of the Act thereof was void being - Beyond the jurisdiction of the District Council - Appeal dismissed.
