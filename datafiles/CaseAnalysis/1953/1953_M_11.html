Satyabrata Ghose v Mugneeram Bangur and Company, and Another

Supreme Court of India

16 November 1953

Case Analysis

Bench

Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, Vivian Bose, Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati

Where Reported

1953 Indlaw SC 86; AIR 1954 SC 44; 1954 (1) MLJ(SC) 41; 1954 SCJ 1; [1954] 1 S.C.R. 310

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property; Municipalities & Local Governments; Contract & Commercial

Summary: Land & Property - Specific Relief Act - Constitution Of India, 1950 - Indian Contract Act, 1872, ss. 54 and 56 - Transfer of Property Act, 1882 - Defence of India Act - Defence of India Rules - Frustration of contract - (A) Whether doctrine of English law relating to frustration of contract, has no application to India in view of statutory provision contained in s. 56 of Indian Contract Act? - Held, doctrine of frustration comes into play when a contract becomes impossible of performance, after it is made, on account of circumstances beyond control of parties - Doctrine is a special case of impossibility and as such comes u/s. 56 of Indian Contract Act - Doctrine of frustration is really an aspect or part of law of discharge of contract by reason or supervening impossibility or illegality of act agreed to be done and hence, comes within purview of s. 56 of Indian Contract Act, 1872 - (B) Whether even if English law applies, it can have no application to contracts for sale of land? - Further held, rule of frustration can only put an end to purely contractual obligations, but it cannot destroy an estate in land which has already accrued in favour of a contracting party - According to s. 54 of Transfer of Property Act, 1882 a contract for sale of land does not of itself create any interest in property which is subject-matter of contract - Obligations of parties to a contract for sale of land are, therefore, same as in other ordinary contracts and consequently, there is no conceivable reason why doctrine of frustration should not be applicable to contracts for sale of land in India - (C) Whether there was no frustrating event which could be said to have taken away basis of contract or rendered its performance impossible in any sense of word? - Further held, having regard to nature and terms of contract, actual existence of war conditions at time when it was entered into, extent of work involved in development scheme and last though not least total absence of any definite period of time agreed to by parties within which work was to be completed, it cannot be said that requisition order vitally affected contract or made its performance impossible - Appeal allowed.
