 Chainrup Sampatram v Commissioner of Income Tax, West Bengal

Supreme Court of India

09 October 1953

Case Analysis

Bench

M. Patanjali Sastri, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Vivian Bose, Ghulam Hasan, Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati

Where Reported

1953 Indlaw SC 90; AIR 1953 SC 519; [1953] 24 ITR 481; 1953 SCJ 655; [1954] 1 S.C.R. 211

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes; Land & Property

Keywords: Indian Income-tax Act, 1992

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Income Tax Act, 1961, ss. 4A, 4B, 4(1), 14 (2) and 66 (1) - Assessment of stock in trade - Appellant-registered firm consisting of two brothers as partners, carrying on business as bullion merchants dealing mainly in silver and kept its books of account on the mercantile basis - In the course of the year of account 1997 corresponding to 1941-42, 582 bars of silver were sent to Bikaner where the partners resided, and their value at cost was credited in the books of the firm - Revenue alleged that said silver bars had been sold to the partners for their domestic use and said silver bars still formed part of the stock-in-trade of the firm at the close of the previous year 1997, and they accordingly were included in the taxable profits a sum of Rs. 2, 20, 887 as the excess arising from the valuation of the said 582 bars at market price on the closing day - On appeal, Appellate Tribunal held that silver bars in question formed part of the stock-in-trade of the business at Calcutta and their removal to Bikaner had been effected only for reasons of security, the said bars having remained there during the rest of the accounting year, their value at the market rate at the close of the year being an increment to the goods at Bikaner, the profit accrued at Bikaner (then an Indian State), with the result that it was exempted u/s. 14(2)(c) of IT Act - HC held that what is valued is the firm's business at the site of the firm and all the stock-in-trade of the firm is necessarily drawn into the valuation wherever they may be physically situated - HC also held that no part of the profits of the firm in the accounting year can be said to have accrued or arisen at Bikaner - Hence, present appeal - Whether on a true construction of s. 4(1)(b) and s. 14(2)(c) of IT Act, the sum of Rs. 2,20,887 was in law assessable to tax? - Held, valuation of unsold stock at the close of an accounting period is a necessary part of the process of determining the trading results of that period, and can in no sense be regarded as the 'source' of such profits - Nor can the place where such valuation is made be regarded as the 'situs of their accrual'- The source of the profits and gains of a business is indubitably the business, and the place of their accrual is where the business is carried on - As such profits can be correctly ascertained according to the method adopted by an assessee only after bringing into the trading account his closing stock wherever it may exist, the whole of the profits must be taken to accrue or arise at the place of carrying on the business - On the finding of the Income-tax authorities that the 582 bars of silver lying at Bikaner had not been really sold but remained part of the unsold stock of the firm's business at the end of the accounting year, the whole of the profits of that year must be taken to have accrued or arisen at Calcutta where the business was carried on, no part of that business having admittedly been transacted at Bikaner - Hence, sum of Rs. 2,20,887/- is assessable to tax - Appeal dismissed.
