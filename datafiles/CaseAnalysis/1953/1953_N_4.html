Namdeo Lokman Lodhi v Narmadabai and Others

Supreme Court of India

27 February 1953

Case Analysis

Bench

Mehr Chand Mahajan, Sudhi Ranjan Das

Where Reported

1953 Indlaw SC 102; AIR 1953 SC 228; 1953 (55) BomLR 517; 1953 (1) MLJ(SC) 715; 1953 (55) PLR 312; 1953 SCJ 306; [1953] 4 S.C.R. 1009

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Municipalities & Local Governments

Keywords: Condition Precedent, Conveyancing Act, 1881, Law of property Act, 1925, Possession Of Land

Summary: Land & Property - Transfer of Property Act, 1882, ss. 2, 111(g) - Law of Property Act, 1925 - Conveyancing Act, 1881 - Possession of Land -(A) Whether notice as contemplated by s. 111(g) of Transfer of Property Act, 1882 is necessary for determination of a lease for non-payment of rent even where such lease was executed before coming into force of Act? - Held, lease in this case was executed before Transfer of Property Act came into force in 1882 - Amendment therefore, made in this sub-section by Act XX of 1929 not being retrospective, cannot touch present lease and it is also excluded from reach of Transfer of Property Act, 1882 by provisions of s. 2 - It was strongly argued that amendment made in 1929 to s. 111(g) of Act embodies a principle of justice, equity and good conscience and notwithstanding s. 2 of Act, that principle was applicable in this case and there can be no forfeiture unless notice in writing to lessee of his intention to determine lease by lessor had been given - High Court held, and that this provision in sub-section (g) of s. 111 in regard to notice was not based upon any principle of justice, equity and good conscience - Insistence therein that notice should be given in writing is intrinsic evidence of fact that formality is merely statutory and it cannot trace its origin to any rule of equity - (B) Whether High Court should have interfered with discretion of lower courts in refusing relief against forfeiture in circumstances of this case? - Further held, each case must be judged by itself, delay, conduct of parties and difficulties to which landlord has been put should be weighed against tenant - In this situation, HC was fully justified in finding that in second appeal it would not interfere with discretion of courts below in refusing to grant relief against forfeiture - Appeal dismissed.
