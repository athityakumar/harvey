 State Of Travancore-Cochin And Others v Shanmugha Vilas Cashew Nut Factoryand Others.

Supreme Court of India

08 May 1953

Case Analysis

Bench

M. Patanjali Sastri, Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Vivian Bose, Ghulam Hasan

Where Reported

1953 Indlaw SC 21; AIR 1953 SC 333; 1953 (2) MLJ(SC) 123; 1953 SCJ 471; [1954] 1 S.C.R. 53; 1953 (4) STC 205

Case Digest

Subject: Sales Tax

Keywords: Resale, Tax On The Sale Or Purchase Of Goods, Last Purchase, Indian Sea Customs Act, 1878, Companies Act, 1867, Travancore-Cochin General Sales Tax Act, 1124, English Bankruptcy Act, 1869 (ENG), Madras Sales Tax Act, Marketing of Primary Products Act, 1927, Sales Tax Continuance Order, 1950

Summary: (Majority Order)

(M. Patanjali Sastri, CJ., for himself and B. K. Mukherjea, Vivian Bose and Ghulam Hasan)

Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 286 (1) (a), (1)(b) and (2) - Exemption u/art. 286(1)(b) - Respondents, dealers of cashew nuts in the State, and their business consists in importing raw cashew-nuts from abroad and the neighboring districts in the State of Madras in addition to purchases made in the local market, and, after converting them by means of certain processes into edible kernels, exporting the kernels to other countries, mainly America - The oil pressed from the shells removed from the cashew-nuts was also exported - Respondents claimed exemption u/art. 286(1)(b) of the Constitution in respect of the purchases made from that date till 29.05.1950, the end of the account year - The sales tax authorities rejected said claim - Respondents applied to the HC u/art. 226 of the Constitution who upheld the respondent's claim and quashed the assessments in so far as they related to the said period - Hence, the instant appeals - Whether respondents were entitled to exemption u/art. 286(1) of the Constitution? - Held, sales by export and purchases by import fall within the exemption u/art. 286(1)(b) of the Constitution - Purchases in the State by the exporter for the purpose of export as well as sales in the State by the importer after the goods have crossed the customs barrier were not within the exemption - Sales in the State by the exporter or importer by transfer of shipping documents while the goods were beyond the customs barrier were within the exemption, assuming that the State power of taxation extends to such transactions - In the instant case, purchases made by the respondents fell into 3 groups i.e. (i) purchases made in the local market, (ii) purchases from the neighboring districts of the State of Madras and (iii) goods exported to the American - In group (i), the Bombay party acted merely as the agents of the respondents, privately being established between the latter and the African sellers - The purchases were thus purchases which occasioned the import, and therefore, come within the exemption - As regards group (ii), the Bombay party were the purchasers and they sell the goods as principals to the respondents at the port of destination by issuing separately delivery orders against payment - No privately being established between the respondents and the African sellers, the respondent's purchases can only be described as purchases from the Bombay party of the goods within the State, in other words, they were local purchases and stand on the same footing as purchases falling under Group (i) - Hence, group (ii) do not come within the exemption - In respect of group (iii) it would appear that the cashew-nuts sold and exported to the American buyers were packed in tins placed in wooden boxes - The sales tax authorities have included value of these packing materials in addition to the value of the kernels in computing the turnover of the respondents for purpose of assessment - It was urged that such inclusion was inadmissible inasmuch as these articles could not be regarded as separate articles of sale apart from the kernels which were packed therein, and that even if they were to be so regarded, their sale to the American buyers was a sale which occasioned the export just as much as the sale of the kernels - The latter contention must prevail in view of the previous decision, and no sales tax can be levied in respect of these articles - The decision of the HC quashing the assessments in question was affirmed but the cases will go back to the Sales Tax Officer concerned in the respective appeals for making fresh assessments according to law - Order accordingly.

(Minority Order)

(S. R. Das, J)

Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 286 (1) (a), (1)(b) and (2) - Exemption u/art. 286 (1) (b) - Whether respondents were entitled to exemption u/art. 286(1) of the Constitution? - Held, the Explanation to art. 286 (1) (a) was not an exception or a proviso but only explains cl. (1) (a) of art. 286 (1)(a) of the Constitution - It does not confer taxing power on any State but only takes away the power of taxation of a State in respect of sales and purchases in which delivery does not take place within the State by enacting that such sales shall be deemed to have taken place outside that State within cl. (1) (a) of art. 286 (1) of the Constitution - Consequently, if a sale or purchase takes place outside a State, either under the general law or by virtue of the fiction created by the Explanation, then that State cannot, under cl. (1) (a) of art. 286(1) of the Constitution, tax such sale or purchase - If a sale or purchase takes place within a State, either under the general law or by reason of the Explanation, then, if such a sale or purchase takes place ' in the course of ' inter-State trade and commerce, no State, not even the State where the sale or purchase takes place as aforesaid can tax it by reason of (2), unless and until Parliament by law provides otherwise - In the instant case, as regards local purchases, as local purchases took place within the State they were not entitled to the protection of art. 286(1) of the Constitution - As regards purchases from the neighboring States, if the goods were taken delivery of by the agents of the respondents outside the State, such purchases must, under the Explanation, be regarded as having taken place outside the State and accordingly would be exempt from taxation u/art. 286 (1) (a) of the Constitution - As regards purchases from Africa where the Bombay merchants act as agents of the respondents and pay the price and take delivery of the shipping documents in Bombay the purchases fall within cl. (1) (a) and also (1) (b) of art. 286(1) of the Constitution and were not liable to tax as they take place outside the State within cl. (1) (a) of the Constitution and also 'in the course of import' within cl. (1) (b) of 286 (1) of the Constitution - Hence, the decision of the HC must be upheld only to the extent that the assessments should be quashed - The matter must, however, go back to the Sales Tax Officer who must make a reassessment - Order accordingly.
