Raghbir Singh Gill v Gurcharan Singh Tohra and Others

Supreme Court of India

09 May 1980

Case Analysis

Bench

D.A. Desai, A.C. Gupta

Where Reported

1980 Indlaw SC 463; (1980) Supp SCC 53; AIR 1980 SC 1362; [1980] 3 S.C.R. 1302

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Election; Municipalities & Local Governments; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Election Dispute, Grounds For Declaring Election To Be Void, Council Of States, Delimitation Of Constituencies

Summary: Election - Practice & Procedure - Maintenance of Internal Security Act - Indian Evidence Act, 1872,ss. 87(2), 132 - Conduct of Election Rules, 1961, rr. 23(3), 23(5)(a), 23(5) (b), 31(2), 38, 39(4), 39(5), 39(6), 38A(4)and 39A(1)- Constitution of India, 1950, art. 20(3) - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 115 - Representation of People Act, 1951, ss. 94, 128, 64A, 100(1)(d)(iii) and 80 - Delimitation of constituencies - Prohibition Act - Punjab Legislative Assembly formed a constituency for electing members to Council of States - Notification was issued calling upon members of Punjab Legislative Assembly to elect three members to Council of States - Respondent 4 and respondent 5 were nominated as candidates of Indian National Congress - Appellant an independent candidate - Respondent 4 and 5 and appellant elected - Respondents 2 and 3, two sitting members of Punjab Legislative Assembly and, therefore, eligible electors, filed an election petition challenging election of appellant on ground of corrupt practices and relationship with Giani Zail Singh, CM - (A) Whether s. 94 RP Act prevent anyone from seeking information about how a person has cast has vote from mouth of person or is it privilege of voter not to be compelled to disclose for whom he has voted? - When it is said that no witness shall be required to state for whom he has voted at an election, on a pure grammatical construction uninhibited by any other consideration it would mean that witness cannot be compelled against his will to disclose how he has voted or for whom he has voted - In view of imperative language of s. 132 of Evidence Act a witness cannot refuse to answer a question which is relevant to matter under enquiry in which he is called as a witness even on pain of self-incrimination - Principle of secrecy of ballot necessitated a specific provision executing witness from answering such a question which he would be under an obligation to answer u/s. 132 of Evidence Act of s. 95(1) of RP Act - Held, that s. 94 of RP Act carves out an exception to s. 132 of Evidence Act - (B) Whether s. 94 enacts an absolute prohibition or a total embargo on a voter being questioned about how he voted which will infringe secrecy of a ballot? - S. 94 enacts a privilege in favour of voter in that no one can compel him to disclose for whom he voted but privilege ends there for it he desires to waive privilege and volunteers to give information as do for whom he voted, neither s. 94 nor any provision of RP Act is violated - (C) Whether a recount can be ordered? - Petition for re-count after inspection of ballot papers must contain an adequate statement on material facts on which petitioner relies in support of his case and secondly Tribunal must be prima facie satisfied that in order to decide dispute and to do complete justice between parties an inspection of ballot papers is necessary - Petition for a re-count on allegation of miscount or error in counting is based upon not specific allegation of miscounting but errors which may indicate a miscount, and re-count becomes necessary - Implication in law is that those postal ballot papers have been wrongly received in favour of a candidate not entitled to same and improperly refused in favour of candidate entitled to same, and this is a miscount and a re-count is necessary - Once inspection of ballot papers was permitted and four voters were examined and each one was shown his ballot paper and each one volunteered to give information as to for whom he voted, it became crystal clear that their ballot papers were tampered with - Held, that four ballot papers of four witness have been tampered with and if their evidence is to be accepted, tampering is to effect that each one of them had cast his first preference vote in favour of respondent I but it was altered to show that it was a second preference vote and first preference vote was cast in favour of appellant - Ground on which election is sought to be avoided in election petition is clearly covered by s. 100 (1)(d)(iii) - Appeal dismissed.
