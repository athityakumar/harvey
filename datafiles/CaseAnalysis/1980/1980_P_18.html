Commissioner of Income Tax, Patiala v Piara Singh

Supreme Court of India

08 May 1980

Case Analysis

Bench

R.S. Pathak, P.N. Bhagwati, V.D. Tulzapurkar

Where Reported

1980 Indlaw SC 464; (1980) Supp SCC 166; AIR 1980 SC 1271; 1980 (17) CTR 111; [1980] 124 ITR 40; [1980] 3 S.C.R. 1122; 1980 TAXLR 1090; [1980] 3 TAXMAN 67

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Indian Income Tax Act, 1922 - Smuggling - Tax liability - Deduction - Respondent was apprehended by the Indian Police while crossing the Indo-Pakistan border into Pakistan - A sum of amount in currency notes was recovered from his person - On interrogation he stated that he was taking the currency notes to Pakistan to enable him to purchase gold in that country with a view to smuggling it into India - The Collector of Central Excise and Land Customs ordered the confiscation of the currency notes - Income Tax Officer now took proceedings under the 1922 Act for assessing the assessee's income and determining his tax liability - He came to the finding that out of Rs. 65,500/- an amount of Rs. 60,500/- constituted the income of assessee from undisclosed sources - An appeal by assessee was dismissed by the Appellate Assistant Commissioner - In second appeal, Income Tax Appellate Tribunal upheld the claim to deduction - At the instance of the Revenue, a reference was made to HC - HC answered the question in the affirmative - Hence, the present appeal - Whether confiscation of the currency notes entitles the assessee to the deduction claimed - Held, the currency notes carried by assessee across the border constituted the means for acquiring gold in Pakistan, which gold he subsequently sold in India at a profit - The currency notes were necessary for acquiring the gold - The carriage of currency notes across the border was an essential part of the smuggling operation - If the business was illegal, neither the profits earned nor the losses incurred would be enforceable in law - But, that does not take the profits out of the taxing statute - Similarly, the taint of illegality of the business could not detract from the losses being taken into account for computation of the amount which could be subjected to tax as 'profits' u/s. 10(1) of the 1922 Act - Tax collector could only tax profits of a trade or business - That could not be done without deducting the losses and the legitimate expenses of the business - Therefore, assessee was entitled to the deduction of Rs. 65,500/- and accordingly affirm the view taken by HC on the question of law referred to it - Order accordingly.
