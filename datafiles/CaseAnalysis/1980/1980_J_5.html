Jagdish Saran and Others v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

28 January 1980

Case Analysis

Bench

V.R. Krishna Iyer, R.S. Pathak, O. Chinnappa Reddy

Where Reported

1980 Indlaw SC 110; (1980) 2 SCC 768; AIR 1980 SC 820; [1980] 2 S.C.R. 831

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Education; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Constitutionality, Denied, Admission in post-graduate degree course

Summary: Constitution - Education - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 15, 16 - Reservation - Admission in post-graduate degree course - Denied - Constitutionality - Petitioners (medial graduates) applied for admission into post-graduate course from respondent (Delhi University) - Petitioners having secured enough marks to qualify for admission but were turned down because of a rule reserving 70% of seats, at post-graduate level, to Delhi graduates (students who took their MBBS degree from Delhi university) - Remaining 30% was open to all, including graduates of Delhi - That rule was made in April 1978 in modification of earlier reservation - Aggrieved petitioners filed instant petition challenging its vires as violative of arts. 14, 15 and 16 of Constitution and sought direction against University to admit petitioners - Hence, instant petition - Whether impugned reservation in respondent University was constitutionally valid and petitioner was entitled to the relief claimed for - Held, institution-wise segregation or reservation had no place in the scheme of art. 15 of Constitution, although social and educational destitution might be endemic in some parts of country where a college or university might be started to remedy that glaring imbalance and reservation for those alumi for higher studies might be permissible - Unless there was vital nexus with equal opportunity, broad validation of university-based reservation could not be built on the vague ground that all other universities were practising it-a fact not fully proved before SC either - Universality of illegality, even if the artists of discrimination were universities, could not convert such praxis into constitutionality - All the best talent could not be completely excluded by wholesale reservation - So, a certain percentage, which might be available, should be kept open for meritorious performance regardless of University, State and the like - University-wise preferential treatment might still be consistent with the rule of equality of opportunity where it was calculated to correct an imbalance or handicap and permit equality in the larger sense - Even apart from arts. 15(3) and (4) of Constitution, that equality was not negated or neglected where special provisions were geared to larger goal of the disabled getting over their disablement consistently with the general good and individual merit - Indeed, art. 14 of Constitution implied all that, in its wider connotation, and had to inform the interpretation of art. 15 of Constitution - If university-wise classification for post-graduate medical education was shown to be relevant and reasonable and the differential had a nexus to larger goal of equalisation of educational opportunities the vice of discrimination might not invalidate the rule - Neither Delhi nor the Delhi University medical colleges could be designated as categories which warrant reservation - Reservation for Delhi graduates was not that invidious because, the students were from families drawn from all over India - Not 'sons of the soil' but sons and daughters of persons who were willy nilly pulled into the capital city for reasons beyond their control - That reservation was, thus, qualitatively different - Institution-wise reservation was constitutionally circumscribed and might become ultra vires if recklessly resorted to - But even such rules until revised by competent authority or struck down judicially, would rule the roost - That was why Court has to concede that until the signpost of 'no admission for outsiders' was removed from other universities and some fair percentage of seats in other universities was left for open competition the Delhi students could not be made martyrs of the Constitution - Even so, 'reservation' should be administered in moderation, if it was to be constitutional - Judicial surmise was too weak to be of decisional certainty - 70% plus was too high at the post-graduate level in the half-proved circumstances - Court was stop short of invalidating the rule because the facts were imperfect, course has already started -Judges should not rush in where specialists fear to tread - Impugned regulation even though, prima facie, skeptical about the vires thereof - To doubt was not enough to demolish - When fuller facts were placed, Court would go into that question more confidently - Thus, it was directed that, petitioner, going by marks, deserve admission to postgraduate degree course this year, if rules of attendance etc., did not stand in way and Medical Council makes an exception by agreeing to addition of 1 seat as a special case for the year - It was directed that to appoint a time- bound committee to investigate in depth the justification for and quantum of reservation at the post-graduate level from the angle of equality of opportunity for every Indian but considering other constitutionally relevant criteria - Petition dismissed. PATHAK, J. Held, it was not beyond reason that a student who enters a medical college for his graduate studies and pursues them for the requisite period of years should prefer on graduation to continue in the same institution for his post-graduate studies - There was good reason in an educational institution extending a certain degree of preference to its graduate for admission to its post- graduate classes - Preference was based on a reasonable classification and bears a just relationship to the object of education provided in postgraduate classes - Concept of equality codified in our constitutional system was not violated - It was said sometimes that classification contradict equality - Classification was a feature of very core of equality - It was a vital concept in ensuring equality, for those who were similarly situated alone form a class between themselves, and classification was not vulnerable to challenge if its constituent basis was reasonably related to achieving the object of concerned law - An institutional preference of the kind considered here did not offend the constitutional guarantee of equality - Is a reservation of 70% excessive, material was so scanty, fragmentary and unsatisfactory that Court prevented from expressing any definite decision on that point - Although sufficient opportunity was given to parties, requisite material was not come out - Whether or not a reservation of 70% was called for was not established conclusively - There was hardly anything to show that authorities applied their mind to a cool dispassionate judgment of problem facing them - Unfortunately, there was little evidence of in instant case - Petition dismissed.
