Pradesh Kumar Bajpai v Binod Behari Sarkar

Supreme Court of India

21 March 1980

Case Analysis

Bench

P.S. Kailasam, S. Murtaza Fazal Ali, A.D. Koshal

Where Reported

1980 Indlaw SC 130; (1980) 3 SCC 348; AIR 1980 SC 1214; 1980 ALJ 549; 1980 (6) ALR 241; 1980 (2) RCR(Rent) 369; 1980 (1) RentLR 579; [1980] 3 S.C.R. 93; 1980 UJ 435

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Uttar Pradesh (Temporary) Control of Rent and Eviction Act, 1947

Summary: Rent Control - Uttar Pradesh (Temporary) Control of Rent and Eviction Act, ss. 3 and 3(1) - Transfer of Property Act, 1882, s. 114 - Default in payment of rent - Decree of eviction - Legality - Appellant plaintiff let out a portion of premises to respondent defendant for carrying on his business of manufacturing blocks and other printing materials - Appellant filed suit on ground that respondent had defaulted in payment of rent and prayed for a decree for ejectment - Trial Court dismissed the suit for possession, however, decreed the suit for arrears of rent - On appeal, District Court allowed the appeal holding that tenant was a defaulter within meaning of s. 3 of the 1947 Act - Respondent preferred appeal before HC - HC held that premises were taken for manufacturing purposes in 1955 and on consideration of terms of lease deed of 1955 it found that 6 months' notice was necessary even if premises were not taken for manufacturing purposes and as required notice of 6 months was not given suit was not maintainable and as such liable to be dismissed - Aggrieved, appellant preferred instant appeal - Whether HC was justified in passing the impugned order - Held, notice was based on default of payment of rent for more than 3 months, and called upon respondent to vacate premises a month after receipt of notice if he failed to pay rent as required u/s. 3 of the Act - However, notice was not based on forfeiture of lease under cl. 12 - If cl. 12 was valid on default of payment of rent for 2 months, appellant was entitled to enter into possession forthwith without notice terminating lease - If relief provided for under the section was available, as lessee had tendered rent in arrears along with interest thereon and his full costs in suit, it was open to Court to pass an order relieving lessee against forfeiture - It was not in dispute that after the 1947 Act came into force, landlord cannot avail himself of cl. 12 which provided for forfeiture, even if tenant neglected to pay rent for over 2 months - Landlord could not enter into possession forth with without notice - Only remedy for him was to seek eviction under the provisions of the 1947 Act - Tenant could not rely on s. 114 of the 1882 Act and claim that he should be given opportunity to pay arrears of rent, even though requirements of s.3(1) of the 1947 Act had fulfilled - Once the requirements of 1947 Act were satisfied, tenant could not claim double protection of invoking provisions of the 1882 Act or terms of contract - Impugned order passed by HC was set aside and restored the order of Appellate Court - Appeal allowed.
