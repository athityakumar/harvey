Kasturi Lal Lakshmi Reddy v State Of Jammu And Kashmir & Another

Supreme Court of India

09 May 1980

Case Analysis

Bench

P.N. Bhagwati, V.D. Tulzapurkar, R.S. Pathak

Where Reported

1980 Indlaw SC 186; (1980) 4 SCC 1; AIR 1980 SC 1992; [1980] 3 S.C.R. 1338

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Constitution - Contract & Commercial - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 19, 19(1), 21 and 32 - Government's discretion in grant of Largesse - Challenged - The State of Jammu & Kashmir started tapping operation in respect of its Chir trees since about 1973 by giving contracts to private parties for extraction and collection of resin - Said contracts were of 3 types i.e. wage basis, royalty basis and auction basis - Every year the State auctioned the blazes of the different forests within its territory and about 40% of the forests were given on royalty basis, some with load and some without load, while the balance of about 60% were given on wage contract basis - Most of the contractors bidding at the auction were having their factories outside Jammu & Kashmir - The State, however, in furtherance of its policy to bring about rapid industrialisation, decided that from the year 1979-80 onwards, the resin extracted from its forests should not be allowed to be exported outside the territories of the State and should be utilized only by industries set up within the State - Subsequently, 2nd respondent had addressed a letter to the Minister for Industries, offering to set up a factory for manufacture of resin, turpentine oil and other derivatives the State "with the latest know-how under the supervision of the State Government" and seeking allotment of 10,000 metric tonnes of resin annually for that purpose - The State Government by an order dt. 27.04.1979 sanctioned allotment of 11.85 lacs blazes in the inaccessible areas of Rasi, Ramban and Poonch Divisions to the 2nd respondent for a period of 10 years - Petitioners (Associations of Local Manufacturers) filed writ petition u/art. 32 of the Constitution challenging said order of State Government contending that the impugned order was arbitrary, mala fide and not in public interest, inasmuch as a huge benefit has been conferred on the 2nd respondents at the cost of the State - Petitioners also contended that the said order creates monopoly in favour of the 2nd respondent who was a private party and constitutes unreasonable restriction on the right of the petitioners to carry on tapping contract business u/art. 19(1)(g) of the Constitution - Whether order of State Government sanctioning allotment of 11.85 lacs blazes in the inaccessible areas to the 2nd respondent for a period of 10 years was arbitrary and irrational? - Held, the Court cannot lightly assume that the action taken by the Government was unreasonable or without public interest because there were a large number of policy considerations which must necessarily weigh with the Government in taking action and therefore the Court would not strike down governmental action as invalid on this ground, unless it was clearly satisfied that the action was unreasonable or not in public interest - On the other hand, the Government was also not free like an ordinary individual, in selecting the recipients for its largess and it cannot choose to deal with any person it pleases in its absolute and unfettered discretion - In the instant case, it was not a tapping contract simpliciter which was intended to be given to the 2nd respondent - The whole object of the impugned order was to make available 3500 metric tonnes of resin to the 2nd respondent for the purpose of running the factory to be set up by them - The advantage to the State was that a new factory for manufacture of resin, turpentine oil and other derivatives would come up within its territories offering more job opportunities to the State and increasing their prosperity and augmenting the State revenues and in addition the State would be assured of a definite supply of at least 1500 metric tonnes of resin for itself without any financial involvement or risk and with this additional quantity of resin available to it, it would be able to set up another factory creating more employment opportunities - Moreover, no huge benefit was conferred on the 2nd respondent at the cost of the State as the terms of the contract made it clear that the 2nd respondent would have to extract at least 5000 metric tonnes of resin from the blazes allotted to them in order to be entitled to retain 3500 metric tonnes - Hence, impugned order neither created any monopoly in favour of the 2nd respondent nor imposed reasonable restriction on the right of the petitioners to carry on tapping business u/art. 19(1)(g) of the Act - Writ petitions dismissed.
