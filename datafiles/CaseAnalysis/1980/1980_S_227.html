Kusa and Others v State of Orissa

Supreme Court of India

17 January 1980

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Murtaza Fazal Ali, A.D. Koshal

Where Reported

1980 Indlaw SC 596; (1980) 2 SCC 207; (1980) SCC (Cr) 389; AIR 1980 SC 559; 1980 CRLJ 200; 1980 CrLR(SC) 200; [1980] 2 S.C.R. 801

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Murder - Dying Declaration - Corroboration - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 and 149 - HC convicted appellants u/ss. 302/149 - Conviction based on dying declaration - Challenged - Whenever a particular view taken by authors of medical jurisprudence is adumbrated, same must be put to doctor to assess how far view taken by experts apply to facts of particular case - Last certificate given by doctor towards end of dying declaration that patient became semi-unconscious clearly shows that deceased was fully conscious when he started making dying declaration before doctor - (A) Whether dying declaration was incomplete? - Held, a perusal of entire dying declaration would clearly show that doctor had asked all necessary questions that could be asked from deceased and last question was merely in nature of a formality and therefore unable to hold that present dying declaration is an incomplete one - (B) Whether dying declaration unless corroborated should not be acted upon? - Held, if statement of dying person passes test of careful scrutiny applied by Courts, it becomes a most reliable piece of evidence which does not require any corroboration - Although a dying declaration should be carefully scrutinised but if after perusal of same, Court is satisfied that dying declaration is true and is free from any effort to prompt deceased to make a statement and is coherent and consistent, there is no legal impediment in founding conviction on such a dying declaration even if there is no corroboration - Opinion of HC that even excluding evidence of eye-witnesses, dying declaration is true and reliable and sufficient to found conviction of appellant is correct - Appeal dismissed.
