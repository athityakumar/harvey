State of Karnataka and Another v Messrs Hansa Corporation

Supreme Court of India

25 September 1980

Case Analysis

Bench

D.A. Desai, Y.V. Chandrachud

Where Reported

1980 Indlaw SC 199; (1980) 4 SCC 697; AIR 1981 SC 463; [1981] 1 S.C.R. 823

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Indirect Tax; Municipalities & Local Governments

Keywords: Karnataka Tax on Entry of Goods into Local Areas for Consumption Use or Sale Therein Act, 1979

Summary: Constitution - Indirect Tax - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 226, 14, 304(a), 304(b) and 255 - Karnataka Municipalities Act, 1964 - Karnataka Tax on Entry of Goods into Local Areas for Consumption Use or Sale therein Act, 1979, s. 3 (Act) - (A) Whether Act is violative of art. 14 of the Constitution? - Held, s. 3 permits State Govt. only to specify different rates of tax not exceeding maximum prescribed in section to be levied on entry of scheduled goods into a local area but State Govt. has no power to pick and choose local area - Contended that on a grammatical construction, that discretion was conferred upon State Govt. not only to specify rate but also local area - There is a two-fold answer to contention that upon a literal grammatical construction of s. 3 State has no choice in matter of selecting local areas and that choice is limited to specifying rates but after choosing rates all local areas will have to be covered for levy of tax - Last expression in section 'different rates may be specified for different local areas' would be an adjectival clause to word 'rate' so that power to choose and specify different rates is not implicit in words 'such rate' but in expression 'different rates may be specified for different local areas' - Thus, an express power of choosing and specifying different rates subject to maximum for different local areas is conferred on State Govt. not by expression 'such rate' but by expression 'rates' with adjectival clause 'different rates may be specified for different local areas' - Therefore, not necessary to qualify expression 'such rate' again by expression 'as may be specified by State Govt.' because that is covered by express power conferred by expression 'different rates may be specified for different local areas' - There is thus, a power to choose and specify local areas as well as choose and specify rate of taxation subject to maximum prescribed in section - Choice to select local area is a necessary concomitant of a choice to select rates, which power is admittedly conferred on State Govt. - There is always a presumption of constitutionality of a statute - Impugned measure is a taxing statute and in matter of taxing statute legislature enjoys a larger discretion in matter of classification, so long as, it adheres to fundamental principle underlying doctrine of classification - Legislature which is competent to levy a tax must inevitably be given full freedom to determine which articles should be taxed, in what manner and at what rate - Classification in this case was reasonable - Non-exemption of petty dealers from operation of Act does not lead to conclusion that impugned legislation constitutes unreasonable restrictions on fundamental right of petty dealers to carry on their trade or business - (B) Whether Act violative of Freedom of Trade under art. 301? - Held, art. 304 lifts embargo placed on legislative power of State to enact law which may infringe freedom of inter-State trade and commerce if its requirements are fulfilled - If a State tax law accords identical treatment in matter of levy and collection of tax on goods manufactured within State and identical goods imported from outside State, art. 304(a) would be complied with - This tax is non-discriminatory in that it does not discriminate between scheduled goods manufactured or produced within Karnataka State or those imported from outside - Impugned tax is not discriminatory in character as envisaged by art. 304(a) and it does impose restrictions but restrictions imposed are reasonable and in public interest and Act subsequently having received assent of President, proviso to art. 304(b) is complied with and, therefore, impugned Act is saved by art. 304 and could not be struck down on ground that it was violative of art. 301 - Act is not unconstitutional - Appeal allowed.
