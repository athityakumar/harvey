Mahalaxmi Mills Limited v Commissioner of Income Tax, Bombay

Supreme Court of India

08 April 1960

Case Analysis

Bench

K.C. Das Gupta

Where Reported

1960 Indlaw SC 210; AIR 1967 SC 266; [1963] 50 ITR 741; 1964 (1) SCJ 23; [1964] 5 S.C.R. 216

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Indian Income Tax Act, 1922 - Profits - Deduction - Intention - Claim - Provision - In assessing profits of business by the two appellant companies for the year 1949-50 - Income-tax Officer had to proceed in accordance with the provisions of this Ordinance - For the purpose of calculating depreciation allowance to which the assessee was entitled in computing the profits or gains of the business the written down value of the building machinery and plants or furniture had first to be ascertained in accordance with S. 13(5) of the Ordinance - As the assets of both the assesses had been acquired before the previous year s. 13(5) (b) applied- Whether written down value has to be computed by deduction from the actual cost of depreciation allowance which was allowable under the, 1922 Act even though not claimed - Held, words used are apt and sufficient to express the intention that if the 1922 Act which was not in force in the State before had been in force the depreciation that would have been allowed if proper claim had been made should be deducted in ascertaining the written down value - Court was not called upon to consider the effect of the words under our present consideration, viz., the depreciation which would have been allowed if the 1922 Act had been in force - For the assessment years 1951-52 the controversy arises in a different way - In 1950, Saurashtra became a Part B State of the Union of India- So, in calculating the written down value of assets acquired before the previous year the Income-tax Officer had to apply the provisions of s. 10(5) (b) of the 1922 Act - The words any law relating to tax would also have been omitted and this part of the paragraph would have read as all depreciation actually allowed under any laws or rules of a Part B State relating to Income-tax and super-tax or tax on profits of business - If the intention had not been to include the depreciation allowed under a law which had been law in a component part of the Part B State before it became included in the Part B State it was unnecessary to add the words or any law relating to tax on profits of business- This appears to have been done with the deliberate intention of including depreciation allowed under such laws even though they were not laws of a Part B State but of a component State - Appeals dismissed.
