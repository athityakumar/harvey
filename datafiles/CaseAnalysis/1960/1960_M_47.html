 Messrs Universal Imports Agencyand Another v Chief Controller of Importsand Exports and Others

Supreme Court of India

23 August 1960

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Subba Rao, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, Syed Jaffer Imam, A.K. Sarkar, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1960 Indlaw SC 222; AIR 1961 SC 41; [1961] 1 S.C.R. 305

Case Digest

Subject: Customs

Keywords: Importation, General Clauses Act, Foreign Exchange Regulation Act, Customs Act, Tariff Act, 1934, Foreign Jurisdiction Act, 1947, Confiscation Fine, Sea Customs Act 1878, Bombay Prohibition Act 1949, Import and Export (Control) Act. 1947, repealing Act

Summary: Customs - Indo-French Agreement - Defacto transfer of entire administration of French Settlements in India with Govt. of India from D/- 01-11-1954(Merger) - Foreign Jurisdiction Act, 1947, s. 4 - Notification thereunder, D/- 30-10-1954 - French Establishments' (Application of Laws) Order, 1954 - Sea Customs Act, 1879 - Reserve Bank of India Act, 1934 - Imports and Exports Trade (Control) Act, 1947 - Foreign Exchange Regulation Act, 1947 and Indian Tariff Act, 1934, were extended to Pondicherry - Pondicherry a French establishment merged with India under agreement - Entering of contracts by petitioners, of sales by import with foreign sellers before merger, foreign exchange made available under Letters of Credit or otherwise and goods were shipped either before or after merger, though reached destination after merger - (A) Whether confiscation of goods on their arrival at port of Pondicherry by Collector of Customs and Central Excise with option to pay penalties in lieu of confiscation, justified? - Held, saving clause in Order 'save as respect things done or omitted to be done before such commencement'(of notification) - (i) Question arose, whether transactions in question entered into by petitioners with foreign sellers were 'things done' within meaning of such saving clause and thus saved from operation of Order - Para 6 of Order thus interpreted by Court - Court imposed three questions upon itself, firstly, what were laws specified in Schedule? - Secondly, what were laws in force in French Establishments before commencement of Order corresponding to enactments so specified? - Thirdly, what were 'things done' or omitted to be done under said laws? - (i) Finding, words 'things done' in para 6 can mean not only things done but also legal consequences flowing there from thus, such words are comprehensive enough to take in a transaction effected before merger, though some of its legal effects and consequences projected into post-merger period - (ii) Second question arose, as to what is inter-relation between said 'things done' and act of import or bringing of goods into India? - Connotation of words 'in course of import or export' in a 286 of Constitution, 1950 as considered in a landmark case, referred to - (ii) Finding, imports in question, therefore, were effect or legal consequence of 'things done', i.e., contracts entered into by petitioners with foreign dealers - Held, para 6 of Order saves transactions entered into by petitioners and that respondents had no right to confiscate their goods on ground that they were imported without license - Per majority judgment, petitions allowed.
