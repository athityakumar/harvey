 Kunnathat Thathunni Moopil Nair v State of Kerala and Another (with connected petitions)

Supreme Court of India

09 December 1960

Case Analysis

Bench

Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, Syed Jaffer Imam, A.K. Sarkar, K. Subba Rao, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1960 Indlaw SC 235; AIR 1961 SC 552; 1961 (2) SCJ 269; [1961] 3 S.C.R. 77

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property

Keywords: Bombay Prohibition Act 1949, Travancore Cochin Land Tax Act, 1955, Travancore- Cochin Land Tax Act, 1955

Summary: Sinha, C.J., Imam, Subba Rao and Shah, JJ

(1) Land & Property - Administrative - Constitution - Travancore-Cochin Land Tax Act, 1955, ss. 5A, 7 - Madras Preservation of Private Forests Act, 1949 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 19, 31 - Constitutionality of 1955 Act - Petitioner was the owner of the forest land in question where 1949 Act was applicable before the State re-organization - District Collector, after the re-organization of the State, purporting to act under the provisions of s. 5A of the 1955 Act, issued a notice to the petitioner provisionally assessing the petitioner's forest under the said Act to a sum of fifty thousand rupees per annum and informing the petitioner that, if no representation was made within thirty days, the said provisional assessment would be confirmed and a demand notice would be issued - Hence petitioner challenged the validity of 1955 Act as amended in 1957 in the instant petition - Whether the provisions of 1955 Act was in violation of petitioner's rights under arts. 14, 19 and 31 of the Constitution - Held, 1955 Act obliged every person who held land to pay the tax at the flat rate prescribed, whether or not he made any income out of the property, or whether or not the property was capable of yielding any income - No attempt was made by respondent authority to make classification in the provisions of the Act and it was one of those cases where the lack of classification created inequality - It was therefore hit by the prohibition to deny equality before the law contained in art. 14 - S. 5A of the Act, which enabled the Government to make a provisional assessment of the basic tax payable by the holder of un-surveyed land imposed unreasonable restrictions on the rights to hold property safeguarded by art. 19(1)(f) of the Constitution - 1955 Act did not impose an obligation on the Government to undertake survey proceedings within any prescribed or ascertainable period, with the result that a landholder might be subjected to repeated annual provisional assessments on more or less conjectural basis and liable to pay the tax assessed - 1955 Act being silent as to the machinery and procedure to be followed in making the assessment left it to the Executive, completely ignoring the legal position that the assessment of a tax on a person or property was at least of a quasi-judicial character - S. 7 of the Act which vested the Government with the power wholly or partially to exempt any land from the provisions of the Act did not lay down any principle or policy for the guidance of the exercise of discretion by the Government in respect of the selection contemplated by the section, and was, therefore, discriminatory in effect and offended art. 14 - S. 7 was not severable from the rest of the Act as both the charging ss. 4 and 7, authorizing the Government to grant exemptions from the provisions of the Act were the main provisions of the statute - 1955 Act was also confiscatory in character inasmuch as the provisions ofthe Act had the effect of eliminating the private owners through the machinery of the Act, without proposing to acquire the privately owned forests in the State after satisfying the conditions laid down in art. 31 of the Constitution - Further, art. 265 of the Constitution which provided that the State should not levy or collect a tax except by authority of law referred to a valid law, and in order that the law might be valid, the tax proposed to be levied should be within the legislative competence of the Legislature imposing a tax and authorizing the collection thereof - Tax should be subject to the conditions laid down in art. 13, by which all laws inconsistent with or in derogation of the fundamental rights in Part III should be void - Hence, 1955 Act infringed the provisions of art. 14 of the Constitution - Petition disposed of.

Sarkar, J

(2) Land & Property - Administrative - Constitution - Travancore-Cochin Land Tax Act, 1955, ss. 5A, 7 - Madras Preservation of Private Forests Act, 1949 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 19, 31 - Constitutionality of 1955 Act - Held, object of the Act was to tax land in the State for raising revenues by providing for a low and uniform rate of basic tax replacing all other dues payable to the Government and the tax payers were classified according to the area of lands held by them - Such a classification had an intelligible basis and had a rational relation to the object of the Act - As tax was to be levied not because the land was productive but because the land was held in the State, the classification did not offend art. 14 of the Constitution, even though it might impose unequal burden of the tax on the owners of land on account of owners of less productive land being put on a larger burden - S. 5A did not offend art. 14 and in the absence of express provisions laying down the procedure according to which the provisional assessment was to be made, the Act could not be held invalid on the ground that it was against the rules of natural justice - S. 7, even if it were considered invalid on the ground that it gave arbitrary power to the Government and offended art. 14, was severable from the rest of the Act and would not affect the other provisions of the Act - Act did not infringe the fundamental rights in art. 19(1)(f) as the rate of tax fixed by the Act was a very low rate and the restrictions on those rights were reasonable - Act was not in its nature expropriatary and did not offend art. 31 - As there was no want of legislative competence, the Act could not be assailed as a piece of colourable legislation on the ground that though in form a taxing statute it, in effect, was intended to expropriate lands by imposing a tax too heavy for the land to bear - Word 'land' in Entry 49 of List II, Sch. 7, of the Constitution, included 'land on which a forest stands' and, therefore, under that Entry taxation on land on which forests stood was permissible and legal - 1955Act, therefore, could not be challenged as being beyond the legislative competence of the State Legislature -Petition disposed of.
