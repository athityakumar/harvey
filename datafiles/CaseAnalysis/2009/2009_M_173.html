Mahesh Chandra Gupta v Union Of India & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

06 July 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

S.H. Kapadia, Aftab Alam

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 1106; (2009) 8 SCC 273; 2009 (5) ALD(SC) 18; JT 2009 (9) SC 199; 2009(9) SCALE 206; [2009] 10 S.C.R. 921

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution

Keywords: Right To Practise, Enrolment As An Advocate, Professional Conduct And Etiquette, Enrolment, Practicing, State Bar Councils, Admission And Enrolment Of Advocates, Common Roll Of Advocates, Indian Bar Councils Act, 1926, Indian Nursing Council Act, 1947, Functions Of The Bar Council Of India, Legal Practitioners Act, 1879, Appeal To The Bar Council Of India, Transferred case, dismissed

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts.217(2)(b) r/w expl. (aa) and 217(1) - Advocates Act, 1961, s.17 - Indian Bar Councils Act, 1926, s.2 - Appointment of Additional Judge of High Court - Eligibility - Process of consultation under Constitution - Doctrine of Judicial Review - Writ petition filed challenging appointment of respondent no. 3 as Additional Judge of Allahabad High Court on ground that he was not eligible for such an appointment - Writ of quo warranto prayed for - Further ground of challenge respondent no. 3 was not a suitable candidate and that there was want of effective consultation process as provided under Constitution - (A) Contention raised that for qualifying u/art.217(2)(b) r/w expl. (aa) of Constitution, a person who has held a Judicial Office or office of Member of a Tribunal for more than ten years, but has not practiced as an Advocate for even a day though enrolled as an Advocate, cannot be said to be eligible for appointment as HC Judge; mere enrolment which gives 'a right to practice' is not enough to make a person eligible u/art. 217(2)(b) - Contention whether acceptable? - Held, no - Provisions of art. 217(1) and (2) analysed - Whether 'actual practice' as against 'right to practice' is the pre-requisite constitutional requirement of eligibility criteria u/art. 217(2)(b)? - Court noted and deliberated upon basic difference between 'eligibility' and 'suitability' - Process of judging the fitness of a person for appointed as a HC Judge falls in realm of 'suitability'; consultation is one of such process - While eligibility at threshold stage comes u/art. 217(2)(b) - Consultation finds place in art. 217(1) whereas the word 'qualify' finds place in art. 217(2) prescribing a threshold limit for a person to qualify to be a HC Judge - Art. 217(1) provides the procedure to be followed prior to a person being appointed as a HC Judge - Art. 217(1) and 217(2) operate in different spheres; the former answers question as to who 'should be elevated' whereas later deals with question as to who 'could be elevated' - Enrolment of an advocate under 1961 Act comes in category of who 'could be elevated' whereas number of years of 'actual practice', which is a significant factor, comes in category of who 'should be elevated' - Held further, concept of 'actual practice' will fall u/art. 217(1) whereas concept of 'right or entitlement to practice' will fall u/art. 217(2)(b) - (i) Expression in art. 217(2)(b), 'an advocate of a High Court' - Meaning thereof - Legal history of Indian Bar Councils Act, 1926, Legal Practitioners Act, 1879 and Advocates Act, 1961, noted - All the Acts deal with a person's right or entitlement to practice - 1961 Act only seeks to create a common 'Bar' consisting of one class of members, namely, Advocates - Held, expression 'an advocate of a HC' as understood, both, pre and post 1961, referred to person(s) right to practice - Thus, actual practice cannot be read into qualification provision, namely, art. 217(2)(b) - Legal implication of 1961 Act, which is holding the field currently, is that any person whose name is enrolled on State Bar Council would be regarded as 'an advocate of the High Court' - (B) Contention against appointment raised on basis of lack of effective consultation - Contention raised that reliable information withheld by CJ of Allahabad HC from Supreme Court Collegium; withholding of information would constitute a ground of Judicial Review - Contention whether acceptable? - Held, no - Confidential files meticulously scrutinized - Relevant information before Supreme Court; such information closely vetted - Recommendation of HC Collegium for appointment sent back by Supreme Court Collegium to HC Collegium for reconsideration - Matter re-examined by HC Collegium and it once again recommended name of respondent no. 3 for appointment as HC Judge - Held further, there was effective consultation; since consultation process stood complied with, its content was not amenable to Judicial Review - (i) Whether 'consultation process' provided u/art. 217(1), subject to Judicial Review? - Held, Judicial Review lies only in two cases, namely, 'lack of eligibility' and 'lack of effective consultation'; it will not lie on content of consultation - P/&gt;Hence, respondent no. 3 satisfied qualifications prescribed u/art. 217(2)(b) as he had worked as a Member of Income Tax Appellate Tribunal for 11 years; and he was enrolled as an Advocate of HC in 1975 - Transferred case, dismissed.
