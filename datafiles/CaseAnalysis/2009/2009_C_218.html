University Of Kerala v Council, Principals', Colleges, Kerala & Others

Supreme Court of India

11 November 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

Markandey Katju, A.K. Ganguly

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 1562; (2010) 1 SCC 353; AIR 2010 SC 2532; JT 2009 (14) SC 283; 2010 (3) MLJ(SC) 531; 2009(14) SCALE 14; [2009] 15 S.C.R. 800

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Summary: Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19(1)(a), 32, 141, 142 and 226 - Judicial legislation - Constitutional validity - Doctrine of separation of powers - Writ Petition was filed by Council of Principals of Colleges in Kerala challenging various directions issued by universities in State of Kerala to conduct election to colleges' unions - HC by impugned order, allowed writ petition - Present appeal - During pendency of proceedings in pursuance of SC directions a Committee was constituted headed by former Chief Election Commissioner - SC directed implementation of report of Committee as an interim measure - Whether after getting recommendations of some expert body by a court order, Court itself can implement said recommendations by passing a judicial order or whether Court can only send it to the Legislature or its delegate to consider making a law for implementation of those recommendations? - Justice Katju held that interim order of SC prima facie, amounts to judicial legislation therefore, SC instead of passing a judicial order should have directed implementation of recommendations by sending it to the appropriate Legislature or its delegate - While Justice Asok Kumar Ganguly, was of view that conferment of judicial power u/art. 141, 142, 32 and 226 of Constitution are plenary and very wide and enable SC to declare law which shall be binding on all courts within territories of India and art. 142 of Constitution enables Supreme Court to pass such order as is required to do complete justice in case -Therefore matter referred to Constitutional Bench to decide 1 Whether Court by an interim order can validly direct implementation of Lyngdoh Committee's Report; 2 Whether order amounts to judicial legislation; 3 Whether under Constitution judiciary can legislate, and if so, what is permissible limits of judicial legislation 4 Whether judiciary can legislate when in its opinion there is a pressing social problem of public interest or it can only make a recommendation to legislature or concerned authority in this connection; and 5 Whether art. 19(1)(c) and other fundamental rights are being violated when restrictions are being placed by implementation of Lyngdoh Committee report without authority of law 6 What is scope of art. 141 and 142 of Constitution? Do they permit judiciary to legislate and/or perform functions of executive wing of State - Matter referred to Constitution Bench.
