Katari Suryanarayana & Ors v Koppisetti Subba Rao & Ors

Supreme Court of India

08 April 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, Mukundakam Sharma

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 432; (2009) 11 SCC 183; 2009 (4) ADJ 18; AIR 2009 SC 2907; 2009 (75) ALR 457; 2009 (1) ARC 810; 2009 (2) AWC 2003; 2009 (4) CTC 286; 2009 (3) G.L.R. 2637; 2009 (3) JhrCR(SC) 100; 2009 (4) MLJ(SC) 442; 2009 (3) RCR(Civil) 63; 2009 (107) RD 193B; 2009(5) SCALE 238; [2009] 5 S.C.R. 672

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Insolvency Of Parties, Several Defendants, Effect Of Abatement

Summary: Civil Procedure - Practice and Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 22 rr. 3, 9, 10A - Indian Limitation Act, 1963, s. 5 - Delay - Condonation of - Application for substitution - Abatement of Appeal - (A) Whether O. 22 r. 10A of the CPC mandating the counsel of the deceased to duly inform the Court in regard to their clients passing away? - (B) Whether a liberal view in the matter of condonation of delay should be taken in regard to application of O. 22 r. 9 in a civil suit where the parties are required to appear on each and every date of hearing and a Second Appeal and an appeal as the same where the matter is listed after a few years ? - It is furthermore neither in doubt nor in dispute that applications for substitution should be considered liberally - The Court would take a more liberal attitude in the matter of condonation of delay in filing such an application - There are, however, exceptions to the said rule - It may be true that a distinction exists where an application for setting aside of the abatement is filed in a suit and the one which is required to be filed in a second appeal before the High Court but the same by itself may not be sufficient to arrive at a conclusion that the parties were not aware of the consequences thereof - Appellants themselves rely on the provisions of O. XXII r. 10A of the CPC, which was inserted by reason of Code of Civil Procedure (Amendment) Act, 1976 - It does not, however, provide for consequences - It does not take away the duty on the part of the plaintiff or the appellant, as the case may be, to file an application for condonation of delay in bringing on record the heirs and legal representatives of a deceased plaintiff/appellant or defendant/respondent within the period prescribed - Held, it is not in dispute that the appellants were neighbours - Appellants were co-sharers - The respective dates of death of the respondent Nos.2 and 3, thus, were known to them - It is difficult to conceive that the petitioners were not in touch with their learned advocates from 1999 to December 2006 - If not every week, they are expected to contact their lawyers once in a year - Ignorance of legal consequence without something more would be not sufficient to condone such a huge delay - Appellants are literates - They have been fighting their cases for a long time - The HC in its impugned judgment has categorically arrived at a finding that no sufficient cause has been shown for the purpose of condonation of delay in bringing on record the names of the heirs or legal representatives of the deceased respondent Nos.2 and 3. - Appellants have pleaded about the intimation from their counsel - There is nothing on record to show whether the said intimation was written or oral - Appeal dismissed.
