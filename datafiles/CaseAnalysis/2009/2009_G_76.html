Global Energy Ltd. & Anr. v Central Electricity Regulatory Commission

Supreme Court of India

11 May 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, Cyriac Joseph

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 769; (2009) 15 SCC 570; AIR 2009 SC 3194; 2009 (4) JhrCR(SC) 88; 2009(8) SCALE 108; [2009] 9 S.C.R. 22

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Electricity & Energy

Keywords: Doctrine Of Proportionality, Presumption In Favour Of Constitutionality, Constitutionality, Himachal Pradesh Town and Country Planning Act, 1977, Monopoly Of The State, Parliament enacted Electricity Act, 2003, Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (Procedure, Terms and Conditions for Grant of Trading License and other related matters) (Amendment), Regulation, 2006

Summary: Electricity & Energy - Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (Procedure, Terms and Conditions for Grant of Trading Licences and other related matters), Regulation, 2004 - Central Electricity Regulatory Commission (Procedure, Terms and Conditions for Grant of Trading Licence and other related matters) (Amendment), Regulation, 2006, reg. 6A(b)(f) - Electricity Act, 2003, s. 178, 2(71), 16 and 52 - Central Electricity Regulatory Commission - Regulations framed by Commission in exercise of its jurisdiction conferred by s. 178 of Act - Constitutional validity of cls. (b) and (f) of reg. 6A is in issue - Held, cls. (b) and (f) of reg. 6A to be unconstitutional - Appellant applied for inter-State trading licence under Category 'A' before CERC, and while the pendency of consideration of such application, he applied for interim issuance of licence, which was granted - In the meanwhile Amendment Regulation was passed and was applied retrospectively - CERC rejected the application filed by the appellant no. 1 after applying reg. 6A - Finally High Court dismissed the petition filed by appellant no. 1 - Court considered propriety and constitutionality of generic decision-making process encapsulated under the impugned legislation - Object and scheme of Act, its provisions, provisions of both the Regulations perused; contentions heard - Reg. 6A is imperative in character, is couched in negative language and provides for disqualifications; indisputably, a subordinate legislation should be read in the context of the Act; thus read, it should be construed in terms of the requirements contained in s. 52 of the Act; reg. 6A affected creditworthiness, credit effectiveness, viz. financial integrity, competence, reputation and character, efficiency and honesty, pending proceeding of appellant; well settled principle of law that the rule making power 'for carrying out the purpose of the Act', is a general delegation and such a delegation may not be held to be laying down any guidelines; thus by reason of such a provision alone, the regulation making power cannot be exercised so as to bring into existence substantive rights or obligations or disabilities which are not contemplated in terms of the provisions of the Act - A legislative policy providing for qualification/disqualification should not be vague or uncertain; parameters must be laid down; exp. to cl. (f) of reg. 6A points out various aspects that may be considered while determining the said criteria; however what should be the criteria have not been defined; how and in what manner the said criteria are required to be ascertained have not been laid down; 'essential legislative function cannot be delegated' - Factors enumerated in the exp. to (f) of reg. 6A found to be unlimited - Larger object of the Act noted by Court: to bring in transparency in the work of the public sector, competition from the private sector, consequent development of proper market in the long run; thus power of CERC to impose qualification/restrictions should be read in line with it - A trader of electricity does not deal with consumers; he is merely an intermediary between a generating company and a distribution licencee; tariff to be charged from consumers is regulated; even the margin that a trader can make is regulated; thus contention that reg. 6A is in consumer interest is incorrect as it has not been shown that how it will protect the consumer interest, Court stressed upon - Cl. (b), reg. 6A provides for disqualification only on ground of involvement of the applicant or any of its promoters, partners etc., in any legal proceeding; Court noted that same may or may not by itself be sufficient to disqualify a person, but the Commission must be satisfied that grant of licence in the circumstance may adversely affect the interest of the electricity sector or of the consumers; Court on this, pointed out that it cannot by itself be an objective criteria, as by reason thereof the statutory authority would necessarily be required to enter into the merit of the proceeding - Doctrine of legitimate expectation, Court found from the facts that appellant no. 1 had a legitimate expectation as he had applied for grant of licence and was found to be qualified therefor having satisfied the statutory requirements, it was granted an interim licence, it had started trading in electricity; expectation, that in considering the application, same criteria as laid down in the statute shall be applied - Court at the end deliberated out of concern, that the law sometimes can be written in such subjective manner that it affects efficiency and transparent function of the Govt.; if the statute provides for pointless discretion to agency, it is in essence demolishing the accountability strand within the administrative process as the agency is not under obligation from an objective norm, which can enforce accountability in decision-making process; all law making, be it in the context of delegated legislation or primary legislation, have to conform to the fundamental tenets of 'transparency and openness' on one hand and 'responsiveness and accountability' on the other - In the context of rule making, delegated legislation should establish the structural conditions within which those processes can function effectively; the question which needs to be asked is whether delegated legislation promotes rational and accountable policy implementation - Court posits that when the provision inherently perpetuates injustice in the award of licences and brings uncertainty and arbitrariness it would be best to stop the government in the tracks - Impugned judgement unsustainable - Provisions impugned held to be ultra vires the Constitution and also the Act - Directions issued - Appeals allowed.
