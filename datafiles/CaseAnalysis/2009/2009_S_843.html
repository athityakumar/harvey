Ajoy Kumar Ghose v State of Jharkhand and another

Supreme Court of India

18 March 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

V.S. Sirpurkar, Tarun Chatterjee

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 2118; (2009) 14 SCC 115; AIR 2009 SC 2282; 2009 CRLJ 2824; JT 2009 (4) SC 245; 2009 (3) RCR(Criminal) 345; 2009(4) SCALE 267; [2009] 4 S.C.R. 515

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 177, 181, 182, 192, 196, 199, 209, 466, 468, 471 and 474 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973,ss. 244, 245 and 246 - Framing of charges - Legality - Appellant along with some others, was facing prosecution for the offences u/ss. 177, 181, 182, 192, 196, 199, 209, 466, 468, 471 and 474 of IPC before the Chief Judicial Magistrate - These charges are based on an official complaint filed by the Registrar General, Patna High Court against the accused persons - Cognizance was taken on the basis of complaint and summons were ordered to be issued - Thereafter, appellant filed an application for discharge where the trial was pending, contending therein that there was no material available even for framing the charge - It was specifically pleaded in the said application that the said discharge was being sought for under sub-section (2) of s. 245 of CrPC - Application was dismissed and Trial Court proceeded to frame charges against the appellant - Writ Petition filed thereagainst was also dismissed - Hence, present appeal - (A) Whether the Magistrate was justified in dismissing the discharge application? - Held, u/s. 245(2) of CrPC, a Magistrate can discharge the accused at any previous stage of the case, if he finds the charge to be groundless - Magistrate can take this decision before the accused appears or is brought before the Court or the evidence is led u/s. 244 of CrPC - In the present case, applicable Sections are ss. 244 and 245 of CrPC, since this is a warrant trial instituted otherwise than on police report - In that view, the accused could have made the application - Further, no interference required in an order dismissing discharge application - (B) Whether the Magistrate was justified in straightaway framing a charge u/s. 246(1) of CrPC after dismissing the discharge application? - Held, it is on the basis of the evidence offered by the complainant at the stage of ss. 244(1) of CrPC, that the charge is to be framed, if the Magistrate is of the opinion that there is any ground for presuming that the accused has committed an offence triable under this Chapter - Therefore, ordinarily, when the evidence is offered u/s. 244 of CrPC by the prosecution, the Magistrate has to consider the same, and if he is convinced, the Magistrate can frame the charge - In the present case, at the stage of framing a charge no material, whatsoever, was available with the Trial Court, except the complaint, which was also not supported by any statement on oath, by the complainant or any of his witnesses, which ordinarily are recorded at the stage of s. 200 of CrPC - Also since the complaint was by the Court, no such statement came to be recorded, of the complainant or any of his witnesses - Hence, in the present case, opportunity to the accused to cross-examine the witnesses was lost, as the Trial Court has straightaway proceeded to frame the charge - In that view, charge framed in present case was premature - Order framing the charge, therefore, quashed - Matter remanded back before the Trial Court, where the prosecution may offer the witnesses u/s. 244(1) of CrPC and the opportunity to cross-examine, would be offered to the accused - It is only thereafter, that the Trial Court would proceed to decide as to whether the charge is to be framed or not - Appeal partly allowed.
