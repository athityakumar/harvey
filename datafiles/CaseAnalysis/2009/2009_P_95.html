Perla Somasekhara Reddy And Ors v State Of A.P. Rep. By Public Prosecutor

Supreme Court of India

06 May 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

Arijit Pasayat, A.K. Ganguly

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 760; (2009) 16 SCC 98; 2009 (4) MLJ(Crl) 304; 2010 (5) RCR(Criminal) 360; 2009(7) SCALE 115; [2009] 8 S.C.R. 145; 2010 (1) UC 242

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Demeanour Of Witness, Power Of The Court, Finding Of Facts, Hearing Of Appeal

Summary: Criminal - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 378 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302 r/w 149 - Explosive Substances Act, 1908, ss. 3 and 5 - Appeal against acquittal - Power of Appellate Court - Accused persons attacked deceased and party with explosives - Political rivalry - Deceased and party earlier had attacked accused no. 19 and party, causing grievance hurt - Acquittal awarded by Trial Court - High Court in appeal allowed the appeal in part - Whether interference with acquittal, proper and justified? - Held, Yes - Four injured eye-witnesses - Provisions of appeal in Chapter XXIX and esp. s. 378 perused and decisions of Court on the subject referred - Court deliberated upon ambit and scope of the power of an appellate Court in an appeal against an order of acquittal - No restrictions imposed by the legislature on powers of appellate Court in dealing with such appeals; full power to re-appreciate, review and reconsider the evidence at large and to reach its own conclusions; both questions of fact and of law are open to determination - Power of appellate Court vis-a-vis reinforced presumption of innocence in favour of accused because of the acquittal awarded by Trial Court - Though such position of law was well settled but Court took different views in its various decisions, and Court took notice of such decisions - Finally general principles regarding powers of the appellate Court were culled out from such decisions in 'Chandrappa v. State of Karnataka, 2007 (4) SCC 415'; they were inter alia, various expressions used, such as, 'substantial and compelling reasons', 'good and sufficient grounds', 'very strong circumstances', 'distorted conclusions', 'glaring mistakes', etc. are not intended to curtail extensive powers of an appellate Court in an appeal against acquittal; if two reasonable conclusions are possible on the basis of the evidence on record, the appellate court should not disturb the finding of acquittal recorded by the trial court - There is an unmistakable subjective element in the evaluation of the degrees of probability and the quantum of proof; forensic probability must, in the last analysis, rest on a robust common sense and, ultimately, on the trained intuitions of the Judge; while the protection given by the criminal process to the accused persons is not to be eroded, at the same time, uninformed legitimization of trivialities would make a mockery of administration of criminal justice; 'State of U.P. v. Krishna Gopal (1988 (4) SCC 302)' - Incident took place at 1.40 pm and between 2.40 and 2.45 pm victims were deported to the hospital by PW9 which was 12 kms therefrom - Sending of medical intimation, recording of statement of PW1, and registration of case thereupon with respective police station, holding of inquest and sending of copy of FIR to Elaqa Magistrate was prompt - No delay in sending the FIR to the Magistrate - Impugned judgement upheld - Appeal dismissed.
