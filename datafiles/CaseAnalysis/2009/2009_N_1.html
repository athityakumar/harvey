Supreme Court of India

06 May 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, Mukundakam Sharma

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 876; (2009) 7 SCC 1; 2009 (5) ALD(SC) 115; 2009 (1) CCC 314; 2009(8) SCALE 351; [2009] 7 S.C.R. 668

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges

Keywords: Solicitor General, High Court Judges (Conditions of Service) Act, 1954, City of Nagpur Corporation Act, 1948, Insurance Corporation Act, 1956

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 217 - Consumer Protection Act, 1986, s. 16 and 16(1)(b)(iii) - Appointment of President of State Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission - Issue is, justiciability of the recommendations of the Chief Justice of Madras High Court for appointment of appellant as the President of the Commission in terms of s. 16 of CP Act - Appellant was an advocate practicing in the Madras High Court; was appointed as an Additional Judge of the said Court for a period of two years; during his tenure as AJ a representation was made from the members of the Bar alleging lack of probity against him - Undisputedly appellant was not appointed as a Permanent Judge as a result whereof he demitted his office; thereafter he resumed practice in Madras HC; he was later on appointed as an AAG of the State; on his request his name was included in the list of retired Judges by the Registrar General of Madras HC - Govt. of TN requested the names of eligible candidates for appointment to the post of President of Commission; panel of three retired Judges of the Madras HC was sent and than Govt. appointed the appellant as President of the Commission - Writ petitions were preferred by some legal practitioners against such appointment before Madras HC - Writ petitions were filed seeking issuance of writ of Quo Warranto and for declaring impugned order of Govt. as illegal and unconstitutional; averments were made touching upon the lack of integrity and honesty on the part of the appellant but HC had not touched upon such aspect in detail - HC had allowed two of the petitions - Hence the instant appeal by the appellant/appointee - Submissions made noted, contentions heard, questions considered by HC and findings made thereupon examined - Constitutional provisions, provisions of s. 16 of CP Act perused, and settled precedents referred, relied, and intense deliberations entered into - For giving effective contextual meaning to the Act, Court can change interpretative tools - Court noted that it is one thing to say that an Additional Judge would be on the same pedestal as a Permanent Judge for all practical purposes, namely - judicial function; administrative function; pay and remuneration etc. but would it mean that the same interpretation would be applicable even in a case where an Additional Judge despite his legitimate expectation to become a Permanent Judge has not been conferred on the said stating allegations of lack of probity - Interpretation of constitutional provisions in regard to the status of an Additional Judge, and interpretation of s. 16 of CP Act - Purposive construction - For giving effective contextual meaning Court may have to change the interpretative tool - P /&gt;(i) Court proceeded on the basis that save and except for certain purposes an Additional Judge and a Permanent Judge enjoy equal status; thus it could be said that for the purpose of appointment as Chairperson of some Tribunal, the name of AJ may also be taken into consideration, but would that mean that an AJ whose services were tainted or having regard to his general reputation or lack of integrity, where for he had not been made permanent as opposed to a situation where having regard to the policy decision of the Central Govt. or the purpose for which the AJs are appointed to clear the back log are on the same pedestal as a permanent Judge? - Held, No - An AJ holding a tenure post stricto sensu does not retire; it is one thing to say that having regard to the constitutional embargo, he would not hold office after he attains the age of 62 years but it is another thing to say that for all other purposes, he can be equated with a sitting Judge - An AJ who has not been confirmed, may for the purpose of giving effect to the constitutional provisions be considered to be a former Judge but when it comes to the question of his appointment in the said capacity, it is possible to take somewhat different view having regard to his present status, viz., an advocate etc., Court opined - Appellant despite being a former Judge is entitled to practice in the same HC, which other Judges are not permitted to do so; he may appear before the Tribunal and subordinate Courts - A person must answer the test of his being qualified to be a Judge - For purpose of s. 16 of CP Act, he must be equated with a sitting Judge of a HC - Court noted that if the Collegium of the Supreme Court Judges including the CJI, which is a constitutional authority in the matter of appointment of Judges and re-appointment of Additional Judges did not find him eligible, it would be beyond anybody's comprehension as to how Chief Justice of a High Court find him eligible/suitable for holding a statutory post requiring possession of qualification of holder of a constitutional office - If no recommendation by the Chief Justice is constitutionally permissible, the question of the eligibility criteria being not satisfied certainly is relevant - Court said, question is not whether he is a former Judge or not; question is whether he was eligible for appointment, having not been found fit for re-appointment; if he was ineligible for being recommended, that is the end of the matter - Eligibility of a Judge of a High Court should not be construed in a pedantic manner; it in the context of a large number of decisions of this Court including S.P.Gupta's case must also be held to include suitability of a person concerned; for such purpose principles of purposive interpretation is required to be resorted to, Court observed - Need for the Chief Justice of the High Court to apprise himself the reasons with reference to the backdrop of events as to why the collegium of the Supreme Court did not find the appellant to be a fit person for re-appointment or to be a permanent Judge, further deliberated upon - Vide few decisions the Court has held that only a sitting or retired Judge is suitable for appointment - Court opined that Judge whose tenure ended by way of non extension as a stigma would not come within the purview of the definition of term 'has been a Judge of the High Court' - P /&gt;(ii) Whether the consultative process required to be gone into for the purpose of appointment of Chairman, State Commission was complied with? - Held, No - Relevant facts were not brought to the notice of CJ of HC; had the records been brought to his notice, the CJ would not have made the recommendation - P /&gt;(iii) Whether power of Judicial Review is exercisable in the instant case? - Held, Yes - Such power although is very restricted, cannot be denied to be exercised when relevant facts are not considered as in the instant case - What is not the subject matter of JR is the opinion of the CJ touching upon the merit of the decision but the decision making process is subject to JR - Concededly proviso to s. 16 of CP Act, is imperative in nature; if the State is bound to consult the CJ, such consultation must be an effective and informed one; both the State Govt. as also the CJ before forming opinion must have access to all relevant informations; application of mind on part of consultant and consultee on such relevant information was absolutely imperative, Court emphasized -P /&gt;(iv) Chief Justice of High Court forwarded a panel of three Judges including appellant - Whether s. 16 of CP Act contemplates such a situation? - Held, No - Fact that CJ has the primacy as regards recommendations of the name for appointment to the post of Chairman of the Commission, the method adopted in the instant case, Court opined, is impermissible in law - Being without merit - Appeals dismissed.
