State of Uttar Pradesh v Hari Chand

Supreme Court of India

29 April 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

Arijit Pasayat, A.K. Ganguly

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 566; (2009) 13 SCC 542; AIR 2009 SC (Supp) 1535; 2009 ALL MR (Cri) 1885; 2009 CRLJ 3039; 2010 (5) RCR(Criminal) 572; 2009(6) SCALE 605; [2009] 7 S.C.R. 149; 2009 (2) UC 1196

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Medical Witness

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Ocular evidence vis-a-vis medical evidence - Importance of eye witnesses - Accused four in number - Two convicted - Conviction reversed by High Court - Reversal of conviction whether proper? - Property dispute - Deceased also holding decree for some property against main accused - Main accused a notorious person who had instilled a sense of terror in the mind of deceased - Deceased in fear used to stay at PW 3's house - Incident occurred at 11 in the night when deceased was sleeping in PW3's house and at a little distance his mother and daughter were sleeping in the Verandah - A lantern was glowing at the time - Accused persons arrived armed with firearms - Deceased was caught hold, picked up from his cot and at a distance stopped and than the other two accused opened fire - Death - Mother(PW1), daughter(PW2) and PW3 witnessed the incident - Conclusions drawn by HC perused - Found to be hypothetical and full of surmises and conjectures - HC concluded that there was no necessity for first picking deceased from the cot when they could shoot him on the cot itself, further conclusion that testimonies does not fit in natural probabilities of situation that two of the accused would be catching hold of the victim at the time of actual shooting, risking their own lives - Shooting was done from a very close range - Medical report showed incised wounds and HC concluded thereupon that eye witnesses did not speak of such wounds and raised doubt on their testimonies - PWs had witnessed actual shooting and if during the process of taking the deceased any incised wound is inflicted that obviously could not have been seen by the PWs, Court observed - Evidences of PWs found to be corroborated by medical evidence - Plea raised as to variance between ocular evidence and medical evidence - Court stressed that it has to be noted that it would be erroneous to accord undue primacy to the hypothetical answers of medical witnesses to exclude the eye-witnesses' account which had to be tested independently and not treated as the 'variable' keeping the medical evidence as the 'constant' - Court entered deliberation upon the aspect - Held, reversal of conviction, improper - Appeal allowed.
