State of Himachal Pradesh v Narain Singh

Supreme Court of India

08 July 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Ganguly, Markandey Katju

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 838; (2009) 13 SCC 165; 2009 (6) ALD(SC) 5; JT 2009 (9) SC 265; 2009(9) SCALE 326; [2009] 10 S.C.R. 821

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property

Keywords: Himachal Pradesh Land Revenue Act, 1954, Himachal Pradesh Land Revenue Act, 1953, Weights and Measures Act, 1976

Summary: Constitution - Land & Property - Himachal Pradesh Land Revenue Act, 1953(Principal Act), ss. 32-34, 36, 38 and 47, chap. IV - Himachal Pradesh Land Revenue (Amendment and Validation) Act, 1996(Amendment Act), ss. 1(2), 2(b), 5, 6, 10 and 13 - Validating Act - Constitutionality of amendment Act - Challenged on ground of its being in conflict with the original provisions of the various sections of Principal Act - Challenge whether acceptable? - Held, No - (A) Whether the State can in exercise of its sovereign legislative power enact an amendment Act seeking to remove and cure the defects in the previous law despite there being a judgment on the previous law? - Held, Yes - Judgement impugned examined, statement of objects and reasons of Amendment Act noted, provisions perused and contentions heard - Decisions, referred and relied upon - Amendment was necessitated in view of certain factors which are predominantly in public interest and the said amendment has been made in view of the interest of land revenue, land settlement and for the purpose of updating the same - Court pointed that amendments were in fact made for an effective implementation of the provisions of the Himachal Pradesh Land Revenue Act, 1954 - Certain provisions of amendment Act were made effective retrospectively from 23-09-1976; s. 13 is the provision providing for validation - Court observed that where there is a competent legislative provision which retrospectively removes the substratum of foundation of a judgment, the said exercise is a valid legislative exercise provided it does not transgress any other constitutional limitation - '..Legislature, as a body, cannot be accused of having passed a law for an extraneous purpose'; '..the concept of 'transferred malice' is unknown in the field of legislation provided the legislature enacts the law within its powers', Chandrachud, J. in 'K. Nagraj & Others vs. State of AP, 1985 1 SCC 523' - Held further, reasoning in the impugned HC's judgment that the impugned amendment is invalid because it nullifies some provisions of the earlier Act cannot be upheld - 'If a law passed by a legislature is struck down by the Courts as being invalid for one infirmity or another, it would be competent to the appropriate Legislature to cure the said infirmity and pass a validating law so as to make the provisions of the said earlier law effective from the date when it was passed', Gajendragadkar, J. in 'Rai Ramkrishna vs. State of Bihar, AIR 1963 SC 1667' - Amending Act r/w its validation clause found to have passed the tests laid down by the Court - Impugned judgment set aside - Appeals allowed.
