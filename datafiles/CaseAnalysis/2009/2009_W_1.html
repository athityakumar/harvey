State of West Bengal and Another v (1) West Bengal Regn. Copy Writers Assn. and

Supreme Court of India

08 May 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

V.S. Sirpurkar, Tarun Chatterjee

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 866; (2009) 14 SCC 132; AIR 2010 SC 2184; 2009(7) SCALE 164; [2009] 8 S.C.R. 334

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Keywords: Executive Instructions, Qualification And Experience, State Administrative Tribunal, Temporary Appointees, Assam Land and Revenue Regulation, 1886, Prospective Or Retrospective, Gujarat Panchayats Act, West Bengal Registration (Copywriters) Rules, 1982, West Bengal Registration (Copywriters) Rules, 1999, West Bengal Registration (Filing of True Copies) Rules, 1979

Summary: Service - West Bengal Registration (Copywriters) Rules, 1999, rr. 2(3), 3, 5-10, 15, 17, 19, 20(2), 21, 24 and 26 - West Bengal Registration (Filing of True Copies) Rules, 1979 - Registration Act, 1908, s. 19A(Vide State Amendment) - License holder registration copywriters in State of West Bengal - OA was moved by such licensees (including WB Registration Copywriters' Association) praying for their absorption/regularisation as regular employees in the cadre of Lower Division Clerk(LDC) and further they prayed for injunction against the concerned authority from appointing any person in the cadre of LDC unless all the members of the Association were accommodated in that post of the LDC - Licensees who were given the licenses, under which they alone could prepare the true copies of the documents on receiving fees prescribed in the Rules; such true copies of the documents were compulsorily required to be filled alongwith the documents, which were presented for registration - Claim was based on the plea that they were doing the identical work of preparing copies as was required to be done by the LDCs - Further they based their claim on the plea that previously in 1978, the Govt. had absorbed some copywriters working in the office of Registrars throughput the State as LDCs; these Copywriters were at that time called as 'extra-muharrirs'; thus they pleaded parity with 'extra-muharrirs' on ground that they are identically placed as them and were working from 1984 and had the requisite qualifications, expertise and experience to work as LDC - OAs were dismissed by the Tribunal which was challenged in the High Court - HC set aside order of Tribunal and allowed OAs - Prayer and contentions, whether acceptable? - Held, No - Provisions of Act and Rules perused, contentions heard - Nature of work and profiles of licensees, noted - Language used in r. 3 of Rules 1999 describing the work of licensees as a 'profession of Copywriter' noted as being extremely important; r. 5 of Rules 1999 provided for qualifications for licensee which was VIth class pass with minimum 3 years experience on the date of application - Conclusions drawn - (i) Original writ petitioners-Copywriters are mere licensees - (ii) Though the Rules, 1999 have been formulated by the Govt. for awarding these licenses the Rules do not spell out an absolute control over the working of these Copywriters; the Rules merely pertain to the grant of licenses and control of those licenses, however, did not control the working and duties of the Copywriters - (iii) They do not do any Govt. duty; they are merely required to copy the deeds which are to be presented for registration; though the filing of a fresh copy is necessary for registration, the making of that copy does not amount to a Govt. duty - (iv) They are not controlled in the matters of their attendance, working hours, leave, pension and output of work etc., by the Govt. - (v) They are not on the establishment under the Rules, more particularly the Rules formulated u/art. 309 of Constitution of India - (vi) they are not paid from the Govt. coffers; on the other hand they are paid by the private parties who require those copies for Registration of the deeds; thus there is no fiduciary relationship between the Govt. and the Copywriters nor is the Govt. responsible for any payment - (vii) Grant of license for copywriting does not amount to creating a service; hence there is not master-servant relationship between the Copywriters and the Govt. nor can they said to be Govt. servants entitling them to so-called equal treatment with the other Govt. servants - (viii) HC has erred in directing the creation of service and for that purpose framing the Rules as also providing the nomenclature for such a service; HC has traveled beyond the scope of the OA and the writ petition - Being deserved - Appeals allowed.
