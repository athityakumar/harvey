Ansal Properties and Industries Limited v State of Haryana and Another

Supreme Court of India

23 January 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

Mukundakam Sharma, R.V. Raveendran

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 2067; (2009) 3 SCC 553; JT 2009 (4) SC 174; 2009 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1674; 2009 (2) RCR(Civil) 185; 2009(2) SCALE 140; [2009] 1 S.C.R. 553

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Municipalities & Local Governments

Summary: Land & Property - Municipalities & Local Governments - Haryana Development and Regulation of Urban Areas Act, 1975, s. 3(3)(a)(iv) - Haryana Development and Regulation of Urban Areas Rules, 1976, rr. 4, 5 and 11 - Demand of internal community charges - Respondent no. 2 granted licenses to appellant under provisions of 1975 Act for setting up colonies in Gurgaon District - Respondent No. 2 vide letter dt. 11-01-1988 informed appellant that it was required to pay Rs. 3.72 lakhs per gross acre on account of external development charges which includes Rs. 61,000/- per gross acre on account of internal community buildings for which no recovery should be made from plot holders - Appellant filed writ petition against said demand contending that as per the licence agreement and also as per the provisions of the Act and the Rules the appellant Company was not liable to pay the amount of Rs. 61,000/- per gross acre towards the construction of internal community buildings - Appellant further contended that said demand was not only unjust and arbitrary but also contrary to licence agreement and also against the provisions of the Act and the Rules framed thereunder - HC held that appellant had failed to develop the said buildings which it was obliged to do and thus the Director was justified in demanding the said amount from the appellant Company - Hence, present appeal - Whether respondent's demand from appellant in respect of internal community charges is justified? - Held, no - There is no mention at all of any requirement for the licensee to provide for or to make payment for the cost of construction of internal community buildings when the land is transferred to the Government free of cost - No such statutory basis could be shown either in the statute or in the licence agreement - Since the land has been given free of cost, it is now open for the State Government to get the remaining community buildings constructed either by themselves or through any agency or institution or individual at its cost in terms of the provisions of the Act, in which case the terms and conditions could be laid down by the Government for such community buildings, to be constructed on the land which is transferred to it by the appellant free of cost - Government cannot in law demand that the buildings on the said lands which is to be transferred to them free of cost should also be constructed by the appellant and then transfer the land to them free of cost along with the construction thereon and on failure to construct to pay for the cost of construction - Respondent cannot demand transfer of the land free of cost and also the construction cost of the facilities to be provided in the said land - Demand unreasonable - Appeal allowed.Land & Property - Municipalities & Local Governments - Haryana Development and Regulation of Urban Areas Act, 1975, s. 3(3)(a)(iv) - Haryana Development and Regulation of Urban Areas Rules, 1976, rr. 4, 5 and 11 - Demand of internal community charges - Interpretation of statutes - Held, word used in s. 3(3)(a)(iv) is 'land' and it has been specifically mentioned therein that if the colonizer does not construct the community buildings and facilities on its own or through its agency or organization or individual, then the said licensee would be required to transfer the said land set apart for the aforesaid purpose free of cost to the Government - Government's claim is therefore restricted to lands which the developer has failed to develop as community centres - If the legislature had intended that the licensee is required to transfer the land and also to construct the buildings on it or to make payment for such construction, the legislature would have made specific provisions laying down such conditions explicitly and in clear words in which event the provisions would have been worded in altogether different words and terms - It is well settled principle in law that the court cannot read anything into a statutory provision which is plain and unambiguous - Language employed in a statute is determinative factor of legislative intent - If the language of the enactment is clear and unambiguous, it would not be proper for the courts to add any words thereto and evolve some legislative intent, not found in the statue - Appeal allowed.
