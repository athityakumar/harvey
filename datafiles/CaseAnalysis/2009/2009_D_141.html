Madan Kumar Singh (D) Thr. Lr. v District Magistrate, Sultanpur and Others

Supreme Court of India

07 August 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

Deepak Verma, S.B. Sinha

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 963; (2009) 9 SCC 79; 2009 (6) ALD(SC) 45; 2009 (77) ALR 160; 2010 (2) AWC 1833; 2010 (1) Bom.C.R. 475; 2009 (4) CPJ(Sc) 3; 2009 (5) CTC 274; 2009 (4) G.L.R. 3473; JT 2009 (10) SC 380; 2010 (1) MPLJ 480; 2009 (4) RCR(Civil) 137; 2009(10) SCALE 668; [2009] 12 S.C.R. 1186; 2009 (3) UC 1712

Case Digest

Subject: Consumer Protection

Keywords: Appeals Disposed Of

Summary: Consumer Protection - Consumer Protection Act, 1986, ss. 2(1)(d)/(g)/(o) and 12 - 'Consumer' - Deficiency in service - Truck purchased in auction sale - Auction due to default by the previous owner of the truck in payment of instalments for the loan, taken from bank for the said truck - Respondent authorities conducted the auction, and appellant being the highest bidder, bid was knocked down in his favour and accepted by respondent no. 1 - All terms and conditions on the part of appellant/purchaser was satisfied; auction was confirmed; sale became final in favour of appellant - Possession of truck was given after 6 months from the date of auction - Despite such possession, no papers of truck were handed over - Due to continuous efforts of appellant, papers were delivered after six years from auction - Appellant was constrained to file a petition u/s. 12 of Act before District Forum, claiming damages - Matter reached all the way from State Commission, National Commission to the Court - DF had rejected the petition on the ground that appellant was not a 'consumer' - NC had awarded compensation of Rs. 25,000/- to the appellant - Notable aspects of the case, that respondents never participated in the proceedings before consumer fora, proceedings were thus ex parte - Long chequered history as to litigation, but Court noted that in none of the case, a stay was granted for delivery of documents/papers; Court thus concluded that respondents were unlawfully holding back the papers with them, for which, otherwise they were not entitled to do so - P /&gt;(A) Whether the appellant can be said to be 'consumer' within the definition of s. 2(1)(d) of Act? - Held, yes - Definition perused, contentions heard - Appellant had bought the truck for a consideration which was paid by him; truck was bought to be used exclusively for the purpose of earning his livelihood by means of self-employment; satisfying thus the explanation to s. 2(1)(d) - P /&gt;(B) Whether it can be said that there has been deficiency in the services committed by respondent/authorities as contemplated u/s. 2(1)(g)/(o) of Act? - Held, yes - Facts noted, definitions perused - Respondents were at fault in performance of the services which was otherwise required to be performed by them - Actual handing over of truck was after six months; after the delivery thereof appellant could not have started plying the same unless he was delivered the relevant papers; materials on record pointing towards the fact that papers were handed over after 6 years of auction - Court posits, 'what more could be the deficiency in service cannot be described' - Held further, all the ingredients, to enable the appellant to claim damages under the Act were made out - P /&gt;(C) Whether the amount of compensation awarded to the appellant is just and proper or deserves to be enhanced? - Held, compensation awarded was too meagre and deserves to be enhanced - Appellant suffered loss of earning, firstly due to non-delivery of vehicle, and then due to highly belated supply of requisite documents; value of the truck also was depreciated resulting in further loss to him - Amount of Rs. 1,00,000/- awarded as compensation - Interest @ 6% p.a. further awarded on the amount, from the date of the original application till actual payment - Appeals disposed of.
