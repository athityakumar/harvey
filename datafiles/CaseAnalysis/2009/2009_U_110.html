Consumer Education and Research Society v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

24 August 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

K.G. Balakrishnan, R.V. Raveendran, J.M. Panchal

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 1053; (2009) 9 SCC 648; AIR 2009 SC (Supp) 1680; 2009 (8) MLJ(SC) 1420; 2009 (4) RCR(Civil) 205; 2009(11) SCALE 708; [2009] 13 S.C.R. 664

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: Retrospective Legislation, Parliament (Prevention of Disqualification) Act, 1959, Disqualifications Of Members, Parliamentary Procedure, Parliament (Prevention of Disqualification) Amendment Act, 2006, Parliament (Prevention of Disqualification) Act, 1950, Parliament (Prevention of Disqualification) Act, 1951, National Commission for Women Act, 1990, National Cadet Corps Act, 1948, Reserve and Auxiliary Air Forces Act, 1952, Manipur Legislature (Removal of Disqualifications) (Amendment) Act, 1975

Summary: (A) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts.102 (1) (a) - Parliament (Prevention of Disqualification) Act, 1959 - Parliament (Prevention of Disqualification) Amendment Act, 2006 - PIL - 'Office of profit' - Exemption of 55 offices from list of 'office of profit' by Amendment Act - Retrospective removal of disqualification - Constitutionality - Held, Parliament has power to enact prevention of disqualification law u/art.102 (1) (a) retrospectively - Question of disqualification of a Member of Parliament to be decided by President - Declaration of disqualification by President or Speaker/Chairman of the House is a condition precedent for vacancy of seat - However, decision of President or Chairman/Speaker merely an adjudication and confirmation of pre-existing fact - Vacancy of seat is consequential - Unless disqualification of a Member has been challenged, he could be continued as Member - Therefore, disqualification could be removed by retrospective operation of Amendment Act - Amendment Act was constitutionally valid.(B) Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, art.14 - 'Office of profit' - Exemption of 55 offices from list of 'office of profit' - Non-exemption of other similar offices - Plea of violation of art. 14 raised - Sustainability - An office to be exempted from the purview of 'office of profit', is a matter to be considered by Parliament - Not possible to classify and include offices exempted from disqualification in generic sense - Parliament ensures Members of legislature are free from undue influence of executive while enacting prevention of disqualification laws - Held, newly exempted 'offices' did not suffer any prejudicial to constituency or affect independence of Member - All offices of profits were not equal - Argument based on art.14 was illogical - No violation of art.14(C) Parliament & Legislature - Parliament (Prevention of Disqualification) Amendment Act, 2006 - 'Office of profit' - Exemption of 55 offices from list of 'office of profit' by Amendment Act - Non-reference of matter to Joint Committee of Parliament - Legality - Held, requirement of reference to Joint Committee was merely a parliamentary procedure, not constitutional convention - Parliament has power to exempt offices from disqualification retrospectively - Legislative power of Parliament could not be denuded by violation of any norm or traditional procedure - Non-observation of an occasional procedure do not render any prevention of disqualification law unconstitutional - Therefore, matter not required to be referred to Joint Committee - Writ Petitions dismissed.
