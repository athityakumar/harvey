 Karnail Singh v State of Haryana

Supreme Court of India

29 July 2009

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Sathasivam, K.G. Balakrishnan, R.V. Raveendran, D.K. Jain, J.M. Panchal

Where Reported

2009 Indlaw SC 1135; (2009) 8 SCC 539; (2009) 3 SCC (Cr) 887; 2009 CRLJ 4299; 2009 (4) CTC 881; 2009 (4) G.L.R. 3296; 2009 (4) JCC (Narcotics) 170; JT 2009 (10) SC 360; 2009 (3) MLJ(Crl) 839; 2009(10) SCALE 255; [2009] 11 S.C.R. 470

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Specially Empowered

Summary: Criminal - Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985, s.42 (As stood originally and as stood amended vide amending Act of 2001) - Whether compliance with s.42 mandatory - Conflicting opinions regarding the scope and applicability of s.42 in conducting search, seizure and arrest without warrant or authorization - Three Judge Bench of Supreme Court in Abdul Rashid Ibrahim Mansuri vs. State of Gujarat, (2000) 2 SCC 513, held compliance with s.42 mandatory - Three Judge Bench in Sajan Abraham vs. State of Kerala, (2001) 6 SCC 692, held compliance with s.42 not mandatory and substantial compliance sufficient - Reference made to Constitution Bench - Facts and ratios of two cases noted by Court - Court found facts of two conflicting cases to be entirely different and decisions therein based solely on facts of each case, and there was no proposition of law laid down in any of case - In Abdul Rashid, there was absolute non-compliance with provision of s.42; police officer neither took down information as required u/s.42(1) nor informed his immediate official superior, as required by s.42(2); Court in that context expressed the view that it was imperative that police officer should take down information and forthwith send a copy thereof to his immediate superior officer, otherwise action of police officer on basis of unrecorded information would become suspect, though trial may not be vitiated on that score alone - In Sajan Abraham, it was very difficult for police officer to record in writing information received, and send a copy thereof forthwith to his official superior, as information was given to him when he was on patrol duty while he was moving in a jeep and unless he acted on information immediately, accused would have escaped; police officer therefore acted, without recording information into writing, but had sent a copy of FIR alongwith other records regarding arrest of accused immediately to superior officer; it is in these circumstances that Court held that omission to record in writing information received was not in violation of s.42 - Amendment to s.42 wherein strictness of s.42 relaxed further supports the proposition that provisions are directory and not mandatory - Non-compliance of s.42 may not vitiate trial if it does not cause any prejudice to accused - Court noted that due to advances in communication technology it may not be possible at all times to record information collected through such communication in the event of an emergency situation - If statutory provisions u/ss.42(1) and 42(2) of Act of writing down information is interpreted as a mandatory provision, it will disable prompt action in emergency situation and may hamper criminal search and seizure; provisions should not be misused by wrongdoers/offenders as a major ground for acquittal - Provisions of s.42(1) and 42(2) should be taken as discretionary measure which should check misuse of Act rather than providing an escape to hardened criminals - Appeals placed for disposal before appropriate Bench.
