 Sodan Singh Etc. v New Delhi Municipal Committee And Anr. Etc.

Supreme Court of India

30 August 1989

Case Analysis

Bench

L.M. Sharma, E.S. Venkataramiah, Kuldip Singh, N.D. Ojha, S. Natarajan

Where Reported

1989 Indlaw SC 213; (1989) 4 SCC 155; AIR 1989 SC 1988; 1989 ALJ 1097; JT 1989 (3) SC 553; 1989(2) SCALE 430; [1989] 3 S.C.R. 1038; 1990 (1) UJ 187

Case Digest

Subject: Municipalities & Local Governments; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Challenged, Trading business on pavements

Summary: Municipalities & Local Governments - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1)(g), 21, 14 - Delhi Municipal Corporation Act, 1957 - Punjab Municipal Act, 1911 - Trading business on pavements - Challenged - Petitioners filed instant petitions and claimed the right to engage in trading business on the pavements of roads of the city of Delhi - Petitioners submitted that they have been pursuing their trade with the permission of respondents municipal authorities for some time - However recently there have been illegal interference by respondents - HC dismissed the claim of petitioners.

Held, petitioners alleged that they were allowed by respondents to transact their business by occupying particular area on the pavements, on payment of certain charges described as Tehbazari. It is contended that municipal authorities by their refusal to permit the petitioners to continue with their trade are violating their fundamental right guaranteed u/art. 19(1)(g) and 21 of the Constitution. They have also complained of mala fides, arbitrariness and discriminatory conduct attracting art. 14 of the Constitution. In India there are large number of people who are engaged in the business of 'street trading'. There is hardly household where hawkers do not reach. The house-wives wait for vegetable vendor or fruit seller who conveniently delivers the daily-needs at the door-step. Petitioners are street-traders. Some of them have licences/Tehbazari from Municipal Corporation but most of them are squatters. There is practically no law regulating street trading in Delhi/New Delhi. The skeletal provisions in the 1957 Act and the 1911 Act can hardly provide any regulatory measures to the enormous and complicated problem of street trading in these areas. Street Trading being fundamental right has to be made available to citizens subject to art. 19(6) of the Constitution. It is within the domain of the State to make any law imposing reasonable restrictions in the interest of general public. This can be done by an enactment on the same lines as in England or by any other law permissible u/art. 19(6) of the Constitution. In spite of repeated suggestions by HC nothing has been done. Since a citizen has no right to choose particular place in any street for trading, it is for the State to designate the streets and earmark the places from where street trading can be done. In-action on part of the State would result in negating the fundamental right of the citizens. It is expected that the State will do the needful within a reasonable time failing which it would be left to the courts to protect the rights of the citizens. Order accordingly.
