Narendra Kishan Maheshwari v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

03 May 1989

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Mukharji, S. Ranganathan

Where Reported

1989 Indlaw SC 502; (1990) Supp SCC 440; AIR 1989 SC 2138; 1989 (2) CompLJ 95; JT 1989 (2) SC 338; 1989(1) SCALE 1353; [1989] 3 S.C.R. 43

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Capital Market

Keywords: Shares and Debentures

Summary: Capital Market - Administrative - Shares and Debentures - Public Issue - Validity - Petitioner existing shareholder of the respondent no.3- Company - Respondent No. 3, was newly set up public limited company, maintaining the share price only on an equity capital converted on each debenture - Petitioner filed petition and contended that respondent no.1-Union Bank of India and respondent no.2 Controller of Capital Issues ought not to have granted consent to respondent No. 3 to issue share and debenture capital at an aggregate value of approx. Rs.600 crores - Similar writ petitions were filed in several Courts - Court had restrained issue of injunctions and directed that shares and debentures would be issued irrespective of any order of injunction passed by any Court or authority in India - Different cases were thereafter transferred to SC and dealt together - Held, entrepreneurs with legal and expert assistance could easily trap unwary investors and development of public interest lobby that should scrutinise issues carefully and advise prospective investors on their comparative merits and demerits might not be entirely undesirable - It was necessary that respondent no.2 in considering grant of consent to issues, should have these aspects brought to notice - It might be too cumbersome to have provision that details of every proposed application for consent should be publicised to maximum extent by respondent no.2 that objections and comments from public should be called for that there should be public hearing before respondent no.2 before grant of consent and that respondent no.2 should pass reasoned order granting or withholding consent - Some procedure had to be evolved to ensure that the respondent no.2 got the benefit of comments suggestions and objections from public before arriving at decision whether to grant consent or not - Evolution of certain rules in could be examined at juncture of industrial growth - However there was no undue haste - There was proper application of mind that sanction was for new projects - Sufficient security for debentures was enjoined to be ensured before sanction had been ensured and guidance provided by means of guidelines had been substantially complied with - There had been no infraction of norms required to be followed in granting sanction - Therefore, challenge to sanction should fail - Before the Courts grant injunction it should have regard to principles of comity of Courts in federal structure and regard to self restraint and circumspection - It might be impossible to lay down hard and fast rules of general application because the diverse situations which arose to problems in similar nature - Petition dismissed.
