Harbans Lal and Others v tate of Himachal Pradesh and Others

Supreme Court of India

01 August 1989

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Jagannatha Shetty, A.M. Ahmadi

Where Reported

1989 Indlaw SC 377; (1989) 4 SCC 459; (1990) SCC (L&S) 71; 1989 (11) ATC 869; 1989 (2) CLR 709; 1989 (3) CompLJ 149; 1989 (59) FLR 375; JT 1989 (3) SC 296; 1989 (2) LLJ 466; 1989 (2) LLN 966; 1989(2) SCALE 200; [1989] 3 S.C.R. 662; 1989 (5) SLR 774; 1990 (1) UJ 13

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution of India, 1950, art.32 - Companies Act, 1956 - Request for equal pay for equal work - Denied - Maintainability - Petitioners were carpenters 1st and 2nd grade employed at the Wood Working Centre of Corporation - Petitioners were termed as daily rated employees - Through petition u/art. 32 of the Constitution, petitioners were seeking enforcement of their fundamental right to have equal pay for equal work - Petitioners had demanded payment in terms paid to their counterparts in regular services - Petitioners wanted the same pay of the regular employees as carpenters or in the alternative, minimum wages prescribed by the Deputy Commissioner for like categories of workmen - Petitioners also sought regularization of their services with the benefits of pension, gratuity etc. - Hence instant petition - Whether the equality claims of the petitioners could be allowed - Held, from the averments in the pleadings of the parties it would be clear that the Corporation had no regularly employed carpenters - Evidently the petitioners were claiming wages payable to the carpenters in Govt. service - SC did not think that claim could be accepted - In the first place, even assuming that the petitioners' jobs were comparable with the counterparts in the government service, petitioners could not enforce the right to equal pay for equal work - Discrimination complained of should be within the same establishment owned by the same management - Comparison could not be made with counterparts in other establishments with different management, or even in establishments in different geographical locations though owned by the same master - Unless it was shown that there was a discrimination amongst the same set of employees by the same master in the same establishment, the principle of equal pay for equal work could not be enforced - Meva Ram Kanojia v. A.I.I.M.S., 1989 Indlaw SC 690, relied on - In the instant case, petitioners were employed by a company incorporated under the Act - Petitioners could not claim wages payable to their counterparts in govt. service - Secondly, it might be noted that the petitioners were carpenters; better called as craftsmen - By the general description of their job, one could not come to the conclusion that every carpenter or craftsmen was equal to the other in the performance of his work - Two jobs by the mere nomenclature or by the volume of work performed could not be rated as equal - It was not just a comparison of physical activity - It required the consideration of various dimensions of the job - Accuracy required by the job and the dexterity it entailed might differ from job to job - It could not be evaluated by the mere averments in the self serving affidavits or counter-affidavits of the parties - It should be left to be evaluated and determined by expert body - Principal claim of the petitioners therefore failed and was rejected - Next contention that the petitioners should be paid at least the minimum wages prescribed by the Deputy Commissioner could not also be accepted - It was applicable only to skilled and unskilled workers in class IV employees in Govt. service - It was not extended to employees of the Corporation - Petitioners had treated as construction workers and they were being paid the minimum wages admissible to such workmen - SC, therefore, could not direct the Corporation to apply the rates unless the Govt. could make it applicable to employees of the Corporation - Petition dismissed.
