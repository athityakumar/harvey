Corporation of the City of Bangalore v Kesoram Industries and Cotton Mills Limited and Others

Supreme Court of India

08 December 1989

Case Analysis

Bench

M. Fathima Beevi, G.L. Oza

Where Reported

1989 Indlaw SC 188; (1989) Supp2 SCC 753; AIR 1990 SC 322; JT 1989 (4) SC 491; 1989(2) SCALE 1265; [1989] Supp2 S.C.R. 443

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Municipalities & Local Governments

Summary: Municipalities & Local Governments - Constitution - City of Bangalore Corporation Act, 1949, s.98 - Levying of taxes - After hearing objection - Question raised as to legality of levying tax - Sustainability - S.98 of the Act required Corporation/appellant before passing any resolution imposing a tax or duty for the first time to publish a notice in the Official Gazette and in the local newspapers of its intention and inviting objections - Appellant might, after considering the objections, if any, received within the period specified determine by resolution to levy the tax or duty - On the recommendation of the Standing Committee to levy octroi on certain new items of goods the appellant published a notice as contemplated u/s.98(1) of the Act inviting objections from the public and the said notice was published in the Gazette dt. 17-9-1974 - Several objections were received in pursuance of the said notification - Subject was included in the supplementary agenda at the meeting held on 30-12-1974 - It was taken up as Item No. 146 and the resolution was passed unanimously - Commissioner thereafter issued notification - Respondents challenged the validity of the resolution on the ground inter alia that there had not been consideration of the objections before passing the resolution and, therefore, the mandatory provision u/s.98(1) of Act was violated - HC in allowing petitions had taken the view that what was contemplated trader the statute was a real consideration of the objections and not a mere pretence and since the time was too short there was no opportunity for such consideration and on account of non-consideration of the objections to the purposed levy the condition precedent for the passing of the resolution was not satisfied and therefore, the resolution was invalid - Hence instant appeal - Whether resolution passed was rightly rejected by HC on the ground of short notice - Held, HC was not justified in assuming from the time factor alone that there could not have been a consideration of the objections - It was not the function of the Court to probe into the details of the discussions and the deliberations before legislative will was seen expressed by passing the resolution - Connotation of the word 'consideration' occuring in s.98(1) comprehended 'taking note of' or 'paying heed to' depending upon the nature of the subject - It might be open to the councillors to express their views even within the limited time available - No standard could be prescribed in such matters - When it was shown that the council had the opportunity to consider the objections received, it had to be deemed, that they had taken note of the same before reaching a decision - HC had committed an error in assuming on account of the shortage of time alone and that there had been non-compliance with the requirements under the statute - Appeals allowed.
