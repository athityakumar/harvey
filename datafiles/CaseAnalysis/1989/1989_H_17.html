State of Bihar and Others v Harihar Prasad Debuka and Others

Supreme Court of India

21 February 1989

Case Analysis

Bench

K.N. Saikia, G.L. Oza

Where Reported

1989 Indlaw SC 479; (1989) 2 SCC 192; (1989) SCC (Tax) 250; AIR 1989 SC 1119; JT 1989 (Supp) SC 34; 1989(1) SCALE 464; [1989] 1 S.C.R. 796; 1989 (73) STC 353; 1989 (1) UJ 684

Case Digest

Subject: Indirect Tax; Sales Tax

Keywords: Inter-State Trade, Validity, Notification

Summary: Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 301, 304 - Inter-State Trade - Notification - Validity - Respondent-proprietor was transporting mustard - In road permit bill number was not mentioned - Inspecting Officers seized and detained goods loaded in truck - Show cause notice was issued - Respondent contended that no permit in required form was necessary and it was violative of respondents Constitutional right of freedom of inter-State trade and commerce - Respondents contention was rejected and penalty of Rs.8,330 was imposed and thereafter consequential demand notice was issued - Aggrieved respondent filed petition before HC - HC quashed notification and penalty order with demand notice was set aside - Hence instant appeal - Whether notification and adoption of two Forms would be ultra vires under arts. 301 and 304 of the Constitution - Held, HC had taken view that importer had to send form in advance to consignor so that it could be filled up to accompany goods and that would amount to blockade placed on free movement of goods in inter-State commerce - There was nothing to indicate that carrier would be penalised for not having filled up forms while goods were being carried through other States - There was no provision to the effect that those who had not filled up appropriate form at earlier stages of transit would not be allowed to fill up with State - There was no direct and immediate restriction of inter-State trade, commerce or intercourse as a result of requirement to fill up and carry forms and continuity of transport would not be obstructed or interrupted - A mere stoppage of the movement of vehicle would not have any direct or immediate effect on trade - Checking of documents or filling in and submission of forms and returns, detour to public weigh-bridge and the like might be inconvenience and unless shown to be unreasonable and not in public interest, Court might apply maxim 'de minimise non curat lex' - SC held that notification and adoption of Forms to had been validly made in exercise of powers incidental to power of levying Sales Tax, and that they were reasonable and in public interest, and not ultra vires the arts. 301 & 304 of the Constitution - - Impugned judgment of HC was set aside, - Appeals allowed.
