Hansa V. Gandhi v Deep Shankar Roy and others

Supreme Court of India

18 April 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjan Gogoi, R.M. Lodha

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 253; (2013) 12 SCC 776; AIR 2013 SC 2873; 2013(3) ALL MR 947; 2013 (4) Bom.C.R. 123; 2013 (2) CLT(SC) 177; JT 2013 (8) SC 410; 2013(6) SCALE 163

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Maharashtra Ownership of Flats (Regulation of Promotion of Construction, Sale, Management and Transfer) Act, 1963, s. 4 - Specific performance of agreement - Denied - Maintainability - Developer/Respondent No.2 had entered into agreement to develop the property i.e. land owned by the Society and thereafter to sell the flats constructed on the land in question to the intending purchasers in accordance with the terms and conditions of the agreement - Each plaintiff wanted to purchase one flat to be constructed by the respondent No.2 on the land belonging to the Society, so they had negotiated deals with the respondent No.2 - As per the understanding arrived at between each plaintiff and the respondent No.2, plaintiffs had to pay a total consideration of Rs.4,40,000/- in respect of each flat in certain installments - Accordingly, each plaintiff had started making payment to respondent No.2 as per the amount of installments determined by respondent No.2 - Respondent No.2 had executed letter of intent whereby respondent No.2 had agreed to reserve a flat for each plaintiff - Reservation of the flat was subject to bye-laws of the Society - It was also provided in the letter of intent that the plaintiffs had to bear expenses in relation to registration of the document, stamp duty and certain other expenditure to be incurred for getting motor and electric connection etc. and it was also provided in the letter of intent that delayed payment of the installment would attract interest at the rate of 21% p.a. and if two or more installments remained unpaid, the reservation made in respect of the flat would stand cancelled - In pursuance of the execution of letter of intent, the plaintiffs had started paying installments to respondent No.2 - It might also be noted here that due to some litigation which had taken place between some persons and the Society before the HC, litigation went on till the end of 1996, due to which respondent No.2 could not continue his construction activity and that resulted into delay in the construction work - According to respondent No.2, the said delay had resulted into increase in the cost of construction and therefore, it was constrained to increase the price of the flats and as a result thereof, the amount of installment was also increased from Rs.22,000/- to Rs. 38,500/- - Increase in the price of the flats and the amount of installments had been opposed by the plaintiffs and they had refused to pay the installments on the ground that the increase in the price as well as installments was not justified - Thus, plaintiffs filed suits for specific performance/damages - Respondent No.2 had filed written statements before Trial Court denying its liability on the ground that by virtue of letter dt. 19-12-1997, it had cancelled the reservation of flats in question made for the plaintiffs - Thus, the understanding arrived at among respondent No.2 and the plaintiffs in pursuance of the letter of intent had come to an end and as there was no subsisting agreement with regard to sale of the flats in question with any of the plaintiffs, there was no question of either specific performance of the contract or about breach of the contract resulting into payment of damages by respondent No.2 - Trial Court decreed the suits whereby respondent No.2 was directed to specifically perform the contract with regard to sale of the flats in favour of the plaintiffs upon payment of unpaid amount of consideration by them - Being aggrieved by the judgment and decree of Trial Court, the Subsequent Buyers filed the First Appeals, before HC - After hearing the concerned parties and looking at the facts of the case and after considering the judgment delivered by Trial Court, the HC allowed the appeals of respondent No.2 - HC directed that the plaintiffs would be entitled to get refund of the amount paid by them to the Developer with interest at the rate of 9% per annum from the date on which the letter of termination of the agreement was sent by the Developer to the plaintiffs till the date of payment of the said amount - Whether prayer for specific performance was sustainable - Held, according to the provisions of S. 4 (1) of the Act, agreement, if any, executed between the plaintiffs on one hand and with respondent No.2 on the another, ought to have been registered with the sub- Registrar - In absence of such a registered document, plaintiffs would not get any right in respect of the flats, which they intended to purchase - Letter of intent could not be said to be an agreement to sell for the simple reason that according to the contents of the letter of intent, only upon payment of the entire purchase price, respondent No.2 and the plaintiffs were to enter into an agreement with regard to sale of the flats - Therefore, Trial Court could not have decreed the suit for specific performance - Appeal dismissed.
