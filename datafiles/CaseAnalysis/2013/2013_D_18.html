Deepak Aggarwal v Keshav Kaushik And Others

Supreme Court of India

21 January 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Lodha, Ranjan Gogoi, Anil R. Dave

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 31; (2013) 5 SCC 276; AIR 2013 SC (Supp) 352; 2013 (2) Bom.C.R. 695; 2013 (1) CLT(SC) 127; JT 2013 (2) SC 139; 2013(1) SCALE 564; [2013] 1 S.C.R. 402; 2013 (2) SLR 54

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution; Service

Keywords: Indian Bar Councils Act, 1926, Legal Practitioners Act, 1879, Bar Council of India Rules, 1975, Karnataka Department of Prosecution and Government Litigation Recruitment Rules, 1962, M.P. Uchchatar Nyayik Sewa (Bharti Tatha Sewa Shartein) Niyam, 1994, Legal Advisers and Prosecutors) Recruitment Rules, 2002, Haryana State Prosecution Legal Service (Group 'C') Rules, 1979, Haryana Superior Judicial Service Rules, 2007

Summary: (A) Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Service - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 233(2) - Advocates Act, 1961, ss. 17, 22, 24, 29 and 33 - Bar Council of India Rules, r. 49 - Haryana Superior Judicial Service Rules, 2007, r. 31 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 2(7) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 24 - Appointment of District Judges - Practicing Advocate - Challenged - Appellants who were working as Asst. District Attorney/Public Prosecutor/Dy. Advocate General were appointed as Additional District and Sessions Judges, by State Govt. in pursuant to recommendation of HC by way of direct recruitment - Aggrieved unsuccessful candidates filed petitions before HC raising diverse grounds of challenge - However by impugned judgment HC quashed appointments of appellants holding that they did not have requisite criteria to qualify for recruitment as contemplated u/art. 233 of Constitution and that some of them did not have requisite experience and that an Advocate could not be a full-time salaried employee of any person, government, firm, corporation or concern so long as he continues to practice - Hence, instant appeals - Appellants contended that service of a Public Prosecutor (PP) or Assistant Public Prosecutor (APP) or Government Pleader (GP) did not render a person ineligible for appointment as a District Judge (DJ) if he has been for not less than 7 years an Advocate or a Pleader and that though State Govt. appoints GP and PP in terms of s. 2(7) of CPC, for conducting civil cases and s. 24 of CrPC, for criminal cases, 'appointment', it was in reality and in substance an engagement of an Advocate for conducting cases in Courts - Advocates with experience were only considered for appointment as GP and PP, and they were always Officers of Court - Further contended that 1961 Act and r. 49 of BCI Rules should be read harmoniously with provisions of CPC and CrPC, having regard to object and scheme of appointment of GP, PP, APPs - BCI Rules could not be exercised inconsistent with CPC and CrPC and that bar u/r. 49 of BCI Rules applied to employees engaged for work other than conducting cases in Courts as Advocates - What was meant by 'Advocate' or 'Pleader' and expression 'the service' u/art. 233(2) of Constitution - Held, expression 'the service' in art. 233(2) of Constitution means 'judicial service' - Other members of service of Union or State were as it was excluded because art. 233 of Constitution contemplated only 2 sources from which DJ could be appointed - Those sources were judicial service and Advocate/Pleader or in other words from the Bar - DJs could be appointed from no source other than judicial service or from amongst Advocates - Art. 233(2) of Constitution excluded appointment of DJs from the judicial service and restricted eligibility of appointment as DJ from amongst the Advocates or Pleaders having practice of not less than 7 years and who were recommended by HC - Construction of expression, 'if he has been for not less than 7 years an advocate' in art. 233(2) of Constitution, means 7 years as an Advocate immediately preceding the application and not 7 years any time in the past - That was clear by use of 'has been' - Present perfect continuous tense was used for a position which began at some time in past and was still continuing - Thus, one of essential requirements articulated by expression in art. 233(2) of Constitution was that such person should with requisite period be continuing as an Advocate on the date of application - Impugned judgment was set aside - Appeals allowed.

(B) Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Service - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 233(2) - Advocates Act, 1961, ss. 17, 22, 24, 29 and 33 - Bar Council of India Rules, r. 49 - Haryana Superior Judicial Service Rules, 2007, r. 31 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 2(7) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 24 - Appointment of District Judges - Practicing Advocate - Member of Bar - Validity - Whether appellants, who were full time employee of Govt. and governed and regulated by statutory rules of State, were eligible for appointment to post of DJ u/art. 233(2) of Constitution - Held, SC had consistently held that though PP was a holder of 'public office' and holds a 'post' yet he was not in Govt. service as the term was usually understood - Despite those differences, for the purposes of art. 233(2) of Constitution there was not much difference in a PP and APP and both of them were covered by expression 'Advocate' - PP u/s. 24 of CrPC was appointed by State Govt. or Central Govt. for conduct of prosecution, appeal or other proceeding on its behalf in HC or for a district and APP was appointed u/s. 25 of CrPC by State Govt. or Central Govt. to conduct prosecution on its behalf in courts of Magistrates - Main function of PP and APPS was to act and/or plead on behalf of Govt. in a Court; both of them conduct cases on behalf of Govt. - For the purposes of counting experience as an Advocate as prescribed in ss. 24(7) and 24(8) of CrPC, period, during which a person has rendered service as a PP or APP was treated as being in practice as an Advocate u/s. 24(9) of CrPC -Thus, words rendering of service as a PP or APP was deemed to be practice as an Advocate - In other words, expression 'advocate or pleader' in art. 233(2) of Constitution was used for a member of Bar who conducts cases in Court or, in other words acts and/or pleads in Court on behalf of his client - In Sushma Suri, 1998 Indlaw SC 1130, SC construed the expression 'members of the Bar' to mean class of persons who were actually practicing in Courts of law as Pleaders or Advocates - A PP or a Govt. Counsel on rolls of State Bar Council and entitled to practice under 1961 Act was held to be covered by expression 'Advocate' u/art. 233(2) of Constitution, which was respectfully agreed - SC went on to say that a PP or a Govt. counsel on rolls of the Bar Council was entitled to practice - It was laid down that test was not whether such person was engaged on terms of salary or by payment of remuneration but whether he was engaged to act or plead on its behalf in a Court of law as an advocate - Terms of engagement did not matter at all and what matters was as to what such law officer engaged by Govt. did - Whether he acts or pleads in Court on behalf of his employer or otherwise - If he was not acting or pleading on behalf of his employer then he ceases to be an advocate; if terms of engagement were such that he did not have to act or plead but did other kinds of work then he becomes a mere employee of Govt. or the body corporate - Functions which law officer discharge on his engagement by Govt. were held decisive - Employment spoken of in r. 49 of BCI Rules did not cover employment of an advocate who was solely or, in any case, predominantly employed to act and/or plead on behalf of his client in Courts of law - If a person was engaged to act and/or plead in Court of law as an advocate although by way of employment on terms of salary and other service conditions, such employment was not what was covered by r. 49 of BCI Rules as he continue to practice law but, on other hand, if he was employed not mainly to act and/or plead in a Court of law, but to do other kinds of legal work, the prohibition in r. 49 of BCI Rules immediately come into play and then he becomes a mere employee and ceases to be an advocate - Bar contained in r. 49 of BCI Rules applied to an employment for work other than conduct of cases in Courts as an advocate - Deletion of second and third para by Resolution dt.22.6.2001 passed by BCI has not materially altered the position insofar as advocates who was employed by State or Central Govt. to conduct civil and criminal cases on their behalf in Courts were concerned - Factum of employment was not material but key aspect was whether such employment was consistent with his practising as an advocate or, whether pursuant to such employment, he continues to act and/or plead in Courts - If answer was yes, then despite employment he continues to be an advocate - If answer was in negative, he ceases to be an advocate - PP was is not a mouthpiece of investigating agency, even though PP/APP was in full-time employ with Govt. and was subject to disciplinary control of employer, but once he appear in Court for conduct of a case or prosecution, he was guided by norms consistent with interest of justice - His acts always remain to serve and protect public interest - He has to discharge his functions fairly, objectively and within framework of legal provisions - Thus, it might not be correct to say that APP was not an officer of Court - Appellants did not cease to be advocate and fulfilled eligibility u/art. 233(2) of Constitution and r. 11(b) of 2007 Rules on the date of application - Sushma Suri v. Government of National Capital Territory of Delhi and Another, 1998 Indlaw SC 1130 and Satish Kumar Sharma v. Bar Council of H.P, 2001 Indlaw SC 20363, followed - Impugned judgment was set aside - Appeals allowed.
