M/S. Uniworth Textiles Ltd. v Commissioner Of Central Excise, Raipur

Supreme Court of India

22 January 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

D.K. Jain, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 41; (2013) 9 SCC 753; 2013 (288) E.L.T. 161; JT 2013 (2) SC 113; 2013 (2) RCR(Civil) 472; 2013(1) SCALE 541; [2013] 3 S.C.R. 27

Case Digest

Subject: Customs; Indirect Tax

Summary: Customs - Customs Act, 1962, s. 28, 112 - Exemption - Willful default - Penalty - Challenged - Appellant (company) was engaged in manufacture of wool and ploy-wool worsted grey fabrics - GOI granted status of Export Oriented Unite (EOU) to appellant - Appellant was procuring furnace oil for captive power plant of another unit, it wrote to Development Commissioner seeking clarification that whether duty on supply and receipt of furnace oil and electricity respectively was required to be paid - Thereafter, Commissioner Customs demanded duty for period during which appellant imported furnace oil on behalf of appellant's sister concern stating that procurement of furnace oil was against provisions of the Act and issued show cause notice - Aggrieved appellant filed claims against duty and penalty before Commissioner of Customs, Central Excise (Appeals) and Tribunal, which were rejected - Aggrieved appellant filed an appeal before Appellate Tribunal, which upheld levy of customs duty on import of furnace oil and penalty u/s. 112 of the Act and rejected plea of appellant that demand of duty and penalty was barred by limitation - Hence, instant appeal - Revenue contended that of the 3 categories, conduct of appellant falls under case of 'willful misstatement' - Whether appellant was entitled to exemption of duty and penalty as claimed - Held, observation by Commissioner, unfounded on any material fact or evidence, points to a finding of collusion or suppression or misstatement - Use of word 'willful' introduces a mental element and hence, required looking into the mind of appellant by gauging its actions, which was an indication of one's state of mind - S. 28 of the Act clearly contemplate 2 situations viz., inadvertent non-payment and deliberate default - The former was canvassed in the main body of s. 28 of the Act and was met with a limitation period of 6 months, whereas latter, finds abode in proviso to the section and faces a limitation period of 5 years - For operation of proviso, intention to deliberately default was a mandatory prerequisite - In instant case, from evidence adduced, one would draw an inference of bona fide conduct in favour of appellant - Appellant laboured under the very doubt which forms the basis of issue before Court and, decided to address it to concerned authority, Development Commissioner, thus, in a sense offering its activities to assessment - Development Commissioner answered in favour of appellant and in its reply, even quoted a letter by Ministry of Commerce in favour of an exemption the appellant was seeking, which anybody would have found satisfactory - Only on receiving that satisfactory reply did the appellant decide to claim exemption - It reflect the fact that appellant made efforts in pursuit of adherence to law rather than its breach - It was a cardinal postulate of law that burden of proving any form of mala fide lies on the shoulders of one alleging it - On account of fact that burden of proof of proving mala fide conduct under the proviso to s. 28 of the Act lies with Revenue and no specific averments find a mention in show cause notice which was a mandatory requirement for commencement of action under said proviso - There was no willful default on the part of appellant and extended period of limitation under said provision could not be invoked against appellant - Impugned orders were set aside - Appeal allowed.
