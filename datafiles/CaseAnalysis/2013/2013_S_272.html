Sooguru Subrahmanyam v State of Andhra Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

04 April 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, K.S. Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 201; (2013) 4 SCC 244; AIR 2013 SC 1643; 2013 ALL MR (Cri) 2642; 2013 CRLJ 2253; JT 2013 (8) SC 441; 2014 (1) RCR(Criminal) 518; 2013(5) SCALE 322; [2013] 2 S.C.R. 514

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 313 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Accused/appellant was married to deceased - Appellant found deceased in company of 'x' in an auto-rickshaw, appellant dragged 'x' out from auto-rickshaw and assaulted him - Appellant took deceased to house and warned her - Differences between couple grew to bitterness which resulted murder of deceased - A case was registered against appellant u/s. 302 of IPC - Trial Court convicted appellant and sentenced him to suffer rigorous imprisonment for life - Appellant appealed before DB and same was dismissed - Hence instant appeal - Whether Courts below were right in passing conviction order - Held, it was sound proposition that every criminal act was done with motive, it was unsound to suggest that no such criminal act could be presumed unless motive was proved - After all, motive was psychological phenomenon - Mere fact that prosecution failed to translate that mental disposition of appellant into evidence does not mean that no such mental condition existed in mind of assailant - It was also observed that in some cases, it might not be difficult to establish motive through direct evidence, while in some other cases, inferences from circumstances might help in discerning mental propensity of person concerned - In case at hand, as was noticed, there was material on record which suggests that there was some ire that had swelled up in mind of accused to extinguish life spark of wife - It quite often impures mind, takes it to devil's den and leads one to do unjust acts than just deeds - In any case, it does not give license to commit murder - Thus, submission pertaining to absence of motive had no substance - All links in chain of evidence were established beyond reasonable doubt and established circumstances were consistent with singular hypothesis that appellant was guilty of crime and it was totally inconsistent with his innocence - On basis of pronouncements in Sharad Birdhichand Sarda v. State of Maharashtra, 1984 Indlaw SC 432, Padala Veera Reddy v. State of Andhra Pradesh and ors, 1989 Indlaw SC 31, Balwinder Singh v. State of Punjab, 1995 Indlaw SC 798, Harischandra Ladaku Thange v. State of Maharashtra, 2007 Indlaw SC 871 and Jagroop Singh v. State of Punjab, 2012 Indlaw SC 217 - Appeal dismissed.
