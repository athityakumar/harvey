Subhash Popatlal Dave v Union of India and another

Supreme Court of India

16 July 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Altamas Kabir, Gyan Sudha Misra, J. Chelameswar

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 454; (2014) 1 SCC 280; AIR 2013 SC (Supp) 121; 2013 CRLJ 4166; 2013 (298) E.L.T. 3; 2013 (3) RCR(Criminal) 858; 2013(9) SCALE 295

Case Digest

Subject: Fema

Summary: FEMA - Conservation of Foreign Exchange and Prevention of Smuggling Activities Act, 1974 - National Security Act, 1980, ss. 6 and 7 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 82, 83, 84 and 85 - Customs Act, 1962 - Preventive detention under foreign exchange regulation act - Sustainability - Detention orders were passed either against petitioners themselves or the persons represented by them under 1974 Act - Whether detention order passed under the provisions of the Act could be challenged at the pre-execution stage - Held, it would be seen from the provisions of s. 7 of 1974 Act that if the appropriate Govt. had reason to believe that a person in respect of whom a detention order was made, was absconding, or was concealing himself so that the order could not be executed, the Govt. might take recourse to the provisions of ss. 82, 83, 84 and 85 of CrPC and his property, as if the order directing him that he be detained were a warrant issued by the Magistrate - S. 7(1)(b) of 1974 Act also provided for penal consequences, were not complied with by the proposed detenue - Accordingly, s. 7 of 1974 Act empowered the Govt. to take recourse to either the provisions of CrPC relating to absconding persons or pass an order directing the person concerned to appear before the concerned officer and on the detenue's failure to do so, to inflict punishment with imprisonment for a term which could extend to one year or with fine or both - Provisions of ss. 6 and 7 of 1980 Act, were identical to the provisions of ss. 6 and 7 of 1974 Act - Said provisions enumerated the powers vested in authorities when a proposed detenue would abscond - That was the ordinary law of the land, and not preventive detention, which was meant to prevent the commission of offences, and not to punish an individual for violation of statutory provisions - Accordingly, submissions made on behalf of the Union of India and the State could not be accepted and absconsion could not, therefore, be made a ground for making an order of preventive detention - In order to arrive at a decision in these matters and to answer the question as to whether an order of preventive detention could continue to subsist after a long period had lapsed from the date of passing of the order, it would, first of all, be necessary to appreciate the difference between preventive detention and the ordinary criminal law providing for detention and arrest. While the Constitution, which was the cornucopia of all laws, accepted the necessity of providing for preventive detention, it also provided certain safeguards against arbitrariness and making use of the provision as a tool against political opponents - Since the said provision deprived a citizen of some of the basic and fundamental rights guaranteed to him under the Constitution, the Courts had dealt with laws relating to preventive detention with great care and caution to ensure that the provision was not misused by the Investigating Authorities as an easy alternative to proper investigation - Normally, the life of a preventive detention order was one year - Such a period was intended to give the detenue, who was detained without any trial, an opportunity to introspect and reflect into his past deeds, and to dissuade him from indulging in the same in future. In other words, the period of detention is intended not to punish the detenue, but to make him realize the impact of his earlier indiscretions on society and to discontinue the same - Both, the Revenue, as also the police authorities, appeared to be myopic in regard to the dividing line between preventive detention and arrest for a crime actually committed - State and its authorities had attempted to justify the continuance of the validity of detention orders even after the lapse of several years after the passing of the detention order, citing principles such as a person could not take advantage of his own wrong, in support thereof - Respondent authorities had fused the two principles together in dealing with the question of preventive detention - Person evading service of an order of preventive detention could not be treated with the same yard-stick as a person, accused of having committed a criminal offence and evading arrest to thwart the criminal process initiated against him - Two principles stood on different footings - In the first place, the proposed detenue was detained without being made an accused in connection with any particular case, but to prevent him from committing an offence, whereas in the second place, a person actually charged with having committed an offence was on the run to avoid the consequences of his criminal acts - Once this difference was appreciated, an order of detention passed and remaining unexecuted for several years becomes open to question regarding its executability - If the intention of the authorities in passing a detention order was to prevent the commission of an offence by the proposed detenue in the future, then after the passage of a number of years, the concerned authorities would have to consider whether the order of preventive detention should at all be executed in the absence of any information that the proposed detenue had continued with unlawful activities - When the object of a preventive detention order was to prevent the proposed detenue from committing any offence, which was either against the national interest or the interest of society in the future and there was nothing on record to indicate that the proposed detenue had indulged in any such activity after the order of preventive detention was passed, it would be illogical to pursue the execution of the detention order as the arrest and detention of the proposed detenue would become irrelevant and would not achieve the object for which it was passed - Concept of a person being prevented from taking advantage of his own wrong could not, be applied in the case of a detention order where the object of passing such an order was quite different from proceeding against a person charged with having committed a criminal offence - SC was also inclined to hold that orders of detention should not, as a matter of course, be read as an alternative to the ordinary laws of the land to avoid the rigours of investigation in order to make out a case for prosecution against the proposed detenue - SC also held that if a dispute leading to the issuance of the detention order was settled on the basis of a statutory provision such as 1962 Act and in terms of the Statute immunity from prosecution u/s. 127H of 1974 Act was given, the continuance of the order of detention would be completely illogical and even redundant - Accordingly, in such cases, the orders of preventive detention were liable to be quashed along with the Warrants of Arrest and Proclamation and Attachment issued u/ss. 82 and 83 of CrPC - Thus, orders of detention challenged were quashed on the ground that the said orders had become stale and the live link between the orders of detention and the object sought to be achieved by the said orders, stood snapped - Some of the orders were made thirteen years ago and the very purpose of such detention orders were rendered meaningless in the absence of any material that the proposed detenues had continued to indulge in activities which form the basis of the preventive detention orders - Order accordingly.
