Sushil Sharma v State of (NCT of Delhi)

Supreme Court of India

08 October 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjana Prakash Desai, P. Sathasivam, Ranjan Gogoi

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 667; (2014) 4 SCC 317; 2013 (4) MLJ(Crl) 421; 2013 (4) RCR(Criminal) 653; 2013(12) SCALE 622

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Ballistic Expert

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120-B, 201, 212 and 302 - Conspiracy - Murder - Conviction - Challenged - Appellant was tried for offences punishable u/ss. 302, 120-B r/w ss. 302 and 201 of IPC - A2-Keshav was tried u/s. 120-B r/w ss. 302 and 201 of the IPC - A3, A4 and A5 were tried u/s. 212 of the IPC - It was alleged that the deceased was killed by the appellant since he was suspecting that she was having some relationship with PW-12 - Appellant also did not want to make his marriage with the deceased public and the deceased was insisting on that - After killing her, the appellant with the help of A2 burnt her dead body in the tandoor of Bagia Bar-be-Que - Additional Sessions Judge convicted the appellant u/s. 302 of the IPC - Appellant and A2 u/s. 120-B r/w s. 201 of the IPC - Charge u/s. 212 of the IPC was held not proved against A3, A4 and A5 and they were acquitted - Additional Sessions Judge forwarded the death reference to the HC - HC confirmed the death sentence awarded to the appellant - Hence, instant appeal - (A) Whether conviction of appellant was correct - Held, prosecution successfully proved beyond reasonable doubt that the (a) the appellant and the deceased were married and they were staying together (b) the relations between the appellant and the deceased were strained and that the appellant was suspecting the fidelity of the deceased; thus, a strong motive to murder; (c) the appellant and the deceased were last seen together in the evening of 2-7-1995; (d) on 2-7-1995, at about 11.00 p.m. there was a fire in Bagia Restaurant and the appellant was seen at around 10.15 p.m. at the Bagia Restaurant; (e) A2, who was an employee of the Bagia Restaurant owned by the appellant, was seen shuffling the wood in the tandoor (f) charred corpse found in the tandoor was identified to be that of the deceased; (g) blood stained articles were recovered from the said flat where the appellant and the deceased were staying together; (h) car was found to contain dry blood (i) five empty cartridges, one lead bullet, a ply with bullet hole and an air pistol were recovered from the said flat where the appellant and the deceased were staying together; (j) CFSL report stated that the two bullets recovered from the skull of the deceased and the one lead bullet recovered from the said flat were fired from the .32 Arminius revolver recovered by the police (k) the death of the deceased was homicidal and was consequent upon firearm injuries to the head of the deceased caused by the appellant alone with his .32 bore Arminius revolver; (l) as per the CFSL Report, blood found on various articles seized from the said flat and from Bagia Restaurant and the blood found on the bullets recovered from the skull tallied; it was of the blood group of the deceased (m) the defence of alibi pleaded by the appellant was found to be false; and (p) the appellant and A2 conspired to cause disappearance of the evidence of murder by burning the dead body of the deceased in tandoor of Bagia Restaurant - Circumstances was complete - Prosecution, therefore, proved that the appellant alone committed the murder of the deceased in the said flat - Appellant conspired with A2 to do away with the dead body of the deceased so as to cause disappearance of the evidence of murder and, at the instance of the appellant, A2 burnt the dead body in the tandoor - Appellant, therefore, rightly been convicted u/s. 302 of the IPC and also for offence u/s. 201 r/w s. 120-B of the IPC - A2 acquitted of offence punishable u/s. 302 r/w s. 120-B of the IPC - However, he was rightly convicted for offence punishable u/s. 201 r/w s. 120-B of the IPC - (B) Whether the death sentence awarded by the Trial Court and confirmed by the HC should be confirmed - Held, medical evidence did not establish that the dead body of the deceased was cut - Murder was the outcome of strained personal relationship - It was not an offence against the Society - Appellant had no criminal antecedents - Offence was brutal but the brutality alone would not justify death sentence in instant case - Hence, death sentence commuted to life imprisonment - Appeal disposed of.
