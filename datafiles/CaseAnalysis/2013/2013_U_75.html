Manoj H. Mishra v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

09 April 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Surinder Singh Nijjar, M.Y. Eqbal

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 229; (2013) 6 SCC 313; AIR 2013 SC 2809; 2013 (3) AWC 2965; 2013 (137) FLR 911; JT 2013 (4) SC 513; 2013 (3) LLJ 289; 2013(5) SCALE 618; [2013] 5 S.C.R. 770; 2013 (5) SLR 45

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Summary: Service - Appointment - Suspension - Justifiability - Appellant was appointed as Tradesman/B Class III post at Kakarapar Atomic Power Project(KAPP) - During the monsoon season, there was heavy rain during the night of 15-6-1994 and water at K Dam had risen beyond danger level - However, more than 25 feet of turbine which was adjacent to Nuclear reactors was submerged under water - In fact, entire record room and computer room were washed away - Appellant wrote letter to Editor, of newspaper narrating about incident - Appellant pointed out that he did not disclose any information which he could had received during his official duty - Respondent authorities placed appellant under suspension and served with charge sheet - Disciplinary Authority ordered removal of appellant from service of KAPP w.e.f. 30-3-1996 - Appellant filed appeal before Station Director, which was dismissed - Appellant preferred revision application before respondent No. 3 which was also dismissed - Appellant filed writ petition before Single Judge, which was dismissed - Appellant preferred appeal before DB, which was also dismissed - Hence, instant appeal - Whether punishment imposed on appellant was shockingly disproportionate to misconduct - Held, appellant was educated only upto 12th standard - Appellant was neither an engineer, nor an expert on functioning of Atomic Energy Plants - Appellant did not fulfilled the criteria for being granted status of 'whistle blower' - One of basic requirements of a person being accepted as a 'whistle blower' was that his primary motive for activity should be in furtherance of public good - However, activity had to be undertaken in public interest, exposing illegal activities of a public organization or authority - Conduct of appellant did not fall within high moral and ethical standard that should be required of a bona fide 'whistle blower' - Newspaper reports as well as other publicity undoubtedly created a great deal of panic among local population as well as throughout State - A 'whistle blower' should be a person who possesses qualities of a crusader and his honesty, integrity and motivation should leave little or no room for doubt - It should not be incidental or byproduct for an action taken for some ulterior or selfish motive - Appellant indulged in making scandalous remarks by alleging that there was widespread corruption within organization - Such allegations should clearly have a deleterious effect throughout organization apart from casting shadows of doubts on integrity of entire project - Appellant had failed to maintain standard of confidentiality and discretion which was required to be maintained - Punishment imposed on appellant was not 'so disproportionate to offence as to shock the conscience' - Appeal dismissed.
