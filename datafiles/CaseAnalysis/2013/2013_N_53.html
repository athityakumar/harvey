Niranjan Hemchandra Sashittal And Another v State Of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

15 March 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 158; (2013) 4 SCC 642; AIR 2013 SC 1682; 2013 CRLJ 2143; JT 2013 (6) SC 632; 2013 (2) RCR(Criminal) 690; 2013 (3) RLW 2411; 2013(3) SCALE 265; [2013] 4 S.C.R. 767

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Summary: Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 21 and 32 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 - Speedy Trial - Maintainability - Grievance of petitioner pertained to procrastination in trial, gradual corrosion of their social reputation, deprivation of respectable livelihood because of order of suspension passed against the petitioner No.1 during which he was getting meagre subsistence allowance and had reached the age of superannuation without being considered for promotion, extreme suffering of emotional and mental stress and strain, and denial of speedy trial that had impaired their Fundamental Right enshrined u/art. 21 of Constitution - Hence instant petition - Whether in such case HC, in exercise of powers u/art. 32 of the Constitution, should quash the criminal trial on the ground of delay - Held, it was requisite to state that in instant petition, HC was only concerned with the time spent after 2-3-2001, i.e., the date of pronouncement of the judgment in the earlier criminal appeals, and further the factual matrix as already exposited showed how the delay had occurred - Factum of delay and its resultant effect were to be tested on the basis of the exposition of law by SC - It was to be kept in mind that on one hand, right of the accused was to have speedy trial and on the other, quashment of the indictment or the acquittal or refusal for sending the matter for re-trial had to be weighed, regard being had to the impact of the crime on the society and the confidence of the people in the judicial system - There could not be mechanical approach - From the principles laid down in many authority of HC, it was clear as crystal that no time limit could be stipulated for disposal of the criminal trial - Delay caused had to be weighed on the factual score, regard being had to the nature of the offence and the concept of social justice and the cry of the collective - In the case at hand, appellant was charge-sheeted under Act for disproportionate assets - Said Act had purpose to serve - Parliament intended to eradicate corruption and provide deterrent punishment when criminal culpability was proven - Intendment of the legislature had immense social relevance - In the present day scenario, corruption was treated to have the potentiality of corroding the marrows of the economy - SC was disposed to think that the balance to continue the proceeding against the accused/appellants tilted in favour of the prosecution and, hence, SC was not inclined to exercise the jurisdiction u/art. 32 of the Constitution to quash the proceedings - However, Special Judge was directed to dispose of the trial by the end of December, 2013 - Petition disposed of.
