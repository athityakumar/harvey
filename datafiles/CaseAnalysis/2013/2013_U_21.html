Novartis AG and others v Union Of India & Others

Supreme Court of India

01 April 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Aftab Alam, Ranjana Prakash Desai

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 172; (2013) 6 SCC 1; AIR 2013 SC 1311; 2013 (4) AWC 3611; 2013 (2) CLT(SC) 182; 2013 (6) CompLJ 131; JT 2013 (4) SC 195; 2013 (3) MLJ 421; 2013 (54) PTC 1; 2013 (2) RCR(Civil) 685; 2013(5) SCALE 12

Case Digest

Subject: Health & Drug; Intellectual Property

Keywords: Intellectual Property Rights, Patents (Amendment) Act, 2002, Patents (Amendment) Act, 1999, Federal Food, Drug and Cosmetic Act, Patents (Amendment) Ordinance, 2004, Patents (Amendment) Ordinance, 1994, Patents (Amendment) Bill, 1995, UK Patents Act, 1977

Summary: (A) Intellectual Property - Health & Drug - Indian Patents Act, 1970, s. 3(d) - Scope of s. 3(d) - Appellant company filed application for grant of patent for Imatinib Mesylate in beta crystalline form - Respondent Nos. 4 to 8 filed pre-grant objections - Assistant Controller of Patents and Designs rejected appellant’s application holding that invention claimed by appellant was obvious to a person skilled in art in view of disclosure provided in Zimmermann patent specifications; thus patentability of alleged invention was disallowed by s. 3(d) of the Act - Appellant filed appeal before Appellate Board - Appellant Board reversed findings of Assistant Controller on issues of anticipation and obviousness, however, held that patentability of the subject product was hit by s. 3(d) of the Act - Hence, instant appeal - Whether s. 3 of the Act was ex majore cautela (Out of abundant caution) - Held, 2005 amendment/addition made in s. 3(d) was meant especially to deal with chemical substances, and more particularly pharmaceutical products - Amended portion of s. 3(d) clearly set up a second tier of qualifying standards for chemical substances/pharmaceutical products in order to leave the door open for true and genuine inventions but, at the same time, to check any attempt at repetitive patenting or extension of patent term on spurious grounds - Further, s. 3(d) as representing ‘patentability’, a concept distinct and separate from ‘invention’ - Thus, no force was found in the submission that s. 3(d) was a provision ex majore cautela - Appeals disposed of.

(B) Intellectual Property - Health & Drug - Indian Patents Act, 1970 - Cancer drug - Patent - Grant of - Whether appellant was entitled to get patent for beta crystalline form of a chemical compound called Imatinib Mesylate which was a therapeutic drug for chronic myeloid leukemia and was marketed under the names ‘Glivec’ or ‘Gleevec’ - Held, drug Gleevec directly emanated from Zimmermann patent and came to market for commercial sale - Since the grant of Zimmermann patent, appellant had maintained that Gleevec (Imatinib Mesylate) was part of Zimmermann patent and obtained drug approval for it - Appellant claimed extension of the term of Zimmermann patent for the period of regulatory review for Gleevec, and it successfully stopped respondent No.5 from marketing its drug in UK on the basis of Zimmermann patent - US Board of Patent Appeals, in its judgment granting patent for beta crystalline form of Imatinib Mesylate, proceeded on the basis that though beta crystal form might not have been covered by Zimmermann patent, the Zimmermann patent had teaching for making of Imatinib Mesylate from Imatinib, and for its use in a pharmacological compositions for treating tumours or in a method of treating warm-blooded animals suffering from a tumoral disease - Appellant was, therefore, fully bound by the finding and could not take any contrary plea in the instant case - Appeals disposed of.

(C) Intellectual Property - Health & Drug - Indian Patents Act, 1970 - Grant of patent - Advancement of knowledge - Extent - Did the product for which appellant claimed patent qualify as a ‘new product’ which came by through an invention that had a feature that involved technical advance over existing knowledge and that made invention ‘not obvious’ to a person skilled in the art - Held, Imatinib Mesylate could not be said to be a new product, having come into being through an ‘invention’ that had a feature that involved technical advance over existing knowledge and that would make invention not obvious to a person skilled in art - Imatinib Mesylate was all there in Zimmermann patent and was a known substance therein - Further, after Zimmermann patent, appellant applied for, and in several cases obtained, patent in the US not only for beta and alpha crystalline forms of Imatinib Mesylate, but also for Imatinib in a number of different forms - Appellant, however, never asked for any patent for Imatinib Mesylate in non-crystalline form, for the simple reason that it had always maintained that Imatinib Mesylate was fully a part of Zimmermann patent and did not call for any separate patent - Hence, no force was found in the submission that development of Imatinib Mesylate from Imatinib was outside Zimmermann patent and constituted an invention as understood in the law of patent in India - Appeals disposed of.

(D) Intellectual Property - Practice & Procedure - Hogan decision by US Court - Applicability - Whether the decision of United States Court of Customs and Patent Appeals in In re Hogan, 559 F.2d 595 was applicable to the instant case - Held, in Hogan, Court of Customs and Patent Appeals held that a patent application that disclosed and enabled a method of making crystalline form of polymer was entitled to a claim for method of making a solid polymer, because the only known method for making a solid polymer at the time was applicants’ method of making crystalline form - Further, even in the US, Hogan represented a decision given in the context of special set of facts and circumstances of the litigation over polypropylene - In later decisions, Federal Circuit appeared to have drastically narrowed Hogan’s scope as a precedent - In Plant Genetics System, N.V. v. DeKalb Genetics Corp, 315 F. 3d 1335, 1341 (Fed. Cir. 2003) the effect of Hogan was considerably constricted and its effect was virtually eliminated in Chiron Corp. v. Genentech, Inc, 363 F. 3d 1247, 1257 (Fed. Cir. 2004) - Since Chiron, Federal Circuit had not referred to Hogan in any of its cases that involved claims to a genus where a single species was enabled - In the instant case, finding that Imatinib Mesylate was a known substance from Zimmermann patent was not based on conduct of appellant alone, but the finding was arrived at on an objective consideration of all material facts and circumstances - Hence, there was no application of the Hogan decision to the facts of instant case - Appeals disposed of.

(E) Intellectual Property - Health & Drug - Indian Patents Act, 1970, ss. 2(1)(i)(ja), 3(d) - Grant of patent - Scope of invention - Whether appellant’s product satisfied tests and thus qualified as ‘invention’ within the meaning of cls. (j) and (ja) of s. 2(1) and was patentable u/s. 3(d) in the category of ‘invention’ - Held, Imatinib Mesylate was a known substance from Zimmermann patent itself and its pharmacological properties were also known in the Zimmermann patent , which was referred in the article published in Cancer Research journal - Thus, Imatinib Mesylate did not qualify test of ‘invention’ as laid down in s. 2(1)(j) and s. 2(1)(ja) of the Act - Further, no material was offered to indicate that beta crystalline form of Imatinib Mesylate would produce an enhanced or superior efficacy (therapeutic) on molecular basis than what could be achieved with Imatinib free base in vivo animal model - Beta crystalline form of Imatinib Mesylate, did not qualify the test of s. 3(d) of the Act but that was not to say that s. 3(d) barred patent protection for all incremental inventions of chemical and pharmaceutical substances - Appellant’s claim for patent for beta crystalline form of Imatinib Mesylate would only appear as an attempt to obtain patent for Imatinib Mesylate, which would otherwise not be permissible in India - Thus, subject product, that is, the beta crystalline form of Imatinib Mesylate, failed the test of s. 3(d) - Appeals disposed of.
