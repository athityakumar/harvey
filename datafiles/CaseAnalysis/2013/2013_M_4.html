M. Manohar Reddy & Anr. v Union Of India & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

04 February 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Aftab Alam, Ranjana Prakash Desai

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 61; (2013) 3 SCC 99; AIR 2013 SC 795; 2013 (3) ALD(SC) 131; 2013(2) ALL MR 408; 2013 (2) AWC 1296; JT 2013 (2) SC 469; 2013(2) SCALE 171; [2013] 1 S.C.R. 711; 2013 (2) SLR 584; 2014 (3) WLN(SC) 59

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution

Keywords: Enrolment

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 124(4), 217(1) - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 147, 342, 427 and 324 - Appointment of HC judge - Consultation process - Challenged - Petitioners (Advocates) filed instant PIL to quash appointment of respondent no. 3, who was working as a Judge of a HC, and to direct State Bar Council to cancel enrolment of respondent no. 3, on the ground that consultation process leading to respondent no. 3's appointment was vitiated as HC, SC and Central Govt. failed to consider facts that respondent no. 3 was an accused, at the time of his appointment, a criminal trial was pending against respondent No.3 for offences u/ss. 147, 342, 427 and 324 of IPC and a proclaimed offender and that respondent no. 3 at the time of his enrolment as an Advocate had concealed the criminal proceedings and in the relevant column of application for enrolment with Bar Council, respondent no. 3 falsely stated that there was no pending proceeding against him - Hence, instant petition - Petitioners contended that failure to take into account pendency of criminal case while respondent no. 3's name was recommended by HC Collegium and approval and consent was accorded by SC Colllegium and Central Govt. for his appointment as a Judge of HC deeply flawed the participatory consultative process as envisaged in art. 217(1) of Constitution - UOI contended that Judge of any of superior Courts could only be removed from office on the basis of an impeachment motion passed by Parliament as provided u/art. 124(4) of Constitution (in case of a judge of SC) and u/art. 217 r/w art. 124(4) of Constitution (in case of a judge of HC) - Constitution did not recognize any other mode for removal of a Judge and that any deviation from Constitutional process in the garb of quashing appointment by a writ of quo warranto would be violative of scheme of Constitution and deleterious for the independence of judiciary - Whether consultation process for appointment of respondent no. 3 was vitiated on account of disposed criminal proceedings - Held, from record of criminal case which it was very difficult to hold that respondent No. 3 was even aware that in some record buried in Courts, he was named as an accused and he was required to appear in Court in connection with that case - Before appointment of respondent no. 3 as a judge of HC, he was Additional Advocate General of Andhra Pradesh - If said case would have been within his knowledge it was unimaginable that he would not have attended to it and got it concluded one way or the other - Prior to filing of instant petition, petitioners had made a representation, both before the Chief Justice of India and Law Minister, asking for removal of respondent No. 3 as a judge of HC on same allegations - Chief Justice of India received full consideration and called for a report on matter from Chief Justice of Andhra Pradesh HC vide letter dt.18.1.2012 - In report of Chief Justice, Andhra Pradesh HC, came to same conclusion as arrived at on an independent appraisal of record of case - Thus, at the time respondent No. 3 was being considered for appointment as a judge of HC, he was unaware of any case being pending in which he was named as an accused and it was quite wrong to refer to him as 'an absconder and a proclaimed offender' and respondent no. 3 did not suppressed of any material fact - It was clear that had it been a case of deliberate and conscious suppression of material fact by respondent no. 3 the position would have been entirely different, but that was not case - From record it was evident that none of members of HC or SC Collegia was aware of fact - State Govt. was equally unaware of fact and so was Central Govt. as was evident from the resume prepared by Law Ministry and intelligence bureau report - It was clear beyond doubt that not only respondent no. 3 himself but practically no one was aware of pendency of case in which respondent no. 3 was named as an accused - To fault the consultative process for not taking into account a fact that was not known at that time would put an impossible burden on Constitutional Authorities engaged in consultative process and would introduce a dangerous element of uncertainty in appointments - Fact was unknown and there was no suppression of that fact, a writ of quo warranto would certainly not lie on the plea that consultative process was faulty - Petition dismissed.
