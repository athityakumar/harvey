Neena Vikram Verma v Balmukund Singh Gautam and others

Supreme Court of India

12 April 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

H.L. Gokhale, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 235; (2013) 5 SCC 673; AIR 2013 SC 1632; 2013(4) ALL MR 486; 2013 (98) ALR 897; 2013 (2) CLT(SC) 258; 2013 (3) CTC 105; 2013 (2) JabLJ 365; JT 2013 (6) SC 572; 2013 (5) MahLJ 526; 2013 (4) MPLJ 14; 2013 (2) RCR(Civil) 847; 2013(5) SCALE 633; [2013] 5 S.C.R. 845

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Election

Keywords: Rajasthan Excise Act, Recrimination Petition, Gujarat Excise Act, Legislative assembly election, Corrupt practice Election, Identical pleadings

Summary: Election - Civil Procedure - Representation of Peoples Act, 1951, ss. 116A, 97 - Conduct of Election Rules, 1961, r. 3 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 6 r. 16, O. 7 r. 11 - Legislative Assembly election - Corrupt practice Election - Election petition - Recrimination petition - Identical pleadings - Legality - Appellant contested Legislative Assembly election from 'D' (General) Constituency and was declared elected defeating respondent no. 1 by 1 vote - Respondent no. 1 filed election petition before HC on the ground of improper reception, refusal and rejection of votes under the Act and that counting of postal ballot was done in violation of r. 63 of the Rules - Appellant in turn filed a Recrimination petition u/s. 97 of the Act within the time provided, which was dismissed on filing of application under O. 7 r. 11 of CPC for rejection of Recrimination petition - Aggrieved appellant filed SLP which was allowed and recrimination petition was restored to the election petition - Thereafter, HC by impugned judgment allowed election petition setting aside the election of petitioner - Aggrieved appellant filed statutory appeal u/s. 116A of the Act and respondent no. 1 filed another application under O. 6 r. 16 of CPC for striking off the pleadings in recrimination petition, which was allowed - Aggrieved appellant filed instant appeal - Appellant contended that contents of application under O. 6 r. 16 of CPC were identical to those in the earlier application filed under O. 7 r. 11 of CPC - Thus, it was pointed out that paragraphs 1 to 9 of application under O. 6 r. 16 of CPC were identical to paras 8 (d), 8 (e), 8(f), 8 (h), 8(i), 8 (j), 8 (k), 8(l) and 8 (m) respectively of the earlier application and that those paras of two applications specifically dealt with paras 3 (A) to 3 (G) and paras 4 (A) to 4 (D) of the Recrimination petition - Thus, if that application under O. 6 r. 16 of CPC was allowed, all the pleadings from pars 3 and 4 of the Recrimination Petition would be struck off - Those paras contained the main grounds of Recrimination petition, and if those were struck off nothing would remain in the Recrimination Petition -

Held, once it was accepted by a party by consent that a particular petition (in instant case Recrimination Petition) was to be heard by Court, by giving up the objection under O. 7 r. 11 of CPC, the very party could not be subsequently permitted to seek the striking off the pleadings containing the cause of action under the garb that the pleadings containing the cause of action were unnecessary, vexatious or scandalous - One was expected to take all necessary pleas at the same time - Party concerned was expected to raise such a contention at the time of passing of the Court order (consent order in instant case) or seek the liberty to raise it at a later point of time that some of the pleadings were unnecessary or vexatious or scandalous - No Court was expected to permit any matter to be raised which might and ought to have been made ground of defence or attack, once the same was relinquished by the party concerned - Single Judge ought to have noted that basic principle of any litigation - When looked into objections raised in instant matter under O. 6 r. 16 of CPC, the same was based on the requirement of s. 83 of the Act that the applicant was required to place material facts before the Court - As far as the allegation of criminality was concerned, sufficient material facts were placed on record alongwith the Recrimination Petition - Subsequently, a notice to admit facts was given, wherein, particulars of specific cases were given, wherein, the charge-sheets were filed for the charges which would result into imprisonment of 2 years or more, as required by s. 33A of the Act - Respondent chose not to reply to that notice - In fact Single Judge ought to have drawn an adverse inference, but he failed in doing so - As far as the ground of corrupt practice was concerned, as could be seen from the pleadings, on that aspect also material facts were placed on record as rightly pointed out by appellant - Impugned order passed by Single Judge in allowing the application of respondent no. 1 under O. 6 r. 16 of CPC was clearly untenable and bad in law - HC could not have entertained the application under O. 6 r. 16 of CPC when SC had restored the Recrimination Petition to the file of that Court by consent in order to decide it expeditiously - HC erred in holding that pleadings in paras 3 and 4 of Recrimination Petition were vague, vexatious, non-specific and without any material facts - Impugned order was set-aside - Appeal allowed.
