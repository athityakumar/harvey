M/S. Gian Chand And Brothers And Another v Rattan Lal @ Rattan Singh

Supreme Court of India

08 January 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 327; (2013) 2 SCC 606; 2013 (3) ALD(SC) 49; 2013 (96) ALR 839; 2013 (1) AWC 778; JT 2013 (1) SC 314; 2013 (3) MahLJ 6; 2013 (2) MPLJ 337; 2013 (170) PLR 252; 2013 (1) RCR(Civil) 961; [2013] 3 S.C.R. 601

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Civil Procedure

Keywords: Suit for money recovery

Summary: Banking & Finance - Civil Procedure - Suit for money recovery - Books of account - Burden of proof - Legality - Plaintiffs (appellants) initiated a civil action forming the subject matter of for recovery of money along with pendente lite and future interest at @18% p.a - It was case of plaintiffs that plaintiff no. 1 was a registered partnership firm carrying the business of commission agent for sale and purchase of food grains in a shop and plaintiff no. 2 was the partner of the said partnership firm - Respondent (defendant) had been maintaining regular and long standing current account with plaintiff and had taken advance from time to time from the plaintiffs which he had promised to return at the shop of plaintiffs - All the transactions between parties were entered in the books of accounts - However, defendant neither returned the money nor brought any agricultural produce for sale to the shop of plaintiffs - Trial Court held that plaintiffs had been able to establish that firm was engaged in business of a commission agent which lends money to agriculturists; that business transaction with the plaintiff's firm had not been denied by defendant and that plaintiffs were entitled to recover the amount along with pendente lite and future interest and decreed the suit - Aggrieved defendant filed an appeal - First Appellate Court held that plaintiffs were entitled to recover the amount excluding the sums covered under 2 entries along with proportionate interest and, accordingly, partly allowed the appeal and modified the judgment and decree of Trial Court - Aggrieved defendant filed second appeal before HC - HC by impugned judgment overturned the decision of First Appellate Court - Hence, instant appeal - Whether impugned judgment passed by HC was justified -

Held, if plaintiff asserts that defendant had acknowledged the signature, it was obligatory on his part to substantiate the same - But question would be what would be the consequence in a situation where the signatures are proven and there was an evasive reply in the written statement and what should be construed as substantiating the assertion made by the plaintiff - HC had fallen into error in holding that it was obligatory on the part of the plaintiffs to examine the handwriting expert to prove the signatures - Finding that plaintiffs had failed to discharge the burden was absolutely misconceived in the facts of case - O. 8 r. 4 of CPC stipulated that a defendant should not evasively answer the point of substance - It as alleged that if he receives a certain sum of money, it should not be sufficient to deny that he received that particular amount, but he should deny that he received that sum or any part thereof, or else set out how much he received, and that if an allegation was made with diverse circumstances, it should not be sufficient to deny it along with those circumstances - Defendants could not have been permitted to lead any evidence when nothing was stated in the pleadings - Courts below had correctly rested the burden of proof on the defendant but HC Court, in an erroneous impression, has overturned the said finding - In instant case, it could not be said that the evidence was not in line with the pleading and in total variance with it or there was virtual contradiction - Thus, finding returned by HC on that score was unacceptable - Plaintiff no. 2, his accountant and other witness have categorically stated that the books of accounts were maintained in the regular course of business - Same had not been disputed by defendant - Court found no reason that the books of accounts maintained the plaintiff firm in the regular course of business should have been rejected without any kind of rebuttal or discarded without any reason - HC had erroneously recorded that findings returned by Courts below were perverse and warranted interference and, thus, judgment rendered by it was legally unsustainable - Appeal allowed.
