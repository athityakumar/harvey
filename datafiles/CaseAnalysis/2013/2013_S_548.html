Sunder @ Sundararajan v State By Inspector Of Police

Supreme Court of India

05 February 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

J. S. KHEHAR, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 65; (2013) 3 SCC 215; AIR 2013 SC 777; JT 2013 (2) SC 483; 2013 (1) RCR(Criminal) 943; 2013(2) SCALE 204; [2013] 4 S.C.R. 25

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 364-A, 302 and 201 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 106 - Kidnapping for ransom - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant (accused) allegedly committed offences u/ss. 364-A, 302 and 201 of IPC - Trial Court convicted appellant and awarded with death penalty for offences u/ss. 364-A and 302 of IPC - Thereafter, matter was referred to HC for confirmation of death penalty - Aggrieved appellant filed an appeal before HC - HC by common judgment dismissed appeal and confirmed death sentence - Hence, instant appeal - Appellant contended that in absence of direct evidence, the slightest of a discrepancy, depicting the possibility of two views would exculpate appellant of guilt, on the basis of benefit of doubt - Whether impugned death sentence awarded and confirmed by HC was justified - Held, prosecution had placed sufficient material on record of the case to substantiate the factum of kidnapping of deceased, at the hands of appellant - Having proved the factum of kidnapping, inference of consequential murder of kidnapped person, was liable to be presumed - Once person concerned was shown as having been kidnapped, the onus would shift on the kidnapper to establish how and when the kidnapped individual came to be released from his custody - In absence of any such proof produced by the kidnapper, it would be natural to infer/presume, that kidnapped person continued in kidnapper's custody, till he was eliminated - Facts and circumstances of instant case, it was duly established, that deceased was kidnapped by appellant; appellant was not been able to produce any material on record of that case to show the release of deceased from his custody - S. 106 of 1872 Act placed onus on appellant - In absence of any such material produced by appellant, it had to be accepted, that custody of deceased had remained with appellant, till he was murdered - Motive/reason for appellant, for taking the extreme step was, that ransom as demanded by him, had not been paid - There was sufficient evidence on record of that case, on the basis whereof even factum of murder of deceased at the hands of appellant stood established - Appellant was found guilty of offence u/s. 364A of IPC - Perusal of s. 364A of IPC leaves no room for any doubt, that offence of kidnapping for ransom accompanied by a threat to cause death contemplates punishment with death - Thus, even without an accused actually having committed murder of the individual kidnapped for ransom, provision contemplated the death penalty - There was no doubt, that appellant was found to have kidnapped deceased for ransom, and had actually committed his murder - Thus, guilt of appellant u/s. 364A of IPC should be considered to be of the gravest nature, justifying harshest punishment prescribed for offence - It was apparent that appellant was found guilty of another offence u/s. 302 of IPC, which independently of one another, provided for death penalty - Appellant could not point even a single mitigating circumstances, but to affirm death penalty imposed on appellant - Impugned judgment imposing death penalty by HC was upheld - Appeal dismissed.
