Justice Chandrashekaraiah (Retd.) v Janekere C. Krishna and others

Supreme Court of India

11 January 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 450; (2013) 3 SCC 117; 2013 (2) KarLJ 377; [2013] 3 S.C.R. 987

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution

Keywords: Kerala Lok Ayukta Act, 1999, Judges (Inquiry) Act, 1968, Orissa Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 1995, Delhi Lokayukta and Upalokayukta Act, 1995, Gujarat Lokayukta Act, 1986, Orissa Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 1999, Chhattisgarh Lok Aayog Adhyadesh, 2002, Jharkhand Lokayukta Act, 2001, Haryana Lokayukta Act, 2002, Andhra Pradesh Lokayukta and Upa Lokayukta Act, 1983, Assam Lokayukta And Upa-Lokayuktas Act, 1985, Orissa Lokpal and Lokayukta Act, 1985, Andhra Pradesh Lokayukta Act, Bihar Lokayukta Act, 1973

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950 - Karnataka Lokayukta Act, 1984 - Kerala Lokayukta Act, 1999 - Orissa Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 1995 - Appointment of Upa-lokayukta - Consultation - Process - Sustainability - Upa-lokayukta was appointed by Governor on advice of Chief Minister of State - Respondent had filed writ petition challenging appointment of Upa-lokayukta pleading that his appointment was not in accordance with provisions of s. 3(2)(b) of Act which required consultation, inter alia, with Chief Justice of HC, but no such consultation was done before making appointment - HC held that there was no consultation with Chief Justice as such his appointment of Upa-lokayukta, was held void ab initio - Aggrieved instant appeal - Several questions were raised before SC, through this appeal which was streamline process and also give correct interpretation about provisions of Act - Whether Upa-lokayukta was a quasi-judicial authority or was only an investigator and who should initiate process of appointment of an Upa-lokayukta, what was meant by 'consultation' in context of s. 3(2)(b) of Act, whether consultation was at all mandatory u/s. 3(2)(b) of Act - How was process of consultation required to be carried out - Whether view of Chief Justice of HC regarding suitability of a person for appointment as Upa-lokayukta had primacy over views of others involved in consultation and finally, Whether HC was right in directing a particular procedure to be followed for appointment of an Upa-lokayukta - Held, as far as s. 3(2)(b) of Act was concerned, primary 'responsibility' for appointment of Upa-Lokayukta rests with Chief Minister who had to advice Governor, since Chief Justice was only one of Constitutional authorities required to be consulted by Chief Minister before advice was tendered to Governor, it could not be said that only his view would prevail over views of other constitutional authorities - If that were so, then consultation with other constitutional authorities including Chairman of Legislative Council, Speaker of Legislative Council and Leader of Opposition in Legislative Council and in Legislative Assembly would be reduced to a farce - It must be appreciated that these constitutional authorities also have an equal say in executive governance of State and there was nothing to suggest that their opinion should be subordinated to opinion of Chief Justice or that Chief Justice could veto their views, on other hand, since it was ultimately Chief Minister who had to advice Governor, it was he alone who had to take final call and shoulder responsibility of correctly advising Governor in matter of appointing most suitable person as an Upa-lokayukta - Mechanics of working of a statute had to be decoded from contents of statute and words used therein, otherwise there was a possibility of committing a serious error - If, as a general principle, it was held that view of Chief Justice must have primacy over views of everybody else, how would one explain omission of Chief Justice in consultation process in 1999, Act - Similarly, if as a general principle, it was held that view of Chief Minister must had primacy over views of everybody else, how would one explain omission of Chief Minister in consultation process in 1995, Act - It was for this reason that SC, held that a statute must be considered and understood on its own terms, in so construing Act, there was no reason to accord primacy to views of Chief Justice in appointment of an Upa-lokayukta under 1984,Act - Appeals partly allowed.
