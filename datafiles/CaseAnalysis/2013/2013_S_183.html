Shivasharanappa and another v State Of Karnataka

Supreme Court of India

07 May 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 296; (2013) 5 SCC 705; AIR 2013 SC 2144; 2013 CRLJ 2658; JT 2013 (7) SC 66; 2013 (5) KarLJ 148; 2013 (3) MLJ(Crl) 194; 2013 (3) RCR(Criminal) 86; 2013(6) SCALE 757; [2013] 5 S.C.R. 1104

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 143, 147, 448, 302, 201 r/w s. 149 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellants (accused) allegedly formed an unlawful assembly with common object to commit murder of deceased and thereby committed offences u/ss. 143, 147, 448, 302, 201 r/w s. 149 of IPC - Trial Court acquitted all accused persons - However on appeal before HC, DB by its impugned judgment reversed Trial Court judgment and convicted appellants u/s. 302 r/w s. 149 of IPC and sentenced appellants to undergo imprisonment for life - Hence, instant appeal - Appellants contended that HC reviewed the entire evidence in an unusual manner which was impermissible - Whether HC was justified in reversing the judgment of acquittal of appellants passed by Trial Court - Held, it was well settled in law that Court could rely upon the testimony of a child witness and it could form the basis of conviction if same was credible, truthful and was corroborated by other evidence brought on record - In instant case, trustworthiness of version of PW7 (mother of deceased and grandmother of PW9) and PW9 (daughter of deceased) were to be tested on touchstone and was to be seen whether circumstances did support prosecution case or to put it differently, whether evidence brought on record proves guilt of accused beyond reasonable doubt - PW9 testified to have witnessed appellants being exhorted by her paternal grandmother (mother-in-law of deceased), who had trespassed into house and forcibly took out her mother - PW9 immediately rushed to house of her maternal grandmother and disclosed it to her - It was elicited in cross-examination that her maternal grandmother was staying with her another married daughter and both the daughter and son-in-law were at home, PW9 did not choose it appropriate to inform them about the incident - It was manifest, grandmother (PW7), came with her granddaughter (PW9), to the house of deceased and tried to search for her - Despite search becoming a Sisyphean endeavour and non-effective, she chosen to remain silent and did not inform any one - HC accepted the version of those witnesses on 2 counts, that daughter was threatened and both of them were in state of fear - Trial Court, on contrary, found conduct of both witnesses to be highly unnatural - Definitely, there would have been fear because, as alleged, mother-in-law had forcibly taken away the deceased, but it was totally contrary to normal behaviour that she would have maintained a sphinx-like silence and not inform others - It was worthy to note that she did not tell it to anyone for almost 2 days and it was not explained why she had thought it apt to search for her daughter without even informing anyone else in the family or in village or without going to police station - Trial Court absolutely justified in treating conduct of said witnesses unnatural and, thus, felt that it was unsafe to convict accused persons on the basis of their testimony - It was a plausible view and there were no compelling circumstances requiring a reversal of judgment of acquittal - Impugned judgment of HC was set aside and appellants were acquitted - Appeals allowed.
