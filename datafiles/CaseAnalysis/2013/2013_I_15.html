 Sarah Mathew and others v Institute of Cardio Vascular Diseases by its Director, Dr. K. M. Cherian and another

Supreme Court of India

26 November 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjana Prakash Desai, Balbir Singh Chauhan, P. Sathasivam, Ranjan Gogoi, Sharad Arvind Bobde

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 762; (2014) 2 SCC 62; AIR 2014 SC 448; 2014 CRLJ 586; 2013 (205) DLT 188; 2013 (4) KLT 797; 2014 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 449; 2013 (4) MLJ(Crl) 720; 2014 (1) RCR(Criminal) 590; 2014 (1) RLW 569; 2013(14) SCALE 404; [2013] 12 S.C.R. 674

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1952, General Law, Indian Merchandise Marks Act, 1889

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 468, 469, 470 - Criminal proceedings - Limitation u/s. 468 of CrPC - Relevant date - Determination - Which was the relevant date for the purpose of computing period of limitation u/s. 468 CrPC - Held, Magistrate took cognizance when he applied his mind or took judicial notice of an offence for initiating proceedings in respect of offence committed - Taking cognizance was entirely an act of Magistrate - Taking cognizance would be delayed because of several reasons - It would be delayed because of systemic reasons or Magistrate's personal reasons - It was absurd to hold that Court should issue notice to accused for condonation of delay, explain the delay caused at its end - Only harmonious construction which could be placed on ss. 468, 469 and 470 CrPC was that Magistrate could take cognizance of an offence only if complaint in respect of it was filed within the prescribed limitation period - Magistrate would, however, be entitled to exclude such time as was legally excludable - Further, it would be unreasonable to take a view that delay caused by court in taking cognizance of a case would deny justice to a diligent complainant - Such an interpretation of s. 468 CrPC would be unsustainable and would render it unconstitutional - Provisions of Chapter 36 CrPC had construed harmoniously so as to strike a balance between the right of the complainant and the right of the accused - Thus, for the purpose of computing period of limitation u/s. 468 CrPC the relevant date was the date of filing of complaint or date of institution of prosecution and not the date on which Magistrate took cognizance - Reference answered.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 4, 5, 468, 473 - Child Marriage Restraint Act, 1929 - Criminal proceedings - Limitation - Conflict of decisions - Which of the two cases i.e. Krishna Pillai v. T. A. Rajendran and Another, 1989 Indlaw SC 313 or Bharat Damodar Kale and Another v. State of Andhra Pradesh, 2003 Indlaw SC 830 (which was followed in Japani Sahoo v. Chandra Sekhar Mohanty, 2007 Indlaw SC 767) laid down correct law - Held, Krishna Pillai should have to be restricted to its own facts - Krishna Pillai decision was not the authority for deciding relevant date for the purpose of computing the period of limitation u/s. 468 CrPC - In Krishna Pillai case, it was dealing with s. 9 of the Act which was a special Act - It specifically stated that no court should take cognizance of any offence under the Act after the expiry of one year from the date of offence - There was no reference either to s. 468 or s. 473 CrPC in Krishna Pillai case - It did not refer to ss. 4 and 5 CrPC which carved out exceptions for special Acts - Further, decision of A. R. Antulay v. Ramdas Sriniwas Nayak and Another, 1984 Indlaw SC 337, could not have been usefully referred to in Krishna Pillai while construing provisions of Chapter 36 CrPC - View taken in Krishna Pillai was unable to endorse - However, Bharat Kale which was followed in Japani Sahoo laid down correct law - Use of legal maxims as guiding principles in Bharat Kale and Japani Sahoo was perfectly justified - Reference answered.
