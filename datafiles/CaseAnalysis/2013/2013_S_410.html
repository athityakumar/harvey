Ankush Shivaji Gaikwad v State of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

03 May 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

T.S. Thakur, Gyan Sudha Misra

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 287; (2013) 6 SCC 770; AIR 2013 SC 2454; 2013 ALL MR (Cri) 2221; 2013 CRLJ 3044; JT 2013 (7) SC 26; 2013(4) M.P.H.T. 249; 2013 (2) RCR(Criminal) 1036; 2013(6) SCALE 778; [2013] 8 S.C.R. 863

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Entitlement of, Code of Criminal Procedure Bill, 1970, United States of America, the Victim and Witness Protection Act, 1982, Criminal Injuries Compensation Scheme, 1964, Criminal Justice Act, 1972, Victims of crime, Compensation to bereaved f

Summary: (A)Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 304 Part II - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant (accused) and 2 other co-accused allegedly committed offences u/ss. 326, 504 and 323 r/w s. 34 of IPC - Trial Court convicted appellant and co-accused and sentenced them to imprisonment for life - Aggrieved Appellant and co-accused filed an appeal before HC - HC by impugned judgment dismissed appeal of appellant but allowed the appeal of other co-accused - Aggrieved appellant filed instant appeal - Appellant contended that incident in question took place on a sudden fight without any premeditation - Whether in facts and circumstances of case act attributed to appellant would constitute offence u/s. 304 Part I or II of IPC - Held, there was not even a suggestion that appellant had any enmity or motive to commit any offence against deceased, leave alone a serious offence like murder - It was the barking of dog that provoked the appellant to beat the dog with rod that he was carrying apparently to protect himself against being harmed by any stray dog or animal - Deceased took objection to the beating of dog without in the least anticipating that same would escalate into a serious incident in the heat of moment - Exchange of hot words in quarrel over the barking of dog led to a sudden fight which in turn culminated in the deceased being hit with rod unfortunately on a vital part like head - Weapon used was not lethal nor was deceased given a second blow once he had collapsed to ground - Circumstance shows that appellant had not acted in an unusual or cruel manner in prevailing situation so as to deprive him of the benefit of Exception 4 to s. 300 of IPC - During exchange of hot words between deceased and appellant all that was said by appellant was that if deceased did not keep quiet even he would be beaten like a dog - Use of those words clearly shows that the intention of appellant and his companions was at best to be labour him and not to kill him as such - Cumulative effect of all circumstances, should entitle the appellant to benefit of Exception 4 to s. 300 of IPC - Appellant had the knowledge that injury inflicted by him was likely to cause death of deceased - Thus, case more appropriately fall u/s. 304 Part II of IPC - Instead of s. 302 of IPC appellant was convicted for offence of culpable homicide not amounting to murder u/s. 304 Part II of IPC and sentenced to undergo RI for 5 years - Appeal disposed of.

(B)Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 357, 357-A - Victims of crime - Compensation to bereaved family - Entitlement of - Whether Courts have a duty to advert to question of awarding compensation to victim and record reasons while granting or refusing relief to them u/ss. 357 and 357-A of CrPC - Held, s. 357 of CrPC conferred power coupled with a duty on Courts to apply its mind to question of awarding compensation in every criminal case - Power to award compensation was intended to reassure the victim that he or she was not forgotten in criminal justice system - Victim would remain forgotten in criminal justice system if despite Legislature having gone so far as to enact specific provisions relating to victim compensation, Courts choose to ignore the provisions altogether and did not even apply their mind to question of compensation - Unless s. 357 of CrPC is read to confer an obligation on Courts to apply their mind to question of compensation, it would defeat the very object behind the introduction of s. 357 of CrPC - If application of mind is not considered mandatory, the entire provision would be rendered a dead letter - In instant case, Trial Court and HC appeared to have remained oblivious to provisions of s. 357 of CrPC - Impugned judgments under appeal betray ignorance of Courts below about statutory provisions and duty cast upon Courts - Appeal disposed of.
