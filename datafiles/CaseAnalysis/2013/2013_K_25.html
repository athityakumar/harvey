Kamlesh Kumar and others v State of Jharkhand and others

Supreme Court of India

26 September 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

H.L. Gokhale, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 638; (2013) 15 SCC 460; 2014 CRLJ 22; 2013 (4) JCC 2752; 2013 (4) RCR(Criminal) 754; 2013(12) SCALE 216

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Fema; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1952

Summary: (A) FEMA - Practice & Procedure - Foreign Exchange Regulation Act, 1973, s. 61 - Foreign Exchange Management Act, 1999 - Cases under FEMA - Transfer of trial - Legality - Petitioners were prosecuted under FERA - Full Court of HC passed resolution trasferring trial of FEMA cases to Special Judge since FERA was replaced by FEMA - State issued notification empowering Special Judge to try cases under FEMA - Petitioners challenged transfer of trial to Special Judge - HC dismissed petitioners' writ petitions - Hence, instant SLPs - Whether transfer of petitioners' trial to Special Judge, who was hearing the related matter Fodder scam, was legal -

Held, it was merely lawful for Magistrate to try offences u/s. 61 FERA, but Court of Magistrate was not a court of exclusive jurisdiction as in A.R. Antulay v R.S. Nayak And Anr, 1988 Indlaw SC 467 - Offence was a non-cognizable one, and therefore it was not mandatory that it ought to have been tried only by Magistrate of First Class - Thus petitioner could not claim that Magistrate had special jurisdiction to try offence, and that State could not transfer the case to Sessions Judge or Special Judge - Further, accused and many of witnesses were common in Fodder scam and instant case and so also their evidence - Administrative power of HC in such a situation to effect transfer was upheld by SC in Ranbir Yadav v. State of Bihar, 1995 Indlaw SC 394 - No reason found to take a different view - Petitions dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 407 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 227 - Transfer of cases - Power of HC - Scope -

Held, HC did have power to transfer cases and appeals u/s. 407 CrPC which was essentially a judicial power - S. 407(1)(c) CrPC laid down that, where it would tend to general convenience of parties or witnesses, or where it was expedient for the ends of justice, HC could transfer such a case for trial to a Court of Sessions - That did not mean that HC should not transfer cases by exercising its administrative power of superintendence which was available to it u/art. 227 Constitution - Petitions dismissed.
