Vyas Ram @ Vyas Kahar and others v State of Bihar and another

Supreme Court of India

20 September 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

H.L. Gokhale, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 626; (2013) 12 SCC 349; 2014 CRLJ 50; 2013 (4) MLJ(Crl) 409; 2013 (4) RCR(Criminal) 371; 2013(11) SCALE 645; [2013] 11 S.C.R. 212

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Rules, 1987

Summary: (A) Criminal - Terrorists and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, s. 3 (1) - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 r/w ss. 149, 364 r/w ss. 149, 307 r/w s. 149 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant Maoists allegedly caused the death of deceased villagers by slitting their throats - Designated Court convicted appellants u/s. 3 (1) of the Act and u/s. 302 r/w ss. 149, 364 r/w ss. 149, 307 r/w s. 149 IPC - Appellants were sentenced to death - Hence, instant appeal - Whether conviction recorded against appellants was sustainable - Held, prosecution case against appellant no. 2 was weak and his name was not mentioned in FIR - PW2 injured witness, though stated in dock that he had seen appellants slitting throats, he failed to identify appellant No. 2 in Court - None of other witnesses including PW3, another injured witness, attributed any role to appellant No. 2 - No witness said that appellant was a member of Maoist group (MCC) - Appellant No. 2 was entitled to have the same yardstick applied to as was applied to other acquitted accused - However, in addition to appellant No.3's name being mentioned in FIR as one who was slitting throats, he was identified by PW2 injured witness in Court - Though other witnesses did not attribute any specific role to appellant No. 3, he was identified by them as a participant in crime - In the case of appellant No. 1, though his name was mentioned in FIR, heinous act of slitting throats was not attributed to him in FIR - PW2 had however stated in oral evidence that appellant No. 1 was slitting throats, and he identified him in court as well, though no other witness had attributed any particular role to him - PW2 being an injured witness, his testimony should not be ignored - PW2 attributed a specific role to appellants Nos. 3 and 1 - Hence, judgment convicting appellant no.2 was set aside and conviction of appellant Nos. 1 and 3 was upheld - Order accordingly.

(B) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Murder - Death reference - Sustainability - Whether death sentence awarded to appellant was warranted to be commuted - Held, occurrence of crime was of February 1992 and charges were framed in May 2004 - More than nine years had gone thereafter also, and appellants had been facing trauma of crime and trial all this period - Manner in which investigation had proceeded was far from satisfactory - Present incident was claimed to be a retaliatory attack by members of MCC - MCC members were essentially persons belonging to scheduled castes and backward classes, and economically weaker and exploited sections of society - Attack was supposed to be in retaliation to an earlier attack by victims' community - None of witnesses attributed to appellants that they belonged to MCC - It was quite possible that due to their poverty and caste conflict in villages appellants were drawn in the melee and participated in crime - At the same time no harm was done to women and children - Hence, death sentence awarded to appellant was commuted to imprisonment for life, means rest of their natural life - Order accordingly.
