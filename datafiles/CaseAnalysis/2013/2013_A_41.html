Ropan Sahoo and another v Ananda Kumar Sharma & Others

Supreme Court of India

22 January 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 32; (2013) 14 SCC 184; AIR 2013 SC (Supp) 569; JT 2013 (2) SC 130; 2013 (3) MLJ 189; 2013(1) SCALE 687; [2013] 3 S.C.R. 1129

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Excise

Keywords: Orissa Excise Rules, 1965, Transaction of Business Rules, 1961

Summary: R. M. Lodha, J.

Constitution - Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 15(3), 39(e), (f), 45 and 47 - Juvenile Justice Act, 1986 - Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2000, s. 7A - Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Rules, 2007, r. 12 - West Bengal Children Act, 1959, s. 24 - Uttar Pradesh Children Act, 1951, s. 2(4) - Bihar Children Act, 1970, 29 - Claim of Juvenility - After conviction - Procedure - Entitled to - To uniform juvenile justice system in country the 1986 Act was enacted, for protection, treatment, development and rehabilitation of juveniles - Thereafter, 2000 Act was enacted to carry forward constitutional philosophy engrafted in arts. 15(3), 39(e) and (f), 45 and 47 of Constitution Delinquent juvenile - By Act 33 of 2006, s. 7A was inserted in 2000 Act, which provided procedure to be followed when claim of juvenility was raised before any Court - U/s. 68(1) of 2000 Act the 2007 Rules were framed, for determination of age - In case of Gopinath Ghosh v. State of West Bengal, 1983 Indlaw SC 167, wherein accused/appellant filed a SLP and claimed to be 'child' within meaning of 1959 Act and juvenile, and SC for determination; remanded matter to Sessions Judge, which held that delinquent was juvenile on the date of offence - Thereafter, SC found that s. 24 of 1959 Act had an overriding effect taking away power of Court to impose sentence of imprisonment - Thereafter, claim of juvenility u/s. 29 of 1970 Act and u/s. 2(4) of 1951 Act, before SC, wherein SC held claimants juvenile based on school certificate and medical reports - Appellants contended that by insertion of s. 7A of 2000 Act clearly provided that claim of juvenility might be raised before any Court and it should be recognised at any stage, even after final disposal of case and such claim should be determined in terms of provisions contained in 2000 Act and the Rules, even if juvenile has ceased to be so on or before commencement of 2000 Act - Further contended that even if question of juvenility was not raised by juvenile even up to SC and there was some material to show that a person was a juvenile on the date of commission of crime, it could be recognised at any stage even at stage of undergoing sentence and that once prima facie test was satisfied, determination might be made in terms of r. 12 of the Rules and that forming an opinion on oral evidence would not serve purposes of 2000 Act - Hence, instant reference - Whether a different approach was required with regard to delinquent juveniles as and when plea of juvenility was raised before Courts - Held, claim of juvenility might be raised at any stage even after final disposal of case - It might be raised for the first time before SC as well after final disposal of case - Delay in raising claim of juvenility could not be a ground for rejection of such claim - Claim of juvenility could be raised in appeal even if not pressed before Trial Court and could be raised for the first time before SC though not pressed before Trial Court and in appeal Court - Claimant of juvenility should procedure some material which was prima facie satisfy Court that an inquiry into the claim was necessary and initial burden has to be discharged by the claimant - Documents referred in r. 12(3)(a)(i) to (iii) of the Rules should be sufficient for prima facie satisfaction of Court about the age of delinquent necessitating enquiry u/r. 12 of the Rules - Statement recorded u/s. 313 of CrPC was too tentative and might not be itself be sufficient ordinarily to justify or reject claim of juvenility - Credibility and/or acceptability of school leaving certificate or Voter's list, obtained after conviction would depend on facts and circumstances of each case and no hard and fast rule could be prescribed that they should be prima facie accepted or rejected - If school leaving certificate or Voter's list prima facie inspired confidence of Court, Court might act upon such documents for the purpose of s. 7A of 2000 Act and order an enquiry for determination of age of delinquent - An affidavit of claimant or any of parents, sibling, relative in support of claim of juvenility raised for the first time in appeal, revision or before SC during pendency of matter or after disposal of case should not be sufficient justifying an enquiry to determine the age of such person unless circumstances of case were so glaring that satisfy judicial conscience of Court to order an enquiry into determination of age of delinquent - Beneficent and salutary provisions contained in 2000 Act should not be defeated by hyper-technical approach of Courts and persons who were entitled to get benefits of 2000 Act should get such benefits - Age determination by medical examination was not very precise and should be considered prima facie on the touchstone of preponderance of probability - Whenever frivolous claim of juvenility raised, should be rejected at the threshold - Reference answered.

T. S. Thakur, J. (Concurred)

Held, u/rr. 12(3)(a)(i) to (iii) of the Rules, expression 'absence' was not defined under 2000 Act or the Rules - Mere non-production of document as referred in said Rules might not, therefore, disentitle the accused (claimant) of the benefit of 2000 Act nor could it tantamount to deliberate non-production, giving rise to an adverse inference unless Court was in peculiar facts and circumstances of a case of opinion that nonproduction was deliberate or intended to either mislead the Court or suppress the truth -Large number of persons engaged in criminal activities, might never have had opportunity to go to school - Poor education and poor economic set up were generally the main attributes of juvenile delinquents - Advantage of a beneficial legislation could not be denied to unfortunate and wayward delinquents - Misfortune of accused never going to a school could not be followed or compounded by denial of benefit that legislation provided in such emphatic terms, as to permit an enquiry even after last Court has disposed of appeal and upheld conviction - Affidavits might not be generally accepted as a good enough basis for directing an enquiry, that they were not so accepted was not a rule of law but a rule of prudence - Reference answered.
