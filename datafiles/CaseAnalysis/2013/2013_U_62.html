Christian Medical College Vellore and others v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

18 July 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Altamas Kabir, Vikramajit Sen

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 452; (2014) 2 SCC 305; 2013(9) SCALE 226; [2013] 7 S.C.R. 908

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution; Education

Summary: Education - Administrative - Constitution - Indian Medical Council Act, 1956, ss.19A and 20 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1)(g), 25, 26, 29(1) and 30 - Conducting entrance examination for aspiring medical students by one body across all institutions be it private or Government - Legality - Medical Council of India should not be entrusted with a right to conduct National Eligibility-cum-Entrance Test (NEET) - Petitioners added that if MCI or any other body would conduct examination in the nature of the NEET, the petitioners, who were managing medical colleges, would not be in a position to exercise their discretion in relation to giving admission to the students in their colleges and therefore, their fundamental right guaranteed u/art.19(1)(g) of Constitution and the rights of the minority institutions u/art.29 and 30 of Constitution would be violated - Submission was to the effect that the minority institutions should have full and unfettered right to select the students who were to be imparted education in their colleges - Any restriction or regulation of whatsoever type, would violate their fundamental rights - Whether introduction of the NEET would violate fundamental rights of the petitioners guaranteed under the provisions of arts.19(1)(g), 25, 26, 29(1) and 30 of the Constitution of India - Held, ss.19A and 20 of Act permitted the MCI to prescribe the minimum standards of medical education - S.33 of Act also empowered the MCI to make regulations to carry out the purposes of the Act - Thus, the said provisions enable the MCI to regulate the system of medical education throughout the country - It was a matter of sound common sense that to have doctors well versed in the subject of medicine and having proficiency in their field, there should be suitable and deserving students who should be imparted good medical education and there should be strict supervision over the education system so as to see that the students who were not up to the mark or were not having the highest standards of education were not declared successful at the examinations - System should be such that it should have effective regulations at three different stages - First stage was the admission of the students to medical colleges - Students who were admitted to the medical course should be suitable and should have the right aptitude so that they could be shaped well into the medical profession after being imparted proper education - Second stage was with regard to determination of syllabus and the manner of imparting education and for the said purpose, the regulating authorities should see that proper medical training was given to the students and for the said purpose sufficiently equipped hospitals should be there as teaching institutes - It should also be seen that sufficient number of patients were treated at the hospitals so that the students could get adequate practical training where the patients were being treated - Finally, the examinations, which the students had to pass to prove their worth as successful students should also be strictly regulated - If there was any lacuna or short-coming at any of the above three stages, it would adversely affect the professional standards of the students passing out from the educational institutions as physicians, who were trusted by the citizens of India at critical moments, when someone's life was at stake - By virtue of introduction of the NEET to be conducted under the supervision of the MCI, standards of the students at the stage of their admission to the medical colleges, be it for admission to the M.B.B.S. course or the post graduation studies in medical faculties, would be regulated - Similarly, for imparting education to the students studying in the field of Dentistry, Dental Council of India (DCI) had to regulate admissions so as to see that eligible and suitable students were admitted to the different courses in the field of dentistry - There was no need to discuss the importance of quality of input, when something was to be produced, manufactured or developed - Even when one thought of manufacturing an article, the manufacturer was conscious about the quality of the input and he would invariably select the best input i.e. such raw material so as to make his final product excellent - Principle was not different in the field of education - If an educational institution wanted an excellent output in the nature of a well trained, well educated, well groomed professional, the institution should saw that suitable and deserving students having an aptitude for becoming good doctors were admitted to the medical college - If among all good students, there were students who were not up to the mark, who were lagging behind in their studies, who were weak in studies, it would not be possible to educate or groom such students effectively and efficiently - Weak student might lag behind due to his lower level of grasping or education or training - In the circumstances, it became the duty of the regulating authority to saw that quality of the students at the stage of admission was thoroughly examined and only deserving and suitable students were given admission to the medical colleges so as to make them suitable members of a noble profession upon completion of their studies - So as to see that only deserving and suitable students were admitted to the medical colleges, the MCI had introduced the NEET - By virtue of introduction of the NEET, the students aspiring to become physicians or pursue further medical studies would have to pass the NEET - NEET would be a nationwide common examination to be held at different places in the country so that all students aspiring to have medical education, could appear in the examination and ultimately, on the basis of the result of the examination, suitability and eligibility of the students for admission to the medical profession could be determined - System was a part of regulation whereby entry to the field of medical education was regulated in such a way that only eligible and suitable students were given admission to medical colleges - If the NEET was conducted under the supervision of the apex professional body, it would inspire confidence in the system and in that event, the selection of the students for admission to the medical profession would be on merit based selection - No extraneous consideration would come into play in the process of selection - Process of selection would not be influenced by irrelevant factors like caste and creed, community, race, lineage, gender, social or economic standing, place of residence - Whether rural or urban, influence of wealth or power; and admission would be given only to the students who really deserved to be well qualified physicians or dentists - Thus, there would not be any discrimination or influence in the process of selection - SC added that though the students could be selected only on the basis of their merit, it would be open to the States to follow their reservation policy and it would also be open to the institutions based on religious or linguistic minority to select students of their choice, provided the students so selected have secured minimum marks prescribed at the NEET - From and among those students, who had secured prescribed qualifying marks, the concerned institutions, who wanted to give priority to the students belonging to a particular class or caste or creed or religion or region, etc. would be in a position to give preference to such students in the matter of their admission to the concerned medical college - Thus, the purpose with which arts. 25, 26, 29, and 30 were incorporated in Constitution would be fully respected and implemented - Furthermore, centralization of the selection process under holding the NEET would help the students to appear at the examination from any corner of our nation - Result of the examination would be published at the same time on one particular day and with the same standard - There would not be any problem with regard to equalizing marks and merits of different students passing different examinations from different regions or states or universities or colleges - Process of selection would be equal, fair, just and transparent - All the students would be in a position to compete from a common platform and the test would have credibility in the eyes of the students and the society - There were number of professional institutions which were having only one professional examination and there were some institutions which also had one common entrance test which would decide competence and capability of a student for being admitted to the professional course and the system which was followed by them for years was quite satisfactory and successful - Students would be benefited because they would not have to appear at different places on different days at different examinations for the same purpose - Thus such factors, in practical life, would surely help the students, the profession and the institutions which were not money minded and are sincere in their object of imparting medical education to the aspiring students - Cost of appearing at the NEET would be much less as the aspiring students would not have to purchase several expensive admission forms and would not have to travel to different places - Apprehension was voiced by the counsel for the petitioners that the minority institutions or the educational institutions belonging to special classes would be adversely affected because of the introduction of the NEET - In fact, the said apprehension was not well founded - Policy with regard to the reservation could be very well implemented if the NEET was introduced because the NEET would determine standard or eligibility of a student who was to be imparted education in the field of medicine - Institution imparting medical education would have to see that the student to be admitted was having minimum standard of suitability and the institution would be at a liberty to select a student of its choice if it wanted to promote a particular class of persons - By admitting suitable and deserving students having an aptitude for becoming doctors, the religious institutions would be in a position to have better doctors for fulfilling their objective - Moreover, policy with regard to reservation for certain classes, followed by the States would also not be adversely affected - From the deserving eligible students, who had procured qualifying marks at the NEET and who belonged to the reserved classes would be given preference so as to fulfill the policy with regard to reservation - Thus, students belonging to the reserved classes would also not suffer on account of holding the NEET - Petitions dismissed.
