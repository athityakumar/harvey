State of Rajasthan and others v Basant Agrotech (India) Limited and others

Supreme Court of India

06 December 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 802; (2013) 15 SCC 1; AIR 2014 SC 487; 2014 (302) E.L.T. 3; 2014 (1) RLW 604; 2013(15) SCALE 1

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Mines & Minerals

Keywords: Punjab Reorganisation Act, 1966, Sikh Gurdwaras Act, 1925, Kerala Building Tax Act, 1961, Madhya Pradesh Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Act, 1961, Madhya Pradesh Foreign Liquor Rules, 1996, Travancore Taxation on Income (Investigation, Commission) Act, Rajasthan Finance Act, 2008, Rajasthan Environment and Health Cess Rules, 2008

Summary: Administrative - Constitution - Rajasthan Finance Act, 2008, ss.16 and 19 - Rajasthan Environment and Health Cess Rules, 2008, rr.13 and 14 - Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, 1957 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Madhya Pradesh Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Act, 1961, s.2 - Electricity (Supply) Act, 1948, 79(c) - Issue a notification for collection of cess by State Govt. with retrospective effect - Legality - Respondent preferred petition before the HC challenging the constitutional validity of Chapter VII of 2008 Act which provided for levy of cess on mineral rights - Respondent was granted a mining lease for extraction on major minerals - As per the amendment brought in the year 2008 it was required to pay the environment and health cess imposed u/s. 16 of 2008 Act - State Government/appellant, in exercise of the powers conferred by S. 19 of 2008 Act, framed a set of rules called Rules - R.13 of Rules provided for the head under which the cess collected u/s. 16 of 2008 Act was to be credited - R.14 of Rules provided for the allocation of the funds for implementation of environment and health projects in mining areas in various parts of the State - Questioning the constitutional validity of the impost under 2008 Act it was contended before the HC that the State Legislature had no competence to impose environment and health cess on major minerals as the field was occupied by the provisions contained in 1957 Act, which was an enactment by the Parliament - It was urged that the imposition of such cess was not a fee but a tax which was covered by 1957 Act whereunder the power to levy tax on the mineral rights in respect of major minerals was vested in the Parliament - It was further put forth that the Parliament, in exercise of the powers conferred on it by Entries 54 and 55 of List-I of the Seventh Schedule to the Constitution of India, had enacted 1957 Act and the Rules framed thereunder and under 1957 Act, the power vested from all spectrums in the Central Govt. in respect of major minerals and, therefore, the State Legislature could not have enacted such law for imposing such cess on major minerals - It was contended that the cess in question was a nature of fee and the levy of fee on major minerals was governed by the provisions contained in 1957 Act and the Rules framed thereunder and hence, the State Legislature did not have competence to impose such cess - HC interpreting s.16 of 2008 Act had opined that such a power did not confer on the Govt. to issue a notification retrospectively and, therefore, it could only apply with prospective effect - Appellant contended that wherever a statutory power would be conferred, there would be no limitation with regard to exercise of that power and the same could be exercised from time to time and even if the words "time to time" would be absent in the statute, the power conferred under 2008 Act could be exercised all over again and there would be no limitation to the number of times the power would be exercised and if the power exercised once, it could not be stated that the power stood exhausted - Held, it could definitely be stated that despite reading the entire Act in a holistic manner SC was unable to trace any other provision throwing any light on the words 'from time to time' and, therefore, the conferment of power should rest upon the construction that was exclusively placed on s.16 of 2008 Act - In one case before SC controversy that arose for determination was whether an agreement despite expiry would prevail over a regulation made u/s. 79(c) of 1948 Act pertaining to the age of superannuation of employees of the Board having regard to the use of the words 'time to time' in S. 2 of 1961 Act - Decision of SC was referred to solely for the purpose that the words, namely, 'from time to time' may be associated with any number of times, of course subject to the principle of reasonableness and its impact but did not engulf the spectrum of retrospectivity or retroactivity in its ambit and sweep - In M.P. Vidyut Karamchari Sangh v. M.P. Electricity Board, 2004 Indlaw SC 198, relied on - Thus, the conspectus of authorities and the meaning bestowed in the common parlance admitted no room of doubt that the words 'from time to time' had a futuristic tenor and they did not have the etymological potentiality to operate from a previous date - Use of the said words in the s.16 of 2008 Act could not be said to have conferred the jurisdiction on the State Govt. or delegate to issue a notification in respect of the rate with retrospective effect - Such an interpretation did not flow from the statute which was the source of power - Therefore, the notification as far as it covered the period prior to the date of publication of the notification in the official Gazette was really a transgression of the statutory postulate - Thus analysed, view expressed by the High court on that score was absolutely flawless and SC concurred with the same - Appeals dismissed.
