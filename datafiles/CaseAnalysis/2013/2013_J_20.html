Jatya Pal Singh and others v Union of India and another

Supreme Court of India

17 April 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Surinder Singh Nijjar

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 248; (2013) 6 SCC 452; AIR 2013 SC (Supp) 298; 2013 (5) AWC 4378; 2013 (2) CLT(SC) 157; 2013 (138) FLR 987; JT 2013 (6) SC 376; 2013(6) SCALE 125; [2013] 2 S.C.R. 970; 2013 (4) SLR 76; 2013 (2) UPLBEC 1509

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Keywords: Agreement of retrenchment

Summary: Constitution of India-Articles 226 and 12-Employment-Termination-Writ petition-Maintainability-T.C.L. erstwhile V.S.N.L. not performing public function or mandatory public duty-State divested its control by transferring functions performed by O.C.S. prior to 1986 on V.S.N.L./T.C.L. - Government companies like M.T.N.L. and B.S.N.L. are competitors of T.C.L. in respect of I.L.D.S.-T.C.L. does not enjoy monopoly status in I.L.DS.-There is open competition between numerous telecom operators-Government of India does not exercised deep and pervasive control in either management or policy making of T.C.L. which are purely private enterprise-All telecom operators are providing commercial services for commercial considerations-Function performed by V.S.N.L./T.C.L. cannot be put on same pedestal as function performed by private institution in imparting education to children-Private education service is in nature of sovereign function which is required to be performed by Union of India-V.S.N.L. merely promised not to retrench any employee who had come from O.C.S. for period of two years-Such condition not clothe same characteristic of public duty which employer was bound to perform-Employee had individual contract with employer-T.C.L. cannot be said to be other authority within Article 12-Claim of appellants (employees) that their service wrongly terminated V.S.N.L./T.C.L. in breach of assurance given by Government of India and V.S.N.L. in Clause 5.13 of share holding agreement-In case employer is actually in breach of contract-Appellants at liberty to seek redress by taking recourse to normal remedies available under law-High Courts fully justified in rejecting claim of appellants that T.C.L. would be amenable to writ jurisdiction of High Court by virtue of 'other authority' within purview of Article 12.

The functions performed by V.S.N.L./T.C.L. are not of such nature which could be said to be a public function. Undoubtedly, these operators provide a service to the subscribers. The service is available upon payment of commercial charges.

In order to be held that the body is performing a public function, the appellant would have to prove that the body seeks to achieve some collective benefit for the public or a section of public and accepted by the public as having authority to do so. In the present case, as noticed earlier, all telecom operators are providing commercial service for commercial considerations. Such an activity in substance is no different from the activities of a bookshop selling books. The Supreme Court is unable to appreciate the submission of the counsel for the appellants that the activities of T.C.L. are in aid of enforcing the fundamental rights under Article 21 (1) (a) of the Constitution. The recipients of the service of the telecom service voluntarily enter into a commercial agreement for receipt and transmission of information. The function performed by V.S.N.L./T.C.L. cannot be put on the same pedestal as the function performed by private institution in imparting education to children. It has been repeatedly held by this Court that private education service is in the nature of sovereign function which is required to be performed by the Union of India. Right to education is a fundamental right for children upto the age of 14 as provided in Article 21A. Therefore, reliance placed by the learned counsel for the appellants on the judgment of the Supreme Court in Andi Mukta Trust, (1989) 2 SCC 691, would be of no avail.

In these appeals, the claim of the appellants is that their services have been wrongly terminated by V.S.N.L./T.C.L. in breach of the assurances given by the Government of India and V.S.N.L. in Clause 5.13 of the share holding agreement. If that be so, they would be at liberty to seek redress by taking recourse to the normal remedies available under law.

A perusal of the documents, however, would show that V.S.N.L. had merely promised not to retrench any employee who had come from O.C.S. for a period of two years from 13th February, 2002. Such a condition, in our opinion, would not clothe the same with the characteristic of a public duty which the employer was bound to perform. The employees had individual contacts with the employer. In case the employer is actually in breach of the contract, the appellants are at liberty to approach the appropriate forum to enforce their rights.

The Supreme Court is, therefore, of the firm opinion that the High Court of Delhi and the High Court of Bombay were fully justified in rejecting the claim of the appellants that T.C.L. would be amenable to writ jurisdiction of the High Court by virtue of the other authority within the purview of Article 12 of the Constitution of India.
