Standard Chartered Bank and another v V. Noble Kumar and another

Supreme Court of India

22 August 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

J. Chelameswar, H.L. Gokhale

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 732; (2013) 9 SCC 620; 2013 (100) ALR 683; 2016 (3) BC 405; [2013] 180 Comp Cas 137; 2013 (4) CompLJ 417; 2013 (6) CTC 683; 2013 (2) DRTC 609; JT 2013 (12) SC 177; 2013 (4) KLT 531; 2014 (1) MahLJ 627; 2014 (1) MPLJ 396; 2013 (121) RD 262; 2013(10) SCALE 540; [2013] 10 S.C.R. 762

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Civil Procedure

Keywords: Suit for declaration, Denied, Secured creditor, Amendment to written statement

Summary: Land & Property - Civil Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 227 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 6 rr. 16, 17 and O. 8 r. 9 - Code of Civil Procedure (Amendment) Act, 1976 - Suit for declaration - Amendment to written statement - Denied - Justifiability -Appellants (defendants) filed instant appeal against common order passed by Single Judge, whereby revision petitions filed by respondent (plaintiffs) u/art. 227 of Constitution were allowed and the order passed by Trial Court allowing amendment in the written statement was set aside - After the defendants lost the claim up to SC and their prayer was refused, fresh petition under O. 6 r. 17 of CPC was filed seeking leave of Court to amend the written statement - Trial court rejected the said application and against which Order was challenged in HC in revision which was disposed of with the directions to Trial Court to register the application and disposed of the same by passing a reasoned order - Trial Court in compliance of directions allowed the petition permitting the defendants to amend the written statement - Whether impugned rejection of application for amendment of written statement was justified -

Held, O. 6 r. 16 of CPC was substituted by Amendment Act - Provision dealt with the amendment or striking out of the pleadings, which party desired to be made in his opponent's pleadings - Defendant might ask the Court for striking out pleadings of his opponent on the ground that the pleadings were shown to be unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious - Such Rule was based on the principle of ex debito justitia - SC was empowered under the Rule to strike out any matter in the pleadings that appeared to be unnecessary, scandalous, frivolous or vexatious or which tends to prejudice, embarrassed or delay the fair trial of the suit - On the other hand, O. 6 r. 17 of CPC empowered the Court to allow either party to alter or amend his own pleading and on such application the court might allow the parties to amend their pleadings subject to certain conditions enumerated in the said Rule - Although the appellants filed the petition for striking out their own pleading i.e. written statement, labelling the petition as under O. 6 r. 16 of CPC, but in substance the application was dealt with as if under O. 6 r. 17 of CPC inasmuch as Trial Court discussed the facts of the case and did not permit the defendants to substitute the written statement whereunder there was admission of the suit claim of defendant - Relevant portion of the order revealed that Trial Court while rejecting the petition held that appellants could not be allowed to substitute their earlier written statement filed in the suit whereunder there was an admission of the claim of defendant - Appellants could not be allowed to resile from the admission made in the written statement by taking recourse to O. 8 r. 9 of CPC or O. 6 r. 16 CPC by seeking to file fresh written statement - Filing of fresh petition by appellants under O. 6 r. 17 CPC after about 13 years when the hearing of the suit had already commenced and some of the witnesses were examined, was wholly misconceived - HC in impugned order had rightly held that filing of subsequent application for the same relief was an abuse of the process of the court - Relief sought for by appellants in subsequent petition under O. 6 r. 17 of CPC was elaborately dealt with on the two earlier petitions filed by appellants under O. 6 r. 16 of CPC and O. 8 r. 9 of CPC and, therefore, the subsequent petition filed by the appellants labelling the petition under O. 6 r. 17 of CPC was wholly misconceived and was not entertainable - Appeals dismissed.
