Hiraman v State Of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

31 January 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

H.L. Gokhale, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 64; (2013) 12 SCC 586; 2013 CRLJ 2191; JT 2013 (2) SC 258; 2013 (2) RCR(Criminal) 994; 2013(2) SCALE 141; [2013] 3 S.C.R. 119

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Women & Children - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 498-A, 302 - Evidence Act, 1872, s. 32(1) - Murder - Dying declaration - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant (accused) was husband of deceased, allegedly poured kerosene on deceased and set her on fire - Deceased sustained 91% burn injuries and died - Appellant allegedly committed offences u/ss. 498-A and 302 of IPC - Trial Court convicted appellant u/ss. 498-A and 302 of IPC and sentenced him to under imprisonment for life - Aggrieved appellant filed an appeal before HC - HC set aside conviction u/s. 498-A of IPC and confirmed conviction u/s. 302 of IPC - Hence, instant appeal - Appellant contended that there was a variation between 2 dying declarations with respect to reasons for setting her on fire and that uncorroborated dying declarations could not be accepted - Whether impugned conviction of order passed by Trial Court and affirmed by HC was justified - Held, deceased suffered 91% burn injuries - Duty Doctor had asked her as to how incident had occurred, and later on Head Constable on duty had repeated the query - Any person in such a condition would state only that much which he or she could remember on such an occasion - When asked once again, person concerned could not be expected to repeat entire statement in a parrot-like fashion - It was very clear in both statements viz., the greed of appellant and her being harassed on that count - It was relevant to note that mother and brother of deceased, both corroborated statement of deceased that appellant was demanding gold and land from deceased - Initially deceased spoke about demand for gold and later for the land - That could not in any way mean an attempt to improve - Similarly, non-mention on second occasion of appellant's insistence to marry sister of deceased could not mean an omission to discredit statement of deceased - Deceased having suffered 91% burn injuries, there was hardly any time to secure presence of competent magistrate or to record her statement in a detailed question-answer form - Absence of those factors itself would not take away the evidentiary value of recorded statement - U/s. 32(1) of the Act, legislature accorded a special sanctity to statement made by a dying person as to the cause of his own death - When statement was made at the earliest opportunity without any influence being brought on dying person, there was absolutely no reason to take any other view for cause of his or her death - Statement had to be accepted as relevant and truthful one, revealing circumstances which resulted into death - Absence of any corroboration could not take away its relevance - Exaggerated doubts, on account of absence of corroboration, would only lead to unmerited acquittals, causing grave harm to cause of justice - In instant case there were 2 dying declarations recorded at the earliest opportunity, which contained motive for crime and reasons as to why deceased suffered burn injuries - Statements that appellant had poured kerosene and set her on fire was concerned, there was no reason to discard it considering the fact that it was made at the earliest opportunity and on a solemn occasion - Defence story was totally inconsistent with facts and was a clear afterthought - Prosecution undoubtedly proved its case beyond any reasonable doubt - Impugned judgment of HC was upheld - Appeal dismissed.
