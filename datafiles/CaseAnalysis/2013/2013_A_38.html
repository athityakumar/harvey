Tribhuvanshankar v Amrutlal

Supreme Court of India

13 November 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 739; (2014) 2 SCC 788; 2014(1) ALL MR 421; 2014 (106) ALR 160; 2014 (1) ALT(SC) 1; 2014 (1) CLT(SC) 169; 2014 (2) JabLJ 244; 2014 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 1976; 2014 (2) MPLJ 648; 2014 (1) RCR(Civil) 206; 2013 (2) RCR(Rent) 700; 2014 (124) RD 379; 2013(13) SCALE 627; [2013] 12 S.C.R. 368; 2013 (4) WLN(SC) 152

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property

Keywords: Bihar Buildings (Lease, Rent and Eviction) Control Act, 1982, Plea of adverse possession

Summary: Land & Property - Civil Procedure - Madhya Pradesh Accommodation Control Act, 1961, ss. 12(1)(a), 12(1)(e) - Suit for eviction - Plea of adverse possession - Legality - Appellant (plaintiff) filed a suit for eviction of respondent (defendant) from the suit premises and for mesne profits - Respondent was in possession of property as a tenant under the earlier owner - Trial Court dismissed suit holding that sale deed executed by earlier owner in favour of appellant was without any sale consideration; that relationship of landlord and tenant between the parties was not established; and that respondent had become owner of suit accommodation on the basis of adverse possession - Aggrieved appellant filed an appeal before Lower Appellate Court, which was allowed - Aggrieved respondent filed an appeal before HC - Single Judge dislodged the judgment and decree passed by Lower Appellate Court and affirmed that of Trial Court - Hence, instant appeal - Whether impugned judgment passed by HC was justified - Held, a plea was advanced that respondent had already perfected his title by prescription as he was in possession for 18 to 19 years - HC could not have affirmed the judgment and decree passed by Trial Court as it had already decided the issue of adverse possession in favour of respondent, though it had neither jurisdiction to enquire into the title nor that of perfection of title by way of adverse possession as raised by respondent - Under those circumstances, plaintiff was entitled under law to file a fresh suit for title and recovery of possession and such other reliefs - If when a fresh suit was filed the respondent would be in a position to advance a plea that right of appellant was extinguished as he had not filed the suit for recovery of possession within time allowed by law - It was evincible that suit for eviction was instituted on 21-3-1978 and if time was computed from that day suit for which Court granted liberty would definitely be barred by limitation - Thus, grant of liberty by SC would be absolutely futile - Judgment passed by HC was affirmed only to the extent that as the relationship of landlord and tenant was not established the respondent was not liable for eviction under the Act - Issue of right, title and interest was definitely open - Appellant was required to establish the same in a fresh suit as required under law and respondent was entitled to resist the same by putting forth all his stand and stance including the plea of adverse possession - By virtue of remaining in possession the possessor takes an adverse stance to the title of true owner - In fact, he disputes the same - A mere possession or user or permissive possession did not remotely come near the spectrum of adverse possession - Possession to be adverse has to be actual, open, notorious, exclusive and continuous for the requisite frame of time as provided in law so that the possessor perfects his title by adverse possession - Litigant could not deprive the benefit acquired by another in equity by his own inaction and negligence, as assumed by appellant, has been lost forever as he had not able to prove the relationship of landlord and tenant in a suit for eviction which includes delivery of possession - In instant case, after the institution of suit, time for acquiring title by adverse possession was arrested or remained in a state of suspension till the entire proceedings arising out of suit were terminated - Be it ingeminated that if by the date of suit the respondent had already perfected title by adverse possession that would stand on a different footing - Appellant-plaintiff was permitted to institute a suit for title and recovery of possession - Appeal allowed.
