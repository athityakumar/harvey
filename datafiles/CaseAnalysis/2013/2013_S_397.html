Dr. P. B. Desai v State of Maharashtra and another

Supreme Court of India

13 September 2013

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2013 Indlaw SC 594; (2013) 15 SCC 481; 2013 (4) ACC 68; AIR 2014 SC 795; 2013 ALL MR (Cri) 3647; 2013 (4) CPJ(Sc) 63; 2014 CRLJ 385; JT 2013 (12) SC 425; 2013 (4) MLJ(Crl) 259; 2013 (4) RCR(Criminal) 289; 2013(11) SCALE 429; [2013] 11 S.C.R. 863

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Liability, Causing grievous hurt by act endangering life or personal safety of others

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 338 r/w s. 109 - Causing grievous hurt by act endangering life or personal safety of others - Conviction - Liability- Complainants filed complaint against appellant-accused alleging that accused did not take personal care and attention by preferring operation himself - It was alleged that advise of accused for surgical operation, even when doctors at USA. had opined to contrary, was inappropriate - Thus, acts of omission and commission amounted to professional misconduct - Accused was charged for offence punishable u/s. 338 r/w s. 109 IPC - Trial Court convicted and sentences accused - Appeal before Lower Appellate Court was dismissed - HC confirmed conviction and sentence of accused - Hence instant appeal - Whether accused was criminally liable u/s. 338 IPC - Held, no doubt, accused did not operate himself but it was not case of prosecution that Dr. X did not do it deftly either - It was because of deplorable condition of patient, surgery could not be completed as on opening of abdomen other complications were revealed - Which would have happened in any case, irrespective whether abdomen was opened by Dr. X or by accused himself - On contrary complainant's own case was that Dr. X performance was not lacking nay it was of superlative quality - Accused omission in not rendering complete and undivided legally owed duty to patient and not performing procedure himself had not made any difference - It was not cause of patient's death which was undoubtedly because of acute chronic cancer condition - Negligent conduct in nature of omission of accused was not so gross as to entail criminal liability on accused u/s. 338 IPC - It was to be kept in mind that crime as mentioned in s. 338 IPC required proof that accused caused patient's condition to acute - Though conduct of accused constituted not only professional misconduct for which adequate penalty had been meted out to him by Medical Council, and negligence on accused part also amounted to actionable wrong in tort, it did not transcend into criminal liability, and in no case made accused liable for offence u/s. 338 - Appeal allowed.
