Jai Jai Ram Manohar Lal v National Building Material Supply, Gurgaon

Supreme Court of India

17 March 1969

Case Analysis

Bench

J.C. Shah, A.N. Grover

Where Reported

1969 Indlaw SC 191; (1969) 1 SCC 869; AIR 1969 SC 1267; [1970] 1 S.C.R. 22

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Keywords: Permissibility, Suit for recovery of money, Wrong description

Summary: Civil Procedure - Suit for recovery of money - Wrong description - Amendment - Permissibility - Appellant (plaintiff) was a firm, filed a suit for recovery of money against respondent (defendant) - Respondent contested suit stating that suit was barred by limitation and that appellant was not the sole owner of alleged firm - Trial Court decreed the claim of appellant - Aggrieved respondent filed an appeal before HC - HC by its impugned judgment held that action was instituted in name of a 'non-existing person' and 'ML' failed to aver in application for amendment that action was instituted in the name of 'appellant' on account of some bona fide mistake or omission and that Trial Court was incompetent to grant leave to amend of plaint - Further HC held that substitution of name of 'ML' as a plaintiff during pendency of action took effect from 18-7-1952, and action should be deemed to be instituted on that date the amendment could not take effect retrospectively and on the date of amendment the action was barred by law of limitation - Hence, instant appeal - Whether impugned decree passed by HC was sustainable - Held, it settled rule that all amendments should be permitted as might be necessary for the purpose of determining real question in controversy between parties, unless by permitting the amendment injustice might result to other side - In instant case, appellant was carrying on business as commission agent in name of "JJR ML' - Appellant was competent to sue in his own name as Manager of Hindu undivided family (HUF) to which business belonged; appellant says he sued on behalf of family in the business name - Observation of HC that application for amendment of plaint could not be granted, because there was no averment therein that the mis-description was on account of a bona fide mistake, and on that account suit should fail, could not be accepted - There was no rule that unless in an application for amendment of plaint it was expressly averred that error, omission or mis-description was due to a bonafide mistake, Court had no power to grant leave to amend the plaint - Power to grant amendment of pleadings was intended to serve the ends of justice and was not governed by any such narrow or technical limitations - Since name in which action was instituted was merely a mis-description of original plaintiff, no question of limitation aroused; plaint should be deemed on amendment to have instituted in the name of real plaintiff, on the date on which it was originally instituted - Order passed by Trial Court in granting amendment was clearly right - Impugned decree passed by HC was set aside - Appeal allowed.
