Dewan Singh v Champat Singh and others

Supreme Court of India

17 October 1969

Case Analysis

Bench

K.S. Hegde, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1969 Indlaw SC 599; AIR 1970 SC 967; [1970] 2 S.C.R. 903

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR; Civil Procedure

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 115 - Uttar Pradesh Act 1 of 1951 - Appointment of Arbitrator - Award- Validity - Arbitration agreement was executed between appellant and respondents - Arbitrators made their award - Appellant filed suit before trial Court and prayed that arbitration award, be made a rule of court and decree was passed in accordance - Respondents challenged validity of award - Trial Court accepted contention of respondents and held that arbitration agreement was not executed by respondents according to their free will - Appellate court reversed decree of trial Court and held that same could have been referred to arbitration, as respondents had not taken their objection to award within time prescribed, same could not have been entertained by trial Court - On revision, HC differed from appellate court and concluded that arbitrators were guilty of legal misconduct and consequently award made by them was vitiated - Hence, instant appeal - Appellant contended that in exercise of powers u/s. 115 of CPC, HC could not have corrected erroneous interpretation placed by appellate court as to scope of arbitration agreement - Whether appellate court was right in concluding that under arbitration agreement, arbitrators were empowered to decide disputes referred to them on basis of their personal knowledge - Held, proceedings before arbitrators were quasi-judicial proceedings, they must be conducted in accordance with principles of natural justice, parties to the submission may be in dark as regards the personal knowledge of arbitrators - There might be misconceptions or wrong assumptions in mind of arbitrators, if parties were not given opportunity to correct those misconceptions or wrong assumptions, grave injustice may result - It was nobody's case that parties to submission were informed about nature of personal knowledge, arbitrators had and that they were given opportunity to correct any misconception or wrong assumption - There were five arbitrators - It was not known whether award was made on basis of personal knowledge of all of them or only some of them - Arbitration was a reference of a dispute for hearing in a judicial manner - It was true that parties to an agreement of reference may include in it such clauses as they think fit unless prohibited by law - It was normally an implied term of an arbitration agreement that arbitrators must decide dispute in accordance with the ordinary law - Appellate Court, had misread arbitration agreement and hence it erroneously come to conclusion that arbitrators had been empowered to decide dispute on the basis of their personal knowledge - Decision reached by HC was an eminently just one - Appeal dismissed.
