A. K. Kraipak and Others v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

29 April 1969

Case Analysis

Bench

K.S. Hegde

Where Reported

1969 Indlaw SC 271; (1969) 2 SCC 262; AIR 1970 SC 150; [1970] 1 S.C.R. 457; 1969 SLR 445

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Keywords: Indian Forest Service (Initial Recruitment) Regulations, 1966

Summary: Service - Constitution - All India Services Act, 1951, s. 3(1) - Selection - Challenged - In pursuance of All India Services Act, 1951, a Special Selection Board was constituted for selecting officers to the Indian Forest Service in the senior and junior scales from officers serving in the forest department of the State of Jammu and Kashmir - One of the members of the Board was the Chief Conservator of Forests of the State, appointed as Acting Chief Conservator superseding another Conservator of Forests Conservator of Forests whose appeal to the State Government against his supersession was pending at the time the selections by the Board were made - Said Acting Chief Conservator was also one of the candidates seeking to be selected to the Indian Forest Service - The Board made the selection of officers in the senior and junior scales and the name of the Acting Chief Conservator was at the top of the list of selected officers while the names of the three conservators (including officers who were superseded) were omitted - The Acting Chief Conservator did not sit in the Selection Board at the time his name was considered, but participated in the deliberations when the names of his rivals were considered - The 3 conservators, whose names were not included in the list filed writ petitions u/art. 32 of the Constitution challenging impugned selection on the ground that rules of natural justice were not followed in said selection - Whether impugned selection could be quashed on the ground that the rules of natural justice were not followed? - Held, the rules of natural justice operate in areas not covered by any law validly made - They were not embodied rules and their aim was to secure justice or to prevent miscarriage of justice - There was no reason why they should not be made applicable to administrative proceeding also, especially when it was not easy to draw the line that demarcates administrative enquiries from quasi-judicial ones, and an unjust decision in an administrative enquiry may have a more far-reaching effect than a decision in a quasi-judicial enquiry - In the instant case, at the time of selection, the other members of the Board did not know that the appeal of the superseded conservator was pending before the State Government and hence, there was no occasion for them to distrust the opinion of the Acting Chief Conservator - There was a conflict between his interest and duty and he was a judge in his own cause - Taking into consideration human probabilities and the ordinary course of human conduct, there was reasonable ground for believing that the Acting Chief Conservator was likely to have been biased - The Acting Chief Conservator did not participate in some of the deliberations of the Board, but the facts that he was a member of the Board and that he participated in the deliberations when the claims of his rivals were considered and in the preparation of the list, must have had its impact on the selection, as the Board, in making the selection, must necessarily have given weight to his opinion - Hence, selection by the Board could not be considered to have been taken fairly and justly as it was influenced by a member who was biased - Moreover, the selections to both senior and junior scales were made from the same pool and so, it was not possible to separate the two sets of Officers - Hence, impugned selection had to be set aside - Writ petitions allowed.
