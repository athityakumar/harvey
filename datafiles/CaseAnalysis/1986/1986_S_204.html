Brij Bhushan and Others v State of Jammu and Kashmir and Others

Supreme Court of India

07 March 1986

Case Analysis

Bench

P.N. Bhagwati, V.D. Tulzapurkar, R.S. Pathak

Where Reported

1986 Indlaw SC 771; (1986) 2 SCC 354; AIR 1986 SC 1003; 1986 (1) CCC 1001; 1986(1) SCALE 328

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Summary: Corporate - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14 and 19 (1)(g) - Monopoly in trade - Constitutionality of - (A) Whether three impugned orders created monopoly in favour of respondents 2, 3 and 4 and constituted unreasonable restriction on right of petitioners to carry on business of manufacture of resin and turpentine derivatives u/art. 19(1)(g) of Constitution? - Held, annual production of oleo resin in State was in neighbourhood, of about 36,000 tonnes and out of them only 17,000 tonnes per annum were given to respondents 2, 3 and 4 and about 7000 tonnes were reserved for units in public sector, leaving as much as 12,000 tonnes of oleo resin available for other manufacturers - Petitioners and other manufacturers did, therefore, have about 12,000 tonnes of oleo resin available for them - In circumstances, no monopoly in regard to oleo resin could possibly be said to have been created by State Govt. in favour of respondents 2, 3 and 4 - Moreover, it may be noted that what State Govt. did under three impugned orders was not sale simpliciter of oleo resin but assured supply of oleo resin was guaranteed to respondents 2 to 4 for purpose of feeding factories to be set up by them in State - This ground of attack against validity of three impugned orders must, therefore, be rejected - (B) Whether three impugned orders was that they were arbitrary, mala fide and not in public interest inasmuch as a huge favour was conferred on respondents 2, 3 and 4 at cost of State? - Held, if State were simply selling resin, there can be no doubt that State must endeavour to obtain highest price subject, of course, to any other overriding considerations of public interest and in that event, its action in giving resin to a private individual at a lesser price would be arbitrary and contrary to public interest - But, where State has, as a matter of policy, stopped selling resin to outsiders and decided to allot it only to industries set up within State for purpose of encouraging industrialisation, there can be no scope for complaint that State is giving resin at a lesser price than that which could be obtained in open market - Yardstick of price in open market would be wholly inept, because in view of State policy, there would be no question of any resin being sold in open market - Object of State in such a case is not to earn revenue from sale of resin, but to promote setting up of industries within State - Further held, this ground must be rejected - (C) Whether State Govt. had acted arbitrarily in selecting respondents 2, 3 and 4 for setting up factories within State for manufacture of resin and turpentine derivatives without affording any opportunity to petitioners and others for obtaining such contract and this action of State Govt. was not based on any rational or relevant principles and was, therefore violative of art. 14 of Constitution? - Held, State must be free in such a case to negotiate with a private entrepreneur with a view to inducing him to set up an industry within State and if State enters into a contract with such entrepreneur for providing resources and other facilities for setting up an industry, contract cannot be assailed as invalid so long as State had acted bona fide, reasonably and in public interest - In present case, there is nothing at all to show that State acted mala fide or out of improper or corrupt motive or in order to promote private interest of someone at cost of State - Further held, three impugned orders do not suffer from any infirmity and they cannot be assailed as invalid - Writ Petition dismissed.
