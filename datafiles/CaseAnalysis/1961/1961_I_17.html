Messrs Jeewanlal (1929) Limited, Calcutta v Its Workmen

Supreme Court of India

03 March 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 241; AIR 1961 SC 1567; 1961 (2) FLR 537; 1961 (1) LLJ 517; 1961 (2) SCJ 659; [1962] 1 S.C.R. 717

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Keywords: Factories Act, 1934

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, s. 36A(1) - Interpretation of Word Continues Service - Respondent/ Bhanu Bala was workmen of Appellant / Jeewanlal (1929) Ltd - Respondent remained absent from duty without permission or leave for nearly 81 months and according to appellant, respondent was not entitled to any gratuity under scheme framed by award even so appellant offered him Rs. 1,165 and odd on compassionate grounds - Unsatisfied with said amount respondent raised dispute before labour court which was decided in his favour and directed appellant to pay Rs. 1,781-80 np. as gratuity - Appellant filled petition before HC on grounds of lack of jurisdiction of labour court to entertain application of respondent which was allowed and order passed by Labour Court was quashed - It was at that stage Government referred question of interpretation of term "continuous service" contained in award of 1951 to Industrial Court under s. 36A(1) of the Act - That is how Industrial Court was possessed of matter it has held that words continuous service as used by tribunal when it framed award under question mean service not broken or interrupted by termination of contract of employment by either employer or employee or by operation of law - It was this interpretation for correctness of which was challenged by appellant in instant appeal - Whether award of Tribunal was justified - Held, relevant part of gratuity scheme which was framed by Tribunal in earlier reference was (i) On death of an employee or becoming physically or mentally disabled to continue further in service half a months wages for each year of service subject to a maximum of ten months' wages to be paid to him or to his heirs, executors, assigns or nominees as case may be (ii) On the termination of his service by the company after five years' continuous service Gratuity at same rate as above. (iii)On voluntary retirement or resignation of an employee after 15 years' continuous service-Gratuity at same rate as above - Respondent falls under cl. (iii) whereas appellant contended that respondent had not been employed in continuous service for requisite period because there was a break in his service between 2-14-1945 to end of October, 1945, and that affected continuity of his employment which made his claim incompetent under cl.(iii) and this contention had been rejected by Tribunal - Hence Continuous service in context of scheme of gratuity framed by Tribunal in reference postulates continuance of relationship of master and servant between employer and his employees - If servant resigns his employment service automatically comes to an end and if employer terminates service, of his employee that again brings continuity of service to an end and If service of an employee is brought to an end by operation of any law that again is another instance where continuance is disrupted however it would be difficult to hold that merely because an employee was absent without obtaining leave that itself would bring to an end to continuity of his service - It would thus always be a question of fact to be decided on circumstances of each case whether or not a particular employee can claim continuity of service for requisite period or not - Thus view taken by tribunal was right though we would like to make it clear that in addition to cases where according to Tribunal continuity of service would come to an end there would be class of cases where long unauthorised absence may reasonably give rise to an inference that such service is intended to be abandoned by employee - Tribunal award was conformed - Appeal dismissed.
