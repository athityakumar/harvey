Standard Vacuum Refining Company of India v Its Workmen and Another

Supreme Court of India

20 January 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, K.C. Das Gupta

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 53; AIR 1961 SC 895; 1961 (2) FLR 224; 1961 (1) LLJ 227; 1961 (1) SCJ 582; [1961] 3 S.C.R. 536

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Keywords: Fair Wage, Labour Appellate Tribunal, Living Wage, Available Surplus, Housing

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 6, 19(1)(g) - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 - Minimum Wages Act, 1948 - Working Journalists (Conditions of Service) and Miscellaneous Provisions Act, 1955 - Kerala State Govt. notification - Fixing of minimum wages - Challenged - Sustainability - Govt. of Kerala/ Respondent appointed a Committee to hold enquiries and advise Govt. in fixing minimum rates of wages in respect of employment in tile industry and nominated 8 persons to constitute the said Committee u/s. 9 of the 1947 Act - Committee made its report and govt. issued a notification prescribing minimum rates of wages - Petition was filed u/art. 32 of constitution by petitioners who represented 6 tile factories challenging validity of the Act as well as validity of notification issued by Govt. of Kerala stating that notification had fixed not minimum wages but fair wages and neither govt. nor committee while fixing them had considered capacity of employers to pay - Hence, instant petition - Whether notification passed by Kerala govt. was sustainable - Held, in view of decisions taken up by SC constitutional validity of the Act would no longer be in question and any hardship that might have been caused to employers by the fixed wages under the Act or their incapacity to pay the same were irrelevant considerations in fixing of minimum wages - In fixing minimum wage nothing could be added to its components that might take it near lower level of fair wage, minimum wage must ensure not only sustenance of employee and his family but also preserve his efficiency as a worker - It was an error to think that minimum wage was just what a worker required to cover his physical needs and to keep himself above starvation - There could be no analogy between Minimum Wages Act which prescribed economic and industrial minimum and a statute which prescribed its own minimum that approximates more or less to fair wage standard and so made it necessary to consider employer's capacity to pay - SC would ordinarily refuse to consider merits of wage structure prescribed by notification -Under the Act notification had to apply to all tile factories in the State of Kerala and breach of provisions of notification would have been rendered penal under s. 22 of the 1947 Act - An agreement or contract contrary to notification would have been held void u/s. 25 of the 1947 Act - Respondent had acted and assisted in bringing about a settlement contrary to terms of the 1947 Act- If respondent thought that such a settlement was necessary in respect of factories it should have considered the question of withdrawing notification in respect of that area and in fairness should have also reconsidered the problem in respect of all other areas and decided whether any modification in notification was required- It was not appropriate that respondent should have been associated, though indirectly, with any settlement which was in breach of provisions of the 1947 Act and should not hesitate to do what might have been just, reasonable and fair on an objective consideration of the whole problem - Petition dismissed.
