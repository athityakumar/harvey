E. V. Balakrishnan v Mahalakshmi Ammal and Another

Supreme Court of India

24 February 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

Kailas Nath Wanchoo, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 353; AIR 1961 SC 1128; 1961 (2) KerLR 261; 1962 (1) MLJ(SC) 242; 1962 (1) SCJ 546; [1961] 3 S.C.R. 974

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal

Keywords: Succession Act 1925

Summary: Family & Personal - Indian Succession Act, 1925, s. 89 - (A) Whether legatees have a right to make a selection in a will? - English rule of benevolent construction - Held, s. 89 of Indian Succession Act, lays down that will or bequest not expressive of any definite intention is void for uncertainty but there may be wills which use words which are not so uncertain that a definite intention cannot be ascribed to testator under those words - It is to meet such cases in which english rule of selection by legatees was evolved - There are three possibilities which may arise in cases where a will is not so uncertain as not to be capable of ascribing a definite intention to testator - In first case testator himself may indicate what he intends to bequeath and that indication is sufficient to identify property bequeathed - In such cases there is no difficulty, for testator has himself made selection and selection must be given effect to - Second case may be where testator himself does not make a selection but nominates a third person who may select object of his bounty meant for legatee - In such a case also there can be no difficulty and person so nominated will make selection - Third case is where testator has not indicated selection himself and has not nominated a third person to make selection but still gift is not so uncertain as to be void - It is in such cases that English Courts have evolved benevolent rule that testator intended to give selection to legatee and once selection is made by legatee will takes effect - This rule of benevolent construction which is based on common sense and by which wills not quite uncertain can be made certain cannot be called an artificial rule and there is no reason why it should not be extended to India in appropriate cases - (B) Whether testator on construction of this will intended his daughters to select? - Further held, direction of testator was that as soon as children obtain majority guardian will deliver possession to them of respective lands bequeathed to them - Court cannot therefore read this sentence in will to mean that testator was giving right of selection in case of property which he was bequeathing to his daughters nor is there anything in words of will which would lead to inference that testator intended that daughters would get their lands after taking into account good and bad quality of land - There are no words in will from which it can be inferred that a particular person was nominated by testator to make selection - Case, therefore, squarely comes in third class of cases i.e., the testator had indicated with sufficient clarity what he wanted his daughters to get - It must in circumstances be held that testator intended that each daughter will select land devised out of vattam - Appeal dismissed.
