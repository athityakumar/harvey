 Amar Jyoti Stone Crusting Company v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

08 August 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, S.K. Das, A.K. Sarkar, J.R. Madholkar

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 173; AIR 1967 SC 46; [1962] 3 S.C.R. 62

Case Digest

Subject: Rent Control

Keywords: Indian Evidence Act, misa, Punjab Land Revenue Act,1887, Delhi Minor Mineral Rules, 1938

Summary: Mines & Minerals - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 s. 80 - Delhi Minor Mineral Rules, 1938 - Punjab Land Revenue Act, 1887 s. 42 - Delhi Development Act of 1957 - Refusal of mining license - Legality - Appellant-firm was lessee working for certain stone-quarries in fields under lease dt. 21-12-1955 within administration of state Commissioner and obtained permits under Rules of 1938 and were paying royalty prescribed thereof from commencement of their lease right up to 30-6-1957 thereafter its application for a permit was not granted - Appellant thereupon issued notice to govt. authorities u/s. 80 of CPC and filed suit out of which instant appeal arises - Trial Court dismissed suit holding against appellant on every crucial issue and judgment of Trial Court had been affirmed by Courts right up to HC - Hence instant appeal - Appellant prayed under suit for two main reliefs: For declaration that it had right to quarry stones from suit-land apparently even without permit, and alternatively for a perpetual injunction directing defendants-UOI and Collector and DD Authorities to issue required permit on payment of royalties as before - In support of his plea appellant contented that HC had misunderstood and misapplied provisions of s. 42 of the Act of 1887 and Collector was under a legal obligation to grant a permit to appellants unless there were proper grounds for refusing permit and that grounds of his refusal in instant case were improper and mala fide - Whether relief seeking by appellant can be granted under instant appeal - Held, it was no doubt HC had wrongly treated cl. 4 of s. 42 of the Act of 1887 as it was equally applicable to presumption raised in favour of landowner by cl. 2 of s. 42 of the Act of 1887 but this does not, however, establish that appellant was on facts of instant case, entitled to invoke presumption enacted u/cl. 2 of s. 42 of the Act of 1887 as it was common ground that records of-rights had been prepared in respect of the village of twice after 1871 though relevant entries in documents were not placed before SC by either side and persons who comes to Court with an allegation that he was owner of minerals would have to prove his title to property before he could succeed under suit, but appellant led no evidence to prove his title to mineral rights in suit-lands and that its claim for a declaration on that basis was properly dismissed by Courts below - Other point urged by appellant that even if it be that Govt. were proprietors of minerals and permission of Collector was necessary to be obtained under Rules of 1938, in this connection it was pointed out that Collector had refused permit sought by appellant because of a resolution of DDA constituted under the Act 53 of 1955 by which land in dispute was prohibited to carry on stone-quarrying in it here appellant urged that Act 53 of 1955 had ceased to be operative after 30-12-1957 when it was replaced by the Act of 1957 - Having regard to this changed situation contention was, that at date when Trial Court passed judgment it should have taken judicial notice of fact that Act 53 of 1955 had ceased to be in force and that notification there under had lapsed, and that if these matters were taken into account appellant had a clear legal right to relief of mandamus which he prayed for but under instant case argument in SC opinion proceeds on a fallacy If application of appellant was properly refused by Collector before suit, result was that there was no pending application before authority for grant of a permit as It was common ground that during pendency of proceedings in trial Court no fresh application was made to Collector on basis of altered state of facts - Hence it was clear that change in law in shape of Act 53 of 1955 ceasing to be operative does not assist appellant to obtain any relief in instant suit - Thus it was not necessary for to 'canvass point which had been discussed in Courts below as to whether in cases where Govt. was owner of a property its discretion in its management and control could be subject of directions by Court unless, of course, statute or statutory rule enables individuals to claim any particular rights - Appeal dismissed.
