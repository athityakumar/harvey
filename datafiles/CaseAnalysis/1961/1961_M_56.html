 Messrs Sainik Motors, Jodhpur and Others v State of Rajasthan

Supreme Court of India

22 March 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

Mohammad Hidayatullah, J.C. Shah, S.K. Das, J.L. Kapur, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 488; AIR 1961 SC 1480; 1963 (1) SCJ 292; [1962] 1 S.C.R. 517

Case Digest

Subject: Carriers & Transportation; Constitution

Keywords: Lump Sum Payment

Summary: Constitution - Carriers & Transportation - Constitution of India, 1950 - Motor Vehicles Act, 1939 - Rajasthan Passengers and Goods Taxation Act, 1959 - Freight - Petitioner was a registered firm - Petitioners nos. 2 to 6 were the partners of that firm, and petitioner no. 7 was the General Manager of the firm - Petitioner no. 7 held a public carrier permit for the whole of Rajasthan in his individual name and also held 59 stage carriage permits from the Regional Transport Authority, Jodhpur, for diverse routes over roads which had different kinds of surfaces - The 1959 Act and Rules were passed for levying a tax on passengers and goods carried by road in motor vehicles - Petitioners challenged the 1959 Act, the Rules and the notification and the main objection to the Act was that the tax had not been laid upon 'passengers and goods' as authorised by Entry no. 56 but upon 'fares and freights' - It was contended that a tax on fares and freights being a different tax, could not be levied under the Entry, and thus, the tax was without authority of law - Whether the imposition of a higher rate of tax for cemented, tarred, asphalted, metalled, gravel and kankar roads than that for other roads was a discrimination between operators - Held, that the contention that there was discrimination between road transport and rail transport was without force - Entry in the State List was limited to a tax on passengers and goods transported by road or inland waterways - Comparison with Railways was not admissible, because tax on railway fares and freights was a Union subject, and was not available to the State Legislature - There was thus a clear classification made by the Constitution itself - No discrimination between operators of public motor vehicles using roads had been pointed out, and all operators were equally affected by the Act - In addition, imposition of the tax was justified on the assumption of a return in the shape of conveniences - If this be the true import of a tax, it was but natural that taxes would be graded accordingly as they involved more or less of such conveniences - They would be heavy in case of roads requiring greater expenditure to construct and to maintain, than in case of roads not requiring such expenditure - All operators using the better kind of roads had to pay the heavier tax, and there was no discrimination between them as a class - Discrimination could only be found if it existed between persons who were comparable, and there was no comparison between persons using the better kind of roads and those who used roads which were not so good - Petition dismissed.
