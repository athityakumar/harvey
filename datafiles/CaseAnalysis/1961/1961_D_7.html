Abhiraj Kuer v Debendra Singh

Supreme Court of India

15 September 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

K.C. Das Gupta, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 326; AIR 1962 SC 351; 1962 (2) SCJ 174; [1962] 3 S.C.R. 627

Case Digest

Subject: Municipalities & Local Governments

Summary: Family & Personal - Adoption by widow - Legality - Main question under instant appeal was can wife's sister's daughter's son be validly adopted to person governed by Schools of Mitakshara - HC answered instant question in affirmative and dismissed suit - Hence instant appeal - whether adoption of a wife's sister's daughters son was valid under eyes of law - Held, it was urged that in view of specific exclusion of a wife's sister's daughter's son from list of those who are fit for adoption there was no escape from conclusion that such an adoption would be invalid in law - There can be no doubt that in laying down rule that adoption of son of woman who could not have been married by adoptive father because of incongruous relationship (Viruddha Sambandha) Nanda Pandit was adding to existing state of law it 'was interesting to notice here that commenting on what Saunaka had said in describing ' ritual of adoption that a son should be adopted as Dattaka Chandrika observed:

(Reflection of a son- resemblance of a son,or in other words capability to had been begotten, by adopter, through appointment, and so forth) Dattak Mimansa adopts this view, and introduces further doctrine of (Viruddha Sambandha) relationship as a bar to adoption - It was unnecessary for SC to examine what authority should be attached to this serious addition to texts for determining who can be adopted, as for reasons to be presently mentioned SC assuming that this rule should be accepted as of authority Nanda Pandit had stated this merely as a recommendation and not as a mandatory prohibition as for many years now courts had recognised position that not only Dharma Sutras and Grihya Sutras but also commentaries thereon and digests mingle without hesitation statements of law which are intended to be recommendations merely with statements which are intended to be mandatory - It was therefore necessary to examine words used by Nanda Pandit himself in laying down this rule against Viruddha Sambandha adoption it had to be noticed that while he says (One who if begotten by adopter would have been son of a prohibited connection must be excepted-Sutherland's translation), he does not say anything about what would happen if Viruddha Sambandha Putra was adopted if rule was intended to be mandatory it was reasonable to expect that author who as treatise itself shows was master of logic and well acquainted with rules of logic and other rules which deal with question of mandatory injunctions would give clear indication of that view this was all more reasonable to expect as he was introducing a new rule but he contents himself with saying that we do not think this language that adoption of a son of a Viruddha Sambandha girl should be avoided, can properly be taken as mandatory so that rule must be obeyed on pain of adoption being otherwise invalid in law - Real question which was before Full Bench 'Was whether there can be valid adoption under Hindu law if a legal marriage was not possible between person for whom adoption was made and mother of boy who was adopted, in her maiden state In that case adoptee's mother was a sagotra of adoptive father and so, there could be no legal marriage between them it was not necessary under instant case to consider whether Viruddha Sambandha rule against marriage was mandatory or not as SC were not aware of any decision in any of HC where Nanda Pandit's rule against Viruddha Sambandha adoption had been considered to be a mandatory, prohibition for reasons discussed above SC was of opinion that this rule introduced by Wanda Pandit was only a recommendation and consequently it was of no avail to appellant to show that adoption of wife's sister's daughter's son was invalid - Thus HC was right in its conclusion that adoption of a wife's sister's daughter's son was valid in law - Appeal dismissed.
