 Babulal Parate v State of Maharashtra and Others

Supreme Court of India

12 January 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

J.R. Madholkar, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, S.K. Das, A.K. Sarkar, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 509; AIR 1961 SC 884; 1961 CRLJ 16; 1961 (1) MLJ(SC) 154; 1961 (1) SCJ 554; [1961] 3 S.C.R. 423

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Keywords: Freedom Of Speech And Expression

Summary: Criminal - Constitution - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Freedom of speech and expression - There were two unions of textile workers in Nagpur, one known as the Rashtriya Mill Majdoor Sangh and the other as Nagpur Mill Majdoor Sangh - The former was a branch of the Indian National Trade Union Congress - The Rashtriya Mill Majdoor Sangh entered into an agreement with the management of the Empress Mills regarding the closure of Empress Mill no. 1 for rebuilding it and regarding the employment of workers who were employed therein in a third shift - Agreement was opposed by the Nagpur Mill Majdoor Sangh - A group of workers belonging to the Nagpur Mill Majdoor Sangh went in a procession to Gujar's Wada, Mahal, Nagpur, where the office of the Rashtriya Mill Majdoor Sangh was located - It was said that a scuffle took place there between some members of the procession and some workers belonging to Rashtriya Mill Majdoor Sangh - Thereupon an offence under s. 452 r/w s. 147 of the IPC was registered by the police - A large procession consisting of the workers of the Nagpur Mill Majdoor Sangh was taken out - Procession marched through the city of Nagpur shouting slogans which, according to the District Magistrate, were provocative - On the same night a meeting was held in which it was alleged that the workers belonging to the Nagpur Mill Majdoor Sangh were instigated by the speakers who addressed the meeting to offer satyagraha in front of the Empress Mill no. 1 and also to take out a procession to the office of the Rashtriya Mill Majdoor Sangh - Workers belonging to the Nagpur Mill Majdoor Sangh assembled in large numbers blocking the traffic on the road - Petitioner entertained the view that the order promulgated by the District Magistrate u/s. 144 of the CrPC was an encroachment on the fundamental rights of the citizens to freedom of speech and expression and to assemble peaceably and without arms, guaranteed u/art. 19(1)(a) and (b) of the Constitution and, therefore, he held a public meeting outside the area covered by the aforesaid order - It was alleged that at that meeting he criticised the District Magistrate and exhorted the workers to contravene his order and take out processions in the area covered by the order - Thereupon, he was arrested for having committed the offences already referred to and produced him before the magistrate who remanded him to jail - Petitioner's application for bail was rejected on the ground that the accusation against him related to a non-bailable offence - Petitioner presented a petition before the HC u/s. 491 of the CrPC for a writ of habeas corpus - Petitioner then moved the HC for granting a certificate u/art. 132 of the Constitution - Whether the order passed by the District Magistrate was unconstitutional - Held, s. 144 of the CrPC conferred very wide powers upon certain magistrates and that in exercise of those powers, the magistrates could place very severe restrictions upon the rights of citizens to freedom of speech and expression and to assemble peaceably and without arms - Anticipatory action of the kind permissible u/s. 144 of the CrPC was not impermissible u/cls. (2) and (3) of art. 19 of the Constitution - Public order had to be maintained in advance in order to ensure it and, therefore, it was competent to a legislature to pass a law permitting an appropriate authority to take anticipatory action or place anticipatory restrictions upon particular kinds of acts in an emergency for the purpose of maintaining public order - Order of the District Magistrate was not unconstitutional either because s. 144 of the CrPC was itself violative of fundamental rights recognised in art. 19 of the Constitution or on the ground that it was vague and placed unreasonable restrictions on those fundamental rights - Omission of the District Magistrate to make the exemption clause of the order more comprehensive would not vitiate the order on the ground that it placed unreasonable restrictions on certain fundamental rights of citizens - Petition dismissed.
