Sheodhyan Singh and Others v Musammat Santchara Kuer and Others

Supreme Court of India

04 May 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

Kailas Nath Wanchoo, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 140; AIR 1963 SC 1879; 1962 BLJR 273; 1962 (41) ILR(Pat) 402; 1961 (2) KerLR 431; 1961 (2) MLJ 116; 1961 (2) MLJ(SC) 116; 1961 (2) SCJ 540; [1962] 2 S.C.R. 753

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Final Decree For Sale

Summary: Land & Property - Criminal Procedure Code, 1898, ss. 144 and 107 - Civil Procedure Code, 1908 - The respondent prayed that ten plots belonged to them, and prayed for possession of plots by ejectment of defendent appellant and for mesne profit - The respondents' case was that appellant had taken a loan from other defendants on a mortgage bond on basis of which, those defendants instituted a suit - This suit was decreed against appellants, and, thereafter the, other defendants got mortgaged property, sold by auction in execution and purchased it themselves in 1936 - Thereafter other defendants entered into possession of property, delivery of which was made to them by Court - Other defendants remained in possession of the property sold it to respondents in 1943 - Thereafter respondents came into possession of property, appellants however began to create trouble from 1942 - After sale to the, respondents, appellants created further trouble which led to proceedings in a criminal court u/s. 144 of CrPC, 1898 and appellants were forbidden from going to the property in dispute - Later on, appellants were bound down u/s. 107 of CrPC, 1898 to keep peace - In 1945, there was a murder in connection with this property on account of which, some of appellants were tried by, court of session but were acquitted - There were further troubles over crop of these plots in 1945 - Eventually after their acquittal by Court of Session, appellants took possession of property by forcibly dispossessing respondents - Consequently respondents filed suit out of which this appeal has arisen in July 1946 - Whether Courts below were wrong in granting possession to respondents with respect to these, three plots? - Held, no - Court was not wrong in granting possession to respondent - The area, khata number and boundaries all refer to plot No. 1060 and what has happened is that in writing plot number, one zero has been missed and 1060 has become 160 - It is also important to remember that there is no plot bearing No. 160, in khata No. 97 - In these circumstances, High Court was right in holding that this is a case of mis-description only and that identity of property - As to contention that Ramzan Mian and another were not made parties to mortgage suit and therefore their rights are not affected and if appellants held land from Ramzan Mian and another they would still be entitled to possession and could not be dispossessed, it is enough to say that this argument could be raised if it were established that mortgage of 1927 was still subsisting when suit was brought in 1932 - On that point however there is no evidence and it is not known whether mortgage of Ramzan Mian and another was subsisting in 1932 - Further finding of HC is that whatever evidence is on record shows that at any rate in 1935 appellants were in possession of plot No. 427 - In these circumstances, it cannot be held that positively that mortgage of Ramzan Mian and another was subsisting in 1932 when suit was brought and that appellants were in possession of this plot on behalf of Ramzan Mian and another - Appellants therefore cannot resist claim of respondents for possession on ground that they are holding this plot on behalf of Ramzan Mian and another without any proof of this on record - The appellants contention therefore with respect to plot No. 427 must also fail - Plot No. 1128 case of appellants with respect to this plot is similar to case with reference to plot 427 - In circumstances appellants' contention with respect to this plot must also fail - Appeal dismissed.
