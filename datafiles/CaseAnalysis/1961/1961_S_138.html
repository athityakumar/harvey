Teeka and Others v State of Uttar Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

15 February 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Subba Rao, Raghubar Dayal

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 104; AIR 1961 SC 803; 1961 AllCriR 198; 1961 CRLJ 859; 1962 (1) SCJ 439; [1962] 1 S.C.R. 75

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Criminal Procedure Code, 1898 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 147, 424, 452, 325, 149, 323 and 149 - One Har Narain had obtained a decree from court of Addl. Munsif, Ghaziabad, against one Sunehri Jogi for a sum of money - In execution of that decree Munsif issued a warrant for attachment of judgment-debtor's property - Inter alia, three buffaloes and two cows, which were in house of judgment-debtor, were also attached as his property - The animals with permission of decree-holder were kept in his premises - Next day at about 7 a.m., nine appellants, armed with lathies, went to enclosure of decree-holder and began to untie two of attached buffaloes - Decree-holder, his son and his nephew protested against acts of appellants whereupon appellants struck three inmates of house with lathies, and when PW 4 intervened, they struck him also with lathies - Thereafter, appellants 1, 2 and 3 took away two buffaloes followed by other appellants - Defence took all together version - Sessions Judge disbelieved defence version - On appeal, Judges of High Court accepted finding arrived at by learned Sessions Judge and confirmed convictions and sentences passed by him on accused - Appellants have preferred these two appeals against judgment of HC - (A) Whether attachment of buffaloes was illegal? -(B) Whether cattle were illegally possessed by decree holder? - (C) Whether appellant caused a wrongful loss to court by releasing cattles? - In these circumstances, attachment had been validly made in strict compliance with all requirements of law - Possession of amin or sapurdar is in law possession of court and, so long as attachment is not raised, possession of court continues to subsist - Would it make any difference in legal position if sapurdar, for convenience or out of necessity, keeps said animals with a responsible third party - In law said third party would be a bailee of sapurdar - Would it make any difference in law when bailee happens to be decree-holder - Obviously it cannot, for decree-holder's custody is not in his capacity as decree-holder but only as bailee of sapurdar - Decree-holder's possession of buffaloes, in present case was only as a bailee of sapurdar - If owner removes it by unlawful means, he is certainly causing wrongful loss to court or its officers, as case may be, within meaning of words 'wrongful loss' - In present case when owner of buffaloes removed them unlawfully from possession of decree-holder, bailee of sapurdar, he definitely caused wrongful gain to himself and wrongful loss to court - In this view, appellant 1 dishonestly removed buffaloes within meaning of s. 424 of IPC, 1860, and, therefore, he was guilty under that, section - In this case there is no doubt, on evidence, that appellants entered house of decree-holder with intent to remove attached cattle constituting an offence u/s. 424 of IPC, 1860 - Appellants are, therefore, guilty of offence and have been rightly convicted u/s. 441 of IPC, 1860 - Prosecution adduced evidence to prove that accused armed themselves with lathies and entered premises of decree-holder to recover their cattle and gave lathi blows to inmates of house causing thereby serious injuries to them - Accused had ample opportunity to meet that case - Both courts below accepted evidence and convicted accused u/s. 325 r/w s. 149, IPC, 1860 - Evidence leaves no room to doubt that accused had knowledge that grievous hurt was likely to be caused to inmates of decree-holder's house in prosecution of their common object, namely, to recover their cattle - Appeals dismissed.
