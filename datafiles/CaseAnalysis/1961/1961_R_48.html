Ramlal, Motilal and Chhotelal v Rewa Coalfields Limited

Supreme Court of India

04 May 1961

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo

Where Reported

1961 Indlaw SC 462; AIR 1962 SC 361; 1961 ALJ 815; 1961 (2) MLJ(SC) 131; 1961 (2) SCJ 556; [1962] 2 S.C.R. 762

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Ex-Parte

Summary: Practice & Procedure - Civil Procedure - Indian Limitation Act, 1908, s. 5 - Construction of s. 5 of the Indian Limitation Act - Condonation of delay - Appellant purchased coal from respondent - Since the appellant did not pay the price due from it the respondent filed the suit and claimed a decree of amount due - As appellant did not appear, an ex-parte decree was passed against appellant - Appeal was filed thereagainst alongwith an application u/s. 5 of the Limitation Act for condonation of one day delay on ground that one of the partners of the appellant's firm, who was in charge of the litigation, fell ill on the last date for filing the appeal - Judicial Commissioner refused to excuse delay on the ground that the appellant's partner had showed lack of diligence and negligence during the whole of the period of limitation allowed for the appeal - Application for condonation of delay was rejected and the appeal was dismissed - Whether Judicial Commissioner erred in holding that in determining the question as to whether sufficient cause had been shown within the meaning of s. 5 of the Limitation Act it was necessary for the appellant to explain his conduct during the whole of the period prescribed for the appeal? - Held, s. 5 of the Limitation Act provides for extension of period in certain cases - Words "within such period" means within the period which ends with the last day of limitation prescribed - In all cases falling u/s. 5 what the party has to show is why he did not file an appeal on the last day of limitation prescribed - Hence, party will have to show sufficient cause not only for not filing the appeal on the last day but to explain the delay made thereafter day by day - Hence, Judicial Commissioner was in error taking the view that the failure of the appellant to account for its non-diligence during the whole of the period of limitation prescribed for the appeal necessarily disqualified it from praying for the condonation of delay, even though the delay in question was only for one day; and that too was caused by the party's illness - Delay of one day made in filing the appeal condoned - Matter sent back to the Court of the Judicial Commissioner for disposal on the merits - Appeal allowed.
