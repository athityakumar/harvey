Gainda Ram And Others v M.C.D. And Others

Supreme Court of India

08 October 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Ganguly, G.S. Singhvi

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 1099; (2010) 10 SCC 715; JT 2010 (11) SC 228; 2010(11) SCALE 36; [2010] 12 S.C.R. 996

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Municipalities & Local Governments; Socio-Economic

Keywords: National Capital Territory of Delhi Laws (Special Provisions) Act, 2009, Model Street Vendors (Protection of Livelihood and Regulation of Street Vending) Bill, 2009, Delhi Control of Vehicular and other Traffic on Roads and Streets Regulations, 1980

Summary: (A) Constitution - Socio-Economic - Municipalities & Local Governments - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19 - New Delhi Municipal Council Act, 1994, s. 2, 3, 202 - Delhi Municipal Corporation Act, 1957, ss. 3, 42, 320, 322 - Issue of sale of cooked food on public streets which was creating problems of unhygienic conditions came up before the Constitution Bench of SC in Pyare Lal vs. New Delhi Municipal Committee and another, 1967 INDLAW SC 441, in the context of a resolution passed by the New Delhi Municipal Committee (NDMC) for stopping such sale - SC held that no person carrying on the business of selling cooked food had any fundamental right to carry on street vending particularly in a manner which would create unsanitary and unhygienic conditions in the neighborhood - SC, subsequently, in Sodan Singh and others v. New Delhi Municipal Committee and others, 1989 INDLAW SC 213, took a broader view and held that the hawkers and squatters had a fundamental right to carry on business on the public street, but the same should be regulated - Municipal Corporation of Delhi (MCD), consequently, promulgated a scheme and placed the scheme before the Lok Adalat for implementation - Lok Adalat constituted a Committee for considering the cases of individual squatters - Since MCD did not have sufficient land to accommodate all the hawkers/squatters as per the scheme, MCD made a request to the Central Government for allotting land for the said purpose -Committee examined 5627 claims in great detail and passed detailed order in every case and in its final report found that 761 out of 5627 persons were entitled for allotment of sites - Further, Committee also identified 977 sites for squatting in Municipal area - In the meantime, SC, in Sodan Singh v. NDMC and others, 1998 INDLAW SC 1622, considered the report of the Committee and came to conclusion that places identified for eligible squatters, as decided by the Committee, was only tentative - However, SC accepted the procedure recommended by the Committee and also accepted its recommendation about payment of arrears of dues towards MCD and also noted its recommendation that in case of failure to pay such dues the claimant was not entitled to the benefit under the scheme - SC directed certain procedures to be followed for the purpose of making final allotment of sites and prescribed a cut-off date for filing of application and hearing of applicants - Pursuant to the said decision, SC reconstituted the Committee for disposing the applications relating to final allotment of sites - Again the matter came up before the SC in Sudhir Madan and others v. Municipal Corporation of Delhi and others, 2007 INDLAW SC 562, wherein SC directed the 1st respondent to frame a scheme - 1st respondent, consequently, framed National Policy on Urban Street Vendors, 2004 (2004 Policy) - Thereafter, the matter was taken up by SC from time to time, wherein it was discussed and certain modifications were suggested, which subsequently were incorporated in the scheme - Whether NDMC and MCD have the jurisdiction to verify the vending zones and scrutinize the application for final allotment of sites - Held, both, the NDMC and MCD, are statutory bodies under 1994 Act and 1957 Act respectively and both the Acts are parliamentary legislations - MCD was established u/s. 3 of the Act as a body corporate composing of Councilors - Under 1957 Act a public street means a street which vests in the Corporation as a public street or the soil below, the surface of which vests in the Corporation and which under the provision of the Act becomes or is declared to be a public street - All public streets vest in the Corporation u/s. 298 of the 1957 Act - S. 42 of the 1957 Act enumerates the obligatory functions of the Corporation, one of which is the removal of obstructions and projections in or upon the streets, bridges and other public places - U/s. 320(1) of the 1957 Act there is a clear mandate that no person should encroach or obstruct any public place or street - S. 322 of the Act also empowers Commissioner to remove any stall, chair, bench, box, ladder, bale or anything whatsoever placed, deposited or projected in, upon, from or to any place in the street - If any such object has been placed in contravention of the Act, the Commissioner can remove any article hawked or exposed for sale on any public street or in any other place in contravention of 1957 Act along with any vehicle, package or box or any other thing in or on which such article is placed - Further, u/s. 481 of the 1957 Act, the MCD may frame bye-laws relating to permission, regulation or prohibition of use or occupation of any street or place by itinerant vendors/hawkers or by any person, for the sale of articles or the exercise of any calling or the sitting of any booth or stall and make regulation for fees chargeable for such occupation - Under the 1994 Act almost similar provisions as of 1957 Act are available - Definition of public street u/s. 2(39) of 1994 Act is virtually the same as the definition of public street u/s. 2(44) of the 1954 Act - Similarly u/s. 3 of the 1994 Act, NDMC has been formed as a body corporate having perpetual succession and a common seal - NDMC is also equally empowered to remove obstructions and projections in and upon the streets, bridges and other public places -U/s. 202 of the 1994 Act all public streets vest in the Council - 1994 Act also contains similar provisions prohibiting erection of structures/fixtures which causes obstructions in the street - NDMC is also authorized to prevent any nuisance in any public street or public place, or picketing of animals or collection of carts, displacement, damaging or making any alteration to the pavement, water- drain etc. without any authority - Therefore, NDMC and MCD have the jurisdiction to verify the vending zones and scrutinize the applications of hawkers for allotment - Petitions disposed of.

(B) Constitution - Socio-Economic - Municipalities & Local Governments - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19 - New Delhi Municipal Council Act, 1994 - Delhi Municipal Corporation Act, 1957 - Right to hawk - Constitutional protection - Whether the right to hawk on the streets of Delhi is a fundamental right u/art. 19(1)(g) of the Constitution - Held, Constitution Bench of SC in Sodan Singh held that right to hawk on the streets of Delhi was a fundamental right u/art. 19(1)(g) of the Constitution but such right was not absolute and was subject to reasonable restrictions u/art. 19(6) of the Constitution - Rights u/art. 19(1)(g) can only be controlled by law as contemplated in art. 19(6) of the Constitution and such law can impose reasonable restrictions - Reasonable restrictions on the fundamental right u/art. 19(1)(g) can be imposed either by existing law or by a law which may be made by a State in the interest of general public - Therefore, nothing short of law can impose reasonable restrictions on a citizen's fundamental right to carry on hawking u/art. 19(1)(g) of the Constitution - Scheme which was framed by NDMC for regulation of squatting and hawking in the NDMC areas was on the basis of guidelines given by SC in its judgment in Sodan Singh's case - In the said scheme, NDMC had divided its area into four zones and some of the zones had been made non-hawking zones - Subsequently, another scheme was prepared by the NDMC pursuant to the 2004 Policy - In the said scheme the NDMC had referred to ss. 225 and 330 of the 1994 Act - S. 225 of the Act permits squatting only on the permission given by the Chairman and on payment of such fees in each case as a Chairman may think fit - S. 330 of the Act provides for license for hawking articles which authorizes the NDMC to prevent hawking unless there is a license to that effect granted by the Chairperson - However, neither the said policy nor the scheme framed by the NDMC can be called law except mentioning of provisions of ss. 225, 226, 330 and 369(2) of the 1994 Act - NDMC did not have made any bye-law u/s. 388 of the 1994 Act so as to regulate the fundamental right of the hawkers to hawk or squat on the streets of Delhi - Schemes which have been framed under the direction of SC or the 2004 Policy which has been framed by the Government, cannot said to be framed under the said power to frame bye-laws and do not have the status of law or even subordinate legislation - Subsequent to the 2004 Policy a new National Policy on Urban Street Vendors, 2009 (2009 Policy) was framed - Further, Model Street Vendors (Protection of Livelihood and Regulation of Street Vending) Bill, 2009 was enacted in order to prima facie recognizes the rights of hawkers and vendors u/art. 21of the Constitution since it seeks to protect their livelihood - In the background of the provisions in the Bill and the 2009 Policy, it is clear that an attempt is made to regulate the fundamental right of street hawking and street vending by law, since it has been declared by SC that the right to hawk on the streets or right to carry on street vending is part of fundamental right u/art. 19(1)(g) of the Constitution - Therefore, direction issued to NDMC and MCD to regulate the hawking and street vending through present schemes up to 30-06-2011 and within that time, the appropriate Government is to legislate and bring out the law to regulate hawking and hawkers' fundamental right - Petitions disposed of.
