Bharat Sanchar Nigam Limited v Telephone Cables Limited

Supreme Court of India

22 January 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

R.V. Raveendran, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 411; (2010) 5 SCC 213; AIR 2010 SC 2671; 2010 (3) AWC 2875; JT 2010 (3) SC 541; 2010 (6) MLJ(SC) 99; 2010(3) SCALE 364; [2010] 3 S.C.R. 291

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR; Contract & Commercial

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Contract & Commercial - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, s. 11(6) - Appellant, by 'Notice Inviting Tenders' invited bids for supply of Polythene Insulated Jelly Filled cables - Tender procedure required an evaluation of the bids - BSNL awarded the highest vendor rating (V-1) to NICCO - Respondent filed Writ Petition alleging that BSNL had arbitrarily adjudged NICCO as the person with the highest Vendor Rating thereby pushing it down to the category of 'other bidders' which adversely affected the size of its order accordingly - HC held that assessment of Vendor Rating done by BSNL in regard to NICCO was not proper and directed BSNL to redo the Vendor Rating - Meanwhile, BSNL, as per its policy, had already carried forward the balance quantity of the Tender to the next tender and had even placed the purchase orders on the successful bidders against the said tender - No balance quantity was available and no order for any further quantity could be placed with the respondent - Respondent claimed damages from BSNL - Respondent therefore filed a second writ petition - However, second writ petition was dismissed as withdrawn - Thereafter, respondent filed an application u/s. 11(6) of the Act for appointment of an Arbitrator to decide its claim - HC by the impugned order allowed the said application and appointed an Arbitrator - Hence, present appeal - Whether there exists an arbitration agreement between the parties? - Held, to constitute an arbitration agreement for the purpose of ss. 7 and 11 of the Act, two requirements should be satisfied - First is that there should be an arbitration agreement between the parties to the dispute; second is that it should relate to or be applicable to the dispute in regard to which appointment of Arbitrator is sought - In the present case, as per the scheme of Bid documents, there is a clear division of the terms that will govern the tender process, and the terms that will govern the contract, when the bids are accepted - As per scheme, only when a purchase order was placed, a 'contract' would be entered; and only when a contract was entered, the General Conditions of Contract including the arbitration clause would become a part of the contract - However, there was no contract or agreement between the parties - Bid documents did not constitute a contract, or an agreement to enter into a contract - It was merely an invitation to make an offer - Respondent cannot invoke the arbitration clause in regard to that dispute as the arbitration agreement was non-existent in the absence of a purchase order - In the absence of an arbitration agreement, the application u/s. 11 of the Act was not maintainable - Appeal allowed.
