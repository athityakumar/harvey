S.D. Joshi & Ors. v High Court Of Judicature At Bombay & Ors

Supreme Court of India

11 November 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, Chandramauli Kumar Prasad

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 979; (2011) 1 SCC 252; AIR 2011 SC 848; 2011 (3) ALD(SC) 1; 2011 (2) Bom.C.R. 117; 2011 (2) G.L.R. 1248; 2011 (1) JhrCR(SC) 120; JT 2010 (12) SC 346; 2010(11) SCALE 712; 2011 (5) SLR 347

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution

Keywords: Practicing, Bombay Industrial Relations act, Bombay Judicial Services Recruitment Rules, 1956, Family Court (Recruitment and Service Conditions) Rules, 1990, Bombay Judicial Services Recruitment Rules, 2008

Summary: Constitution - Advocates & Judges - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 217, 236 and 309 - Family Courts Act, 1984 - Family Court (Recruitment and Service Conditions) Rules, 1990 - Bombay Judicial Service Recruitment Rules, 2008, r. 3 - Judicial Office - Petitioner-Principal Judges and Judges of Family Courts, filed writ petition submitting that they hold a 'judicial office' as contemplated u/art. 217 of the Constitution and are at parity with functional jurisdiction, while satisfying all the trappings of a Civil Court and, as such, they should be deemed to be qualified for elevation to the HC - Respondent contended that art. 233(2) of Constitution clearly states that Judges of the Family Court stand excluded from the appointment of the District Judges - (A) Whether the 'Family Courts' established under the 1984 Act are Courts for all intents and purposes generally or otherwise? - Held, yes - The Family Court, a creature of statute, has been vested with power to adjudicate and determine the disputes between the parties which fall within the scope and ambit of Explanation to s. 7(1) of the 1984 Act - The persons, who are appointed as Judge of the Family Court, perform all duties and functions which are akin to the functions being performed by the Presiding Officer of a Civil or a Criminal Court, though to a very limited extent - The primary object and duty of the Family Court Judges is to endeavour and persuade the parties in arriving at a settlement in respect of the suit or proceedings, in which it may follow such procedure, as it may deem fit - Hence, the Family Court has all the trappings of a Court and, thus, is a court and the Presiding Officer, that is, Judge of the Family Court is a 'Judge' though of limited jurisdiction - (B) Whether Petitioners can be treated as part of the 'Judicial Services' of the State of Maharashtra? - Held, no - In exercise of the powers conferred by arts. 233, 234 and 309 of the Constitution of India, the Governor of Maharashtra, framed the Rules known as 'The Bombay Judicial Services Recruitment Rules, 2008' - After coming into force of the Rules of 2008, appointments were made to the State Judicial Services including the Higher Judicial Services strictly in accordance with these Rules in which it is clearly stated that a person has to be member of the judicial service before he could be considered for appointment to the Higher Judicial Services of the State - Once the legislature has framed the Rules and kept out the Principal or other Family Court Judges from the cadre of the 'Judicial Services' of the State of Maharashtra, then they cannot be treated as part of the cadre by inference or on the doctrine of parity - Moreover, art. 236 of Constitution shows expression 'District Judge' which includes different kinds of Judges but not Family Court Judges - (C) Is the claim of parity put forward by the petitioners sustainable in law? - Held, no - The kind of jurisdiction the Judges of the Family Court and the members of the Higher Judicial Services of the State of Maharashtra exercise while deciding cases, it is difficult for the Court to hold that they are at parity and their services are interchangeable to the extent that the Presiding Officers of the Family Court would be granted the stature of the members of the Higher Judicial Services of the State - (D) Whether petitioner hold a 'judicial office' within the meaning of art. 217 which is a condition precedent for consideration for elevation to the HC? - Held, no - Art. 233(2) of Constitution provides that a person in service of the Union or the State Government is not eligible to be considered for appointment as District Judge and who has not been an advocate or a pleader for not less than seven years - In contradistinction to this, a person in service of the Union or the State would be eligible to be appointed as Judge of the Family Court - In terms of art. 217(2)(a) of Constitution, a person should have at least for 10 years held a 'judicial office' in the territory of India - Hence, petitioner do not hold a 'judicial office' within the meaning of art. 217 - (E) Whether Family Court Judges are eligible and entitled to be considered for elevation as Judge of the HC in terms of art. 217 of the Constitution of India? - Family court judge can't be elevated to HC - Held, the Principal and other Judges of the Family Court may be 'Judges' presiding over such courts in its 'generic sense' but stricto sensu are neither Members/integral part of the 'Judicial Services' of the State of Maharashtra as defined u/art. 236 of Constitution nor do they hold a 'judicial office' as contemplated u/art. 217 of the Constitution of India - Thus, they do not have any jus legitimum to be considered for elevation to the HC - Writ petition dismissed.
