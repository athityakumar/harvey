Mandvi Co-operative Bank Limited v Nimesh B. Thakore

Supreme Court of India

11 January 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Aftab Alam, Tarun Chatterjee

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 1543; 2010 (1) JCC (NI) 42

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Presumption, Defect, Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, Indian Evidence Act, 1872, Proof, Summons, Cheque, Cognizance, Credibility, Field, Dealing, Deposition, History, Consultation, Inadmissible, Negotiable Instruments (Amendment And Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 2002, Hearsay, Public Financial Institutions and Negotiable Instruments Laws (Amendment) Act, 1988, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Complainant

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Negotiable Instruments (Amendment and Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 2002 - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, ss. 138, 142, 145, 146, 145(1), 147, 145(2) - Dishonour of Cheques - Evidence by Affidavit - Challenged - Complaint was filed against accused for dishonor of cheque - Instant appeals were filed against impugned order of HC, where by it allowed claim of accused that any evidence in defence like complainant's evidence could also be given on affidavit in absences of any bar on accused to give such evidence either in the Act or in CrPC and s. 145(2) of the Act being procedural nature, would applicable to case pending on date they came into force.

Held, ss. 142 to 147 of the Act lay down a kind of a special code for the trial of offences under Chapter XVII of the Act and ss. 143 to 147 were inserted in 1881 Act by the amended Act 2002 to do away with all the stages and processes in a regular criminal trial that normally cause inordinate delay in its conclusion and to make the trial procedure as expeditious as possible without in any way compromising on the right of the accused for a fair trial. In other words, these are the provisions which have been provided for so that the parties drawing the cheques are careful enough to see that there are enough resources available in their bank account and if a cheque is drawn, it will not be returned. However, vast multitude of cases coming to the court, the claim of the accused that on being summoned u/s. 145(2) of the Act, the complainant or any of his witnesses whose evidence is given on affidavit must be made to depose in examination-in- chief all over again plainly appears to be a demand for meaningless duplication, apparently aimed at delaying the trial. Further, it is not the duty of the court either to enlarge the scope of the legislation or the intention of the legislature when the language of the provision is plain and unambiguous. In the field of statute law the judge must be obedient to the will of Parliament as expressed in its enactments. In this field Parliament makes and unmakes the law. The judge's duty is to interpret and to apply the law not to change it to meet the judge's idea of what justice requires. Interpretation does, of course, imply in the interpreter a power of choice where differing construction are possible. But our law require the judge to choose the construction which in his judgment best meets the legislative purpose of the enactment. If the result be unjust but inevitable, the judge may say so' and invite Parliament to reconsider its provision. But he must not deny the statute. In instant case, the HC was in error in taking the view that on a request made by the accused the magistrate may allow him to tender his evidence on affidavit. Hence, the impugned judgment is set aside. Appeal allowed.
