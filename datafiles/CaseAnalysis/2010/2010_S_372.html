Santosh Kumar Singh v State Thr. Cbi

Supreme Court of India

06 October 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

H.S. Bedi, Chandramauli Kumar Prasad

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 822; (2010) 9 SCC 747; 2010 ALL MR (Cri) 3668; JT 2010 (11) SC 1; 2010 (4) MLJ(Crl) 455; 2010 (4) RCR(Criminal) 593; 2010(10) SCALE 422; 2010 (3) UC 1794

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: University Rules

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302 and 376 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 313 - Murder - Rape - Conviction - Challenged - Circumstantial evidence - Appellant (son of a senior police officer) was harassing the deceased (student of the LL.B. course at University of Delhi) over a period of two years - Deceased thereupon made several complaints against the appellant in the Police Stations - As a consequence thereof the appellant tendered two apologies and an undertaking not to harass her - Subsequently, appellant allegedly murder deceased - Case u/s. 302 of the IPC was registered on the complaint of the father of the deceased - On the completion of the investigation, appellant was charged for offences punishable u/ss. 376/302 of the IPC - Trial Court held that case against the appellant could not be proved and acquitted him - On appeal, HC reversed the judgment of the Trial Court and awarded death sentence - Hence, present appeal - (A) Circumstantial evidence - Held, from the year 1994 to January 1996, the appellant was continuously harassing the deceased and this allegation was proved by ocular and documentary evidence - He was also seen by PW-2 outside the house of the deceased on the day of incident carrying a helmet with an intact fixed visor which matched up with the recovery of the helmet with a broken visor and the recovery of glass pieces apparently of the visor from near the dead body - (B) Injuries suffered by appellant - Whether it could be said that the injury suffered by the appellant on his right hand proved his presence at the spot? - Held, medical evidence clearly supported the version that the injury was sustained by the appellant during the course of the rape and murder - (C) Whether observation of the HC that the DNA test conclusively proved the involvement of the appellant in the rape was tenable? - Trial Court found against the prosecution on this aspect - Held, Court cannot substitute its own opinion for that of an expert, more particularly in a science such as DNA profiling which is a recent development - In the present case, two scientists gave very comprehensive statements supported by documents that the DNA of the semen stains on the swabs and slides and the clothes of the deceased and the blood samples of the appellant was from a single source and that source was the appellant - Hence, Trial court was not justified in rejecting the DNA Report, as nothing adverse could be pointed out against the two experts who had submitted it - Therefore, DNA report accepted as being scientifically accurate and an exact science - (D) Suspicion with regard to the taking and storage of the blood samples of the appellant - Held, there were ample evidence to suggest that the blood samples had been kept in the hospital in a proper way - HC was, therefore, fully justified in holding that the Trial Court's conclusions on the question of the retention and dispatch of the swabs and slides and the clothes of the deceased the blood samples was faulty, and based on a perverse assessment of the evidence - (E) Motive - It was contended that appellant felt frustrated as the deceased was not giving in to his overtures despite having been pursued relentlessly over two years, and had in anger and frustration, committed the rape and murder - Held, appellant had been continuously harassing deceased - It appears that as the appellant's overtures had been rebuffed by the deceased, he had resorted to harassing her in a manner which became more and more aggressive and crude as time went by - (F) Propriety of HC interference in an acquittal appeal - Held, a judgment of acquittal rendered by a Trial Court must be given the greatest consideration and the appellate court would be slow in setting aside that judgment, and where two views are possible, the one taken by the Trial Court would not be disturbed - On the contrary if the trial court's judgment was perverse, meaning thereby that it was not only against the weight of evidence but was all together against the evidence, interference was called for - In the present case, judgment of the Trial Court was held to be perverse and against the evidence - Hence, HC observations were justified on the fact - (G) Omission in putting certain questions to the appellant - Effect thereof - Held, all incriminating material circumstances must be put to an accused while recording his statement u/s. 313 of the CrPC, but if any material circumstance was left out that would not ipso facto result in the exclusion of that evidence from consideration unless it could further be shown by the accused that prejudice and miscarriage of justice had been sustained by him - In the present case, no prejudice or miscarriage of justice was occasioned - (H) Sentence - Whether present case fell in the category of 'rarest of rare cases'? - Held, where the option is between a life sentence and a death sentence, the options are indeed extremely limited and if the court itself feels some difficulty in awarding one or the other, it is only appropriate that the lesser sentence should be awarded - In the present case, balance sheet tilts marginally in favour of the appellant, and the ends of justice would be met if the sentence awarded to him is commuted from death to life imprisonment u/s. 302 of the IPC - Appeal dismissed.
