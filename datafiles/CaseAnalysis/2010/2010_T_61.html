Transport & Dock Workers Union & Ors v Mumbai Port Trust & Anr

Supreme Court of India

15 November 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Markandey Katju, Gyan Sudha Misra

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 980; (2011) 2 SCC 575; 2010(6) ALL MR 1011; 2011 (4) AWC 3471; 2011 (4) Bom.C.R. 699; 2011 (1) JhrCR(SC) 151; JT 2010 (13) SC 26; 2011 (1) LLJ 1; 2011 (1) MLJ(SC) 755; 2010(12) SCALE 217

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Labour & Industrial Law; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Lunch

Summary: (A) Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 - Major Port Trusts Act, 1963 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 14 - Dispute relating to fixation of duty hours - Availability of alternative remedy - Appellant/Trade Union filed a writ petition before the HC alleging that typist-cum-computer clerks with 1st respondent/Port Trust were recruited after 1-11-1996, having the duty hours of seven and half hours per day, however, typist-cum-computer clerks appointed before 1-11-1996, only had six and half hours of duty per day - Appellant contended that discrimination in duty hours amounted to violation of art. 14 of the Constitution - HC dismissed appellants' writ petition by holding that there was no violation of art. 14 of the Constitution - Whether the writ petition before the HC would be maintainable in view of availability of alternative remedy - Held, writ petition filed by the appellants should have been dismissed by the HC on the ground of existence of an alternative remedy under the provisions of 1947 Act - Writ jurisdiction is discretionary jurisdiction, and the discretion should not ordinarily be exercised if there is an alternative remedy available to the appellant - In the present case there was a clear alternative remedy available to the appellant by raising an industrial dispute and hence the HC should not have entertained the writ petition - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 14 - Indoor and outdoor employees - Fixation of different duty hours - Violation of art. 14 - Whether the action of the 1st respondent/Port Trust in prescribing different working hours for typist-cum-computer clerks working in their indoor establishment with reference to their date of appointment is contrary to the guarantee contained in art. 14 of the Constitution - Held, respondent/Port Trust adopted the practice of prescribing different working hours for typist-cum-computer clerks recruited after 1-11-1996 was due to change in the situation, change in technology, the desire to bring in uniformity in working hours of the personnel working in indoor establishment and outdoor establishment - Respondent/Port Trust decided to change the duty hours of the personnel recruited on indoor establishment after 1-11-1996 without disturbing the duty hours of the personnel working on the outdoor establishment, after giving the personnel, to be newly recruited, a clear understanding that in case they accept the offer of appointment, they will have to work for eight hours and it was only on their acceptance of that term that they were given the appointment - Thus, for the achievement of the object i.e. bringing in uniformity in the duty hours of the personnel working on the indoor establishment and outdoor establishment, the respondent/Port Trust classified persons working on the indoor establishment for the purpose of duty hours into two classes, the basis for classification being the date of their appointment, and the object being to become competitive in business and efficient - Art. 14 does not prohibit reasonable classification for the purpose of legislation or for the purposes of adoption of a policy of the legislature or the executive, provided the policy takes care to reasonably classify persons for achieving the purpose of the policy and it deals equally with all persons belonging to a well defined class - In the present case, the purpose of the classification was to make the activities of the Port competitive and efficient - With the introduction of privatization and setting up private Ports, the respondent had to face competition and it also wanted to rationalize its activities by having uniform working hours for its indoor and outdoor establishment employees, while at the same time avoiding labour disputes with employees appointed before 1-11-1996 - Hence classification in question is clearly reasonable as it satisfies the test of art. 14 of the Constitution - Further, for the purpose of bringing about uniformity in the working hours of the personnel working on the indoor establishment, two options were available to the respondent/Port Trust; (i) either to take steps to bring about change in the working hours of the personnel presently working on the establishment and then apply that change to the personnel who are recruited in future, or (ii) to apply the changed practice in case of new recruits after obtaining their consent for adoption of the new practice and thus introduce the change gradually because personnel recruited before 1-11-1996 were bound to retire sooner or later with their retirement, and a day would come when in the indoor establishment the only personnel working will be those who have been recruited after 1-11-1996 - Of the said two options the respondent/Port Trust had chosen the second option because in the opinion of the Port it would be relatively hassle free - Appellant did not contend that the decision of the respondent/Port Trust choosing the second option was not bona fide - Since the decision of the Port was bona fide, and hence no fault can be found with the said decision and it cannot be said that it violates art. 14 of the Constitution - No merit in appeal - Appeal dismissed.
