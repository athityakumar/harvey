Iridium India Telecom Limited v Motorola Incorporated and others

Supreme Court of India

20 October 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Surinder Singh Nijjar, B. Sudarshan Reddy

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 932; (2011) 1 SCC 74; AIR 2011 SC 20; 2011 ALL MR (Cri) 989; [2010] 160 Comp Cas 147; 2010 (4) JCC 2945; JT 2010 (11) SC 492; 2011 (3) MLJ(Crl) 785; 2011 (1) RCR(Criminal) 235; 2010(11) SCALE 417

Case Digest

Subject: Corporate; Criminal; Telecom

Keywords: Inducement

Summary: Criminal - Corporate - Telecom - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 415 and 420 r/w 120B - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 482 - Quashing of Criminal Complaint - Appellant filed criminal complaint of cheating u/s. 420 r/w. 120B IPC against respondent no. 1 alleging that respondent no.1-Primary contractor for Iridium system/project issued Private Placement Memorandum (PPM) with an object to obtain funds/ investments to finance the said project - Appellant further submitted that the respondent no. 1 made representations to individual institutions and entities handling public money so as to induce them to believe that Iridium was a company worth participating and investing in by purchasing shares and operating a gateway - Relying upon the aforesaid representations of the respondent no. 1, the appellant as well as the banks and institutions collectively invested a sum of US $70 million for purchasing equity of said project as well as spent a sum of about Rs.150 crores in setting up a gateway - Appellant further alleged that the representations made by respondent no.1 proved to be false, dishonest, fraudulent and deceitful - Appellant also alleged that respondent no.1 had full knowledge about the un-viability of the Iridium system - Appellant, therefore, addressed a notice calling respondent No.1 to make payment of a sum of US $ 250 million, being the loss suffered by the appellant as also seeking punitive damages - Judicial Magistrate Ist Class issued process against the respondent No.1 to 7 for offences u/s. 420 r/w. s. 120 IPC - Respondents filed petition before HC seeking quashing of the said order issuing process on the ground that the complaint does not even prima facie show that any of the representations made by the respondent no. 1 were false nor is there any material to even prima facie establish any dishonest intention ab intio on their part while making the open offer investment in Iridium Inc - HC allowed the petition and quashed the order issuing process passed by the Judicial Magistrate Ist Class - Appellant filed appeal against said order submitting that the HC committed a serious error of law in concluding that the respondent being a corporation was incapable of committing the offence of cheating - Appellant also submitted that averments made in the complaint if taken on their face value would not prima facie disclose the ingredients for the offence of cheating as defined u/s. 415 IPC - (A) Whether the HC has adopted the correct approach while exercising its inherent power u/s. 482 CrPC? - Held, a corporation is virtually in the same position as any individual and may be convicted of common law as well as statutory offences including those requiring mens rea - The criminal liability of a corporation would arise when an offence is committed in relation to the business of the corporation by a person or body of persons in control of its affairs - In such circumstances, it would be necessary to ascertain that the degree and control of the person or body of persons is so intense that a corporation may be said to think and act through the person or the body of persons - Hence, a company/corporation cannot escape liability for a criminal offence, merely because the punishment prescribed is that of imprisonment and fine - Conclusion reached by the HC that the respondent could not have the necessary mens rea is clearly erroneous - (B) Whether the averments made in the complaint if taken on their face value would not prima facie disclose the ingredients for the offence of cheating as defined u/s. 415 IPC? - Held, a bare perusal of the s. 415 IPC shows that the deception by the accused of the person deceived, must be fraudulent or dishonest - It also requires that the accused must by deception intentionally induce the person deceived either to do or omit to do anything which he would not do or omit, if he was not so deceived - Surprisingly, HC notices the representations that were made and contrasted the same with the actual realities and yet concluded that the averments made in the complaint even if taken at their face value would not lead to the conclusion that the respondent has committed the offence of cheating - HC ought to have refrained from indulging in detailed analysis of very complicated commercial documents and reaching any definite conclusions as parties are yet to place on the record the entire material in support of their claims - Hence, HC clearly exceeded its jurisdiction in quashing the criminal proceeding - The allegations in the complaint will have to be accepted on the face of it and the truth or falsity cannot be entered into by the Court at this stage - Hence, impugned order of the HC set aside - Appeal allowed.
