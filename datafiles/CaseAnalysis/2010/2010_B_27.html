State Of Uttaranchal v Balwant Singh Chaufal And Others

Supreme Court of India

18 January 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Dalveer Bhandari, Mukundakam Sharma

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 46; (2010) 3 SCC 402; AIR 2010 SC 2550; 2010 (4) AWC 4306; JT 2010 (1) SC 329; 2010 (2) MLJ(SC) 1127; 2010 (1) RCR(Civil) 842; 2010 (1) RLW 506; 2010(1) SCALE 492; [2010] 1 S.C.R. 678; 2010 (1) SLR 581

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Preventive Detention Act, 1950, Government of India Act,1935, Representation of the People Act, 1976, Federal Rules, Model Rules

Summary: Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 165, 217 - Post of the Advocate General - Qualifications for appointment - PIL filed before the HC challenging the appointment of 'N' as the Advocate General on the ground that he could not hold the Office of the Advocate General of Uttarakhand in view of art. 165 r/w art. 217 of the Constitution as he had attained the age of 62 years much before he was appointed as the Advocate General - The High Court entertained the petition and directed the State Government to take decision on the issue raised within 15 days and apprise the High Court on the issue - Present special leave petition - Held, controversy raised by the petitioner in this case was decided 58 years ago in the judgment of G.D. Karkare v. T.L. Shevde & Others AIR 1952 Nagpur 330 which was approved by the Constitution Bench of the SC - DB of HC observed that the provisions regarding duration in the first clause of art. 217 of the Constitution cannot be made applicable to the Advocate General because the Constitution contains a specific provision about the duration of the appointment of the Advocate General in the third clause of art. 165 of the Constitution which says that the Advocate General shall hold office during the pleasure of the Governor - Advocate General for the State can be appointed after he/she attains the age of 62 years - Attorney General for India can be appointed after he/she attains the age of 65 years - When the controversy is no longer res-integra and the same controversy is raised repeatedly, it is a clear case of the abuse of process of court in the name of the PIL - Petition also has the potentiality of demeaning a very important constitutional office - Such petition deserves to be discarded and discouraged so that no one in future would attempt to file a similar petition - Order accordingly.Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 165, 217 - PIL - Held, PIL is the product of realization of the constitutional obligation of the court - PIL is not in the nature of adversary litigation but it is a challenge and an opportunity to the government and its officers to make basic human rights meaningful to the deprived and vulnerable sections of the community and to assure them social and economic justice which is the signature tune of Constitution - Order accordingly

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 165, 217 - Evolution of PIL in India - Held, development of PIL has been extremely significant development in the history of the Indian jurisprudence - Remedies sought from the courts in the PIL goes beyond award of remedies to the affected individuals and groups - Order accordingly

Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 165, 217 - Frivolous petition - Abuse of remedy - Held, genuine and bona fide PIL must be encouraged whereas frivolous PIL should be discouraged - Court must take effective steps to prevent and cure its abuse on the basis of monetary and non-monetary directions by the courts, in order to preserve the purity and sanctity of the PIL, Guidelines framed - Order accordingly.
