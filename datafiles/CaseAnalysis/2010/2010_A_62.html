Amarjit Singh and another v State of Punjab and others

Supreme Court of India

29 September 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

T.S. Thakur, R.V. Raveendran, R.M. Lodha

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 926; (2010) 10 SCC 43; JT 2010 (11) SC 82; 2010 (4) Law Herald (P&H) 3420; 2010(10) SCALE 334; [2010] 12 S.C.R. 163

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property

Keywords: Punjab Regional and Town Planning and Development Act, 1995, Punjab New Capital (Periphery) Control Act, 1952, Punjab Urban Estate (Development and Regulation) Act, 1964, Gujarat Panchayats (Amendment) Act, 1974

Summary: (A) Land & Property - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss.4, 6 - Punjab Regional and Town Planning and Development Act, 1995, ss. 14, 178 - Land acquisition - Validity - Notification u/s. 4 of the 1894 Act was issued proposing to acquire appellants' land for development and expansion of an existing township - Declaration u/s. 6 of the 1894 Act was also issued thereafter - 1st respondent/State exempted appellants' land from the provisions of s. 14 and Chapters VIII, X and XII of the 1995 Act by invoking the powers u/s. 178(2) of the said Act in order to avoid unnecessary hurdles in the process of acquisition - 1st respondent, subsequently, issued an exemption notice under the provisions of 1995 Act - Appellants challenged the notification u/s. 4 and declaration u/s. 6 of the 1894 Act apart from the vires of s. 23(1) of the 1894 Act - HC dismissed appellants writ petitions - Hence the present appeal - (a) Whether the exemption of land under acquisition from the provisions of s.14 and Chapters VIII, X and XII of the 1995 Act in terms of exemption notification issued u/s. 178(2) of the said Act suffers from any legal infirmity - Held, exemption notification under 1995 Act made a reference to the fact that city in which lands in question situated had recorded the highest rate of growth of population of Class I cities giving rise to considerable increase in the demand for housing, in turn giving rise to haphazard development in the area if planned development of the lands in question was not immediately taken up and plots made available to the allottees - Said circumstances referred to in the exemption notification are not irrelevant nor extraneous to the exercise of the power of exemption vested in the State Government u/s. 178(2) of the 1995 Act - Exemption notification also indicated that compliance with the provisions of the 1995 Act was found to be impracticable primarily because of the tremendous pressure on land in and around the city in which lands in question situated for housing purposes especially because the township had witnessed phenomenal growth over the years - Exemption notification, therefore, rightly stated that if immediate steps were not taken to develop the outskirts of the township it would lead to large scale unplanned and haphazard mushrooming of housing colonies and commercial establishments in the area - Therefore, State Government was in the said view well within its power to evaluate the options available to it, making a choice and taking appropriate action to prevent any such disorganized and haphazard development, hence in as much as the 1st respondent/State did so and decided to invoke its powers u/s. 178(2) of the Act, it committed no illegality - Further, s. 178(2) of the 1995 Act empowers the State Government to grant exemption from the operation of the Act on the twin grounds of hardship and expediency and such power is also intended to relieve hardship arising from the operation of the 1995 Act - Therefore, exemption notifications issued under the provisions of 1995 Act does not suffer any legal infirmity - (b) Whether the acquisition under challenge is rendered bad for non-compliance with the provisions of the 1995 Act - Held, validity of the acquisition proceedings was not challenged on any ground that it might have been available to the appellants by reference to the 1894 Act - Neither before the HC nor in the present proceedings was it argued that the provisions of the 1894 Act were not followed in letter and spirit, while acquiring the land in question - Notification u/s. 4 of the 1894 Act gave a sufficient basis for the State Government to initiate proceedings for the acquisition of land needed for the proper expansion of the township - Notification in question was not assailed before the HC in the writ petitions filed by the appellants, therefore, it is not, now, open to the appellants to argue that the notification suffered from any illegality - Therefore, acquisition under the provisions of the 1894 Act is valid in law - (c) Whether the Land Pooling Scheme formulated by the 1st respondent for rehabilitating the ousted agricultural landowners should be made operative retrospectively in order to extend the benefit of such Scheme to appellants - Held, Scheme envisages a kind of public-private partnership in the development of areas involving acquisition of large extents of land - Authorities for whom acquisition is being made will have to take a broader initiative at the appropriate stage to make provision for allocation to the owners of what is due to them under the Scheme - Such administrative exercise can be done only when an acquisition is tailored according to the Scheme - Scheme cannot be introduced after the acquisition and even allotment process is over - A mechanical extension of the Scheme to acquisitions that have since become final cannot help and any such attempt would be a potential recipe for considerable confusion and resultant litigation - Therefore, it is not feasible at present to super impose the Land Pooling Scheme on the acquisition under challenge and make a provision for allocation to the owners in the lands in question that are under development or those that have already been developed - Further, when the number of appellants is limited in the present proceedings, any direction for rehabilitation based on a retrospective operation of the Scheme would deprive other land owners the benefit of such Scheme only on account of their acceptance of the acquisition proceedings - Appeals dismissed.

(B) Land & Property - Constitution - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 300A - Compulsory land acquisition - Absence of rehabilitation measure of landowners - Legality - Whether the absence of any rehabilitation measures renders the acquisition in question legally bad - Held, lands owned by the appellants have been acquired in terms of the provisions of the 1894 Act which is a validly enacted piece of legislation - Sum total of compensation, solatium and interest undoubtedly constitutes a reasonable compensation for the land acquired from the expropriated owners - Neither art. 300-A of the Constitution nor the 1894 Act make any measures for rehabilitation of the expropriated owners a condition precedent for compulsory acquisition of land - In the absence of any such obligation arising either u/art. 300-A or under any other statutory provision, rehabilitation of the owners cannot be treated as an essential requirement for a valid acquisition of property - Compulsory acquisition in accordance with the procedure sanctioned by law is a valid exercise of power vested in the State hence cannot be taken to deprive the right to livelihood especially when compensation is paid for the acquired land at the rates prevailing on the date of publication of the preliminary notification - Therefore, there is no gainsaying that rehabilitation is an essential requirement of law for any compulsory acquisition and any such acquisition violates the right to livelihood of the owners who may be dependant on the land being acquired from them - Appeals dismissed.
