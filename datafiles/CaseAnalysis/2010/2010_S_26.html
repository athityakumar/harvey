Safiya Bee v Mohd. Vajahath Hussain Alias Fasi

Supreme Court of India

16 December 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Cyriac Joseph, V.S. Sirpurkar

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 1080; (2011) 2 SCC 94; 2011 (5) ALD(SC) 128; JT 2010 (13) SC 688; 2011 (5) MLJ(SC) 334; 2010(13) SCALE 471

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Earth

Summary: (A) Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Andhra Pradesh Land Grabbing (Prohibition) Act, 1982, ss. 2(c), 7A - Land Grabbing - Application before the Special Tribunal - Maintainability - Appellant allegedly purchased suit building and land appurtenant thereto from predecessor-in-title - Respondent forcibly occupied the suit property - Appellant filed application u/s. 7A of the Act before the Special Tribunal seeking vacant possession of building and appurtenant land from respondent - Appellant contended that respondent was a 'land grabber' under the provisions of the Act - Special Tribunal allowed appellant's application and directed the respondent to deliver the vacant possession of suit property - Respondent filed appeal before the Special Court - Special Court upheld the Special Tribunal order - Respondent challenged the Special Court order before the HC - Respondent contended that Act was applicable only to building and not land appurtenant thereto, hence the application before the Special Tribunal was not maintainable - HC allowed respondent's writ petition holding that since the dispute was in respect of a building with its appurtenant land, the matter would not come squarely within the jurisdiction of the Special Tribunal and hence appellant's application before the Special Tribunal was not maintainable - Whether the HC was correct in holding that the appellant's application u/s. 7A of the Act was not maintainable before the Special Tribunal 'as the property in dispute was a building with its appurtenant land' - Held, term 'land' defines u/s. 2(c) of the Act makes it clear that the expression 'land' includes 'buildings, structures and other things attached to the earth' - In view of such inclusive definition of 'land', grabbing a building attached to the earth amounts to land grabbing for the purposes of the Act - Hence, HC erred in holding that the Act applies to the land but not to the buildings - Further, HC was clearly wrong in holding that 'if an application is filed seeking possession of building along with its appurtenant land, because the building in question is in existence on the land and is surrounded by the vacant land, it cannot be said that it is a case of grabbing of land' - If a building along with the land on which it stands is the subject matter of the application u/s. 7A of the Act, such application is maintainable before the Special Tribunal - Hence, HC erred in holding that the application filed by the appellant was not maintainable before the Special Tribunal - Appellant's application u/s. 7A raised the claim specifically in respect of 'house and appurtenant land' - Appellant specifically alleged that the respondent forcibly occupied the house and since then he was in the occupation of the said house and open land - In such circumstances, it cannot be said that the subject matter of the dispute was only the building - Therefore, impugned HC order is set aside and orders of Special Tribunal and Special Court are restored - Appeal allowed.

(B) Advocates & Judges - Practice & Procedure - Andhra Pradesh Land Grabbing (Prohibition) Act, 1982, s. 2(c) - Judicial Process - Land - Definition - Interpretation by Co-ordinate Bench - Overruling by DB with equal strength - Propriety - Held, even if the DB who decided the impugned matter did not agree with the view taken by a Co-ordinate Bench of equal strength in the earlier matter regarding the interpretation of s. 2(c) of the Act and its application, judicial discipline and practice required them to refer the issue to a larger Bench - DB were not right in over-ruling the statement of the law by a Co-ordinate Bench of equal strength - It is an accepted rule or principle that the statement of the law by a Bench is considered binding on a Bench of the same or lesser number of Judges, however, in case of doubt or disagreement about the decision of the earlier Bench, the well accepted and desirable practice is that the later Bench would refer the case to a larger Bench - Appeal allowed.
