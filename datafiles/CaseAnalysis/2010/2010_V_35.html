Vikram Singh and Others v State of Punjab

Supreme Court of India

25 January 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

H.S. Bedi, J.M. Panchal

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 135; (2010) 3 SCC 56; AIR 2010 SC 1007; 2010 ALL MR (Cri) 982; JT 2010 (1) SC 568; 2010 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 561; 2010 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 713; 2010 (3) MLJ(Crl) 319; 2010 (1) RCR(Criminal) 783; 2010(1) SCALE 633; [2010] 2 S.C.R. 22; 2010 (1) UC 366

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120-B, 201, 302 and 364A - Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 25 and 27 - Murder - Kidnapping for ransom - Conviction - Death sentence - Deceased, a school going boy was kidnapped for ransom and murdered by administering chloroform and fortwin injections in heavy dozes - Sessions Judge held appellants deserved no mercy and accordingly identifying the case as being in the category of the "rarest of the rare", convicted them for offences punishable u/ss. 302, 364A, 201 and 120-B IPC and sentenced them to death - HC confirmed the death sentence - Hence, present appeal - (A) Chain of circumstances - Held, in the present case, there was the direct evidence and eye witness evidence of PW who had seen appellants (known to him from earlier) kidnapping deceased - (B) It was contended that recoveries made at the instance of the appellants u/s. 27 of the Evidence Act could not be taken into evidence as the appellants were not under arrest at that point of time - Held, s. 27 of the Evidence Act reveal that a "person must be accused of any offence" and that he must be "in the custody of a police officer" and it is not essential that such an accused must be under formal arrest - Relied on State of Uttar Pradesh v. Deoman Upadhyaya 1960 INDLAW SC 462 wherein it was held that expression, "accused of any offence" in s. 27, talks about custody of a person accused of an offence - (C) Death sentence - Whether present case fall in the category of the rarest of the rare case? - In Machhi Singh & Ors. v. State of Punjab 1983 INDLAW SC 116 gave an indication as to what could constitute this category - It was observed that in determining the culpability of an accused and the final decision as to the nature of sentence, a balance sheet of the aggravating and mitigating circumstances vis-'-vis the accused had to be drawn up and in doing so the mitigating circumstances had to be given full weight so that all factors were considered before the "option is exercised" - Held, in the present case, balance sheet drawn up by the HC was that (i) kidnapping of a school going innocent boy for ransom; (ii) All three accused-appellant committed offence of murder in a pre-planned manner by using scientific methods and injecting fatal dozes of chemicals in order to ensure that the offence was not detected and they were not fastened with criminal liability;(iii) Right from pre-planning through death till recovery of dead body of the deceased, all three accused-appellants remained closely associated (iv) Murder of the deceased was committed by administering chloroform and fortwin injections in heavy dozes (v) Soon after kidnapping, the deceased was done to death in inhuman, diabolical and dastardly manner - Deceased was the only son of his parents - Balance sheet drawn by HC adopted and death sentence of two appellants confirmed - Order accordingly.
