Siddharam Satlingappa Mhetre v State Of Maharashtra And Others

Supreme Court of India

02 December 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Dalveer Bhandari, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 1026; (2011) 1 SCC 694; AIR 2011 SC 312; 2011 CRLJ 3905; 2011 (2) G.L.R. 1614; 2011 (1) JCC 1; JT 2010 (13) SC 247; 2010 (4) KLT 930; 2010 (4) MLJ(Crl) 745; 2011(1) M.P.H.T. 430; 2011 (1) OLR 405; 2011 (1) RCR(Criminal) 126; 2010(12) SCALE 691

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Deem, Fetters, Canadian Bill of Rights Act, 1960

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 438 - Anticipatory Bail - Scope and Ambit - An FIR was filed against appellant disclosing that appellant and his brother had instigated their men to murder the deceased - Appellant sought anticipatory bail before the HC - HC, by impugned order, declined to grant anticipatory bail - Hence the present appeal - Whether the appellant, in the present case, entitled to enlarge on anticipatory bail - Held, no inflexible guidelines or straitjacket formula can be provided for grant or refusal of anticipatory bail - No attempt should be made to provide rigid and inflexible guidelines in that respect because all circumstances and situations of future cannot be clearly visualized for the grant or refusal of anticipatory bail - In consonance with the legislative intention the grant or refusal of anticipatory bail should necessarily depend on facts and circumstances of each case - Court must carefully examine the entire available record and particularly the allegations which have been directly attributed to the accused and these allegations are corroborated by other material and circumstances on record - Various factors should be taken into consideration while deciding the anticipatory bail applications - If a wise discretion is exercised by the concerned judge, after consideration of entire material on record then most of the grievances in favour of grant of or refusal of bail would be taken care of - Legislature in its wisdom has entrusted the power to exercise the said jurisdiction only to the judges of the superior courts - In case the arrest is imperative, according to the facts of the case, in that event, the arresting officer must clearly record the reasons for the arrest of the accused before the arrest in the case diary, but in exceptional cases where it becomes imperative to arrest the accused immediately, the reasons be recorded in the case diary immediately after the arrest is made without loss of any time so that the court has an opportunity to properly consider the case for grant or refusal of bail in the light of reasons recorded by the arresting officer - Exercise of jurisdiction u/s. 438 of CrPC is extremely important judicial function of a judge and must be entrusted to judicial officers with some experience and good track record - Both individual and society have vital interest in orders passed by the courts in anticipatory bail applications - Therefore, impugned judgment and order of the HC declining anticipatory bail to the appellant cannot be sustained and is consequently set aside - Appeal allowed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 438 - Anticipatory Bail - Grant - Exercise of discretion - Whether the powers u/s. 438 CrPC to grant anticipatory bail is discretionary - Held, after evaluating the averments and accusation available on the record if the court is inclined to grant anticipatory bail then an interim bail be granted and notice be issued to the public prosecutor - After hearing the public prosecutor the court may either reject the bail application or confirm the initial order of granting bail with conditions - Public prosecutor or complainant would be at liberty to move the same court for cancellation or modifying the conditions of bail any time if liberty granted by the court is misused and bail granted by the court should ordinarily be continued till the trial of the case - Intention of the legislature is quite clear that the power of grant or refusal of bail is entirely discretionary - Discretion of grant or cancellation of bail can be exercised either at the instance of the accused, the public prosecutor or the complainant on finding new material or circumstances at any point of time - Appeal allowed.

(C) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 437, 438, 439 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 21 - Anticipatory Bail - Duration - Whether grant of bail for limited period is contrary to the legislative intention and law declared by the Constitution Bench of SC in Gurbaksh Singh Sibbia and Others v. State of Punjab, 1980 INDLAW SC 316 - Held, court which grants the bail has the right to cancel the bail according to the provisions of the General Clauses Act, 1897 but ordinarily after hearing the public prosecutor when the bail order is confirmed then the benefit of the grant of the bail should continue till the end of the trial of that case - Any restriction on the provision of anticipatory bail u/s. 438 CrPC limits the personal liberty of the accused granted u/art. 21 of the Constitution - S. 438 CrPC does not mention anything about the duration for which a direction for release on bail in the event of arrest can be granted - Order granting anticipatory bail is a direction specifically to release the accused on bail in the event of his arrest - Once such a direction of anticipatory bail is executed by the accused and he is released on bail, the concerned court would be fully justified in imposing conditions including direction of joining investigation - Court does not use the expression 'anticipatory bail' but it provides for issuance of direction for the release on bail by the HC or the Court of Sessions in the event of arrest - In Salauddin Abdulsamad Shaikh v. State of Maharashtra,1995 INDLAW SC 1866, SC held that the accused had to surrender before the Trial Court and only thereafter he/she could make prayer for grant of bail by the Trial Court - However, once the accused is released on bail by the Trial Court pursuant to the order of the Court of Sessions or the HC , then it would be unreasonable to compel the accused to surrender before the Trial Court and again apply for regular bail - Applicant who may not have otherwise lost his liberty loses it because he chose to file application of anticipatory bail on mere apprehension of being arrested on accusation of having committed a non-bailable offence - Therefore, finding of the Salauddin's case is contrary to the legislative intention and law which has been declared by a Constitution Bench of SC in Sibbia's case - Restrictions imposed by the SC in Salauddin's case, namely, that the accused released on anticipatory bail must submit himself to custody and only thereafter can apply for regular bail, is contrary to the basic intention and spirit of s. 438 CrPC and art. 21 of the Constitution - Directing the accused to surrender to custody after the limited period amounts to deprivation of his personal liberty - Constitution Bench of the SC in Sibbia's case crystallized the position that the courts should not impose restrictions on the ambit and scope of s. 438 CrPC which were not envisaged by the Legislature - Further, SC, in Salauddin's case, held that anticipatory bail should be granted only for a limited period and on the expiry of that duration it should be left to the regular court to deal with the matter, is not the correct view - Reasons quoted in the Salauddin's case is that anticipatory bail is granted at a stage when an investigation is incomplete and the court is not informed about the nature of evidence against the alleged offender, however, said reason would not be right as the restriction is not seen in CrPC and bail orders by the HC and Sessions Court are granted u/ss. 437 and 439 also at such stages and they are granted till the trial - Anticipatory bail is for limited duration and thereafter the accused should apply to the regular court for bail, that means the life of s. 438 CrPC would come to an end after that limited duration and such limitation has not been envisaged by the legislature - Constitution Bench of the SC in Sibbia's case has comprehensively dealt with almost all aspects of the concept of anticipatory bail u/s. 438 CrPC and in view of the said judgment, it would not be proper to limit the life of anticipatory bail - Appeal allowed.

(D) Advocates & Judges - Practice & Procedure - Judicial Process - Judgment of the SC - Binding nature - Whether the Constitution Bench decision of the SC in Sibbia's case has the mandatory binding nature - Held, only the judgment of a larger strength is binding on a judgment of smaller strength but the judgment of a co-equal strength is also binding on a Bench of judges of co-equal strength - Judgments of Salauddin Abdulsamad Shaikh v. State of Maharashtra, 1995 INDLAW SC 1866, K. L. Verma v. State and Another, 1996 INDLAW SC 1762, Adri Dharan Das v. State of West Bengal, 2005 INDLAW SC 127, Sunita Devi v. State of Bihar and Another, 2004 INDLAW SC 1553 and Naresh Kumar Yadav v. Ravindra Kumar, 2007 INDLAW SC 1063 are by two or three judges of SC and those judgments had clearly ignored a Constitution Bench judgment of SC in Sibbia's case which had comprehensively dealt with all the facets of anticipatory bail enumerated u/s. 438 of CrPC - Said judgments of two or three judges Bench are per incuriam - However, in case there is no judgment of a Constitution Bench or larger Bench of binding nature and if the court doubts the correctness of the judgments by two or three judges, then the proper course would be to request the Chief Justice to refer the matter to a larger Bench of appropriate strength - Since the controversy in the present case is no longer res integra, it is bound to follow the judgment of Constitution Bench of the SC in view of the judicial discipline - Appeal allowed.
