Ranveer Yadav v State of Bihar

Supreme Court of India

12 May 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Ganguly, G.S. Singhvi

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 381; (2010) 11 SCC 493; 2010 (4) ADJ 741; 2010 ALL MR (Cri) 1957; 2010 CRLJ 3431; 2010 (4) CTC 103; 2010 (3) JhrCR(SC) 133; JT 2010 (5) SC 335; 2010 (4) MLJ(Crl) 661; 2010 (3) RCR(Criminal) 213; 2010(5) SCALE 765; [2010] 6 S.C.R. 1073

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971, ss. 15 (2) and 19(1) - Supreme Court Rules, 1966, o. 21 r. 15(1)(e) - Contempt petition - During the course of the Sessions Trial, five of the alleged contemnors were on one side and the sixth contemnor, the appellant, on the other side, and all of them disrupted the proceedings by aggressively exchanging heated words and creating unpleasant scenes in Court - HC issued notice to show cause why the alleged contemnors should not be held guilty of Criminal Contempt for their acts set out in the reference - Five contemnors tendered their apologies for creating the disturbance and stated that the main person responsible for the ruckus was the appellant - HC held that all the contemnors were guilty of having committed criminal contempt but main person responsible for the disruption was the appellant who acted in a motivated and high handed manner to interfere in the due conduct of the proceeding - HC while accepting the unqualified apology of the other five contemnors and letting them off with admonition and severe warning, sentenced the appellant to simple imprisonment for two months with a fine of Rs.2,000/- - Hence, present appeal - Whether order of HC could be upheld? - Held, offending acts of the appellant constitute contempt in the face of Court - When contempt takes place in the face of the Court, peoples' faith in the administration of justice receives a severe jolt and precious judicial time is wasted - Therefore, the offending acts of the appellant certainly come within the ambit of interference with the due course of judicial proceeding and are a clear case of criminal contempt in the face of the Court - Moreover, appellant initially did not offer any apology - Rather the appellant tried to justify and apology was offered in a subsequent show cause reply - Therefore, it is a belated apology - HC in the impugned judgment, therefore was correct in holding the appellant guilty and also in punishing him with the sentence it has imposed - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971, ss. 15 (2) and 19(1) - Supreme Court Rules, 1966, o. 21 r. 15(1)(e) - Contempt petition - Belated apology - An apology in a contempt proceeding must be offered at the earliest possible opportunity. A belated apology hardly shows the 'contrition which is the essence of the purging of a contempt' - Appeal dismissed.
