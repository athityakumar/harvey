B. Manmad Reddy And Others v Chandra Prakash Reddy And Others

Supreme Court of India

17 February 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

T.S. Thakur, Markandey Katju

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 105; (2010) 3 SCC 314; AIR 2010 SC 1001; 2010 (2) ALD(SC) 109; 2010 (2) ESC 161; 2010 (124) FLR 941; JT 2010 (2) SC 298; 2010(2) SCALE 407; [2010] 2 S.C.R. 860; 2010 (2) SLR 201

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Education; Service

Keywords: Andhra Pradesh Educational Service Rules

Summary: Service - Education - Constitution - Andhra Pradesh Educational Service Rules, r. 3 - Andhra Pradesh Education Act, 1982 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 16 - Constitutionality of Note 6 to r. 3 - Government composed various services under four categories by virtue of r. 2 of the Rules for the purpose of promotion - Note 6 to r. 3 prescribed a roster for promotion to the post of District Educational Officer and Deputy Director wherein a vacancy at roster Point 2 would go to a direct recruit Deputy Educational Officer, however, promote Deputy Educational Officer would not be eligible for consideration against a vacancy falling under roster Point 2 - Respondent/promotees challenged the said classification before the Tribunal and the HC - Respondents contended that direct recruits and the promotees might have come from different sources but once they were integrated into one class and any further classification was unconstitutional - Tribunal and the HC held that inasmuch as Note 6 to r. 3 classifies the promotees and direct recruits for the purpose of future promotion, was discriminatory and hence ultra vires of arts. 14 and 16 of the Constitution - Whether persons drawn from different sources and integrated into one class/cadre/category can be classified into separate categories for the purposes of promotion on the basis of the source from which they were drawn - Held, Tribunal and the HC were justified in holding that Note 6 to Rule 3 was unconstitutional inasmuch as the same classified officers eligible for appointment against roster Point 2 vacancies depending upon whether they were direct recruits or promotees - Such a classification stood obliterated after integration of officers coming from different source into a common cadre/category would be wholly unjustified and discriminatory - It cannot be said that classification must rest on a reasonable and intelligible basis and the same must bear a nexus to the object sought to be achieved by the statute - By its very nature classification can and is often fraught with the danger of resulting in artificial inequalities which make it necessary to subject the power to classify to restraints lest the guarantee of equality becomes illusory on account of classifications being fanciful instead of fair, intelligible or reasonable - Therefore, classification contemplated in Note 6 to r. 3 is ultra vires of the Constitution - Appeals dismissed.
