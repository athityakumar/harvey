V.P. Shrivastava v Indian Explosives Ltd. & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

24 September 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

D.K. Jain, H.L. Dattu

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 780; (2010) 10 SCC 361; 2010 ALL MR (Cri) 3637; 2010 (4) BC 436; [2010] 159 Comp Cas 529; 2010 (4) JCC 2975; JT 2010 (10) SC 479; 2011 (2) MLJ(Crl) 365; 2010(10) SCALE 177; [2010] 11 S.C.R. 788

Case Digest

Subject: Corporate; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Corporate - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 482 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120-B, 406 and 420 - Companies Act, 1956, ss. 540 and 542 - Quashing of complaint - Appellants were senior employees of the Fertilizer Corporation of India Limited (FCIL"), a government company - In 1992, FCIL was declared a "sick company" - However, in 2001, FCIL entered into a tripartite agreement with Bharat Coking Coal Limited (BCCL) and IEL (complainant) - Thereafter, BIFR directed the winding up of FCIL and sent its opinion to the HC for confirmation - Meanwhile, appellants were discharged from the service of FCIL - Thereafter, IEL instituted a criminal complaint u/ss. 406, 420 and 120B of the IPC r/w ss. 540 and 542 of the Companies Act, 1956 against appellants and summons were issued against them - Petition filed u/s. 482 of the CrPC for quashing of the order summoning them was dismissed by HC - HC held that the if the fact that FCIL, of which the appellants were senior functionaries, had become sick and the question of its winding up was under consideration by the BIFR was made known to the complainant company, it would not have agreed to the proposal of the appellants - Hence, present appeals - Whether in the light of the allegations in the complaint against the appellants, the HC was correct in law in declining to exercise its jurisdiction u/s. 482 of the CrPC? - Held, to hold a person guilty of cheating, as defined in s. 415 of the IPC, it is necessary to show that at the time of making the promise he had fraudulent or dishonest intention to retain the property or to induce the person so deceived to do something which he would not otherwise do - In the present case, there was no specific averment that the appellants had dishonestly "induced" IEL to enter into the agreement/arrangement - Present case, at best, was a case of breach of contract on the part of FCIL but do not constitute an offence of "cheating", punishable u/s. 420 of the IPC - Further, there was nothing in the complaint to suggest that the IEL had entrusted any property to the appellants or that the appellants had dominion over any of the properties of the IEL, which they dishonestly converted to their own use so as to satisfy the ingredients of s. 405 of the IPC, punishable u/s. 406 of IPC - As no prima facie case made out against the appellants in respect of the alleged offences u/ss. 420 and 406 of IPC, the question of alleged conspiracy between the appellants does not arise - No prima facie case made out against the appellants in respect of alleged offences u/ss. 420, 406 and 120B of the IPC and, it was a fit case where the HC should have exercised its jurisdiction u/s. 482 of the Code quashing the complaint against the appellants - Impugned order set aside - Appeals allowed.
