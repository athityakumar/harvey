Commissioner Of Customs (Preventive), Mumbai v M/S M. Ambalal And Company

Supreme Court of India

09 December 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

H.L. Dattu, D.K. Jain

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 1066; (2011) 2 SCC 74; 2010 (260) E.L.T. 487; JT 2010 (13) SC 566; 2010(13) SCALE 266; [2011] 196 TAXMAN 584

Case Digest

Subject: Customs; Indirect Tax

Keywords: Importation, Baggage Rules, 1998, Land Customs Act, Import Control Act, Kar Vivad Samadhan Scheme, 1998

Summary: Customs - Indirect Tax - Customs Act, 1962, ss. 111(d) and 112 - Kar Vivad Samadhan Scheme, 1998 - Baggage Rules, 1998 - Confiscation - Penalty - Exemption - Notification no. 247/76-Cus. dt. 02.08.1976 - In a search and seizure in the office premises of the respondent, appellant-Department recovered a large quantity of rough diamonds - Appellant confiscate said diamonds and issued show cause notice to the respondent - After adjudication, the adjudicating authority passed an order confiscating the seized diamonds u/s. 111(d) of the 1962 Act - However, an option was given to the respondent to redeem the seized goods on payment of redemption fine - On appeal, Tribunal confirmed the redemption fine of Rs. 60,00,000/- and penalty of Rs. 25,00,000/- on the respondent - Subsequently, respondent requested the appellant for release of diamonds by placing reliance on the Notification No.247/76-Cus - Said request was turned down by the appellant and the respondent was informed that the seized diamonds would be released only after payment of redemption fine - HC dismissed writ petition filed against said order - SC dismissed petition filed against said order and directed the Commissioner to decide the amount of duty payable under the Customs Act in respect of seized goods - Commissioner applied the Baggage Rules and quantified the duty payable by the respondent for an amount of Rs. 2,20,50,125/- - On appeal, Tribunal held that the exemption would be available to the goods imported by the respondent in the light of the Notification No.247/76- Cus - Appellant filed appeal against said order contending that the benefit of the exemption notification cannot be extended to a person who/which had illegally imported rough diamonds into the country - Whether the benefit of the exemption notification has been rightly granted to the respondent by the Tribunal? - Held, from the wording of the exemption notification no. 247/76-Cus, it is clear that the benefit of the exemption envisaged is for those goods that are imported - It is necessary that the said notification is read along with ss. 11, 111 and 112 of the 1962 Act, which provides for detection of illegally imported goods and prevention of the disposal thereof, confiscation of the goods and conveyances and imposition of penalties respectively - Under s. 111(d) of the 1962 Act, any goods which are imported contrary to any prohibition imposed by or under this Act or any other law for the time being in force shall be liable for confiscation - The goods which have been seized in the present case cannot be imported into India without a licence under the Import Control Act and there is, therefore, a prohibition in law for the import of goods except in compliance with the Import Control Act - Goods so imported cannot therefore, be treated to be lawfully 'imported goods' within the definition of that term in s. 2(25) of the 1962 Act - Therefore, the respondent was not entitled to the benefit of the notification - Impugned order of tribunal set aside - Appeals disposed of.
