Jai Singh and another v Municipal Corporation of Delhi and another

Supreme Court of India

23 September 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Surinder Singh Nijjar, B. SUDARSHAN REDDY

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 785; (2010) 9 SCC 385; 2010(6) ALL MR 410; 2010 (172) DLT 377; JT 2010 (10) SC 241; 2011 (1) MLJ(SC) 153; 2010 (2) RCR(Rent) 365; 2010(10) SCALE 209; [2010] 12 S.C.R. 358

Case Digest

Subject: Practice & Procedure; Rent Control

Keywords: Delhi Road Transport Corporation Act, 1950

Summary: Rent Control - Practice & Procedure - Delhi Rent Control Act, 1958, s. 39(1) - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 227 - Decree of eviction - Revision before the HC u/art. 227 of the Constitution - Maintainability - Appellant/landlord let out the demised premises to 2nd respondent/ Transport Corporation - 2nd respondent allegedly sub let a part of the demised premises to 1st respondent/Municipal Corporation without written consent of appellants - Appellants filed a suit for eviction against respondents - Rent Control Court (RCC) decreed the eviction and Appellate Tribunal upheld the same - Respondent filed revision before the HC u/s. 39 (1) of the Act, however, respondent withdrew the same on the ground that they had failed to raise any substantial question of law therein - Respondent, subsequently, filed a revision petition u/art. 227 of the Constitution challenging the orders of RCC and Appellate Tribunal - Appellant contended that the revision petition was non-maintainable since there was some delay and laches - HC rejected appellant's objection on the ground of delay and laches and set aside the concurrent findings of RCC and Appellate Tribunal - Whether the exercise of power u/art. 227 of the Constitution, by the HC, in the peculiar facts of the present case was proper - Held, HC, u/art. 227 of the Constitution, cannot lightly or liberally act as an appellate court and re-appreciate the evidence - HC cannot substitute its own conclusions for the conclusions reached by the courts below or the statutory/quasi judicial tribunals - Power to re-appreciate evidence would only be justified in rare and exceptional situations where grave injustice would be done unless the HC interferes - HC, in the present case, had travelled beyond the limits of its jurisdiction u/art. 227 of the Constitution - Both RCC and Appellate Tribunal had acted within the limits of the jurisdiction vested in them and their conclusions cannot be said to be based on no evidence - RCC and Appellate Tribunal took all the materials into consideration - Therefore, there was hardly any justification for the HC to undertake an investigation into issues which did not even arise in the lis - HC failed to bestow proper attention to the objections taken by the appellants to the maintainability of the writ petition on the ground of delay and laches - Proceedings u/art. 227 can be initiated in the absence of the availability of an alternative efficacious remedy, however, in the present case, respondent had consciously withdrawn revision filed u/s. 39(1) of the Act - Further, HC brushed aside appellant's objection on the ground of delay and laches on untenable grounds - Having made an admission that no substantial question of law was raised in the revision u/s. 39(1) of the Act, withdrawal of the same could not possibly have been used as a justification for filing a petition u/art. 227 of the Constitution - If the revision u/s. 39(1) of the Act was devoid of any substantial question of law, the petition u/art. 227, based on the same facts, would be equally devoid of any substantial question of law - However, said categorical admission of the respondent was ignored by the HC whilst recording the finding that the orders of RCC and Appellate Tribunal were passed in blatant violation of fundamental principles of law and justice - HC committed a serious error of jurisdiction in entertaining the writ petition filed by respondent u/art. 227 of the Constitution in the peculiar circumstances of the present case - Impugned order of HC is set aside - Appeal allowed.
