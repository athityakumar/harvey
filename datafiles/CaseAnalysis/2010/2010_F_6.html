Pepsico India Holdings Pvt. Ltd v Food Inspector & Anr

Supreme Court of India

18 November 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

Altamas Kabir, Cyriac Joseph, Deepak Verma

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 970; (2011) 1 SCC 176; [2011] 161 Comp Cas 197; 2011 (1) CompLJ 241; 2011 CRLJ 1012; 2010 (4) JCC 3055; JT 2010 (12) SC 531; 2010 (4) KLT 706; 2011 (2) MLJ(Crl) 170; 2011 (1) OLR 376; 2011 (1) RCR(Criminal) 93; 2010(12) SCALE 136; 2010 (4) WLN(SC) 62

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Health & Drug; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Injurious To Health

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Health & Drug - Prevention of Food Adulteration Act, 1954, ss. 2(ia)(h), 23(1-A)(ee) and 17 - Prevention of Food Adulteration Rules, 1955, r. 65 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 482 - Maintainability of criminal prosecutions - Appellants, Pepsico India Holdings Pvt. Ltd., manufacturer of Sweetened Carbonated Water, were prosecuted for the presence of Carbofuran in its product - Appellants filed petition u/s. 482 of CrPC for quashing criminal prosecutions, which was dismissed - Hence, present appeals - (A) Whether presence of 0.001 mg of Carbofuran per litre found in the sweetened carbonated water, manufactured by the appellant-company, can be said to be adulterated as per r. 65 of the Rules and u/s. 2(ia)(h) of the Act, particularly in the absence of any validated standard of analysis provided for under the Act or Rules? - Held, although, carbonated water was not included in the original Table appended to r. 65 of the Rules, it was introduced in Item 23 of the Table under the heading "Chlorpyrifos" w.e.f 17.06.2009, and tolerance limit of the presence of insecticide residue was prescribed as 0.001 mg/litre, which, in fact, was the amount of insecticide residue found by the Public Analyst in the sample of Pepsi submitted for such analysis - Hence, after the tolerance limit was prescribed, the sample of Pepsi could not be said to be adulterated being within the prescribed tolerance limit - (B) Effect of non-specification of the level of tolerance in respect of the presence of pesticide residue in Sweetened Carbonated Water in the Table appended to r. 65(2) of the Rules - Held, since the range indicated as to the limits of tolerance of the presence of pesticides in different articles of food, including Sweetened Carbonated Water, which was included in the Table appended to r. 65(2) w.e.f 17.06.2009, provides very little or practically no margin for error, the selection of Laboratories and the prescription of tolerance limits for different articles of food acquires great significance - Tolerance limits have been prescribed in the Table for this very purpose - Further, mere presence of pesticide residue does not ipso facto render the article of food adulterated - In the present case, HC overlooked the fact that the percentage of pesticides found by the Public Analyst in the Sweetened Carbonated Water manufactured by the Appellants was within the tolerance limits subsequently prescribed in respect of such product - (C) Liability of the Directors of a company which is said to have committed defaults within the meaning of s. 17 of the Act, in the light of the decision of SC in S.M.S. Pharmaceuticals Ltd. v. Neeta Bhalla & Anr. 2005 INDLAW SC 560, when they were neither in charge of nor responsible for the conduct of the business of the Company - Held, in the present case, HC went beyond the ratio of the decision of SC in S.M.S. Pharmaceuticals Ltd. case upon holding that the principles set out in the said decision could not be understood in any mechanical or rigid manner - HC erred in giving its own interpretation to the decision of SC in S.M.S. Pharmaceuticals Ltd.'s case - (D) Whether non-formulation of Rules under the aforesaid provisions of the Act could be said to be fatal for the prosecution? - Held, HC misconstrued provisions of s. 23(1-A)(ee) and (hh) in holding that the same were basically enabling provisions and were not mandatory and could, in any event, be solved by the Central Government by framing Rules thereunder, by which specified tests to be held in designated Laboratories could be spelt out - Consequently, HC also erred in holding that the non-formulation of Rules under the aforesaid provisions of the Act could not be said to be fatal for the prosecution - Prosecution of the appellants in respect of various complaints quashed - Appeals allowed.
