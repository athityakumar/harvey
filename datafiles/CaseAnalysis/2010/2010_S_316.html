Smt. Pebam Ningol Mikoi Devi v State Of Manipur And Ors.

Supreme Court of India

27 September 2010

Case Analysis

Bench

H.L. Dattu, D.K. Jain

Where Reported

2010 Indlaw SC 789; (2010) 9 SCC 618; 2010 ALL MR (Cri) 3704; JT 2010 (10) SC 456; 2011 (1) MLJ(Crl) 572; 2010 (4) RCR(Criminal) 563; 2010(10) SCALE 248; [2010] 12 S.C.R. 429; 2010 (3) UC 1782

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - National Security Act, 1980, s. 3(2) - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 22(5) - Preventive detention - Legality - Detenu, Editor of a Manipuri Daily evening paper, was detained under National Security Act - It was alleged that he was involved in extorting money from contractors and engineers of Public Health Engineering Department and Forest Department of Manipur Government by delivering demand letters which he printed in his own press - This extortion resulted in a terror wave in the general public which was prejudicial to the maintenance of public order - Appellant (wife of the detenu) filed a habeas corpus petition before HC questioning the detention order, which was dismissed - Hence, present appeal - (A) Whether detaining authority was justified in arriving at a finding that the detenu should be detained under the National Security Act without any trial? - Held, there must be a reasonable basis for the detention order, and there must be material to support the same - Detaining authority must be satisfied that the person to be detained is likely to act in any manner prejudicial to the security of the State or from acting in any manner prejudicial to the maintenance of the public order and the authority must be further satisfied that it is necessary to detain the said person in order to prevent from so acting - However, if one of the grounds or reasons which lead to the subjective satisfaction of the detaining authority under Act, is non-existent or misconceived or irrelevant, the order of detention would be invalid - In the present case, grounds on which detention order was passed has no probative value and were extraneous to the scope, purpose and the object of the National Security Act - Furthermore, none of the documents substantiate the involvement of the detenu in unlawful activities as alleged in the detention order - Thus, there was no pertinent or relevant material on the basis of which, the detention order could be passed - (B) Delay of 7 days in forwarding the representation to the Central Government - Whether failure to comply with the request of the detenu for the onward transmission of the representation to the Central Government deprived the detenu of his right to have his detention revoked by that Government? - Held, art. 22(5) of the Constitution mandates in preventive detention matters the detenu should be afforded the earliest possible opportunity to make a representation against the order - In the present case, delay of 7 days may not be inordinate; however, there was no explanation given for this delay - Therefore, in view of fact that none of the documents relied on by the detaining Authority in passing the detention order can be deemed to be pertinent, and the fact that the delay remained unexplained, sufficient ground made out to quash the order of preventive detention made against the detenu - Impugned judgment and order of the HC and the order of detention passed by the detaining authority set aside - Appeal allowed.
