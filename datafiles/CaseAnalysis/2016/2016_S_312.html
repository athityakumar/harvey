Manganese Ore India Limited v State of M. P. and others

Supreme Court of India

10 November 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Shiva Kirti Singh

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 818; (2017) 1 SCC 81; AIR 2017 SC 87; 2016(10) SCALE 642

Case Digest

Subject: Electricity & Energy

Keywords: Central Excise Act, 1944, Customs Act, 1962, Consumption, Rates, Tariff, Mines, Energy, Mines Act, 1952, Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, 1957, M.P. Electricity Duty (Amendment) Act, 1986, Madhya Pradesh Electricity Duty Act, 1949, Constitution of India, 1950, Berar Electricity Duty Act, 1949, Government of India Act, 1953

Summary: Electricity & Energy - Mines & Minerals - Madhya Pradesh Electricity Duty Act, 1949, s.3(1) - Tariff on products - Extraction of mineral - SC setting aside judgment of HC, remanded matter to HC, for fresh consideration on the issue whether copper concentrate is a mineral and whether Explanation to Part B of the Act applies even though manufacturing process is involved to bring it into existence - It was contended that copper ore is extracted at mine pit and then it is subjected to processing whereafter copper ore becomes copper concentrate which is a different commodity which is an excisable product and on that basis it was urged that copper concentrate is not a mineral and consequently, s.3 Explanation (b) to Part B is not applicable - HC after considering rivalised submissions, held that copper concentrate is a mineral as defined in s.3 Explanation (b) to Part B of the Act and so, the same is applicable to it - Further, 'copper concentrate is the end product and what is crushed, processed, treated or transported is not copper concentrate but the ore. As such, the electricity in question is being consumed for such 'crushing, processing, treating or transportation - Hence, instant appeals - Whether the activity after the mineral i.e. ore has been mined is covered by word 'processing' as used in Item 3(1) Explanation B, which defines the term 'mine'.

Held, mining would comprehend every activity by which the mineral is extracted or obtained from earth irrespective of whether such activity is carried on at the surface or in the bowel, but it has to be an activity for winning a mineral. Further, for the purpose of Item 3, 'mine' to which electrical energy is sold, supplied or consumed, it would include machinery or premises situated in the adjacent to the mine, provided the electricity is used for crushing, processing, treating or transporting the minerals. Further, the Ferro Manganese Plant, being a unit involved in manufacturing of ferromanganese alloy as opposed to a unit involved in crushing, treating, processing, etc. of manganese ore, could not be treated within the extended definition of 'mine' within the Explanation (b) of Part B of Table of Rates of Duty to s.3(1) of the Act. The Ferromanganese Alloy so manufactured by appellant using the mineral Manganese at its Ferromanganese plant is an entirely different product from its mineral raw material both physically and even chemically. Further, unlike Manganese ore a ferromanganese alloy could not be found in the natural state and it has to be manufactured from the manganese ore and other minerals only and the same logic is applicable to copper concentrate as a different and distinct product comes into existence. Hence, in both cases, different products in commercial parlance have emerged and so, principle of noscitur a sociis has to be applied and as logical corollary, tariff has to be levied as meant for manufacturing unit. Hence, analysis made by HC is not correct and, accordingly, the judgments of HC are set aside and appeals are allowed. However, if any amount is paid by appellant to revenue during intervening period, the same is to be adjusted towards future demands. Appeals disposed of.
