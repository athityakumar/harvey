Management of TNSTC (Coimbatore) Limited v M. Chandrasekaran

Supreme Court of India

02 September 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

A.M. Khanwilkar, T.S. Thakur

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 628; 2016 (4) ACC 1; AIR 2016 SC 4055; 2016 (5) AWC 4738; 2016 (151) FLR 210; JT 2016 (8) SC 532; 2016 (4) LLJ 682; 2017 (1) RLW 249; 2016(8) SCALE 477; [2016] 4 S.C.R. 266

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Keywords: Evidence, Punishment, Jurisdiction, SERVICE, Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, Enquiry Officer, Appeal, Dismissal, Charges, Writ Petition

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, ss. 10, 33(2)(b) - Industrial dispute - Dismissal - Reinstatement with backwages - Respondent was employed as a driver in appellant/management - Respondent while on duty during a trip caused an accident with a car coming from opposite direction resulting in fatal injuries to persons travelling in that car - Enquiry Officer found respondent guilty of charges - Disciplinary Authority passed order of dismissal of respondent on 13-10-2003 - Appellant submitted an approval petition u/s. 33(2)(b) of the Act before Commissioner as an industrial dispute was pending for conciliation - Commissioner found that respondent was not cross-examined by Dept. and no eye witness was examined by Dept. nor conductor of bus or passengers travelling in the same bus were examined and held that Enquiry Officer's report was vitiated being perverse and refused to accord approval for dismissal of respondent - Aggrieved, appellant filed writ petition before HC and respondent filed writ petition against appellant to reinstate him with continuity of service, back-wages and all other attendant benefits - Single Judge dismissed writ petition preferred by appellant and allowed writ petition preferred by respondent and issued direction to appellant to reinstate respondent with back-wages and continuity of service and all other attendant benefits - On appeal, DB dismissed appeal and affirmed view taken by Single Judge - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, neither Commissioner nor HC considered the matter on that basis nor posed unto themselves the correct question which was relevant for deciding the application u/s. 33(2)(b) of the Act. Significantly, the order of punishment also adverts to past history of respondent indicative of respondent having faced similar departmental action on thirty two occasions, including for having committed minor as well as fatal accidents while performing his duty. Commissioner exceeded his jurisdiction in reappreciating the evidence adduced before Enquiry Officer and in substituting his own judgment to that of Disciplinary Authority. It was not a case of no legal evidence produced during the enquiry by Dept., in relation to the charges framed against respondent. Whether decision of Disciplinary Authority of dismissing respondent is just and proper, could be assailed by respondent in appropriate proceedings. Considering the fact that there was adequate material produced in Departmental enquiry evidencing that fatal accident was caused by respondent while driving the vehicle on duty, burden to prove that accident happened due to some other cause than his own negligence was on respondent. The doctrine of Res ipsa loquitur squarely applies to the fact situation in instant case. Ordinarily, SC would have remitted the matter back to Commissioner for consideration afresh, but as the matter is pending for a long time and as SC is satisfied that in the fact situation of instant case approval to the order of punishment passed by appellant against respondent should have been granted, SC allow the application u/s. 33(2)(b) of the Act preferred by appellant but with liberty to respondent to take recourse to appropriate remedy as may be available in law to question the said order of dismissal. Impugned decisions of HC as well as of Commissioner were set aside. Appeals allowed.
