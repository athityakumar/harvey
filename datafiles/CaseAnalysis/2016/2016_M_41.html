Mahipal Singh Rana v State of Uttar Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

05 July 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Anil R. Dave, Kurian Joseph, Adarsh Kumar Goel

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 487; (2016) 8 SCC 335; AIR 2016 SC 3302; 2016 CRLJ 3734; 2016 (2) ILR(Cut) 707; 2016 (3) JCC 2157; JT 2016 (6) SC 307; 2016 (4) KLT 306; 2017 (1) MLJ(Crl) 318; 2016 (3) RCR(Criminal) 933; [2016] 4 S.C.R. 184

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Contempt Of Court, Criminal Contempt, Disciplinary Committee, Jurisdiction, Companies Act, 1956, Administration Of Justice, Advocates Act, 1961, Review, Contempt of Courts Act, 1971, National Council for Teacher Education Act, 1993, Apology, Cost And Works Accountants Act, 1959, Chartered Accountants Act, 1949, Representation Of The People Act, 1951, Architects Act, 1972, Company Secretaries Act, 1980, Actuaries Act, 2006, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Gujarat Professional Civil Engineers Act, 2006

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Advocates Act, 1961, ss. 24A, 34, 34(1), 35, 38 -

Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971, ss. 15(2), 19, 2(c) -

Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 142, 145, 19, 19(1)(g), 226, s. 129 - Professional misconduct - Contempt proceedings - Challenged - Appellant was found guilty of Criminal Contempt for intimidating and threatening a Civil Judge - HC convicted appellant for criminal contempt and sentenced him simple imprisonment of 2 months with fine - HC, further directed Bar Council of Uttar Pradesh to initiate appropriate proceedings against appellant for professional misconduct - Hence, instant appeal - Whether a case has been made out for interference with order passed by HC convicting appellant for criminal contempt.

Held, no error was committed by HC while coming to conclusion that appellant had committed contempt of Court under the provisions of the 1971 Act. Appellant did appear before Court and used the language which was contemptuous in nature. Appellant should not use such contemptuous language in Court against presiding Judge. It is for judge to decide as to whether action should be taken under the 1971 Act or under any other law. HC had rightly convicted appellant under the 1971 Act after having come to a conclusion that denial of incidents and allegations of malafides against complainant Judge was made by appellant to save himself from consequences of contempt proceedings. Appellant had refused to tender apology for his conduct. His affidavit in support of stay vacation/modification and supplementary affidavit did not show any remorse and he had justified himself again and again, which also shows that he had no regards for the majesty of law. Thus, HC has rightly held that appellant was guilty of criminal contempt. Appeal disposed of.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Advocates Act, 1961, ss. 24A, 34, 34(1), 35, 38 -

Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971, ss. 15(2), 19, 2(c) -

Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 142, 145, 19, 19(1)(g), 226, s. 129 - Contempt proceedings - Debarred from practice - Legality - Whether on conviction for criminal contempt appellant can be allowed to practise.

Held, SC while examining its powers u/art.129 r/w art. 142 of Constitution with regard to awarding sentence of imprisonment together with suspension of his practice as an Advocate. In Supreme Court Bar Association v Union Of India & Anr. 1998 Indlaw SC 688 it was held that while in exercise of contempt jurisdiction, SC cannot take over jurisdiction of disciplinary committee of Bar Council and it is for Bar Council to punish advocate by debarring him from practice or suspending his licence as may be warranted on the basis of his having been found guilty of contempt. If Bar Council fails to take action, SC could invoke its appellate power u/s.38 of the 1961 Act. In instant case HC can prevent contemnor advocate from appearing before it or other courts till he purges himself of contempt which is different from suspending or revoking licence or debarring him to practise. Therefore, licence of appellant will remain suspended for further 5years.

Conviction of appellant is justified and is upheld. Appeal disposed of.

Ratio - In deciding contempt is serious enough to merit imprisonment, Court will take into account likelihood of interference with administration of justice and culpability of offender.
