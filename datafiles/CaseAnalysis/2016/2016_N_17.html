Afcons Infrastructure Limited v Nagpur Metro Rail Corporation Limited and another

Supreme Court of India

15 September 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 648; AIR 2016 SC 4305; 2017(1) ALL MR 448; 2016 (6) AWC 5533; 2016 (4) BC 485; JT 2016 (9) SC 165; 2016 (4) KLT 6; 2016 (7) MLJ 172; 2016(8) SCALE 765; [2016] 3 S.C.R. 551

Case Digest

Subject: Contract & Commercial

Keywords: Contract, Metro Railways (Construction of Works) Act, 1978, Delhi Metro Railway (Operation and Maintenance) Act, 2002

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Delhi Metro Railway (Operation and Maintenance) Act, 2002 - Metro Railways (Construction of Works) Act, 1978, s. 2(i) - Tender invitation - Eligibility - Respondents invited bids for design and construction of a viaduct in Reach-3 between stations on East-West Corridor of Metro Rail Project - M/s. Guangdong Yuantian Engineering Company (GYT) and M/s. TATA Projects Limited (TPL) as a Joint Venture ('GYT-TPL JV') gave its bid for contract but respondents disqualified at technical bid opening - GYT-TPL JV filed writ petition before HC questioning whether the Pearl River Delta Intercity High Speed Railway Project met requirement of a 'metro civil construction work' - HC held that GYT-TPL JV were eligible to bid for a tender invited by respondents - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, a mere disagreement with the decision making process or decision of administrative authority is no reason for a constitutional Court to interfere. The threshold of mala fides, intention to favour someone or arbitrariness, irrationality or perversity must be met before constitutional Court interferes with the decision making process or the decision. HC was of opinion that eligible bidders were not entitled to be either impleaded in the petition filed in HC by the ineligible bidder GYT-TPL JV or were not entitled to be heard. With respect, this is not the appropriate view to take in matters such as the instant one. There could be occasions where an eligible bidder could bring to the notice of owner or employer of project that ineligible bidder was ineligible for additional reasons or reasons that were not within the contemplation of owner or employer of the project. Though there does not appear to be any ambiguity or doubt about the interpretation given by respondents to the tender conditions, SC was of the view that even if there was such an ambiguity or doubt, HC ought to have refrained from giving its own interpretation unless it had come to a clear conclusion that interpretation given by respondents was perverse or mala fide or intended to favour one of the bidders. This was certainly not the case either before HC or before SC. Impugned judgment and orders passed by HC and restore the decision of respondents to the effect that GYT-TPL JV was not eligible to bid for the contract under consideration. Appeals allowed.
