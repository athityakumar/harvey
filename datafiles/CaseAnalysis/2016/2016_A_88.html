 Anita Kushwaha and others v Pushap Sudan and others

Supreme Court of India

19 July 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

T.S. Thakur, Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla, A.K. Sikri, Sharad Arvind Bobde, R. Banumathi

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 828; (2016) 8 SCC 509; AIR 2016 SC 3506; 2017(1) ALL MR 883; 2016 (6) AWC 5962; 2016 CRLJ 4151; JT 2016 (8) SC 268; 2016 (3) KLT 799; 2016 (3) RCR(Criminal) 852; 2016(7) SCALE 235; [2016] 9 S.C.R. 560

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Fundamental Right, Right To Life, Jurisdiction, Opportunity, Denial, Legal Aid, Speedy Trial, Prisoner, Access, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, Defence of India Act, 1939, Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987, Constitution (Forty-Second Amendment) Act, 1976, Litigant, Prisons Act, 1894, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Criminal, South African Constitution, 1996, Jammu and Kashmir Code of Criminal Procedure, 1989, Jammu and Kashmir Code of Civil Procedure, 1977, Prisons Rules, 1964

Summary: (A) Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, ss. 1, 25 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, ss. 340, 491 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 1, 397, 406, 482 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 139A, 14, 142, 143, 21, 226, 30, 32, 34, 39A - Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987 - Civil or criminal case - Power to transfer - Validity - Petitioners filed instant transfer petitions which were referred to a Constitution Bench of SC to examine whether SC has power to transfer a civil or criminal case pending in any Court in State to a Court outside that State and vice versa - Whether access to justice was indeed a fundamental right and if so, what was the sweep and content of that right.

Held, access to justice was indeed a facet of right to life guaranteed u/art. 21 of Constitution. The Citizen's inability to access Courts or any other adjudicatory mechanism provided for determination of rights and obligations was bound to result in denial of guarantee contained in art. 14 of Constitution both in relation to equality before law as well as equal protection of laws. Absence of any adjudicatory mechanism or the inadequacy of such mechanism, needless to say, is bound to prevent those looking for enforcement of their right to equality before laws and equal protection of the laws from seeking redress and thereby negate the guarantee of equality before laws or equal protection of laws and reduce it to a mere teasing illusion. Art. 21 of Constitution apart, access to justice could be said to be part of the guarantee contained in art. 14 of Constitution as well. State should provide an effective adjudicatory mechanism. The mechanism so provided should be reasonably accessible in terms of distance. The process of adjudication should be speedy and litigant's access to adjudicatory process should be affordable. Art. 39A of Constitution promotes a laudable objective of providing legal aid to needy litigants and obliges State to make access to justice affordable for less fortunate sections of society. Affordability of access to justice was to an extent, taken care by State sponsored legal aid programmes under the Act. Legal aid programmes have been providing the much needed support to the poorer sections of society in accessing justice in Courts. Order accordingly.

(B) Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, ss. 1, 25 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, ss. 340, 491 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 1, 397, 406, 482 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 139A, 14, 142, 143, 21, 226, 30, 32, 34, 39A - Legal Services Authorities Act, 1987 - Transfer of cases - Prohibition - Applicability - Whether arts. 32 and 142 of Constitution empower SC to issue suitable directions for transfer of cases to and from State in appropriate situations.

Held, access to justice was a facet of right to life guaranteed u/art. 21 of Constitution, a violation actual or threatened of that right would justify the invocation of SC's powers u/art. 32 of Constitution. Further, there was no prohibition against use of power u/art. 142 of Constitution to direct transfer of cases from a Court in State to a Court outside State or vice versa. The absence of an enabling provision, however, cannot be construed as a prohibition against transfer of cases to or from State. At any rate, a prohibition simplicitor was not enough. What was equally important was to see whether there was any fundamental principle of public policy underlying any such prohibition. No such prohibition nor any public policy could be seen in cases at hand much less a public policy based on any fundamental principle. The extraordinary power available to SC u/art. 142 of Constitution could be usefully invoked in a situation where Court was satisfied that denial of an order of transfer from or to Court in State would deny citizen his/her right of access to justice. The provisions of arts. 32, 136 and 142 of Constitution were wide enough to empower SC to direct such transfer in appropriate situations, no matter Central Code of Civil and Criminal Procedures do not extend to State nor do State Codes of Civil and Criminal Procedure contain any provision that empowers SC to transfer cases. Order accordingly.
