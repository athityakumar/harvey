Mahavir Singh v State of Madhya Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

09 November 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

N.V. Ramana, A.K. Sikri

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 772; (2016) 10 SCC 220; AIR 2016 SC 5231; 2016 (4) CGLJ 541; 2017 CRLJ 749; 2016 (3) JabLJ 363; JT 2016 (11) SC 52; 2016 (4) MLJ(Crl) 624; 2017 (1) RCR(Criminal) 414; [2016] 8 S.C.R. 394

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Reasonable Doubt, Blood, Medical Evidence, Ocular Evidence, Compelling, Bullet, Interfere, Interested Witness, Deposition, Chance Witness, Distance, Cartridges, Height, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Investigating Officer, Seizure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 161 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 147, 148, 149, 302 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant/accused along with co-accused armed with deadly weapons rushed to deceased's house hurling abusive filthy words and fired a gunshot in the abdomen of deceased - Charge-sheet was filed against appellant u/ss. 302, 147, 148 and 149 IPC - Trial Court acquitted appellant from alleged offences on ground that there were contradictions in the evidence of eyewitnesses to that of medical evidence, prosecution has failed to prove beyond reasonable doubt formation of unlawful assembly with a motive of committing murder of the deceased and also failed to establish that the bullet had been fired with the firearm seized from appellant - On appeal, HC partly allowed appeal by confirming judgment of Trial Court in respect of charge u/s. 148 IPC and convicted appellant for the offence u/s. 302 IPC and sentenced him to undergo imprisonment for life - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, HC which has set aside the acquittal order of Trial Court has observed that Trial Court has based its reasoning on guess work. SC found it that even HC has committed the same mistake and basing on the same facts and guess work has arrived at the conclusion that appellant was guilty. In view of contradictory statements by prosecution witnesses coupled with unmatched medical evidence, delay in recording of statements of witnesses by I.O., non-availability of proper site plan and in the absence of authenticated ballistic expert report that the bullet was fired with the seized gun of appellant, Trial Court had to decide the case against prosecution and discharge appellant from charges. In instant case, approach of IO in recording the statements of witnesses, collecting the evidence and preparation of site map has remained unmindful. There was no firm and sincere effort with the needed zeal and spirit to bring home the guilt of accused. SC felt that no compelling and substantial reasons for HC to interfere with the order of acquittal when prosecution has miserably failed to establish the guilt of accused. Impugned judgment of conviction and order of sentence passed by HC was set aside. Appeal allowed.
