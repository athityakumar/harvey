State of Karnataka v State of Tamil Nadu and others

Supreme Court of India

09 December 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Amitava Roy, A.M. Khanwilkar

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 1037; (2017) 3 SCC 362; JT 2016 (12) SC 577; 2016(12) SCALE 679; [2016] 8 S.C.R. 499

Case Digest

Subject: Water

Keywords: Government of India Act, 1935, Inter State River Water Disputes Act, 1956, Constitution of India, 1950, Karnataka Cauvery Basic Irrigation Protection Ordinance, 1991

Summary: Water - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 262(2) - Inter State River Water Disputes Act, 1956, ss. 11, 2(c) - Water dispute between States - Jurisdiction of SC to hear appeals in water disputes - Instant matter is with regard to sharing of Cauvery river water among the States of Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Kerala and Union Territory of Puducherry - Instant appeals by States of Karnataka and Tamil Nadu assailing final order dt. 5-2-2007 passed by Tribunal constituted under 1956 Act - Whether art. 262(2) of the Constitution read with s. 11 of the Act bars the jurisdiction of SC to adjudicate upon any water dispute as defined u/s. 2(c) of the Act.

Held, the language used in art.262(1) and s.11 relate to a water dispute or complaint. It pertains to a dispute or a complaint at the pre-adjudicatory stage. Further, after a water dispute is adjudicated, it could not be put under 'any water dispute' and once a water dispute as defined u/art.262(1) read with provisions of the Act is adjudicated by Tribunal, it loses the nature of dispute. After the adjudication, one of the States or both the States could have a grievance but a contention could not be advanced by them or by UOI that the controversy is still at the stage of dispute that has been intended to be covered either u/art.262(1) of the Constitution or under the scheme of the Act. A person aggrieved could always have his remedy invoking the jurisdiction u/art.136 of Constitution and also founding fathers of Constitution had not wanted the Award or final order passed by Tribunal to remain immune from challenge. Further, that is neither the express language of art.262(1) nor it impliedly so states. Hence, contention of UOI that appeals are not maintainable by special leave u/art.136 of Constitution could not be accepted. Further, u/s.6(2) of the Act, Parliament had not intended to create any kind of embargo on the jurisdiction of SC and the provision was inserted to give the binding effect to the award passed by Tribunal. The fiction was created for that limited purpose. S.11 of the Act bars the jurisdiction of the Courts and the same is in consonance with the language employed in art.262 of the Constitution. Hence, contention of UOI that s.6(2) of the Act bars the jurisdiction conferred on SC u/art.136 could not be accepted. As such, appeals are maintainable. Appeals are to be listed on 15-12-2016 for further orders. Matter listed - Order accordingly.
