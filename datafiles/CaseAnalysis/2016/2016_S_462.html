FOR EDUCATIONAL USE ONLY

Kedar Nath Yadav v State of West Bengal and others

Supreme Court of India

31 August 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

V. Gopala Gowda, Arun Mishra

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 595; AIR 2016 SC 4156; 2017 (1) ALD(SC) 96; 2016 (3) CLT(SC) 227; JT 2016 (8) SC 539; 2016 (7) MLJ 419; 2016 (4) RCR(Civil) 255; 2016(8) SCALE 346

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Land & Property

Keywords: Colourable Exercise Of Power, Jurisdiction, Premium, Acquisition Of Land, Accommodation, Private Company, Expenses, Public Purpose, Rental, Land Acquisition Officer, Reports, Land Acquisition (Amendment) Act, 1984, Local Authority, Societies, Investment, Objector, Requisition, Boundaries, Highway, Public Revenues, Land Acquisition (Companies) Rules, 1963, Singur Land Rehabilitation and Development Act, 2011, Constitution of India, 1950, Land Acquisition Act, 1894, Inquiry

Summary: Majority View

(A) Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 142 - Land Acquisition (Amendment) Act, 1984, ss. 4, 6 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 11, 11(1), 18, 3(e), 3(f), 3(f)(iii), 3(f)(iv), 3(f)(vii), 3(f)(viii), 39, 4, 4(1), 40, 41, 5, 55, 5A, 5A(2), 6, 6(1), 9, 9(3) - Singur Land Rehabilitation and Development Act, 2011, ss. 2, 4(3), 5, 6 - Land Acquisition (Companies) Rules, 1963, rr. 3, 4, 5 - Land acquisition - Company purpose - Challenged - State Govt. issued notification for land acquisition u/s. 4 of the 1894 Act to establish automobile industries to cater to the needs of people and solve problem of unemployment - Objection petitions were received from land owners/cultivators after publication of notification - Land Acquisition Collector awarded compensation - West Bengal Industrial Development Corporation (WBIDC) took possession of land in question, and asked respondent/Tata Motors Ltd. (TML) to take "permissive possession of 950 acres of land pending finalization of lease deed and terms and conditions - Subsequently, acquisition proceedings were challenged before HC - DB dismissed writ petitions and upheld acquisition of land holding the same to be in the interest of public and for public purpose - State Govt. and TML went ahead with development of land and setting up of factory for 'Small Car Project' - TML by a letter addressed to Director General of Police, informed that it is suspending operations as the circumstances were no longer conducive for them to work in a peaceful manner and said plant was then relocated to another State - State Govt. enacted a legislation on 20-06-2011 titled the 2011 Act for taking over land covered by lease granted in favour of TML - TML challenged Constitutional validity of the 2011 Act by before HC - Single Judge by an order upheld validity of the 2011Act - On appeals, DB allowed appeals and struck down ss. 2, 4(3), 5 and 6 of the 2011 Act as unconstitutional as they were in direct conflict with the provisions of the 1894 Act and the same had not received assent from President of India - Hence, instant appeals - Whether acquisition of land is for a company and if so procedure provided under Part VII of the 1894 Act is required to be complied with by State Govt.

Held, for the purpose of acquiring land in instant case it was not necessary to have recourse to the provisions contained in Chapter VII of the 1894 Act. WBIDC was associated with the project right from beginning and was instrumental in getting the land identified by TML for the purpose of selection. Non-mention of WBIDC cannot be taken to be an illegality impinging validity of notification u/s. 4 of the 1894 Act. The fact that application was filed by TML indicating its willingness for setting up industry would not also make it an acquisition for a company but how State has dealt with the same, would be the decisive factor. It was not necessary for State Govt. to deposit the amount of compensation as compensation paid by corporation is also to be treated out of public revenue. The aims and objects of the amendment of s. 3(f) of the 1894 Act when taken into consideration would not alter aforesaid position of law. When corporation is the acquiring authority and amount of compensation is borne by it in entirety and land has been ultimately leased out to TML for its project by it the acquisition would remain for a public purpose u/s. 3(f) of the 1894 Act attracting Part II of the 1894 Act. In case cost of acquisition is borne either wholly or partly by the Govt. acquisition can be said to be for a public purpose. If the cost is entirely borne by company then acquisition is for a company under Part VII of the 1894 Act. The procedure adopted under Part II of the 1894 Act cannot be said to be impermissible. It cannot be said to be acquisition under guise of public purpose so as to violate intendment of exclusion of company from s. 3(f) of the 1894 Act as amended. Appeals allowed.

(B) Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 142 - Land Acquisition (Amendment) Act, 1984, ss. 4, 6 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 11, 11(1), 18, 3(e), 3(f), 3(f)(iii), 3(f)(iv), 3(f)(vii), 3(f)(viii), 39, 4, 4(1), 40, 41, 5, 55, 5A, 5A(2), 6, 6(1), 9, 9(3) - Singur Land Rehabilitation and Development Act, 2011, ss. 2, 4(3), 5, 6 - Land Acquisition (Companies) Rules, 1963, rr. 3, 4, 5 - Notification - Inquiry conducted - Validity - Whether the inquiry as contemplated u/s. 5A(2) of the 1894 Act has been duly conducted by Land Acquisition Collector and report of Land Acquisition Collector is based on decision of State Govt. taken prior to issuing notification u/s. 6 of the 1894 Act.

Held, inquiry held and the report sent u/s. 5A of the 1894 Act was influenced by decision of Cabinet taken before issuance of notification u/s. 4 of the 1894 Act to acquire land in certain numbers in particular mouza's as per the choice of location by TML which has prevailed whereas in the matter of acquisition of such vast area comparative fertility aspect of chunk of land to be selected ought to have been considered and land which is more or less barren ought to have been preferred which exercise has not been resorted to. Though State Govt. could have taken decision before issuance of notification u/s. 4 of the 1894 Act for setting up of project however it could not have taken decision to acquire particular land in various mouza's before survey is undertaken as authorized by provisions contained in s. 4 of the 1894 Act, action of State has the effect of frustrating very purpose of holding inquiry u/s. 5A of the 1894 Act. The case need not be relegated to the stage of inquiry as project itself has been abandoned. It is not provided in the 1894 Act that individual notices should be issued. However, for enquiry u/s. 5A of the 1894 Act individual notices are not provided. It is not provided in the 1894 Act that individual notices should be issued. The publications as envisaged u/s. 4 of the 1894 Act are enough and are the only requirement of law to be mandatorily observed pursuant to which objections u/s. 5A of the 1894 Act are required to be filed. Thus non-service of individual notices on farmers would not vitiate the enquiry. Appeals allowed.

(C) Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 142 - Land Acquisition (Amendment) Act, 1984, ss. 4, 6 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 11, 11(1), 18, 3(e), 3(f), 3(f)(iii), 3(f)(iv), 3(f)(vii), 3(f)(viii), 39, 4, 4(1), 40, 41, 5, 55, 5A, 5A(2), 6, 6(1), 9, 9(3) - Singur Land Rehabilitation and Development Act, 2011, ss. 2, 4(3), 5, 6 - Land Acquisition (Companies) Rules, 1963, rr. 3, 4, 5 - Acquisition proceedings - Composite awards - Legality - Whether there is legal effect on acquisition proceedings of not conducting an inquiry u/s. 5A (2) of the Act and passing composite awards u/s. 11 of the 1894 Act.

Held, award has been passed without issuance of notices to holders on pretext that it was not possible to serve them due to prevailing situation. For determination of compensation individual notices are required to be issued. Further, service of personal notice is mandatory as required u/s. 9(3) of the 1894 Act. Non-compliance of provision would render the award invalid requiring determination of compensation afresh at the same time it would not have the effect on validity of notification u/s. 4 of the 1894 Act and declaration made u/s. 6 of the 1894 Act. The award cannot be questioned in writ jurisdiction and non-issuance of individual notices u/s. 9 of the 1894 would not vitiate notification issued u/s. 4 and declaration made u/s. 6 of the 1894 Act. However, fact remains that proper procedure has not been followed in instant matter. The question of adequacy of compensation determined cannot vitiate acquisition. It was also not disputed before SC that after the award was passed on merits, further consent awards were passed in favour of certain persons for which no authority or provision of law could be shown. It would have no impact on validity of notification u/s. 4 of the 1894 Act or declaration made u/s. 6 of the 1894 Act. Therefore, question as to determination of proper compensation cannot be considered in writ jurisdiction as any person aggrieved by inadequacy of compensation has the remedy to seek reference as provided in s. 18 of the 1894 Act. Appeals allowed.

(D) Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 142 - Land Acquisition (Amendment) Act, 1984, ss. 4, 6 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 11, 11(1), 18, 3(e), 3(f), 3(f)(iii), 3(f)(iv), 3(f)(vii), 3(f)(viii), 39, 4, 4(1), 40, 41, 5, 55, 5A, 5A(2), 6, 6(1), 9, 9(3) - Singur Land Rehabilitation and Development Act, 2011, ss. 2, 4(3), 5, 6 - Land Acquisition (Companies) Rules, 1963, rr. 3, 4, 5 - Land acquired - Relief sought - Sustainability - Whether an order can be passed in those proceedings at instant stage.

Held, in instant case it would be appropriate to direct that land is given back to all land owners since they have been deprived of usufruct of land for a decade as such the compensation paid to them shall not be recovered. They are permitted to retain it or claim it in full and final settlement of claim towards damages for deprivation of use of their land etc. Therefore, entire proceedings pertaining to land acquisition are quashed and case is not relegated in instant case to the stage of inquiry u/s. 5A of the 1894 Act as ordinarily resorted to as the very purpose of acquisition has failed and directing an inquiry afresh would be an exercise in futility. The land shall be given back to the land owners and compensation if any paid to them shall not be recovered from them those who have not collected it are free to collect the same in lieu of damages for deprivation of possession for 10 years. Impugned orders of Courts below were set aside. Appeals allowed.

Minority View

(E) Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 142 - Land Acquisition (Amendment) Act, 1984, ss. 4, 6 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 11, 11(1), 18, 3(e), 3(f), 3(f)(iii), 3(f)(iv), 3(f)(vii), 3(f)(viii), 39, 4, 4(1), 40, 41, 5, 55, 5A, 5A(2), 6, 6(1), 9, 9(3) - Singur Land Rehabilitation and Development Act, 2011, ss. 2, 4(3), 5, 6 - Land Acquisition (Companies) Rules, 1963, rr. 3, 4, 5 - Acquisition proceedings - Public purpose - Perverse - Whether the lands involved in these proceedings have been acquired for a public purpose or for TML and whether the procedure provided for under Part VII of the 1894 Act has been complied with by State Govt.

Held, in instant case, what makes acquisition proceedings perverse that the proper procedure as laid down under Part VII of the 1894 Act read with Rules was not followed by State Govt. The acquisition of land for and at the instance of the company was sought to be disguised as acquisition of land for 'public purpose' in order to circumvent compliance with the mandatory provisions of Part VII of the 1894 Act. This action of the State Government is grossly perverse and illegal and void ab initio in law and such an exercise of power by State Govt. for acquisition of lands cannot be allowed under any circumstance. It is clear that acquisition of lands in instant case is for TML. Admittedly, the procedure for acquisition as contemplated u/ss. 39, 40 and 41 of Part VII of the 1894 Act r/w rr. 3, 4 and 5 of the Rules has not been followed, as the acquisition was sought to be guised as one for 'public purpose' u/ss. 3(f) (iii), (iv) and (vii) of the 1894 Act. Therefore acquisition proceedings are liable to be quashed. Further, even after the lands were acquired in its favour, TML could not start operations in accordance with the terms of the lease deed. Thus, it is an undisputed fact that even once the cost of acquisition was borne by WBIDC by way of raising loan from banks, TML did not start operations and held on to possession of land. It did not engage in any other manufacturing activity either. Subsequently, TML also removed the machinery from concerned plant and shifted the same to another State and lands in question have since been resumed by WBIDC. Therefore, question was answered in favour of land owners/cultivators. Appeal allowed.

(F) Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 142 - Land Acquisition (Amendment) Act, 1984, ss. 4, 6 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 11, 11(1), 18, 3(e), 3(f), 3(f)(iii), 3(f)(iv), 3(f)(vii), 3(f)(viii), 39, 4, 4(1), 40, 41, 5, 55, 5A, 5A(2), 6, 6(1), 9, 9(3) - Singur Land Rehabilitation and Development Act, 2011, ss. 2, 4(3), 5, 6 - Land Acquisition (Companies) Rules, 1963, rr. 3, 4, 5 - No individual notices - Composite awards - Challenged - Whether there is legal effect on acquisition proceedings of not conducting an inquiry u/s. 5A (2) of the Act and passing composite awards u/s. 11 of the 1894 Act.

Held, after issuing notifications u/s. 6 of the 1894 Act declaring that lands have been acquired for purpose of industrial development, a statutory duty is cast upon Collector to issue notice to the land owners/cultivators, as required u/s. 9 of the 1894 Act, to determine the market value of acquired land and award compensation as required u/s. 11 of the 1894 Act which is mandatory for taking possession of land by State Govt. However, no individual notices were served upon land owners/cultivators. A joint inquiry appears to have been conducted by Land Acquisition Collector without giving them an adequate opportunity to establish their claim for determination of reasonable compensation for acquisition of lands by presenting true and correct market value of lands. The determination of market value of lands by clubbing a number of cases together and passing a composite award is no award in the eyes of law. The inquiry, as contemplated u/s. 11 of the 1894 Act is a quasi-judicial exercise of power on part of Collector in awarding just and reasonable compensation to landowners/cultivators. That has not been done in instant case. Further, the proviso to s. 11(1) of the 1894 Act provides that no award shall be made by Collector without the previous approval of either appropriate govt. or such officer authorised by it for above purpose. It was also brought to the notice of SC that supplementary awards were also passed which is not legally permissible in law. For non-compliance of above provisions of the 1894 Act, composite awards are vitiated in law and therefore, the same are also liable to be quashed. Thus, question was answered in favour of land owners/cultivators. Appeal allowed.
