Meera Mydeen etc. v State of Tamil Nadu

Supreme Court of India

15 July 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 536; AIR 2016 SC 3759; 2016 ALL MR (Cri) 3587; 2017 CRLJ 1569; JT 2016 (6) SC 559; 2016(7) SCALE 67

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Conspiracy, Motor, Driver, Identification Parade, Test Identification Parade, Doctor, Dead Body, Scene Of Occurrence, Police Custody, Further Investigation, Identification, Interfere, Knife, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Husband, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 161, 164(5), 313 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120, 120B, 147, 148, 149 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellants/accused committed murder of D1 and D2 in retaliation of murder of one person of which D1 was a suspect - Trial Court convicted accused for offences u/s. 302 r/w ss. 149 and 120B IPC and sentenced to undergo life imprisonment - A1 to A3 were also convicted and sentenced to undergo RI for 2 years for the offences punishable u/s. 147 IPC and to undergo imprisonment for life - A2 to A4 were also convicted and sentenced to undergo RI for 3 years for the offences punishable u/s. 148 IPC - On appeal, HC dismissed appeals and upheld conviction and sentence of Trial Court - Hence, instant appeals - Whether there was any perversity or illegality in impugned judgments of Courts below.

Held, Courts below have returned a finding based on the evidence produced on record that conspiracy between appellants to kill deceased stood established. In the instant appeal, it is not for SC to reappreciate evidence, unless it is found that findings were totally perverse. No attempt was made to demonstrate as to how HC had gone wrong in law while arriving at the conclusion on this aspect. Courts below have given convincing reasons after analysing the evidence of certain witnesses that the presence of appellants at the scene of occurrence stood established. Therefore, conviction is primarily based on testimony of witnesses who were found reliable and creditworthy by Trial Court as well as HC. Deposition of PW3, PW6 and other witnesses is considered and convincing reasons are given for arriving at a conclusion that evidence given by these witnesses for not coming forward with their statements at the early stage was quite understandable and natural and the so called delay did not in any way discredit thier credibility. HC also relied on the decision of SC in Dr. Krishna Pal and another v. State of U.P.1995 Indlaw SC 879. Thus, no reason to interfere with the analysis and conclusion of HC. Appeals dismissed.
