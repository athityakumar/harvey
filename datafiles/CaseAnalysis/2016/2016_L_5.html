State of Rajasthan v LBS B. Ed. College and others

Supreme Court of India

08 September 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, C. Nagappan

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 977; AIR 2016 SC 4428; JT 2017 (1) SC 411; 2016(8) SCALE 757; [2016] 5 S.C.R. 298

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Education

Keywords: National Council for Teacher Education Act, 1993, Maharashtra Universities Act, 1994, National Council for Teacher Education (Recognition Norms & Procedure) Regulations, 2009, National Council for Teacher Education (Recognition Norms and Procedure) Regulations, 2014

Summary: Education - Administrative - Maharashtra Universities Act, 1994, ss. 82, 83 - National Council for Teacher Education Act, 1993, ss. 14(6), 32 - National Council for Teacher Education (Recognition Norms & Procedure) Regulations, 2009 - National Council for Teacher Education (Recognition Norms and Procedure) Regulations, 2014 - Grant of recognition - Regulations framed - Permissibility - Appellants/State filed instant appeals challenging impugned order of HC whereby DB allowed students to be admitted for the academic year 2015-2016 subject to fulfillment of the new guidelines issued by National Council for Teacher Education (NCTE) - Whether State Govt. has any say in the matter of grant of recognition to the institutions who apply for establishing institutions to get recognition from NCTE under the 1993 Act and the Regulations.

Held, it appears that what HC wanted to convey was that the provisions of ss. 82 and 83 of the 1994 Act would not apply to an institution covered by the 1993 Act. As per the scheme of the 1993 Act, once recognition was granted by NCTE u/s. 14(6) of the 1993 Act, every university ('examining body') was obliged to grant affiliation to such institution and ss. 82 and 83 of the 1994 Act do not apply to such cases. However, in normal circumstances, the role of State was a very formal one and was not expected to obstruct the commencement of admission process and academic courses once recognition was granted and affiliation was found to be acceptable. Authorities as well as the Regulations framed by NCTE, State has a say might be a limited one. SC was inclined to use the word 'limited' because State's say was not binding on NCTE. However, NCTE was required to take the same into consideration for the State has a vital role to offer proper comments supported by due reasoning. It needs no special emphasis to say that final authority rests with NCTE and was the clear legal position. As the recognition has already been granted, the controversy with regard to the said institutions should stand closed. Further, in future, whenever an application was received under the Regulations for grant of recognition, NCTE should be guided by its own Regulations and judgments of SC and State should remain bound by the principles set out hereinabove. NCTE should take into consideration the recommendations and views of State despite the fact that it has the final say. Appeals disposed of.
