Central Coalfields Limited and another v SLL - SML (Joint Venture Consortium) and others

Supreme Court of India

17 August 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 990; (2016) 8 SCC 622; AIR 2016 SC 3814

Case Digest

Subject: Contract & Commercial

Keywords: Contract, Cheque

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Tender invitation - Bank guarantee format - Dismissal - Appellant no. 1 issued notification inviting tenders (NIT) for Overburden Removal and Coal Extraction - Respondent issued bank guarantee which was not in the prescribed pro format - Appellant no. 1 rejected respondent's bid on ground that documents were incomplete and would not be allowed to participate in price bid opening - Aggrieved, respondent filed writ petition before HC - Single Judge dismissed petition holding that it was necessary to adhere to strict terms of NIT as well as the prescribed format for bank guarantee - On appeal, DB allowed appeal holding that bank guarantee furnished by respondent had stricter terms than bank guarantee prescribed by appellant no. 1 - Hence, instant appeals - Whether furnishing a bank guarantee in the format prescribed in bid documents was an essential requirement in bidding process of appellant no.1.

Held, whether a term of NIT was essential or not was a decision taken by the employer which should be respected. Even if the term was essential, employer has inherent authority to deviate from it provided the deviation was made applicable to all bidders and potential bidders. HC held that bank guarantee in a format not prescribed by it ought to be accepted since that requirement was a non-essential term of GTC. From the point of view of appellant no. 1 GTC was impermissibly re-written by HC. That was hardly conducive to a smooth and hassle-free bidding process. Since, appellant no. 1 had not relaxed or deviated from the requirement of furnishing a bank guarantee in the prescribed format, in so far as instant appeals were concerned every bidder was obliged to adhere to the prescribed format of bank guarantee. Consequently, failure of respondent to furnish bank guarantee in the prescribed format was sufficient reason for appellant no. 1 to reject its bid. There was nothing to indicate that the process by which the decision was taken by appellant no. 1 that bank guarantee furnished by respondent ought to be rejected was flawed in any manner. Similarly, there was nothing to indicate that the decision taken by appellant no. 1 to reject the bank guarantee furnished by respondent and to adhere to the requirements of NIT and GTC was arbitrary or unreasonable or perverse in any manner. Therefore, decision taken by appellant no. 1 to adhere to the terms and conditions of NIT and GTC was certainly not irrational in any manner or intended to favour anyone and the decision was lawful and not unsound. Impugned judgment and order passed by DB was set aside. Appeals allowed.
