Sampelly Satyanarayana Rao v Indian Renewable Energy Development Agency Limited

Supreme Court of India

19 September 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Adarsh Kumar Goel, Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 670; (2016) 10 SCC 458; AIR 2016 SC 4363; 2016 (4) BC 416; [2016] 198 Comp Cas 691; 2016 (4) CompLJ 113; 2016 (5) CTC 563; 2017 (1) DRTC 6; 2016 (2) ILR(Cut) 740; 2016 (4) JCC (NI) 218; JT 2016 (9) SC 235; 2016 (4) KLT 24; 2016 (4) Law Herald (P&H) 3433; 2017 (2) MPLJ 49; 2016 (2) OLR 1085; 2016 (4) RCR(Civil) 487; 2016 (4) RCR(Criminal) 385; 2016(9) SCALE 11

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Jurisdiction, Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, Cheque, Dishonour Of Cheque, Complainant

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, s. 138 - Dishonour of Cheque - Conviction - Sustainability - Respondent/complainant filed complaint u/s. 138 of the Act against appellant/co-accused for issuance of cheques which were dishonored due to insufficient fund - Aggrieved, appellant filed writ petition before HC seeking for quashing of complaints contending that cheques were given by way of security as mentioned in agreement and that on the date cheques were issued no debt or liability was due - HC dismissed petition holding that when post-dated cheques were issued loan was sanctioned and they were cheque issued for a debt in present but payable in future no reason to quash complaints - Hence, instant appeal - Whether dishonour of a post-dated cheque given for repayment of loan installment which is also described as "security" in the loan agreement is covered by s. 138 of the Act.

Held, question whether a post-dated cheque is for "discharge of debt or liability" depends on the nature of transaction. If on the date of cheque liability or debt exists or the amount has become legally recoverable, s. 138 of the Act is attracted and not otherwise. Though the word "security" is used in cl. 3.1(iii) of agreement, the said expression refers to cheques being towards repayment of installments. The repayment becomes due under the agreement, the moment loan is advanced and installment falls due. Once the loan was disbursed and installments have fallen due on the date of cheque as per the agreement, dishonour of such cheques would fall u/s. 138 of the Act. The cheques undoubtedly represent the outstanding liability. Reference to complaint (Annexure P7) shows that as per the case of complainant, cheques which were subject matter of said complaint were towards partial repayment of dues under the loan agreement. Further, while dealing with a quashing petition Court has ordinarily to proceed on the basis of averments in the complaint. The defence of accused cannot be considered at such stage. Court considering the prayer for quashing does not adjudicate upon a disputed question of fact. In Rangappa v. Sri Mohan, 2010 Indlaw SC 344, SC held that once issuance of a cheque and signature thereon are admitted, presumption of a legally enforceable debt in favour of the holder of the cheque arises. It is for accused to rebut the said presumption though accused need not adduce his own evidence and can rely upon the material submitted by complainant. However, mere statement of accused may not be sufficient to rebut the said presumption. A postdated cheque is a well-recognized mode of payment held in Goaplast Pvt. Ltd. v. Shrichico Ursula D'Souza and Another, 2003 Indlaw SC 218. Thus, the question has to be answered in favour of respondent and against appellant. Dishonour of cheque in instant case being for discharge of existing liability is covered by s. 138 of the Act, as rightly held by HC. Appeal dismissed.
