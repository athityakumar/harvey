State of Karnataka v Dattaraj and others

Supreme Court of India

15 February 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Jagdish Singh Khehar, Sharad Arvind Bobde

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 123; AIR 2016 SC 882; 2016 CRLJ 1434; 2016 (2) JCC 967; JT 2016 (2) SC 316; 2016 (2) KarLJ 369; 2016 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 952; 2016 (1) MLJ(Crl) 660; 2016 (1) RCR(Criminal) 1046; 2016 (2) RLW 1573; 2016(2) SCALE 430; [2016] 2 S.C.R. 570

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Cruelty, Harassment, Gold, Dowry, Matrimonial, Clothes, Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 498A, 304B, 304(B)1, 34 - Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961, ss. 3, 4, 6 - Dowry death - Acquittal - Sustainability - Respondent/accused Nos. 1 to 4 were charged for the offences u/ss. 498A, 304B r/w s. 34 IPC and u/ss. 3, 4 and 6 of the 1961 Act - Trial Court convicted all the four accused persons for the offences charged against them - On appeal filed by respondents DB convicted respondent No. 1 and acquitted respondents No. 2 to 4 from all the charges - Hence instant appeal.

Held, insofar as the demand of Rs.20,000/- for the purchase of agricultural land is concerned, it is apparent that the same was allegedly made when respondent No. 1 was in Dubai. The said demand was allegedly made by respondent No. 2, the father of respondent No. 1, when he had gone to leave deceased at her maternal home. Respondent No. 1 is stated to have returned to India from Dubai eight to ten months, after the above demand. A female child was born to deceased about a year after the return of respondent No. 1 to India. After the birth of the female child, deceased had remained in her maternal house, for about four to five months. Therefore, even if the above oral allegation is accepted as correct, it was a demand made about two years before the occurrence. The same was too remote to the occurrence, and therefore, would not satisfy the requirement of 'soon before her death' contemplated u/s. 304B(1) of IPC. The only remaining alleged dowry demand, besides those referred was, that of a sewing-machine. Yet again the position was clarified by PW1. During her cross-examination she stated, that deceased knew tailoring. And that, the sewing-machine was given to her for tailoring clothes. This was really a gift to deceased, and therefore, cannot be considered as a part of the demand made by respondent No. 1, for himself or for his family members. This allegation, in SC's considered view, is inconsequential, with respect to the provisions under which the accused were charged. There was no further attribution, as against the respondents Nos. 2 to 4. It is therefore not possible to accept, that the prosecution was successful in establishing either harassment or violence towards deceased, as against the aforestated accused, nor of any dowry demand. In such view of the matter, it is difficult to conclude the culpability of respondents Nos. 2 to 4, in the entire occurrence. HC was fully justified in recording that even the statements of PW1 and PW4, did not attribute any kind of overt act to respondents Nos. 2 to 4. The HC was, therefore, fully justified in acquitting respondents Nos. 2 to 4, for the offences punishable u/ss. 498A and 304B r/w s. 34 of IPC, as also, for the charges u/ss. 3, 4 and 6 of the 1961 Act. Appeal dismissed.
