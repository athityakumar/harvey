Montecarlo Limited v NTPC Limited

Supreme Court of India

18 October 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Uday Umesh Lalit

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 1066; [2016] 8 S.C.R. 224

Case Digest

Subject: Contract & Commercial

Keywords: Maintenance, Contract

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Invitation for tender - Qualifying requirements - Validity - Respondents issued invitation for bids for development and operation of coal mines - Appellant uploaded proposal by referring to three mines in support of its stand to meet Qualifying Requirements (QR) - Respondent opined that bid of appellant was technically non-responsive - Aggrieved, appellant file writ petition before HC challenging the same - HC dismissed petition holding that decision taken by respondent was correct and did not adversely affect public interest but subserved public purpose - Hence, instant appeal - Whether appellant meets the qualification criteria as provided under the heading Technical Criteria of QR.

Held, HC has considered the documents and opined that documents filed in support of QR were substantially inadequate. Adverting to the facet of drilling, HC opined that there was specific use of the words "drilling for the purposes of blasting". It was also observed that by way of judicial review court cannot examine the details of the terms of contract which was entered into by public bodies or State. Courts have inherent limitations on the scope of any such enquiry. It was common knowledge in competitive commercial field that technical bids pursuant to the notice inviting tenders were scrutinized by technical experts and sometimes third party assistance from those unconnected with owner's organization was taken which ensures objectivity. Bidder's expertise and technical capability and capacity should be assessed by experts. However, tenders where public largesse was put to auction stand on a different compartment. Exercise of power of judicial review would be called for if the approach was arbitrary or malafide or procedure adopted is meant to favour one. The decision making process should clearly show that the said maladies are kept at bay. But where a decision is taken that is manifestly in consonance with the language of tender document or subserves the purpose for which the tender is floated, Court should follow the principle of restraint. Technical evaluation or comparison by Court would be impermissible. The owner should be allowed to carry out the purpose and there has to be allowance of free play in the joints. SC do not perceive any infirmity in the judgment and order passed by HC. Appeal dismissed.
