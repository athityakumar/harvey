Park Street Properties (Private) Limited v Dipak Kumar Singh and another

Supreme Court of India

29 August 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

V. Gopala Gowda, Adarsh Kumar Goel

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 620; (2016) 9 SCC 268; AIR 2016 SC 4038; 2016 (119) ALR 447; 2016 (4) CLT(SC) 174; JT 2016 (8) SC 396; 2016 (4) Law Herald (P&H) 3409; 2017 (2) MahLJ 32; 2017 (1) MPLJ 550; 2016 (2) OLR 633; 2016 (4) RCR(Civil) 243; 2016 (2) RCR(Rent) 297; 2017 (134) RD 127; 2016(8) SCALE 327

Case Digest

Subject: Rent Control

Keywords: Companies Act, 1956, Transfer Of Property Act, 1882, Registration Act, 1908, Suit Premises

Summary: Rent Control - Registration Act, 1908, s. 17(1)(d) - Transfer of Property Act, 1882, ss. 106, 107 - Suit for recovery - Possession - Validity - Appellant entered into an agreement dt. 15-10-2004 with respondents subletting suit premises for the purpose of carrying out business - Appellant entered into an unregistered agreement dt. 07-08-2006 with respondents for tenancy of suit premises commenced from 01-08-2006, at a rent of Rs. 20,000/- per month - On 30-10-2008, appellant issued a notice u/s. 106 of the 1882 Act terminating the monthly tenancy of respondents - However, respondents did not vacate suit premises - Appellant filed suit for recovery of possession and mesne profits of suit premises before Trial Court - Trial Court decreed suit - On appeal, HC allowed appeal and remanded suit back to Trial Court for reconsideration from the stage of examining the question of validity of notice dt. 30-10-2008 - Hence, instant appeal - Whether the agreement dt. 07-08-2006 can be read in evidence and it is a contract to contrary in terms of s. 106 of the 1882 Act.

Held, in the absence of registration of a document, what is deemed to be created is a month to month tenancy, the termination of which is governed by s. 106 of the 1882 Act. The question of remanding matter back to Trial Court to consider it afresh in view of the fact that the same has been admitted in evidence, as HC has done in the impugned judgment and order, does not arise. While agreement dt. 07-08-2006 can be admitted in evidence and even relied upon by the parties to prove the factum of tenancy, the terms of the same cannot be used to derogate from the statutory provision of s. 106 of the 1882 Act which creates a fiction of tenancy in absence of a registered instrument creating the same. If contention of respondents is taken to its logical conclusion, such lease can never be terminated, save in cases of breach by tenant. Accepting such contention would mean that in a situation where the tenant does not default on rent payment for three consecutive months, or does not commit a breach of the terms of the lease, it is not open to the lessor to terminate the lease even after giving a notice. Such interpretation of cl. 6 of agreement cannot be permitted as the same is wholly contrary to the express provisions of the law. The phrase 'contract to the contrary' in s. 106 of the 1882 Act cannot be read to mean that parties are free to contract out of express provisions of the law thereby defeating its very intent. Therefore, contract between the parties must be in relation to a valid contract for the statutory right u/s. 106 of the 1882 Act available to a lessor to terminate the tenancy at a notice of 15 days to not be applicable. Impugned judgment and order passed by HC was set aside. Appeal allowed.

Ratio - In absence of a registered instrument, Courts are not precluded from determining the factum of tenancy from the other evidence on record as well as the conduct of the parties.
