Sasan Power Limited v North American Coal Corporation India Private Limited

Supreme Court of India

24 August 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

J. Chelameswar, A.M. Sapre

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 625; (2016) 10 SCC 813; AIR 2016 SC 3974; 2017 (1) CompLJ 10; 2016 (5) CTC 434; 2016 (7) MLJ 306

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR; Corporate

Keywords: Arbitration Agreement, Arbitral Award, Arbitral Tribunal, Conflict Of Laws, Jurisdiction, Companies Act, 1956, Contract, CONTRACT & COMMERCIAL, Specific Relief Act, 1963, Transfer Of Property Act, 1882, Indian Contract Act, 1872, Indian Evidence Act, 1872, Arbitration And Conciliation Act, 1996, Arbitration, Arbitration Clause, Foreign Awards (Recognition And Enforcement) Act, 1961, Conciliation, Arbitration (Protocol And Convention) Act, 1937, Assignment, Novation Of Contract, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908

Summary: J. Chelameswar, J.

(A) Corporate - Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration (Protocol and Convention) Act, 1937 - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, ss. 2(2), 44, 45, 8, 85, 9 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 2 r. 2, O. 39 r. 4, O. 7 r. 11, O. 7 r. 11(d) - Companies Act, 1956, s. 4 - Foreign Awards (Recognition and Enforcement) Act, 1961 - Indian Contract Act, 1872, ss. 23, 28(a), 73 - Agreement - Disputes arose - Arbitration - Appellant/company and an American company entered into an agreement dt.1-1-2009 for mine and development operations (AGREEMENT-I) - On 1-4-2011, appellant, American company and respondent subsidiary of American company entered into an agreement (AGREEMENT-II) by said agreement, American company purported to assign all its rights and obligations with the consent of appellant to respondent - Disputes arose between parties - Respondent purported to terminate AGREEMENT-I and made a request for arbitration on 08-08-2014 - Appellant filed suit before Trial Court seeking declaration that request for Arbitration dt. 08-08-2014 is null and void - Trial Court passed an ex-parte order injuncting respondent from proceeding with arbitration - Respondent filed two applications under O. 7 r. 11(d) CPC r/w s. 45 of the 1996 Act praying that dispute be referred to arbitration and vacation of injunction order which was allowed - Aggrieved, appellant filed writ petition before HC challenging impugned order which was dismissed - Hence, instant appeal - Whether it is permissible under the consolidated Indian law of arbitration for two Indian Companies to agree to refer their commercial disputes to a binding arbitration, with place of arbitration outside India, and with governing law being English law.

Held, American company legally cannot claim to have been discharged from the obligations arising under AGREEMENT-I and infact has not been discharged. On the other hand, appellant by an express covenant under AGREEMENT-II retained its rights to enforce obligations (arising under AGREEMENT-I) against American company. AGREEMENT-II perhaps only creates an agency where American company is the principal and respondent its agent or what is described in some cases as sub-contracting or an arrangement for "vicarious performance". The transaction covered by AGREEMENT II is not an assignment. Adjudication of dispute raised by respondent in arbitration would necessarily involve examination of the rights and obligations of American company under AGREEMENT-I and AGREEMENT-II. Therefore, it is a dispute between three parties (of which one is an American company) with a foreign element i.e. rights and obligations of American company. Hence, the stipulation regarding governing law cannot be said to be an agreement between only two Indian companies. The tenor and content and the fact that representatives of three companies signed the document cannot be ignored simply on the basis of an uninformed concession made at the bar. Therefore, question whether two Indian companies could enter into an agreement to be governed by the laws of another country would not arise in instant case. So long as the obligations arising under the AGREEMENT-I subsists and the American company is not discharged of its obligations under the AGREEMENT-I, there is a 'foreign element' therein and the dispute arising therefrom. The autonomy of the parties in such a case to choose the governing law is well recognised in law. Appeal dismissed.

(B) Corporate - Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration (Protocol and Convention) Act, 1937 - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, ss. 2(2), 44, 45, 8, 85, 9 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 2 r. 2, O. 39 r. 4, O. 7 r. 11, O. 7 r. 11(d) - Companies Act, 1956, s. 4 - Foreign Awards (Recognition and Enforcement) Act, 1961 - Indian Contract Act, 1872, ss. 23, 28(a), 73 - Suit for arbitration - Barred - Legality - Whether suit filed by appellant is maintainable or barred by s. 45 of the 1996 Act as contended by respondents or any other provisions of the 1996 Act or any other law.

Held, history and development of law of arbitration in this Country was very succinctly captured by SC in Bharat Aluminium Company v. Kaiser Aluminium Technical Services Inc. etc., 2012 Indlaw SC 297. It traced out the origin and development of not only the domestic law of arbitration in India but also the international arrangements regarding arbitration agreements and awards made in one country but sought recognition or enforcement in another Country. Part-II of the 1996 Act is headed "Enforcement of Certain Foreign Awards". It contains provisions (ss. 44 to 60 of the 1996 Act divided into two Chapters I and II) dealing with the enforcement of foreign awards falling under two categories i.e. New York Convention Award and Geneva Convention Awards. Such foreign awards are deemed to be a decree of a Court. The other provisions deal with matters incidental to the enforcement of foreign awards. It is significant to note that Part II does not deal with any matter pertaining to any step anterior to the making of an (foreign) arbitral award. The case of appellant has been that in view of assignment under AGREEMENT-II, dispute becomes purely a dispute between two Indian companies. Therefore, any arbitration agreement between such companies cannot be an agreement to which (New York) Convention set forth in the First Schedule of the 1996 Act applies. If such Convention does not apply, the question of application of s. 45 of the 1996 Act does not arise. Appeal dismissed.

(C) Corporate - Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration (Protocol and Convention) Act, 1937 - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, ss. 2(2), 44, 45, 8, 85, 9 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 2 r. 2, O. 39 r. 4, O. 7 r. 11, O. 7 r. 11(d) - Companies Act, 1956, s. 4 - Foreign Awards (Recognition and Enforcement) Act, 1961 - Indian Contract Act, 1872, ss. 23, 28(a), 73 - Arbitration agreement - Stipulation - Validity - Whether arbitration agreement in Cl. XII was invalid and void for being in breach of Cl. (a) of s. 28 of the 1872 Act and also void because of the provisions of s. 23 of the 1872 Act and hence not referable to arbitration u/s. 45 of the 1996 Act.

Held, appellant's case as evidenced by the plaint in its suit is that parts of the AGREEMENT-I though created valid rights and obligations between the (original) parties thereto ceased to be valid subsequent to assignment under AGREEMENT-II. Since parties to AGREEMENT-II are only two companies incorporated in India. They could not have agreed that the governing law of agreement should be the law of United Kingdom. Further, such a stipulation in the agreement would be contrary to the public policy and hit by s. 23 of the 1872 Act. Therefore, arbitration agreement initiated by respondent cannot be proceeded with. Arbitration agreement/clause is not that governs rights and obligations arising out of the substantive contract: It only governs the way of settling disputes between the parties. Therefore, scope of enquiry u/s. 45 of the 1996 Act is confined only to the question whether arbitration agreement is "null and void, inoperative or incapable of being performed" but not the legality and validity of the substantive contract. Scope of enquiry u/s. 45 of the 1996 Act does not extend to the examination of legality of substantive contract. Thus, relief claimed by appellant is for decree of declaration "against the defendant", respondent that art. XII of AGREEMENT-I is "null and void, inoperative and unenforceable". Appeal dismissed.

Abhay Manohar Sapre, J.

(D) Corporate - Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration (Protocol and Convention) Act, 1937 - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, ss. 2(2), 44, 45, 8, 85, 9 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 2 r. 2, O. 39 r. 4, O. 7 r. 11, O. 7 r. 11(d) - Companies Act, 1956, s. 4 - Foreign Awards (Recognition and Enforcement) Act, 1961 - Indian Contract Act, 1872, ss. 23, 28(a), 73 - Execution of agreement - Substitution - Validity - Whether Courts below were justified in allowing the application filed by respondent u/s. 45 of the 1996 Act r/w O. 7 r. 11 CPC in a civil suit filed by appellant for declaration and in consequence were justified in dismissing appellant's suit.

Held, execution of Agreement-II has not resulted in substituting or rescinding or extinguishing Agreement-I. On the other hand, it recognized the existence of Agreement-I and resulted in its amendment by adding some new clauses and one party. It is not possible to hold that as a result of execution of Agreement-II, novation of contract has come in to be inter se parties.

Mere reading of s. 45 of the 1996 Act would go to show that use of the words "shall" and "refer the parties to arbitration" in the section makes it legally obligatory on Court to refer the parties to arbitration once it finds that agreement in question is neither null and void nor inoperative and nor incapable of being performed. In other words, once it is found that agreement in question is a legal and valid agreement, which is capable of being performed by the parties to the suit, Court has no discretion but to pass an order by referring parties to arbitration in terms of the agreement. In instant case Trial Court though allowed the application filed by respondent u/s. 45 of the 1996 Act by recording the findings that agreement in question is legal and proper and capable of being performed but it did not pass any consequential order as required u/s. 45 of the 1996 Act by referring the parties to arbitration in terms of s. 12.2(a) and instead simply dismissed the suit as not maintainable. Thus, order needs a modification to this extent only so as to make the order in conformity with the requirement of s. 45 of the 1996 Act. Appeal dismissed.
