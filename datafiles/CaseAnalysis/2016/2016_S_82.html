Sayyed Ratanbhai Sayeed (D) and others v Shirdi Nagar Panchayat and others

Supreme Court of India

22 February 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Amitava Roy, V. Gopala Gowda

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 157; (2016) 4 SCC 631; AIR 2016 SC 1042; 2016 (2) Bom.C.R. 758; JT 2016 (2) SC 377; 2016 (2) MLJ 590; 2016(2) SCALE 486

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Land & Property

Keywords: Compromise Decree, Jurisdiction, CONSTITUTION, Accommodation, Access, Bombay Provincial Municipal Corporations Act, 1949, Failure, Highway, Maharashtra Regional and Town Planning Act, 1966, Maharashtra Housing and Area Development Act, 1976, Bombay Village Panchayats Act, 1958, City of Nagpur Corporation Act, 1948, Maharashtra Zilla Parishads and Panchayat Samitis Act, 1961, Maharashtra Slum Areas (Improvement, Clearance and Redevelopment) Act, 1971, Nagpur Improvement Trust Act, 1936, City of Nagpur Municipal Corporation Act, 1948, Maharashtra Municipal Councils Nagar Panchayats and Industrial Townships Act, 1965, Bombay Highways Act, 1955, Constitution of India, 1950

Summary: Land & Property - Administrative - Bombay Highways Act, 1955 - Development plan in public interest - Eviction of shopkeepers - Justification - In view of pilgrimage center in small village, small small shops grew around temple, catering to essentials of worshippers for their offerings and also their refreshments and conveniences - Plot in question belonged to Gram Panchayat which was out to appellants shop keepers on rent for carrying on their trades - It was alleged that respondents i.e. temple authority and State authority tried to forcibly evict tenants for plot in question - However, compromise was entered between parties where it was agreed that temple authority would arrange temporary accommodation of appellant in plot and would construct shopping complex - However, respondent authority failed to comply with terms entered in compromise - Further, Development Plan was sanctioned by notification where plot in question was reserved for garden and for shopping center - Further, Nagar Panchayat started demolishing shops of appellant - Executing Court directed maintenance of status-quo of subject matter of execution proceedings in view of ongoing demolition drive resorted to by State - Further, Executing Court vide order held that compromise decree was binding and executable, and respondents were bound to provide temporary accommodation to appellants till completion of construction work in suit land and consequently restrained respondents from removing or demolishing shops of appellants till their temporary adjustment in plot in terms of decree - Further, matter was remanded by HC to Executing Court Executing Court following remand, vide order dt. 9-3-2009 held that decree had become inexecutable in face of irreversible intervening events - Further, HC in order to strike balance between two competing interests granted compensation to shopkeepers which will be paid by respondent State and temple authority - Further, respondent authority were allowed to continue its development plan and star project of widening roads - Order passed by HC was affirmed by SC - Whether impugned order passed by HC in favor of respondent is justified and in accordance with law.

Held, compromise decree did not declare appellants' title in land and it vested in State Govt. SC held that decree only protected their occupation of site in possession till they were rehabilitated in proposed shopping complex to come up in future. Further, failure of respondent temple authority to construct shopping complex as undertaken under compromise decree, ipso facto would not insulate appellants from mandate of relevant statutes in force to test legality or otherwise of structures existing allegedly in violation thereof. Further, SC held that in all, having regard to progression of events after compromise decree, contraventions alleged and initiatives proposed in preponderate public interest, hence, it cannot be said that that compromise decree is still executable. Further, Municipal Council was well within its competence and authority as planning authority to issue notice to appellant being of opinion that steps advised therein, were essential for implementation of development plan, already prepared and finalized, for progress and advancement of area. Further, taking into consideration hardship suffered by appellants, SC directed State and its functionaries to undertake exercise to identify suitable site to accommodate appellants. However, in case endeavour to identify alternative plot does not yield any result inspite of sincere efforts, appellants would then be entitled to adequate monetary compensation and lump sum of Rs. 20 lakhs and Rs. 15 lakhs each respectively for bigger and smaller shops/stalls would be reasonable palliative to appellants which would be equally borne by State Govt. and respondent temple authority. Appeals dismissed.

Ratio - Where private interest is pitted against public interest, Court has to give importance to public interest for collective good of society.
