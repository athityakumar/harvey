Mairembam Prithviraj @ Prithviraj Singh v Pukhrem Sharatchandra Singh

Supreme Court of India

28 October 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

L. Nageswara Rao, Anil R. Dave

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 826; (2017) 2 SCC 487; AIR 2016 SC 5087; 2017 (3) ALD(SC) 79; JT 2016 (10) SC 361; 2017 (1) MLJ 20; 2016 (4) RCR(Civil) 978; 2016(10) SCALE 403; [2016] 9 S.C.R. 687

Case Digest

Subject: Election

Keywords: Fundamental Right, Constituency, Dismissed, Agent, MUNICIPALITIES & LOCAL GOVERNMENTS, Material Facts, Result Of The Election, Vote, Rejection, Nomination Paper, Candidate, Rejected, Representation Of The People Act, 1951, Substantial Character, Proving, Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961, Constitution of India, 1950

Summary: Civil Appeal No. 2649 of 2016

(A) Election - Representation of the People Act, 1951, ss. 100, 100(1)(a), 100(1)(d), 100(1)(d)(i), 100(1)(d)(iv), 101, 125A, 127, 33, 33A, 36(4), 53(2), 80(A), 9A - Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961, r. 4(A) - Election - False declaration - Void - Respondent filed writ petition before HC challenging election of appellant seeking a declaration that appellant's election was null and void and that a criminal proceeding should be initiated against appellant u/ss. 125A and 127 of the Act - HC allowed petition holding that appellant's election to Legislative Assembly from the constituency was declared as void - Hence, instant appeals - Whether a false declaration relating to educational qualification was a defect of substantial character warranting rejection of a nomination.

Held, contention of appellant that the declaration relating to his educational qualification in affidavit was a clerical error cannot be accepted. It was not an error committed once. Since 2008, appellant was making the statement that he has an MBA degree. The information provided by him in the affidavit filed in Form 26 would amount to a false declaration. The said false declaration cannot be said to be a defect which was not substantial. He was given an opportunity by Returning Officer to produce relevant document in support of his declaration. At least at that point of time he should have informed Returning Officer that an error crept into the declaration. He did not do so. The false declaration relating to his educational qualification cannot be stated to be not of a substantial character. It was no more res integra that every candidate has to disclose his educational qualification to subserve the right to information of voter. Having made a false declaration relating to his educational qualification, appellant cannot be permitted to contend that declaration was not of a substantial character. Thus, SC uphold findings recorded by HC that the false declaration relating to educational qualification made by appellant was substantial in nature. Appeals dismissed.

(B) Election - Representation of the People Act, 1951, ss. 100, 100(1)(a), 100(1)(d), 100(1)(d)(i), 100(1)(d)(iv), 101, 125A, 127, 33, 33A, 36(4), 53(2), 80(A), 9A - Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961, r. 4(A) - Returned candidate - Nomination - Declaration - Whether it was necessary to plead and prove that result was materially affected when nomination of returned candidate was found to have been improperly accepted when there were only two candidates contesting election.

Held, it was not necessary for respondent to prove that result of election in so far as it concerns returned candidate was materially affected by improper acceptance of his nomination as there were only two candidates contesting election and if appellant's nomination was declared to have been improperly accepted, his election would have to be set aside without any further enquiry and the only candidate left in the fray was entitled to be declared elected. Mere finding that there was an improper acceptance of nomination was not sufficient for a declaration that election was void u/s. 100(1)(d) of the Act. There has to be further pleading and proof that result of election of returned candidate was materially affected. However, there would be no necessity of any proof in the event of nomination of a returned candidate being declared as having been improperly accepted, especially in a case where there were only two candidates in the fray. If returned candidate's nomination was declared to have been improperly accepted it would mean that he could not have contested the election and that the result of election of returned candidate was materially affected need not be proved further. Thus, there was no requirement to prove that the result of election of returned candidate was materially affected once his nomination is declared to have been improperly accepted. Appeals dismissed.

Civil Appeal No. 2829 of 2016

(C) Election - Representation of the People Act, 1951, ss. 100, 100(1)(a), 100(1)(d), 100(1)(d)(i), 100(1)(d)(iv), 101, 125A, 127, 33, 33A, 36(4), 53(2), 80(A), 9A - Conduct of Elections Rules, 1961, r. 4(A) - Relief of elected - Denial - Jurisdiction - Appellant filed instant appeals challenging impugned order of HC wherein appellant sought for a relief that he should be declared to have been elected was rejected.

Held, u/s. 80(A) of the Act, HC would have jurisdiction to try an election petition. It was well settled law that HC hearing an election petition was not an 'authority' and that it remains HC while trying an election petition under the Act. Further, jurisdiction of HC to try an election petition was not by way of constituting a special jurisdiction and conferring it upon HC. It was an extension of original jurisdiction of HC to hear and decide the election disputes. It was clear that the inherent power of HC was not taken away when election disputes were adjudicated. S. 53(2) of the Act was a power conferred on Returning Officer to declare a candidate elected when the number of candidates was equal to the number of seats to be filled. The power of HC was not fettered by s. 53(2) of the Act. HC has taken into consideration an anomalous situation that would arise by a candidate belonging to one party being declared elected after having crossed the floor. Thus, SC was in agreement with HC and do not intend to interfere with the discretion exercised by HC. Appeals dismissed.
