Anjan Dasgupta v State of West Bengal and others

Supreme Court of India

25 November 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Ashok Bhushan, Pinaki Chandra Ghose

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 858; AIR 2016 SC 5510; 2017 CRLJ 529; JT 2016 (11) SC 294; 2017 (1) RCR(Criminal) 52; 2016(12) SCALE 311

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: FIR, Value, Appreciation, Spot, Place Of Occurrence, Dead Body, Inquest Report, Arms Act, 1959, Contradiction, Registered, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 212, 302, 34 - Murder - Reversing order of acquittal to conviction by HC - Deceased was shot with pipe gun and he succumbed to bullet injuries - FIR was duly registered u/s. 302/34 of IPC and ss.25/27 of the Act naming 4 persons as accused and after completion of investigation, charge sheet was filed against accused for offence u/s.302 read with s.34 of IPC and additionally another accused was charged for offence u/s. 212 of IPC - Upon perusal of evidence, Trial Court acquitted accused persons from charge of s.302/34 of IPC and Biswanath Paul for offence u/s. 212 of IPC - Aggrieved, State filed appeal and complainant also filed revisional application challenging order of acquittal - Upon re-appreciating evidence, HC vide its judgment dt.16-2-2006 set aside order of acquittal as regards appellant and another accused but confirmed acquittal with regard to other accused persons - Hence, instant appeal by appellant- Anjan Dasgupta challenging order of conviction and sentence passed against him - Whether order of conviction of appellant could be upheld.

Held, the information of murder was received before 17:35 hrs at police station which is fully proved by arrival of police officers much before 17:40 hrs as proved by IO. As such, mention of the time of 17:35 could be treated as the time of receipt of the information of the offence in the police station and hence, there are no inconsistencies in the FIR to conclude that FIR was ante-timed. Further, where recording of FIR on 16-6-2000 itself was proved and accepted by Trial Court, mere dispatch of FIR on 22-6-2000 from the police station to the Magistrates Court would have no bearing on basis of which any adverse presumption could be drawn. Hence, FIR was genuine and Trial Court committed an error in drawing adverse inference against prosecution and in refusing to attach value to FIR. Further, appreciation of evidence of eye-witnesses and discarding evidences by Trial court was on flimsy ground and based on surmises and conjectures which was correctly re-appreciated by HC. Under the circumstances, minor contradiction pointed out by Trial Court in evidence of other eye-witnesses were rightly been discarded by HC and HC after re-appreciating evidence has rightly concluded that occurrence as well as participation of appellant was proved. Hence, findings and conclusion recorded by HC are based on correct appreciation of evidence and do not suffer from any error and so, judgment of HC reversing acquittal recorded by Trial Court could not be interfered with. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - The receipt and the recording of FIR is not a condition precedent for setting in motion of a criminal investigation and delay in forwarding FIR to court is not fatal in a case in which investigation has commenced promptly on its basis. Further, in case where an order of acquittal has been made on improper and erroneous appreciation of evidence, court of appeal could make proper and reasonable appreciation evidence and differ from the order of acquittal and in such event, court could reverse the same.
