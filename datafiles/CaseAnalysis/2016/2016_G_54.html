Anita Thakur and others v Government of Jammu and Kashmir and others

Supreme Court of India

12 August 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 613; 2016 (4) ACC 109; AIR 2016 SC 3803; JT 2016 (8) SC 359; 2016 (3) RCR(Criminal) 877; 2016(7) SCALE 725; [2016] 4 S.C.R. 50

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Human Rights, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973

Summary: Practice & Procedure - Constitution - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 143, 144 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 19, 19(1)(a), 19(1)(b), 19(1)(c), 21, 22, 32 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 141, 268 - Peaceful protest - Manhandled - Challenged - Petitioners/migrants had planned to take out a peaceful protest march upto State for ventilating their grievances - However, when they reached near a place respondent authorities through their police personnel had beaten up and manhandled these migrants in a most brutal and barbaric manner on 07-08-2007 - Petitioners filed instant writ petition alleging that such incident has violated their rights guaranteed to them u/arts. 14, 19, 21 and 22 of Constitution and sought for taking criminal action against erring officials, including respondent nos. 3 to 9, and to pay compensation to each of the petitioners and other migrants who suffered serious injuries, in the sum of Rs. 10 lakhs.

Held, holding peaceful demonstration in order to air their grievances and to see that their voice is heard in the relevant quarters is the right of people. Such a right can be traced to fundamental freedom that is guaranteed u/arts. 19(1)(a), 19(1)(b) and 19(1)(c) of Constitution. Further, initially it was petitioners who took the law into their hands by turning their peaceful agitation into a violent one and in the process becoming unruly and petting stones at the police. On the other hand, even police personnel continued the use of force beyond limits after they had controlled the mob. They continued to beat up petitioners even after overpowering them. They had virtually apprehended these petitioners making them immobile. However, their attack on petitioners continued even thereafter when it was not at all needed. As far as injuries suffered by petitioners are concerned, such a situation could clearly be avoided. It is apparent that to that extent, respondents misused their power. To that extent, fundamental right of petitioners, due to police excess, has been violated. SC can award compensation to petitioners. First, it is clear that a violation of fundamental rights due to police misconduct can give rise to a liability under public law, apart from criminal and tort law. Secondly, that pecuniary compensation can be awarded for such a violation of fundamental rights. Thirdly, it is State that is held liable and compensation is borne by State and not individual police officers found guilty of misconduct. Fourthly, SC has held that standard of proof required for proving police misconduct such as brutality, torture and custodial violence and for holding State accountable for the same, is high. It is only for patent and incontrovertible violation of fundamental rights that such remedy can be made available. Fifthly, doctrine of sovereign immunity does not apply to cases of fundamental rights violation and hence cannot be used as a defence in public law. SC awarded compensation of Rs. 2,00,000 to petitioner no.1 and Rs. 1,00,000 each to petitioner nos. 2 and 3. Petition disposed of.
