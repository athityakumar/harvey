Brajendra Singh Yambem v Union of India and another

Supreme Court of India

26 August 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

V. Gopala Gowda, Anil R. Dave, C. Nagappan

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 624; (2016) 9 SCC 20; AIR 2016 SC 4107; 2017 (3) AWC 2514

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Defence & Security Forces

Keywords: Retirement, Narcotic Drugs And Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985, Ammunition, Central Civil Services (Pension) Rules, 1972, Missing, Central Civil Services (Classification, Control and Appeal) Rules, 1965, Central Civil Services (Conduct) Rules, 1964, Constitution of India, 1950

Summary: (A) Constitution - Defence & Security Forces - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 142, 166(2), 166(3), 226, 309, 32, 77(2), 77(3) - Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985 - Central Civil Services (Classification, Control and Appeal) Rules, 1965, r. 14 - Central Civil Services (Conduct) Rules, 1964, rr. 3(1)(i), 3(1)(iii) - Central Civil Services (Pension) Rules, 1972, rr. 9, 9(2), 9(2)(a), 9(2)(b)(i), 9(2)(b)(ii) - Contraband articles and rifle - Disciplinary proceedings - Challenged - Appellant was serving as a regular Commandant of 61st Battalion, CRPF - One AK-47 rifle ammunition issued in the name of one bahadur, who was posted at the same battalion of which appellant was commandant went missing - Departmental inquiry u/r. 14 of the 1965 Rules was ordered in connection with the said incident - In the meantime, another set of disciplinary proceedings was initiated against appellant in connection with the arrest of 11 personnel and seizure of two trucks of the unit of appellant carrying contraband ganja - Aggrieved, appellant filed writ petition before HC - Single Judge allowed writ petition and granted liberty to Disciplinary Authority to initiate departmental enquiry afresh against appellant after complying with the directions given in judgment - On appeals, DB allowed appeals and set aside order of Single Judge and directed Enquiry Officer to hold departmental enquiry against appellant and conclude the same after affording adequate opportunity of hearing to him in the enquiry proceedings - Hence, instant appeals - Whether impugned judgment and order passed by DB correctly appreciates the scope of r. 9(2)(b)(ii) of the 1972 Rules in light of fact that disciplinary proceedings were initiated more than 4 years after alleged incidents.

Held, order of sanction granted by President is not in exercise of his executive power u/art. 77(2) of Constitution which speaks of orders and other instruments made and executed in the name of President. The Rules specified u/art. 77(3) of Constitution are rules framed by President for transaction of business of Govt. The said Constitutional immunity conferred either upon Governor or President is confined only to the executive action of appropriate Govt. The order of sanction to be granted by President as provided u/r. 9(2)(b) (i) of the 1972 Rules is for initiation of disciplinary proceedings against appellant, which cannot be treated as an executive action of Govt.. It is a statutory exercise of power by President, u/r. 9(2)(b)(i) of the 1972 Rules. The said Rules are framed by President in exercise of legislative power conferred u/art. 309 of Constitution. Further, the same has no application to the fact situation for the reason that President has exercised his statutory power for grant of sanction u/r. 9(2)(b)(i) of the 1972 Rules to initiate disciplinary action but not the executive action against appellant. In instant case, action of Disciplinary Authority is untenable in law for the reason that interpretation of the 1972 Rules which is sought to be made by respondents amounts to deprivation of Fundamental Rights guaranteed to appellant under Part III of Constitution. Therefore, SC holds that the disciplinary proceedings initiated by disciplinary authority after obtaining sanction from President u/r. 9(2)(b)(i) of the 1972 Rules are liable to be quashed. Appeals partly allowed.

(B) Defence & Security Forces - Disciplinary proceedings - Relief - Whether impugned judgment and order of HC was erroneous and is vitiated in law.

Held, DB erred in allowing appeal and the same was liable to be set aside. SC directs Disciplinary Authority to continue disciplinary proceedings and conclude them within 6 months in accordance with the relevant provisions of law as well as the principles of natural justice. If the same are not completed within the said time period by the disciplinary authority, the said liberty granted by SC in this order to respondents will not ensue to their benefit. Appeals partly allowed.
