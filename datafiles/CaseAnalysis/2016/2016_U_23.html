Extra Judicial Execution Victim Families Association (EEVFAM) and another v Union of India and another

Supreme Court of India

08 July 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, Uday Umesh Lalit

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 525; AIR 2016 SC 3400; 2016 CRLJ 3769; JT 2016 (7) SC 593; 2016 (4) MLJ(Crl) 675; [2016] 4 S.C.R. 909

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Human Rights; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Army Act, 1950, Commissions Of Inquiry Act, 1952, Constitution (Forty-Second Amendment) Act, 1976, Prevention Of Terrorism Act, 2002, Air Force Act, 1950, Constitution (Forty-Fourth Amendment) Act, 1978, Maharashtra Control Of Organised Crime Act, 1999, Protection Of Human Rights Act, 1993, The United Nations, Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act, 1967, Criminal Courts and Court Martial (Adjustment of Jurisdiction) Rules, 1978, Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act, 1958, Criminal Courts and Court Martial (Adjustment of Jurisdiction) Rules, 1952, Armed Forces (Assam and Manipur) Special Powers Act, 1958, Armed Forces (Jammu and Kashmir) Special Powers Act, 1990, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973

Summary: (A) Criminal - Human Rights - Practice & Procedure - Army Act, 1950, ss. 122, 125, 126, 3(x), 69, 70 - Commissions of Inquiry Act, 1952 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 129, 130, 131, 132, 144, 176, 197, 4, 46, 5 - Extra-judicial executions - Legality - Petitioners/association has compiled 1528 alleged extra-judicial executions carried out by police and security forces in State - Petitioners allege that not a single FIR was registered against police or security forces even though several complaints have been made in respect of extra-judicial executions - Petitioners further allege that National Human Rights Commission (NHRC) which is mandated to investigate human rights abuses and recommend punishment of guilty is defunct due to non-appointment of members and non-allocation of resources despite an order of HC - Hence, instant writ petition - Whether to quell internal disturbance, has there been use of excessive force by Police and armed forces in 1528 cases compiled by petitioners through fake encounters or extra-judicial executions. Held, an allegation or complaint of absence of a reasonable connection between an official act and use of excessive force or retaliatory force will not be countenanced and an allegation of this nature would always require to be met regardless of whether State is concerned with a dreaded criminal or a militant, terrorist or insurgent. It should be held that to provide assurance to people such an allegation should be thoroughly enquired into. This is the requirement of a democracy and requirement of preservation of rule of law and preservation of individual liberties. Thus, view taken by Commission was that all these persons were killed in a fake encounter or that force used against them was excessive. Under these circumstances, SC do not wish to comment on nature of internal enquiry conducted by respondents but only record that these cases apparently never reached Human Rights Division of Army or Ministry of Defence. Order accordingly.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Army Act, 1950, ss. 122, 125, 126, 3(x), 69, 70 - Commissions of Inquiry Act, 1952 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 129, 130, 131, 132, 144, 176, 197, 4, 46, 5 - Retaliatory force - Permissibility - Whether use of force by armed forces been retaliatory to the point of causing death and was retaliatory force permissible in law on ground that victims were 'enemy' as defined in s. 3(x) of the 1950 Act.

Held, none of the victims examined had any criminal antecedents or that there was any credible evidence to show that they had affiliations with a banned or unlawful organization. If an offence is committed even by Army personnel, there is no concept of absolute immunity from trial by criminal Court constituted under CrPC. To contend that this would have a deleterious and demoralizing impact on security forces is certainly one way of looking at it, but from the point of view of a citizen, living under the shadow of a gun that can be wielded with impunity, outright acceptance of proposition advanced is equally unsettling and demoralizing, particularly in a constitutional democracy like ours. The result of interplay between ss. 4, 5 CrPC and ss.125 and 126 of the 1950 Act makes it clear that decision to try a person who has committed an offence punishable under the 1950 Act and who is subject to the provisions of the 1950 Act does not always or necessarily lie only with the Army. Criminal Court under CrPC could also try alleged offender in certain circumstances in accordance with the procedure laid down by CrPC. Order accordingly.

(C) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Army Act, 1950, ss. 122, 125, 126, 3(x), 69, 70 - Commissions of Inquiry Act, 1952 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 129, 130, 131, 132, 144, 176, 197, 4, 46, 5 - Magisterial Enquiries - Legality - Whether Army would be entitled to hold a Court of Inquiry and determine whether an offence had been committed by any of its personnel and then punish him in accordance with the provisions of the 1950 Act.

Held, even if armed forces decide to take action and inquire into the allegations at their own level, it would not preclude any other inquiry or investigation into the allegations made. Further, even if State Govt. decides to hold Magisterial Enquiries and take suitable action on report given, it would not preclude any other inquiry or investigation into allegations made. Therefore, there can be no substitute for a judicial inquiry or an inquiry by NHRC or an inquiry under the 1952 Act. However, if the law permits and the Army is so inclined, it may hold a Court of Inquiry in each case. Order accordingly.
