Alcon Electronics Private Limited v CELEM S.A. of FOS 34320 Roujan, France and another

Supreme Court of India

09 December 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

N.V. Ramana, A.K. Sikri

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 880; (2017) 2 SCC 253; AIR 2017 SC 1; 2017(1) ALL MR 439; 2017 (120) ALR 200; 2017 (1) CLT(SC) 72; 2017 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 215; 2017 (3) MahLJ 734; 2017 (1) RCR(Civil) 561; 2017 (134) RD 521; 2016(12) SCALE 645; [2016] 8 S.C.R. 577; 2017 (1) WLN(SC) 58

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Intellectual Property; International; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Jurisdiction, CONSTITUTION, Interest Act, 1978, Civil Procedure Rules, Agricultural Produce (Grading and Marking) Act, 1937, Foreign judgments (Reciprocal Enforcement) Act, 1933, Access to Justice Act, 1999, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, Legal Aid Act, 1988, Civil Procedure Code (Amendment) Act, 1937

Summary: (A) Intellectual Property - Practice & Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, ss. 13, 13(b), 13(c), 14, 144, 2(14), 2(2), 2(9), 35, 35(3), 35A, 36, 44A, 44A(3) - Infringement of patent - Foreign Court - Jurisdiction - Respondents filed suit against appellant before English Court for infringement of patent vested in respondents - Appellant filed an application challenging the jurisdiction of English Court - English Court by its Order dismissed claim of appellant and directed it to pay the costs of application to respondents at £ 12,229.75 - Respondents filed a petition for execution in India which was opposed by appellant on ground that the order of English Court was not executable - Executing Court dismissed the same which was confirmed by HC - Hence, instant appeal - Whether the order passed by Foreign Court falls within the Exceptions to s. 13 CPC.

Held, order passed by English Court was an order on merits. Appellant who has submitted itself to the jurisdiction of Court and on its own requested Court to assess the costs summarily. While passing a reasoned order by dismissing the application filed by appellant, English Court granted the costs against appellant. Had it been the case where appellant's application was allowed and costs were awarded to it, it would have as well filed a petition for the execution of the order. Appellant did not prefer any appeal and indeed sought time to pay the costs. Therefore, appellant cannot be permitted to object the execution. It was a pure abuse of process of law and Courts should be very cautious in entertaining such petitions. The principles of comity of nation demand to respect the order of English Court. Even in regard to an interlocutory order, Indian Courts have to give due weight to such order unless it falls under any of the exceptions u/s. 13 CPC. Thus, SC felt that the order in instant case passed by English Court does not fall under any of the exceptions to s. 13 CPC and it was a conclusive one. Appeal dismissed.

(B)International - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, ss. 13, 13(b), 13(c), 14, 144, 2(14), 2(2), 2(9), 35, 35(3), 35A, 36, 44A, 44A(3) - Foreign judgment - Executable - Whether the order passed by Foreign Court amounts to a "decree" and the same was executable.

Held, "foreign judgment" was defined u/s. 2(6) CPC as judgment of a foreign Court. "Judgment" as per s. 2(9) CPC means the statement given by Judge on the grounds of a decree or order. As per the plain reading of the definition 'Judgment' means the statement given by Judge on the grounds of decree or order and order was a formal expression of Court. Thus, "decree" includes judgment and "judgment" includes "order". On conjoint reading of 'decree', 'judgment' and 'order' from any angle, the order passed by English Court falls within the definition of 'Order' and it was judgment and becomes a "decree" as per Explanation to s. 44A(3) CPC. In instant case, Court at England, after following the principles of natural justice, by recording reasons and very importantly basing on the application of appellant has conclusively decided the issue with regard to jurisdiction and passed the order coupled with costs. Therefore, order passed by Foreign Court was conclusive in that respect and on merits executable as a decree. Appeal dismissed.

(C) Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, ss. 13, 13(b), 13(c), 14, 144, 2(14), 2(2), 2(9), 35, 35(3), 35A, 36, 44A, 44A(3) - Imposition of costs - Barred - Validity -Whether interest on costs would fall within the ambit of Explanation 2 of s. 44A CPC.

Held, explanation II to s. 44A CPC does not refer to the costs as contemplated u/s. 35 CPC. Perusal of s. 35A CPC shows that bar operates on the Indian Courts with regard to imposition of costs in respect of false or vexatious claims or defences. The bar was not attracted in instant case as the Court that has ordered the costs was HC in England which was not governed by the provisions of CPC and that respondent merely approached Indian Courts for the satisfaction of a foreign decree. Further, the nature of compensatory costs prescribed in s. 35A CPC were different from 'costs' dealt with in s. 35 CPC as the former were limited to the claims of defences of a party which were frivolous or vexatious. It was settled that before awarding costs u/s. 35A CPC, Court should satisfy itself that the claim was false or vexatious to the knowledge of the party who put it forward and that the interests of justice require the award of such compensatory costs. In instant case no claim was advanced by appellant that the claim filed by respondents was false or vexatious, bar in s. 35A CPC was not applicable. Appeal dismissed.

(D) Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, ss. 13, 13(b), 13(c), 14, 144, 2(14), 2(2), 2(9), 35, 35(3), 35A, 36, 44A, 44A(3) - Judgments Act, 1838 - Execution petition - Maintainability - Whether the interest on costs could be executed in India in view of deletion of s. 35(3) CPC.

Held, it was to the reciprocal advantage of Courts of all nations to enforce foreign rights as far as practicable. To that end, broad recognition of substantive rights should not be defeated by some vague assumed limitations of Court. When substantive rights were so bound up in a foreign remedy, refusal to adopt the remedy would substantially deprive parties of their rights. The necessity of maintaining foreign rights outweighs the practical difficulties involved in applying foreign remedy. In India, though the interest on costs were not available due to exclusion of s. 35(3) CPC, the same does not mean that Indian Courts were powerless to execute the decree for interest on costs. Indian Courts were very much entitled to address the issue for execution of interest amount. The right to 8% interest as per the Act could be recognized and as well as implemented in India. Therefore, Execution Petition filed by respondents for execution of order passed by English Court was maintainable under the relevant provisions. SC do not find any reason to interfere with impugned order. Appeal dismissed.
