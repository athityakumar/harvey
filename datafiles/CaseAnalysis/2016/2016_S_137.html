Messer Holdings Limited v Shyam Madanmohan Ruia and others

Supreme Court of India

19 April 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

J. Chelameswar, A.M. Sapre

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 272; AIR 2016 SC 1948; 2016 (116) ALR 883; 2016 (3) AWC 3197; 2016 (3) Bom.C.R. 769; 2016 (2) CLT(SC) 155; [2016] 196 Comp Cas 258; 2016 (3) CompLJ 222; JT 2016 (4) SC 318; 2016(4) SCALE 224; [2016] 5 S.C.R. 1

Case Digest

Subject: Contract & Commercial

Keywords: Jurisdiction, Registration, Companies Act, 1956, Arbitration And Conciliation Act, 1996, Settlement, Arbitration, Attorney, Securities And Exchange Board Of India Act, 1992, Depositories Act, 1996, Securities And Exchange Board Of India (Substantial Acquisition Of Shares And Takeovers) Regulations, 1997, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Acquisition of shares - Alleged Breach of agreement - Determination - It is stated that respondent company entered into agreement with other companies for purchase of share in company - Further, it is stated that dispute arose between parties on alleged ground that there is breach of contract by respondent company - Further, suit has been filled before HC where various relief order were passed by HC in favor of parties - Further, vide settlement deed dispute between parties were settled with respect to transfer of share and holding of stake by respondent company - However, parties have filed SLP before Court being aggrieved by HC - Hence, present petition.

Held, existence or otherwise of any right or its enforceability in shares in company by respondent companies is not decided in present petition and parties are open to establish their right in suit. SC held that examination of various questions raised by petitioners in these SLPs is wholly uncalled for. SC held that for last 18 years, litigation is going on and conduct of none of parties to this litigation is wholesome. SC held that instant SLPs arise out of various interlocutory proceedings. SC held that in none of suits even issues have been framed so far. SC held that it is only parties who are to be blamed for state of affairs as there was misleading representations and suppression of facts. SC held present case is abuse judicial process as there was litigation in each and every interlocutory step of suit. Hence, SC imposed exemplary costs of Rupees Twenty Five Lakhs only to be paid by each of three parties as compensation for loss of judicial time. Petitions dismissed.

Ratio - Prolonged litigation based on suppression of facts and misrepresentation is not justified as same result in abuse of Court process.
