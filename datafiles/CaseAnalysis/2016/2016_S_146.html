Somasundaram @ Somu v State, Represented by Deputy Commissioner of Police

Supreme Court of India

28 September 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

V. Gopala Gowda, Arun Mishra

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 690; JT 2016 (10) SC 86; 2016(9) SCALE 249; [2016] 7 S.C.R. 263

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Presumption, Kidnapping, Conspiracy, Pardon, Forensic, Death Certificate, Accomplice, Vehicle, Omission, Final Report, Motor, Indian Evidence Act, 1872, Credit, Preparation, Signature, Proof, Confessional Statement, Abetment, Independent Evidence, Appreciation, Believe, Illustration, Driver, Version, Head Constable, Corroboration, Reliance, Chain, Doctor, Implicate, Dead Body, Imprisonment For Life, Inference, Identification, Admissible, Deposition, Approver, Distance, Instigation, Tainted Evidence, Rigorous Imprisonment, Registered, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Investigating Officer, Seizure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120B, 109, 302, 364 - Evidence of accomplice - Sustainability of conviction - On the basis of missing person complaint lodged by PW1 about deceased, who was his father, case was registered and later accused persons were arrested and put on trial - Upon examination of evidence adduced, Trial Court convicted and sentenced accused nos. 1 to 11, 13 to 17 - However, accused nos. 12 and 18 were acquitted of all charges - On appeal by accused persons against their conviction and State against acquittal of accused nos. 12 and 18, HC considered evidence adduced and upheld conviction and sentences awarded to all accused persons except accused no. 10 and acquitted him of all charges and dismissed appeals - Hence, instant appeal by accused nos. 3,4,15 (accused-appellants) - Whether HC was justified in upholding conviction and sentence imposed on accused-appellants.

Held, it was clear from evidence of PWs 10, 11 that they were accomplice witnesses and prosecution relied on their evidence but said witnesses have not been granted pardon by any Court and were arrayed as prosecution witnesses. Further, Trial Court acquitted accused-appellants of the charge of conspiracy u/s. 120B of IPC but convicted inter alia for offences u/s. 302 read with s. 109 and s.364 read with s. 109 of IPC. However, there is nothing on record to show the direct involvement of accused-appellants in either the abduction of deceased or his murder. The Ford Escort Car recovered at instance of PW10, from house of accused no.3, does not trace back its ownership to accused no.4. Neither PW10 nor PW11 are witnesses to abduction of the deceased. The requirement of corroboration from independent sources in material particulars has not been met in instant case and so, accused could not be convicted for offences u/ss. 302 and 364 of IPC. Further, except that PW32 has admitted to issuing the death certificate without even seeing the dead body of the deceased at the request of PW 33 and PW 33 has stated that he only did so at the instance of accused no.3, there is no other evidence on record to connect accused no.3 to the death certificate. Further, even if the death certificate is taken to be genuine, it does not in any way connect accused no.3 to the deceased, thus rendering the claim of the prosecution doubtful and shaky. With regard to accused no. 15, crucial evidence on which reliance was placed upon by both lower courts to convict him was recovery of Reebok shoe on his direction but evidence of PW 31 could not be used against accused no. 15 which was erroneously been done by lower courts. Further, PWs 1 and 2 both stated in their testimony that said Reebok shoe did not belong to deceased. Hence, there is nothing on record that connects accused no.15 either to the crime, or to the deceased. Under such circumstances, the evidence of PW 10 and 11 is not reliable for recording the finding of guilt on the charges against accused-appellants. Even if it placed reliance upon, accused nos. 3, 4, 15 could not be convicted of the offences of kidnapping and murder, more so, in light of the fact that they had been acquitted of the charge of criminal conspiracy u/s. 120B of IPC by lower courts. When it was found that accused persons were not guilty u/s. 120B of IPC, it was the duty of Trial Court to establish the involvement of each of the accused person individually in each offence for which they had been charged to hold them guilty under the same. Hence, Trial Court erred in convicting accused-appellants, more so, after having acquitted them of the offence of criminal conspiracy punishable u/s. 120B of IPC. Even HC adopted same erroneous approach while re-appreciating the evidence against accused-appellants and same shows a gross non-application of mind on the part of the lower courts, which is not sustainable. The prosecution has not proved its case beyond reasonable doubt against accused-appellants and hence, appeals are allowed and impugned judgment and order of HC upholding judgment and order of Trial Court convicting accused-appellants are set aside and are acquitted of all charges. Appeals disposed of.

Ratio - While evidence of an accomplice could be used to convict an accused, as a rule of prudence, the Court should first ensure that the testimony of the accomplice is corroborated in material particulars by adducing independent evidence. Further, evidence of two accomplices could not be used to corroborate with each other. Further, independent evidence has to corroborate with testimony of accomplice in material particulars, that is, corroboration should be in respect of the crime as well as the identity of the accused.
