Jose @ Pappachan v Sub-inspector of Police, Koyilandy and another

Supreme Court of India

03 October 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Amitava Roy, Pinaki Chandra Ghose

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 726; (2016) 10 SCC 519; AIR 2016 SC 4581; 2017 CRLJ 407; 2016 (4) JCC 2645; JT 2016 (9) SC 547; 2017 (1) OLR 24; 2016 (4) RCR(Criminal) 808; 2016(9) SCALE 455; [2016] 8 S.C.R. 115

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Burden Of Proof, Presumption, Circumstantial Evidence, Marriage, Investigating Agency, Indian Evidence Act, 1872, Proof, Search, Reasonable Doubt, Version, Blood, Chain, Doctor, Dead Body, Medical Evidence, Innocence, Innocent, Oath, Hanging, Knife, Strangulation, Fracture, Alarm, Ignorance, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Investigating Officer, Plea, Association

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 313 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 106 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 34, 498A - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Victim/deceased was married to accused no. 1/appellant - Accused allegedly strangulated deceased with a plastic rope and hanged her from a hook of the roof in the house causing death of deceased - Charge-sheet was filed against both the accused u/ss. 498A/ 302 r/w s. 34 IPC - Trial Court acquitted both the accused u/s. 498A IPC and convicted appellant u/s. 302 IPC and sentenced imprisonment for life and exonerated co-accused u/s. 302 IPC - On appeal, HC dismissed appeal and upheld impugned order of Trial Court - Hence, instant appeal - Whether there was any perversity or illegality in impugned judgment of Courts below.

Held, it would be wholly unsafe to hold appellant guilty of the charge of murder of his wife by strangulating her with the nylon rope as seized and then hanging her from the roof with the saree to complete the act. The circumstantial evidence adduced by prosecution in SC assessment falls short of the requirement in law to return a finding of guilt against appellant without any element of doubt whatsoever. The fact that both the accused persons had been exonerated of the charge of cruelty u/s. 498A IPC and that co-accused who allegedly had assisted appellant in the perpetration of the crime was fully acquitted by Courts below of all the charges also takes away the wind from the sails of the prosecution. The absence of definite medical opinion about the homicidal death of deceased in SC comprehension is a serious set back to prosecution. The facts obtained in instant case present a jigsaw puzzle in which several frames are missing to permit an unreserved opinion of the complicity of appellant. The authenticity of letters and also of the records relied upon by defence to demonstrate that appellant while abroad used to remit money for the sustenance of the family was not impeached. Further, evidence adduced by the prosecution constituting circumstantial evidence in support of the charge does not furnish an unassailable basis to hold appellant guilty of the charge of murder levelled against him. The facts and circumstances admit of a reasonable doubt in his favour. The circumstances brought forth by prosecution do not rule out in absolute terms the hypothesis of the innocence of appellant. SC considers it to be wholly unsafe to maintain accused conviction as recorded by Courts below. Therefore, SC inclined to extend benefit of doubt to him. The conclusions drawn by Courts below are not tenable on the basis of the evidence available. Impugned conviction and sentence recorded by Courts below were set aside. Appellant be released from the jail forthwith if he is not required in any other case. Appeal allowed.

Ratio - If two views were possible on the evidence available, one pointing to the guilt of accused and the other to his innocence, the one favourable to accused ought to be adopted.
