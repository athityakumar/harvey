Madura Coats Limited v Modi Rubber Limited and another

Supreme Court of India

29 June 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, Jagdish Singh Khehar, C. Nagappan

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 437; (2016) 7 SCC 603; AIR 2016 SC 3072; 2016 (118) ALR 232; 2017 (3) AWC 2833; 2016 (3) BC 374; [2016] 197 Comp Cas 261; 2016 (2) DRTC 337; JT 2016 (9) SC 417; 2016 (5) MLJ 569; 2016(6) SCALE 46; [2016] 4 S.C.R. 615

Case Digest

Subject: Corporate

Keywords: BIFR, Winding Up, Dissolution, Registration, Companies Act, 1956, Industrial Company, Reconstruction, Sick Industrial Companies (Special Provisions) Act, 1985, Unsecured, Rehabilitation Scheme, Revival

Summary: Corporate - Companies Act, 1956, s. 391 - Sick Industrial Companies(Special Provisions) Act, 1985, ss. 15, 16, 16(1), 20(4), 22 - Sick Unit - Stay of Winding up Proceedings - Challenged - Respondent/firm obtained financial help from appellant/corporation, however, firm was unable to pay its huge undisputed debts - Company Court allowed application of appellant seeking winding up and liquidator was appointed - However, BIFR issued directions to respondent in an application u/s. 22 of the Act - Further, DB of HC stayed winding up proceedings till final decision was taken by Board on reference made by respondent - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, different situations can arise in the interplay between the Companies Act and SICA in matter of winding up of company. One such situation is where winding up proceedings are pending and a reference is made to the BIFR. Once a reference is registered after scrutiny, it is mandatory for the BIFR to conduct an enquiry. The SICA is intended to revive and rehabilitate a sick industry before it can be wound up. The legislative intention is to ensure that no proceedings against the assets of the company are taken before any decision is taken by the BIFR because if the assets are sold or the company is wound up, it may become difficult to later restore the status quo ante. Thus, the enquiry u/s.16 of SICA must be treated to have commenced as soon as the registration of the reference is completed after scrutiny and that action against the company's assets must remain stayed in view of s. 22 of SICA till a final decision is taken by the BIFR. Another situation is when proceedings are pending both before the BIFR and the Company Court but no order of winding up has been passed against the company, in such a situation the provisions of SICA would prevail over the provisions of the Companies Act. Another situation is where a winding up order is passed by the Company Court but it is stayed in appeal. Hence, different situations can arise in the process of winding up a company under the Companies Act, but whatever be the situation, whenever a reference is made to the BIFR u/ss. 15 and 16 of SICA, the provision of SICA would come into play and they would prevail over the provisions of the Companies Act and proceedings under the Companies Act, must give way to proceedings under the SICA. In instant case, the HC was right in concluding that the provisions of s. 22 of SICA would come into play and the Company Court could not proceed further in the matter pending a final decision in the reference under SICA. Hence, appeal is without any merit and the same is dismissed. Appeal dismissed.
