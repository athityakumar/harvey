Mahanagar Telephone Nigam Limited v Applied Electronics Limited

Supreme Court of India

24 November 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Amitava Roy

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 922; (2017) 2 SCC 37; 2017 (2) CTC 440; 2017 (1) RCR(Civil) 728; 2017(1) SCALE 126

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR

Keywords: Arbitral Award, Arbitral Tribunal, Jurisdiction, Arbitration Act, 1940, Arbitration And Conciliation Act, 1996, Second Appeal, Settlement, Conciliation, Letters Patent Appeal, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, Constitution of India, 1950, Commercial Courts, Commercial Division and Commercial Appellate Division of High Courts Act, 2015

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Practice & Procedure - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, ss. 11, 34, 37, 5, 50, 9 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 115, O.41 r. 22 - Arbitral Award - Exercise of Jurisdiction Challenged - Arbitral award was passed by Arbitrator in favour of appellant/claimant and respondent/defendant filed appeal before HC - DB of HC held provisions of CPC would be applicable to proceedings under the Act - HC further condoned delay and cross objections of respondent was held maintainable - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, the jurisdiction of the civil court to which a right to decide a lis between the parties has been conferred can only be taken away by a statute in specific terms and exclusion of such right cannot be inferred because there is always a strong presumption, that the civil courts have the jurisdiction to decide all questions of civil nature and on that basis the court held that it cannot draw inference merely because the Act has not provided CPC to be applicable and thus it should be held that the CPC is inapplicable. However, once the Act is regarded as a self contained and exhaustive code, it should be held that it carries with it a negative import that only such acts either mentioned in the Act are permissible to be done and acts or things not mentioned therein are not permissible to done. The Act as it manifests, provides restrictions for challenging the award. It does not permit a second appeal to be entertained as per the language employed in s. 37(3) of the Act and also u/s. 5 of the Act. However, it also lays the postulate to assail the award and thus emphasis is on expeditious disposal. Hence, a person aggrieved by the award can file objection u/s. 34 of the Act and if aggrieved on the order passed thereon, can prefer an appeal. The Court can set aside the award or deal with the award as provided by the Act. If a corrective measure is thought of, it has to be done in accordance with the provision as contained in s. 37(1) of the Act, which stipulates for an appeal in case of any grievance which would include setting aside of an arbitral award u/s. 34 of the Act. Hence, the application of provisions of CPC is not conceived of and, therefore, as a natural corollary, the cross-objection cannot be entertained. The decision rendered in ITI Ltd. case deserves to be re-considered by a larger Bench. Order accordingly.
