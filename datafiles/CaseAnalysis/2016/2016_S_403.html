V. Lavanya and others v State of Tamil Nadu and others

Supreme Court of India

09 November 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

R. Banumathi, Shiva Kirti Singh

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 771; (2017) 1 SCC 322; 2017 (1) Bom.C.R. 23; 2016 (6) CTC 273; JT 2016 (10) SC 590; 2016 (8) MLJ 198; 2016(10) SCALE 602; [2016] 9 S.C.R. 835

Case Digest

Subject: Education; Service

Keywords: Backward Classes, Selection, Procedure, Qualification, Teacher, Rule, Examination, Grade, Posts, Qualifications, Recruitment, Criteria, Admission, Interest, Policy, Eligibility, Weightage, Relaxation, Reservation, Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009, Writ Petition, Constitution of India, 1950, Post, Appointment, Tamil Nadu Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2011

Summary: (A) Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 14(4), 15(1), 16(1), 16(4), 162, 309 - TET criteria - Relaxation for reserved - Eligibility - State Govt. issued Order No. G.O.Ms.No.181 dt.15-11-2011, prescribing 60% marks as pass marks for State Teachers Eligibility Test (TET) - Subsequently, State Govt. vide G.O.(Ms.) No.252 School Education (Q) Department dt. 05-10-2012 issued Procedure/Guidelines for TET - - Meanwhile, Chief Minister announced relaxation of 5% marks in passing marks of 60% and prescribed the passing marks as 55% for candidates belonging to SC, ST, BC, BC(M), MBC, DNC and PWD - State Govt. issued orders in G.O.Ms.No.25 School Education (TRB) Department dt. 06-02-2014 in which relaxation of 5% of marks was given to the candidates belonging to SC, ST, BC, BC(M), MBC, DNC and PWD candidates - Petitioners filed writ petitions before Madras HC challenging impugned G.Os - HC dismissed writ petitions holding that challenge to policy decision of Govt. can sustain only if it suffers from arbitrariness and unreasonableness and petitioners were non-suited to challenge the procedure adopted in granting weightage to the marks obtained - Madurai Bench in another set of writ petitions quashed the relaxation given to reserved category candidates and held that in the absence of any statistics to prove that prescription of 60% marks resulted in fewer number of candidates belonging to reserved categories getting appointed, it is not possible to uphold the G.O. - Hence, instant appeals - Whether State Govt. has the competence to give relaxation of 5% marks in TET and whether such relaxation provided by State Govt. by G.O.(Ms.) No.25 dt. 06-02-2014 was legally justified.

Held, granting relaxation to SC, ST, BC, BC(M), MBC, DNC and PWD candidates was in furtherance of Constitutional obligation of State to the under-privileged and create an equal level-playing field. After referring to cl. 9 of the NCTE Guidelines, Madras HC rightly held that the State Govt. has acted in exercise of the powers conferred u/cl. 9 of the Guidelines issued by NCTE. Madurai Bench was not right in quashing G.O.(Ms.) No.25 dt. 06-02-2014 on ground that such relaxation "based upon the theory of social justice was actually destructive of the very fabric of social justice". In SC considered view, judgment of Madurai Bench had not kept in view the Constitutional obligation of State to provide equal level-playing field to the under privileged. In consonance with M. Nagaraj & Others v. Union Of India & Others, 2006 Indlaw SC 722, an affirmative action taken by State Govt. granting relaxation for TET would not amount to dilution of standards and hence the view taken by Madurai Bench was not sustainable and was liable to be set aside. Appeals dismissed.

(B) Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 14(4), 15(1), 16(1), 16(4), 162, 309 - Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009, ss. 2(n), 23, 23(1), 23(2) - Selection norms - Estopped - Whether State Govt. was estopped from granting relaxation.

Held, SC was unable to persuade to accept the view of Madurai Bench quashing the impugned G.O. on ground of alleged change in the stand of Govt. Considering the representation from various quarters, it was a policy decision taken by Govt. to relax marks for TET pass for specified and under-privileged communities. It was a matter of State policy to frame and prescribe selection norms with regard to services and posts connected with the affairs of State. However, it was well-settled that Courts cannot interfere with the policy decisions of State especially when the policy decision was taken in public interest to further the advancement of reserved categories. A policy decision taken by the State in exercise of its jurisdiction u/art. 162 of Constitution was subservient only to the mandate of Constitutional provisions and the recruitment rules framed by State itself, either in terms of a legislative act or an executive order. The relaxation provided by State Govt. and criteria of selection laid down vide impugned government orders are in exercise of powers provided u/art. 309 of Constitution and being a policy decision in terms of its extant reservation policy cannot be impeached on ground that the relaxation was given to suit some specific class of individuals. It was well settled by a catena of decisions that there can be no question of estoppels against Govt. in exercise of its legislative, sovereign or executive powers. Thus, view taken by Madurai Bench as regards the stand of Govt. to relax the norms allegedly in contradiction to its earlier stand was not sustainable in law. Appeals dismissed.

(C) Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 14(4), 15(1), 16(1), 16(4), 162, 309 - Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009, ss. 2(n), 23, 23(1), 23(2) - Selection criteria - Reserved category relaxation - Validity - Whether providing relaxation of 5% marks in TET amounts to change in criteria of selection of teachers after the selection process commenced.

Held, it was the prerogative of State Govt. to relax the passing marks with respect to reserved category candidates so that more qualified candidates could come up and participate in the selection process. State has placed the figures before SC to show that even after granting relaxation of 5% marks, many posts of SCs/STs and other backward categories in various subjects are remaining unfilled. Weightage of marks obtained in TET as well as that of academic qualification was still the same. The entire selection process conforms to the equitable standards laid down by State Govt. in line with the principles enshrined in Constitution and the extant reservation policy of State. Conducting TET and calling for certificate verification thereafter was an exercise which State Govt. was obliged to conduct every year as per the Guidelines issued by NCTE. No prejudice was caused to appellants, since the marks obtained by them in TET are to remain valid for a period of 7years, based on which they can compete for the future vacancies. Merely because appellants were called for certificate verification, it cannot be contended that they have acquired a legal right to the post. SC agree with the views taken by Madras Bench that "by merely allowing more persons to compete, petitioners cannot contend that their accrued right has been taken away". In Tej Prakash Pathak & Others v. Rajasthan High Court & Others, 2013 Indlaw SC 163, the alteration in procedure in effect led to elimination of selected candidates, still Court refrained from finding fault with such an alteration, as it was done in public interest. In instant case, the relaxation afforded to reserved category candidates has in no way eliminated appellants from selection process; rather a fair opportunity was provided to other candidates who can legitimately compete with appellants. Appeals dismissed.

(D) Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 14(4), 15(1), 16(1), 16(4), 162, 309 - Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009, ss. 2(n), 23, 23(1), 23(2) - Academic performance weightage - Applicability - Whether prescribing 40% marks as weightage for academic performance was arbitrary and does not take into consideration different streams of education and subjects of study.

Held, in instant case no arbitrariness was proved on part of respondents in formulating the grading system SC cannot interfere with the same. SC cannot be expected to go into every minute technicalities of decision taken by the experts and perform the job of State. Moreover, HC has also noted that almost all the appellants have completed their High Secondary examination from State Boards. Having taken up examination as per G.O.(Ms.) No.252, appellants cannot challenge the award of weightage for the distribution of marks for academic performance with reference to actual marks secured by each candidate. Appellants were not justified in challenging every rational decision taken by respondents to make the selection process more fair and reasonable merely because the outcome does not favour the limited individual interests of appellants.

Madras HC rightly rejected the challenge to G.O.(Ms.) No.25 dt. 06-02-2014 and G.O.(Ms.)No. 71 dt. 30-05-2014, holding that as per the NCTE Guidelines, State Govt. has the power to grant relaxation on the marks obtained in TET for the candidates belonging to reserved category and the same was affirmed. Madurai Bench did not keep in view the NCTE Guidelines and the power of State Govt. to grant relaxation in terms of their extant reservation policy and erred in quashing G.O.(Ms.) No.25 the same was liable to be set aside. Appeals dismissed.
