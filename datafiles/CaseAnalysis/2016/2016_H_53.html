Jamshed Ansari v High Court of Judicature at Allahabad and others

Supreme Court of India

26 August 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, N.V. Ramana

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 618; (2016) 10 SCC 554; AIR 2016 SC 3997; 2016(6) ALL MR 976; 2016 (118) ALR 873; 2016 (6) AWC 6292; 2016 (3) CLT(SC) 216; JT 2016 (8) SC 447; 2016 (4) KLT 1044; 2016 (4) Law Herald (P&H) 3224; 2016 (6) MLJ 361; 2016 (4) RCR(Civil) 179; [2016] 4 S.C.R. 111

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution

Keywords: Right To Practise, Jurisdiction, Advocates Act, 1961, Bar Councils, Government of India Act, 1935, Practicing, Government of India Act, 1915, Allahabad High Court Rules, 1952, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Advocates Act, 1961, ss. 16(2), 30, 34, 34(1), 36 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 303 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19, 19(1)(g), 19(6), 22, 225 - Government of India Act, 1915, s. 106 - Government of India Act, 1935, s. 223 - Allahabad High Court Rules, 1952, rr. 3, 3A - Advocate enrollment - HCs regulatory provisions - Supervisory power - Appellant/advocate filed writ petition before HC however Registry of HC refused to accept his petition as appellant is not enrolled with Bar Council and he had not fulfilled the requirement of the Rules by filing appointment along with a local Advocate - Consequently, appellant engaged a local advocate and filed writ petition challenging Constitutional validity of the provisions of rr. 3 and 3A of Chapter XXIV of the Rules - HC dismissed writ petition holding that a similar challenge had already been rejected by HC in Shashi Kant Upadhyay, Advocate v. High Court of Judicature at Allahabad, 2015 Indlaw ALL 650 - Hence, instant appeal - Whether the Rules in question are in the nature of restrictions or they are prohibitory in nature.

Held, rr. 3 and 3A of the Rules are regulatory provisions and do not impose a prohibition on practice of law. Right to appear and conduct cases in Court is a matter on which Court must and does have major supervisory and controlling power. Courts cannot be and are not divested of control or supervision of conduct in court merely because it may involve the right of an Advocate. HC is duly empowered to make Rules and Rules in question are not ultra vires u/s. 30 of the 1961 Act. Further, when power u/s. 34 of the 1961 Act is given to HCs, which are Constitutional Courts. SC referred to judgment in case of N.K. Bajpai v. Union Of India & Anr., 2012 Indlaw SC 98, wherein it was held that right to practice as an Advocate is not an absolute right and it was only a statutory right which is controlled by the provisions of the Act. The principle that HC has right to regulate the conduct of its own proceedings can also be found in Pravin C. Shah v. K.A. Mohd. Ali & Anr., 2001 Indlaw SC 19805. In that case, it was held that HC cannot be divested of the control or supervision of Court merely because it may involve the right of an advocate. HC has power to formulate Rules for regulating proceedings inside Court. Such power should not be confused with the right to practice law. The Court has supervisory power over the right of an Advocate to appear and conduct cases in the Court. SC also cited judgment of HC in case of Prayag Das v. Civil Judge, Bulandshahr, 1973 Indlaw ALL 188, wherein HC held that HC has power to regulate the appearance of Advocates in Courts. HC further held that right to practice in the right to appear in Courts are not synonymous. U/s. 34 of the 1961 Act HC has power to make Rules for regulating proceedings inside Court. Therefore, s. 30 of the 1961 Act is also subject to s. 34 of the 1961 Act. The 1961 Act does not confer any absolute right to practice. The right can be regulated by HCs by prescribing conditions. Thus, SC is of opinion that rr. 3 and 3A of the Rules are perfectly valid, legal and do not violate the right of appellant u/art. 19(1)(g) of Constitution. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - Rules in question amount to reasonable restrictions which are imposed in public interest.
