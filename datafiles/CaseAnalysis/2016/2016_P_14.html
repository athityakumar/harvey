 Pankajakshi (Dead) and another v Chandrika and another

Supreme Court of India

25 February 2016

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, Anil R. Dave, Kurian Joseph, Shiva Kirti Singh, Adarsh Kumar Goel

Where Reported

2016 Indlaw SC 196; (2016) 6 SCC 157; AIR 2016 SC 1213; 2016 (1) CLT(SC) 241; 2017 (345) E.L.T. 438; JT 2016 (2) SC 545; 2016 (1) KLT 851; 2016 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1555; 2016 (2) MLJ 448; 2016 (2) RCR(Civil) 245; 2016(2) SCALE 674; [2016] 3 S.C.R. 1018

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Keywords: Central Excise Act, 1944, Central Excises And Salt Act, 1944, Life Insurance Corporation Act, 1956, Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, Letters Patent, English, Government of India Act, 1935, Code Of Civil Procedure (Amendment) Act, 1976, Administrative Tribunals Act, 1985, Presidency Small Cause Courts Act, 1882, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, Kerala High Court Act, 1958, Representation Of The People Act, 1951, West Bengal Land Reforms Act, 1955, States Reorganisation Act, 1956, Chartered High Courts, Government of India Act, 1915, Punjab Courts Act, 1918, Bengal Agricultural Debtors Act, 1936, Code of Civil Procedure Amendment Act, 1951, Travancore-Cochin High Court Act, 1125, Cochin Tobacco Act, 1084, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Finance Act, 2015, yderabad Suits Against Government Act, 1320, Repealing and Amending Act, 1928, Travancore-Cochin Act

Summary: Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, ss. 97, 98, 98(2), 98(3), 100, 100(1), 104, 117, 120 - Kerala High Court Act, 1958, s. 9 - Travancore-Cochin High Court Act, 1125, s. 23 - Disagreement - Exercise of Jurisdiction Challenged - 1125 Act was repealed to extent of its repugnance by 1958 Act - Further, matter was referred to DB of HC, which held that s. 23 of 1125 Act was in nature of special provision while s. 98(2) of CPC was in nature of general law - However, in view of conflict with regard to Hemalatha's case, referred matter to 3 judge bench, which in turn referred matter to larger bench - Hence, instant reference.

Held, u/s. 98 of CPC, when the Judges differ in opinion on a point of law, the matter is required to be placed for opinion of the third Judge or more of other Judges as the Chief Justice of the High Court deems fit and the point of law on which a difference has arisen is decided by the majority and the appeal is decided accordingly. It is to be seen that under the proviso to s. 98 (2) of CPC, hearing by a third Judge or more Judges is only on the point of law on which the DB could not concur. Further, 1958 Act, has provided for the powers of a Bench of two Judges u/s. 4 of 1958 Act. It is clarified there under that if the Judges in the DB are of opinion that the decision involves a question of law, the DB may order that the matter or question of law be referred to a Full Bench. Needless to say, it should be a question of law on which there is no binding precedent. However, u/s. 23 of 1125 Act, if the DB disagrees either on law or facts, the Chief Justice is required to refer the matter or matters of disagreement for the opinion of another Judge and the case will be decided on the opinion of the majority hearing the case. Thus, s. 23 of 1125 Act, alone is to be applied when there is a difference of opinion between two learned Judges of the Kerala High Court in any appeal, be it civil, criminal, or otherwise, before them. Hence, answer the question referred by stating that s. 23 of 1125 Act, remains unaffected by the repealing provision of s. 9 of 1958 Act, and that being in the nature of special provision vis-a-vis s. 98(2) of CPC, would apply to the Kerala HC. Reference answered.

Ratio - In the event of a conflict between a special law and a general law, the special law must always prevail.
