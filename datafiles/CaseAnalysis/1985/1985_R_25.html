Ram and Shyam Company v State of Haryana and Others

Supreme Court of India

08 May 1985

Case Analysis

Bench

D.A. Desai, Baharul Islam

Where Reported

1985 Indlaw SC 409; (1985) 3 SCC 267; AIR 1985 SC 1147; 1985(1) SCALE 1237; [1985] Supp1 S.C.R. 541; 1986 (1) UJ 539

Case Digest

Subject: Mines & Minerals

Keywords: Punjab Minor Mineral Concession Rules, 1964

Summary: Mine & Minerals - Haryana Minor Minerals (Vesting of Rights) Act, 1973 - Grant of lease - Notification was issued by State Government specifying that minor mineral quarries would be auctioned - At the auction, appellant gave the highest bid - Presiding Officer conducting the auction accepted the bid but the State Government did not confirm the same - Fresh auction was notified to be held - Appellant participated and gave the bid for the same plot, his highest bid rising to Rs. 3,87,000 for a period of three years - Presiding Officer accepted the bid and the State Government declined to confirm the same - Respondent No. 4 wrote a letter to the Chief Minister, Haryana State stating that bidders at the auctions have formed a syndicate and want to monopolise the business by not outbidding each other so that the State gets uneconomical rent royalty - Letter further proceeds to make an offer/that if the contract for a period of five years was given to respondent No. 4 it was willing to pay Rs. 4,50,000 per year - Offer was accepted by the Chief Minister - Appellant challenged the action of the Chief Minister in Writ Petition inter alia contending that respondent No. 4, had participated in the auction and then made false allegations against the appellant whose bid was the highest and without giving him any opportunity, the offer of respondent No. 4 was accepted which had denied equality of opportunity to the appellant in the matter of distribution of State largesse - Writ petition was rejected on the ground that the petitioner had alternate remedy - Appeal filed thereagainst was dismissed - Hence, instant appeal - Held, disposal of the state property in public interest must be by such method as would grant an opportunity to the public at large to participate in it - In the instant case, highest bidder whose bid was rejected on the ground that the bid did not represent the market price, was not given an opportunity to raise his own bid when privately a higher offer was received - Further, if the allegations made in the letter influenced the decision of the Chief Minister, fair-plan in action demands that the appellant should have been given an opportunity to counter and correct the same - That having not been done, the grant in favour of the respondent No. 4 must be quashed - Order accordingly.
