Union Of India And Ors v Godfrey Philips India Ltd. Etc. Etc

Supreme Court of India

30 September 1985

Case Analysis

Bench

P.N. Bhagwati, R.S. Pathak, Amarendra Nath Sen

Where Reported

1985 Indlaw SC 275; (1985) 4 SCC 369; (1986) SCC (Tax) 11; AIR 1986 SC 806; 1986 (1) CCC 395; [1986] 59 Comp Cas 526; 1986 (1) CompLJ 46; 1986 CRLR 114; 1986 CrLR(SC) 114; 1985 ECR 1989; 1985 (22) E.L.T. 306; [1986] 158 ITR 574; 1985(2) SCALE 619; [1985] 3 S.C.R. 123; [1985] supp3 S.C.R. 123; 1986 TAXLR 2002

Case Digest

Subject: Excise; Municipalities & Local Governments

Keywords: Bombay Prohibition Act 1949

Summary: (Majority Order)

(R. S. Pathak and Amarendra Nath Sen, JJ.)

Excise - Indirect Tax - Central Excise Act, 1944, s. 4(4)(d)(i) - Assessment - Respondents, manufacturers of cigarettes, packed cigarettes initially in paper/card board packets of 10 and 20 and these packers were then packed together in paper/card board cartons/outers - Said cartons/outers were then placed in corrugated fibre board containers and these corrugated fibre board containers filled with cartons/outers containing packets of cigarettes of 10 and 20 were delivered by the respondents to the wholesale dealers at the factory gate - The wholesale price charged by the respondents for the cigarettes sold to the wholesale dealers included not only the cost of primary packing in packets of 10 and 20, but also the cost of secondary packing in cartons/outers and the cost of final packing in corrugated fibre board containers - On 19.05.1976, the Cigarette Manufacturer's Association made a representation to the Central Board of Excise and Customs pointing out that corrugated fibre board containers 'are not an integral or essential requirement for the sale of cigarettes and are used for the sole purpose of protecting cigarettes from any damage that may arise during transportation' and that the cost of such corrugated fibre board containers should not therefore be included in the value of the goods for the purpose of excise duty - The Board accepted said plea of the Associations by a letter dt. 24.05.1976 intimated to the Cigarette Manufacturer's Association that 'instructions have been issued to the Collector of Central Excise that the cost of corrugated fibre board containers in question does not form part of the value of cigarettes for the purposes of excise duty - Subsequently, on 2.11.1982 Board addressed a circular to all Collectors, stating that the matter had been re-examined and the earlier advice should be treated as cancelled - Hence, the instant appeal - Whether the cost of packing was includible in value of cigarettes for purpose of assessment to excise duty? - Held, on a proper construction of s. 4(4)(d)(i) of the Act r/w. the Explanation any secondary packing done for the purpose of facilitating transport and smooth transit of the goods to be delivered to the buyer in the wholesale trade cannot be included in the value for the purpose of assessment of excise duty - In the instant case, the corrugated fibre board containers were employed only for the purpose of avoiding damage or injury during transit - The wholesale dealer who takes delivery may have his depot a very short distance only from the factory gate or may have such transport arrangements available that damage or injury to the cigarettes can be avoided - Hence, the corrugated fibre board containers were not necessary for selling the cigarettes in the wholesale market at the factory gate - Hence, the position expressed by the Board in its letter dt. 24.05.1976 was right when it declared that the Collector of the Central Excise has been instructed that the cost of corrugated fibre board containers in question does not form part of the value of cigarettes for the purpose of excise duty - Appeal dismissed.

(Minority Order)

(P. N. Bhagwati, CJ)

Excise - Indirect Tax - Central Excise Act, 1944, s. 4(4)(d)(i) - Assessment - Whether the cost of packing was includible in value of cigarettes for purpose of assessment to excise duty? - Held, the condition for applicability of the inclusive definition of value in s. 4 (4)(d)(i) of the Act was that the goods were delivered at the time of removal 'in a packed condition' and where this condition was satisfied, the 'value' of the goods would include 'the cost of such packings and 'such packing' must obviously mean the packing in which the goods were when they were delivered at the time of removal - In the instant case, there can therefore be no doubt that corrugated fibre board containers in which the cigarettes were contained fall within the definition of 'packing' in the Explanation and if they form part of the packing in which the goods are packed when delivered at the time of removal, it was difficult to resist the conclusion that u/s. 4 (4)(d)(i) r/w. the Explanation, the cost of such corrugated fibre board containers would be liable to be included in the value of the cigarettes - Appeal allowed.
