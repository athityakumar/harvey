 Prakash Amichand Shah v State Of Gujarat & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

20 December 1985

Case Analysis

Bench

E.S. Venkataramiah, V. Balakrishna Eradi, R.B. Misra, V. Khalid, O. Chinnappa Reddy

Where Reported

1985 Indlaw SC 367; (1986) 1 SCC 581; AIR 1986 SC 468; 1986 (1) CCC 926; 1985(2) SCALE 1437; [1985] 3 S.C.R. 1025; [1985] supp3 S.C.R. 1025

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property

Keywords: Improvements, Bombay Reorganisation Act, 1960, Bombay Town Planning Rules, 1955, Constitution (Sixteenth Amendment) Act, 1963, Constitution (Fifth Amendment) Act,1955

Summary: Land and Property - Constitution - Constitution, art. 14, 31, 31(2) - Bombay Reorganization Act, 1960 - Bombay Town Planning Act, 1955, ss. 32, 53, 67, 68, 69, 70, 71 - Acquisition of land under Town Planning Scheme - Whether discriminatory ? - Held, the Acquisition of land under Town Planning Scheme by the local Authority under s. 53 cannot be said to be discriminatory as when the final Town Planning Scheme coming into force under s. 53 of the Act there is an automatic vesting of all lands required by the local authority unless otherwise provided, in the local authority - This statutory vesting operates as acquisition - The denial of the solatium of 15 per cent of the market value of the land in addition to the compensation payable for lands taken by the local authority for purposes of the Scheme does not make the Act discriminatory - It is no where provided that wherever land is taken away by the Government under a separate statute compensation should be paid under the Land Acquisition Act, 1894 only and if there is any difference between the compensation payable under the Land Acquisition Act, 1894 and the compensation payable under the statute concerned the acquisition under the statute would be discriminatory - Whether provision for appeal against only some of the decisions made by the Town planning officer violates article 14 ? - Held, no - There is no rule that every decision of every officer under a statute should be made applicable and if it is not so made applicable the statute should be struck down - Unless the Court finds that the absence of an appeal is likely to make the whole procedure oppressive and arbitrary, the Court does not condemn it as unconstitutional - Appeal dismissed.
