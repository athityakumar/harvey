Madhusudan Das v Smt. Narayani Bai and Others

Supreme Court of India

25 November 1982

Case Analysis

Bench

R.S. Pathak, V.D. Tulzapurkar

Where Reported

1982 Indlaw SC 187; (1983) 1 SCC 35; AIR 1983 SC 114; 1983 (9) ALR 146; 1983 HLR 475; 1983 MahLJ 402; 1983 MLJ 402; 1982(2) SCALE 1083B; [1983] 1 S.C.R. 851; 1983 UJ 25

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal

Summary: Family & Personal - Code of Civil procedure, 1908 - Appreciation of evidence - Adoption - Whether upon appreciation of evidence High Court was right in reversing findings of Trial Court as regards adoption of Appellant? - In an appeal from Trial Court decree, Appellate Court considering an issue turning on oral evidence, does not enjoy advantage which Trial Court had in having witnesses before it and of observing them give their testimony - When conflict of oral evidence turns upon credibility of witnesses Appellate Court should permit findings of fact rendered by Trial Court to prevail unless some special feature about witness's evidence appears having escaped notice of Trial Court or there is a sufficient balance of improbability to displace its opinion as to where credibility lies - This should be approach in considering whether HC proceeded correctly in evaluation of evidence when reversing Trial Court - If appraisal of evidence by trial court suffers from material irregularity, based on inadmissible evidence, on a misreading thereof or on conjectures and surmises, appellate court is entitled to interfere with finding of fact - When witness holds a position of relationship favouring party producing him or of possible prejudice against contesting party, it is incumbent on court to exercise appropriate caution - Held, Court cannot reject evidence on mere ground of relationship or favour or possible prejudice - A person seeking to displace natural succession to property by alleging an adoption must discharge burden that lies upon him by proving factum of adoption - Evidence in proof of adoption should be free from all suspicion of fraud and so consistent and probable as to give no occasion for doubting its truth - Fact of adoption must be proved in same way as any other fact - For a valid adoption, physical act of giving and taking is an essential requisite, a ceremony imperative in all adoptions, whatever caste - This requisite is satisfied in its essence only by actual delivery and acceptance of boy, even though there exists an expression of consent or an executed deed of adoption - HC misdirected itself in applying a standard of proof to evidence which circumstances did not warrant - Its appreciation of evidence is founded in that misdirection, leading to findings which are accordingly vitiated - On contrary, Trial Court examined evidence relating to actual adoption with great care - Appeal allowed.
