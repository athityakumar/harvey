 State Of Punjab And Another v M/S. Devans Modern Brewaries And Another

Supreme Court of India

20 November 2003

Case Analysis

Bench

AR. Lakshmanan, B.N. Agrawal, S.B. Sinha, V.N. Khare, R.C. Lahoti

Where Reported

2003 Indlaw SC 1507; (2004) 11 SCC 26; JT 2003 (10) SC 485; 2003(10) SCALE 202; [2003] Supp5 S.C.R. 930

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Excise

Keywords: Delegated Legislation, Betting, Beverage, Enactment, State Action, Morality, Reasonableness, Freedom Of Trade, Tamil Nadu Minor Mineral Concession Rules, 1959, Constitutionality, State Laws, Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951, Public Debt Act, 1944, Foreign Liquor Rules, Parliamentary Act, Abkari Act, 1977, Punjab Liquor Licence Rules, 1956, Maharashtra Debt Relief Act, 1976, Licensing Act, Maharashtra Country Liquor Rules, 1973, Indian Tariff Act, 1894, Government of India on adaptation of Indian Laws Order, 1937, Punjab Excise Fiscal (Second Amendment) Orders, 1996, Punjab Excise Fiscal (10th amendment) Orders, 1992, Punjab Intoxicants Licence and Sale Order, 1956, Punjab Liquor Import, Export Order, 1932, Punjab Excise Fiscal Orders, 1986, Punjab Excise Fiscal Orders, 1932, Constitution Amendment Act, 1951, Punjab Fiscal Orders, 1932, Gun-Free School Zones Act, Fair Labor Standards Act, Kerala Abkari Act, 1902, Pre-Constitution Act, Excise Act, 1984, Lotteries Act, Molasses Act, Tobacco Act, Maharashtra Foreign Liquor (Sale on Cash, Register of Sales etc.) Rules, 1969, U.P. Excise Licenses (Tender-cum-Auction) Rules, 1991, Bombay Sacramental Wine Manufacturing Rules, 1950, Excise Licenses (Tender-cum-Auction) Rules, 1991, Punjab Liquor Permit & Pass Rules, 1932

Summary: Majority View

AR. Lakshmanan, J. (for V.N. Khare, R.C. Lahoti and for himself)

(A) Constitution - Excise - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 301 - Punjab Excise Act, 1914 - Punjab Liquor Permit & Pass Rules, 1932 - Punjab Liquor Import, Export Order, 1932 - Legislative competence of State to levy import fee - State of Punjab imposed tax on import of potable liquor manufactured in other States - Whether import fee levied is price for parting with privilege given to respondent to import liquor into State and, therefore, same is within competence of State to impose import fee? - It was submitted that a combined reading of ss. 33A of Punjab Excise Act, 1914, arts. 301 and 304 of Constitution and Entry 51 of List II of Seventh Schedule to Constitution makes it clear that State of Punjab has no authority to impose import fee over and above countervailing duty - Held, State Government is competent and empowered to regulate import and export of liquor - Arts. 302 and 304A of Constitution are not attracted to present case as imposition of import fee does not, in any way, restrict trade commerce and intercourse among States - Permissive privilege to deal in liquor is not a "right" at all - Levy charged for parting with that privilege is neither a tax nor a fee - It is simply a levy for act of granting permission or for exercise of power to part with privilege - Further, State, is competent and entitled to impose excise duty or countervailing duty - Besides there is no bar on State to charge any other fees on account of consideration for privilege provided to licensee to trade in liquor which privilege he did not otherwise have - Therefore, licensee is liable to comply with other conditions imposed by State Government from time to time - Levy in dispute under challenge is an import levy - It is neither duty nor countervailing duty - It is part of consideration money i.e. price of privilege given to licensees for dealing in liquor - Thus, decision of this Court in case of Kalyani Stores (supra) is not applicable to facts of present case and that Punjab Excise Act, 1914 is an existing law under Clause 10 of Art. 366 of Constitution of India and its continued application is saved by art. 372 of Constitution of India - It is also saved by Art. 305 of Constitution from attack u/art. 301 and 303 of Constitution - It is well within legislative competence of State - Appeals disposed of.

(B) Constitution - Excise - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 301 - Punjab Excise Act, 1914 - Punjab Liquor Permit & Pass Rules, 1932 - Punjab Liquor Import, Export Order, 1932 - Legislative competence of State to levy import fee - State of Punjab imposed tax on import of potable liquor manufactured in other States - Respondent was holding L-1 licence i.e. wholesale and retail vend of foreign liquor to trade only - Said licence given on fixed licence fee, subject to variation as per excise policy of Government based on year to year - State Government incorporated as one of terms and conditions on L-1 holders to pay import fees also at prescribed rate as per Punjab Excise Fiscal Order, 1996 - Respondent accepted terms and conditions from 1992 onwards and acted on same, licence was renewed on yearly basis - (A) Respondent submitted that there is no source of power for imposition of import fee over and above countervailing duty and that appellant-State was not able to show that under which Authority or provision of Punjab Excise Act, 1914, they can impose import fee over and above countervailing duty - Held, right to import liquor is dependent on issue of import permit on payment of import fee as consideration for parting with State's exclusive privilege to import liquor - It is purely a contractual dealing between State and importer and, therefore, no question of violation of Art. 301 can arise - Importer had no anterior right to import liquor and hence cannot complain of any violation of art. 301 at that stage as right to trade in liquor is not a fundamental right - His right to import is referable to import permit which he acquired on payment of import fee - No further impediment has been created in import of liquor so that Art. 301 is not attracted in relation to payment of import fee which was prior to getting his privilege of importing - Licensee having entered into a contractual relationship with State obtained privilege and enjoyed benefit of it - It is not open to them to turn round subsequently and repudiate obligations subject to which they obtained privilege - (B) It was contended that once at L-1 wholesale liquor licence is issued to him, State's permissive privilege in respect of liquor stands permanently parted with and thereafter no additional or further levy of any kind even in respect of activities other than wholesale selling under L-1 licence can be raised - Held, import fee levied in instant case is fully authorized by 1914 Act and delegated legislation thereunder and is clearly intra vires - Provisions confer ample regulatory power upon excise authority to regulate several activity related with liquor in any reasonable manner and in particular to regulate its import - Regulatory power includes power to levy a monthly fee in that regard such as impugned import fee - Appeals disposed of

(C) Constitution - Excise - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 301 and 304 - Punjab Excise Act, 1914 - Punjab Liquor Permit & Pass Rules, 1932 - Punjab Liquor Import, Export Order, 1932 - Interpretation of statutory provisions - Held, statutory provision in question must be interpreted and read broadly and not narrowly - Approach must be to uphold validity of impugned delegated legislation by a process of fair and broad reading of statutory mandate - Even if Act does not specifically provide for levy in question by name to provide statutory authority for its imposition by delegated legislation and levy is actually imposed by delegated legislation made under Statute, same would be valid and not ultra vires - In the instant case, levy has been imposed by Punjab Fiscal Orders as amended from time to time under specific statutory authority to issue such orders u/ss. 58 and 59 of Act, in particular, and other provisions of Act - Since rule making power has not been shown to be bad, Punjab Fiscal Orders, once made have effect of Statute itself and become part of Statute since they have been made under valid rule making power - Statutory provisions of Punjab Act and Rules itemized in paragraphs above amply delineate that regulatory power and impugned import fee is nothing but a facet and manifestation of that regulation by State - Levy in question is valid as a regulatory levy which has consistently been held on touchstone of art. 304 - Appeals disposed of.

Minority View

B.N. Agrawal. J

(D) Constitution - Excise - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 301 - Legislative competence of State to levy import fee - State of Punjab imposed tax on import of potable liquor manufactured in other States - State of Kerala also imposed a similar levy - Punjab and Haryana High Court quashed notification dated 27.03.1996 imposing levy of import duty by State of Punjab in exercise of its powers conferred upon it u/s. 31, 32 and 58 of Punjab Excise Act 1914 on two grounds viz.; (i) State has no power to levy such tax under Punjab Act and (ii) in view of Constitution Bench decision of SC in Kalyani Stores v. State of Orissa and Ors., imposition of duty is ultra vires art. 301 of Constitution - (A) Legislative competence of State - Held, legislative competence of State to levy any fee is limited to levy of countervailing duty - Any levy on import cannot exceed excise duty levied on manufacturers of State - State, therefore, cannot levy any duty in addition to countervailing duty - In present case, notification refers to excise duty and countervailing duty, which in terms of s. 3(6-B) of Punjab Act mean any such excise duty or countervailing duty as case may be, as is mentioned in Entry 51 of List II of Seventh Schedule to Constitution - State, therefore, cannot levy any import fee over and above excise duty/countervailing duty, having regard to said definition - Ss. 17 and 18 of Abkari Act which are in pari materia with ss. 31 and 32 of Punjab Act are referable to Entry 51 alone - Further, functions of State to impose a fee or tax in terms of provisions of statute is a legislative function - Such legislative function must be attributed to source of State's power in terms of Entry 51 of List II to Constitution and not otherwise - State while imposing import duty has exercised its power under statute - Impugned notifications in no uncertain terms and unequivocally refer to source of power therefore - Further, by reason of provisions of Abkari Act or Punjab Act, no power has been conferred upon State to impose any import fee over and above excise duty/countervailing duty - Furthermore, having regard to fact that Financial commissioner is a statutory authority in relation thereto, State cannot be said to have any jurisdiction thereover, particularly, in matter of levy of import fee which clearly is referable to Chapter V of Punjab Act and has nothing to do with grant of licence occurring in Chapter VI - Impugned levy cannot be sustained - (B) Whether State of Punjab, having regard to s. 33A of Punjab Act, could levy such duty? - Held, in Sub-section (1) of s. 33A provision has been made permitting State to continue to levy any duty which it had lawfully been levying immediately before commencement of Constitution - Said provision is in tune with Article 305 of Constitution, therefore, same calls for a strict construction - Sub-section (3) of s. 33A is couched in negative language by reason whereof power of State to levy any duty has been taken away in event thereby removing discrimination made in favour of goods manufactured or produced in State and similar goods manufactured or produced in another locality - If by reason of a statute an embargo has been placed on State's power to levy any fee it is beyond any doubt that such a levy cannot be held to be justified by reason of an executive action or otherwise - Appeals disposed of.

(E) Constitution - Excise - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 301 - Whether import duty can be said to have been validly imposed having regard to doctrine of 'exclusive privilege' of State to deal in obnoxious substance? - Held, a State, may be entitled to either completely prohibit a trade or business in liquor and create monopoly either in itself or in any other agency and furthermore it can for purpose of selling licence adopt any mode with a view to maximize its revenue but while doing so it must, having regard to a large number of decisions of this Court, not act arbitrarily - Privilege, thus, can be claimed by a State in a 'no right' situation, namely, when citizen is not permitted to carry on trade - But once State takes a decision to part with its privilege, it cannot make any discrimination whatsoever - Dealing in liquor by persons in whose favour licences have been granted in terms of statutory enactments derive a right and therefore, cannot be said to be "Res-Extra Commercium" - Thus, when a person has been granted a licence strictly in conformity with Excise Act to carry on his business activities in terms of statute operating in field, same cannot be termed as pernicious, obnoxious and injurious to health, safety and welfare of general public - No public interest can be inferred by any court of law by going beyond statutory provisions - Even monopoly of State either in itself or in any agency created by it for manufacture, possession, sale and distribution of liquor can be created only by a statute which must conform to provisions of Clause (6) of art. 19 of Constitution, i.e., by making a valid law by way of a regulatory legislative enactment - Appeals disposed of.

(F) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 14, 298 and 301 - Right of State to carry on trade or business u/art. 298 of Constitution - Held, right of State would be subject to same constitutional limitations in matter of carrying on trade or business in liquor as in other cases - Distinction being only that State has a monopoly to do so - Once State does not exercise said right and considers it expedient to allow citizens to carry on business or trade, it cannot be said that licensees do not derive any right whatsoever - Even when State exercises such right by creating a monopoly in itself it would be subject to same constitutional limitations as envisaged, inter alia, u/arts. 14 and 301 of Constitution; arts. 14 and 301 of Constitution protect from maladies of discrimination - Such discrimination may be in between persons and persons, persons and State and State and State - Appeals disposed of.

(G) Constitution - Excise - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1)(g), 301 and 303 - Whether a trade in liquor would come within purview of trade, commerce and intercourse, within meaning of Article 301 of Constitution? - Held, for purpose of invoking Part XIII of Constitution, one may safely proceed on assumption that a citizen of India may not have a Fundamental Right in terms of Article 19(1)(g) of Constitution to carry on a trade or business but there could be little difficulty in upholding right to carry on such trade on ground that same has been permitted by State, although a citizen but for such permission would not have a right to deal in commodity in question - Words "relating to" in art. 303 of Constitution are of wide amplitude - These expressions relate to all entries relating to trade or commerce and not one entry in one of Lists - It, thus, refers to all such entries which are referable to trade and commerce occurring in any of three lists - Appeals disposed of

(H) Constitution - Excise - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1)(g), 301 and 303 - Res-Extra-Commercium - Levy of import fee - Held, tobacco is one of goods which would otherwise come within purview of doctrine of "Res extra commercium" - Dealing in tobacco is regulated by Tobacco Act, a Parliamentary Act - In terms of art. 303 of Constitution, Tobacco Act which is made in terms of Entry 52 of List I of Seventh Schedule to Constitution would prohibit States from making any discriminatory legislation - It is, therefore, difficult to understand as to how a prohibition can be imposed in respect of liquor in relation whereto also a legislative power has been conferred upon State specifically in terms of Entries 8 and 51 in List II of Seventh Schedule to Constitution - Thus, once it is held that Legislature has no power to levy any excise duty on imported liquor in excess of countervailing duty within State, having regard to constitutional limitation imposed in terms of Entry 51, List II of Seventh Schedule to Constitution, such discriminatory levy must be held to be violative of art. 303(1) and 304(a) of Constitution - As import fee is an impost thus, levy thereof in addition to countervailing duty would clearly attract art. 304(a) of Constitution - Impugned impost cannot be upheld - Impugned judgment rendered by Punjab and Haryana High Court quashing Notification impugned before it upheld - Appeals disposed of.

S.B. Sinha, J.

(I) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 47 - Whether constitutional validity of a statute can be determined on basis of interpretation given to maxim 'res extra commercium'? - Held, for judging validity of taxing statutes, Part IV of Constitution or art. 47 will have no role to play - Recourse to Part IV or Part IVA of Constitution can only be taken as regard interpretation of a legislative enactment for giving effect to objects and purport thereof and not for any other purpose - If Constitution or relevant statutes do not prohibit carrying out a trade/business, courts cannot do so by taking recourse to interpretive process or on supposed grounds of public morality - Further, State, in its discretion having regard to provisions contained in art. 47 of Constitution of India may part with its right of exclusive privilege but once it does so, grant being subject to terms and conditions of a statute, common law principle based on maxim 'res extra commercium' shall have no application in relation thereto - Constitutionality of a statute could not be determined solely by relying on or on basis of said maxim without any reference to limitations contained in Constitution - Appeals disposed of.

(J) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 301 - Constitutionality of a taxing statute - Held, when constitutionality of a taxing statute is questioned, same has to be judged on touchstone of constitutional provisions including art. 301 thereof - Freedom guaranteed u/art. 301 of Constitution of India may not be considered in isolation having regard to expression contained therein that such freedom is subject to Part XIII of Constitution - Appeals disposed of.

(K) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950 - Interpretation of constitutional provisions - Whether in interpreting a constitutional provision, Court should take into consideration international treaties and covenants covering subject-matter and having regard to social milieu? - Held, in interpreting constitutional provisions, court should take into consideration implication of its decision having regard to international treaties dealing with countervailing duty, etc. - Appeals disposed of.

(L) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19(1)(g) and 19(6) - Freedom to carry on trade or business - Whether freedom to carry on trade or business as envisaged u/art. 301 of Constitution of India can be held to be inapplicable to trade of liquor which is permitted by State itself? - Held, right to carry on trade in liquor is a fundamental right within meaning of art. 19(1)(g) of Constitution of India and State may, however, legislate prohibiting such trade either in whole or in part in terms of Clause (6) of thereof - Appeals disposed of.

(M) Constitution - Excise - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 301 - Legislative competence of State to levy import fee - Held, Entry 8 of List II does not envisage any control over import - Entry 51 of List II empowers State to levy countervailing duty at same or lower rates of excise duty levied on similar goods produced in that State - Import of liquor in terms of licences granted under provisions of a statute cannot be subject-matter of regulation within purview of Entry 8 of List II of Seventh Schedule of Constitution of India - Hence, on a plain reading of Entry 51 no duty can be imposed on import of liquor over and above countervailing duty - Imposition of import duty on liquor is unconstitutional - Appeals disposed of.
