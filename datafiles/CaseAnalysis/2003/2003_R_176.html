Research Foundation For Science Technology National Resource Policy v Union of India and another

Supreme Court of India

14 October 2003

Case Analysis

Bench

Y.K. Sabharwal, B.N. Agrawal

Where Reported

2003 Indlaw SC 1598; (2005) 10 SCC 510; 2003(9) SCALE 303

Case Digest

Subject: Environment, Wildlife & Animal

Keywords: Quantity, Laboratories, Recycled Plastics Manufacture and Usage Rules, 1999, Emergency Planning and Community Right to Act, 1986, Hazardous Wastes (Management and Handling) Amendment Rules, 2003, Implementation of Plastic Waste Recycling Rules, Draft Used Oil (Management and Handling) Rules, Battery Waste Recycling Rules

Summary: (A) Environment, Wildlife & Animal - Hazardous Wastes (Management and Handling) Rules, 1989 - Hazardous Wastes (Management and Handling) Amendment Rules, 2003 - Dumping of hazardous waste - Violation of fundamental rights - Authorization - Due to alarming situation created by dumping of hazardous waste, its generation and serious and irreversible damage, as result thereof, to environment, flora and fauna, health of animals and human beings, petitioner approached SC u/art. 32 complaining of violation of arts. 14 and 21 of the Constitution - Hence, instant Petition - Whether aspect of units that were operating without any authorization or in violation of conditions of authorization issued under the Rules, 1989 as amended up to date - Held, one of directions sought for by petitioner was that authorization for any unit should not be issued or renewed until occupier undertakes that they had programme in place to reduce volume or quantity and toxicity of hazardous wastes to degree determined by them to be economically practicable and that proposed method of treatment, storage or disposal was most practicable method currently available to them which minimizes present and future threat to human health and environment - R. 21 of 2003 Rules, in respect of environmentally sound technologies and standards for re-refining or recycling had been incorporated - Instead of issuing directions as suggested, purpose would be better served if Central Pollution Control Board, from time to time, issues directions to State Pollution Control Board and all PCCs bringing to their notice latest technologies and requiring said Boards/Committees to ensure compliance thereof by the units concerned within fixed time-frame - Order accordingly.

(B) Environment, Wildlife & Animal - Method of incineration - Destruction of wastes - Whether incineration was most important treatment method for destruction of all high calorific and highly toxic wastes - Held, on inspection it was found by High Powered Committee (HPC) that barring few, most of incinerators were mere combustion chambers or industrial boilers where maximum temperature was around 500/550° C, which was much too low - Often they were not equipped with adequate air pollution control devices and all types of wastes, including non-chlorinated and chlorinated hydrocarbons, being burnt in so-called incinerators - In view of HPC such incinerators, rather than destroying hazardous constituent, actually succeed in generating toxic gases - Hence, there seems to be urgent need to develop design criteria for incinerators to safeguard environment so as to have proper and efficient working of incinerators close to place of generation of hazardous waste - Order accordingly.

(C) Environment, Wildlife & Animal - Ship breaking activity - Effect of - Whether there was any effect of ship-breaking activity covered by TOR (14) - Held, it was not suggesting discontinuing of ship-breaking activity, however, it deserves to be strictly and properly regulated - When ship arrives at port for breaking, authorities concerned have to be vigilant about hazardous waste which could be generated if appropriate timely action by various agencies, in particular, Maritime Board and SPCB were not taken -Thus, major ship-breaking activity in India was at Alang in State of Gujarat and, therefore, Gujarat Maritime Board and Gujarat SPCB had to be alive to consequences of appropriate steps to be taken before breaking activities start -According to recommendations of HPC, Inter- Ministerial Committee comprising Ministry of Surface Transport, Ministry of Steel, Ministry of Labour and Ministry of Environment should be constituted with involvement of labour and environment organizations and representatives of ship-breaking industries - Hence, ship-breaking operation could not be permitted to be continued without strictly adhering to all precautionary principles, CPCB guidelines and taking requisite safeguards which had been dealt with extensively in report of HPC which include aspect of working conditions of workmen - Order accordingly.
