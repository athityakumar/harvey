Dharam Dutt & Ors v Union Of India & Ors

Supreme Court of India

24 November 2003

Case Analysis

Bench

R.C. Lahoti, Brijesh Kumar

Where Reported

2003 Indlaw SC 1509; (2004) 1 SCC 712; AIR 2004 SC 1295; 2003(10) SCALE 141; [2003] Supp6 S.C.R. 151

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Trusts & Associations

Keywords: Sovereignty And Integrity Of India, Speech And Expression, Morality, Impugned Act, Constitutionality, Hindi Sahitya Sammelan Act, 1962, Tibbia College Act, 1952, Indian Council of World Affairs Ordinance, 2001, Indian Council of World Affairs Act, 2001

Summary: Constitution - Trusts & Associations - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 19(1)(a), 19(1)(c), 123(1) and 300A - Societies Registration Act, 1860 - Indian Council of World Affairs Act, 2001 - Indian Council of World Affairs Ordinance, 2001 - Constitutional validity of the Indian Council of World Affairs Act, 2001 - In the year 1943, the Indian Council of World Affairs (ICWA) was formed which was later registered as a society under the Societies Registration Act, 1860 - Activities of the Society were housed in a building known as Sapru House - By enacting the impugned Act, the Central Government has taken over the Society as also its movable and immovable properties - Hence, petitions filed u/art. 32 of the Constitution alleging the Ordinance and the Act to be violative of Arts. 14, 19(1)(a), 19(1)(c) and 300A of the Constitution - (A) Freedom of speech - Whether the impugned enactment violative of arts. 19(1)(a) & (c) of the Constitution? - Held, preamble to the Act declares the ICWA to be an institution of national importance and to provide for its incorporation - Impugned Act only deals with ICWA the pre-existing body and ICWA the body corporate under the impugned Act - New body takes over the activities of the pre- existing society by running the institution which too was known as ICWA - So far as the society ICWA was concerned, it was left intact, untouched and un-interfered with - There was no tampering with the membership or the governing body of the society - Hence, as the formation of the society, which was a voluntary association, was not adversely affected and the members of the society were free to continue with such association, the validity of the impugned legislation cannot be tested by reference to sub-Cls. (a) and (c) of Clause (1) of Art. 19 - Impugned Act does not offend the right guaranteed by Art. 19(1)(c) - It also does not in any manner deprive the members of the Society of their freedom of speech and expression u/art. 19(1)(a) - Impugned Act was a reasonable legislation enacted in the interest of the general public and to govern an institution of national importance - It was valid - (B) Whether impugned Act arbitrary and violative of Art. 14? - Held, art. 14 prohibits class legislation and not reasonable classification for the purpose of legislation - Requirements of the validity of legislation by reference to Art. 14 of the Constitution are : that the subject matter of legislation should be a well defined class founded on an intelligible differentia which distinguishes that subject matter from others left out, and such differentia must have a rational relation with the object sought to be achieved by the legislation - Laying down of intelligible differentia does not, however, mean that the legislative classification should be scientifically perfect or logically complete - In the present case, circumstances warranting an emergent action satisfied the President of India, resulting in his promulgating ordinances which earlier could not culminate into legislative enactments on account of fortuitous circumstances - Hence, legislation cannot be said to be arbitrary or unreasonable - Further, a single institution is capable of being treated as a class by itself for the purpose of legislation if there are special circumstances or reasons which are applicable to that institution and such legislation would not incur the wrath of Art. 14 - (C) Doctrine of Separation of Powers - Effect of the previous judgment of HC on the impugned legislation - Whether impugned Act violative of the doctrine of Separation of Power? - It was submitted that HC of Punjab and Haryana had struck down an Ordinance which contained similar provisions and the said judgment attained finality and hence, a subsequent legislation which was in defiance of the judgment of the HC deserves to be struck down solely on this ground - Held, Court cannot strike down a legislation which on an independent scrutiny held to be within the legislative competence of the enacting legislature, merely because the legislature re-enacted the same legal provisions into an Act which, ten years before, were incorporated in an ordinance and were found to be unconstitutional in an judgment of the HC and before the error could be corrected in appeal the Ordinance itself lapsed - Impugned Act not liable to be annulled on the ground of violation of the doctrine of Separation of Powers - (D) Impugned Act covered by Entries 62, 63 of List I of Schedule 7 - Held, legislation is clearly covered by Entries 62 and 63 of List I Schedule 7 - Initially at one time, the institution was receiving financial aid from the Government of India - Institution ICWA was declared to be an 'institution of national importance' by the Act of Parliament - Once an institution is declared to be of national importance, the Parliament is competent to make any law governing the management, administration and affairs of such an institution - Provisions of the Act fall within the field of legislation meant for the Union of India - Writ petitions dismissed.
