Dr. Balbir Singh And Ors. Etc. Etc. v Municipal Corporation, Delhi And Ors

Supreme Court of India

12 December 1984

Case Analysis

Bench

P.N. Bhagwati, A.P. Sen, B.C. Ray

Where Reported

1984 Indlaw SC 248; (1985) SCC (Tax) 35; (1985) 1 SCC 167; AIR 1985 SC 339; 1985 (46) CTR 208; [1985] 152 ITR 388; 1986 (1) PLR 296; [1985] 2 S.C.R. 439; [1985] 20 TAXMAN 56

Case Digest

Subject: Indirect Tax; Land & Property

Keywords: Hypothetical

Summary: Municipalities & Local Governments - Indirect Tax - Delhi Municipal Corporation Act, 1957 - Punjab Municipal Act, 1911 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Delhi Rent Control Act, 1958 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Delhi and Ajmer Rent Control Act, 1952 - Assessment - Rate-able value - Legality - Large number of petitioners/property owners in territory of Delhi did not pay property tax under 1957 Act and 1911 Act - Respondent/Municipal Corporation assessed said properties and passed order - Petitioners appealed before HC challenging assessments made by respondent - Said appeal were subsequently transferred to SC - Hence instant Appeals - Whether Assessing authorities could determine standard rent of additional structure by taking reasonable cost of construction - Held, addition might not be of distinct and separate unit of occupation but might be merely by way of extension of existing premises which were self-occupied, In such case original premises together with additional structure would have to be treated as single unit for purpose of assessment and its rate-able value would have to be determined on basis of rent which owner may reasonably expect to get, if premises as whole were let out, subject to upper limit of standard rent determinable under provisions of s. 6 (I) (A) (2) (b) of 1958 Act - Existing premises before addition might be tenanted and addition might be to tenanted premises so that additional structure also form part of same tenancy - Where such was case, standard rent would be liable to increase under s. 7 and such increased rent would be standard rent of premises as whole and within upper limit fixed by such standard rent, assessing authorities would have to determine rent which owner might reasonably expect to get if premises as whole were let out as single unit to hypothetical tenant and in such case, actual rent received would be fair measure of rent which owner might reasonably expect to receive from such hypothetical tenant unless it was influenced by extra-commercial considerations - Lastly, addition might be of distinct and separate unit of occupation and in such case, rate-able value of premises would have to be determined on basis of formula laid down by SC for assessing rate-able value of premises which were partly self-occupied and partly tenanted - Same principles for determining of rate-able value would obviously apply in case of subsequent additions to existing premises - Basic point to be noted in all these cases was-and this was what SC had already emphasized earlier that formula set out s. 6 (I)(A)(2)(b) and (1)(B)(2)(b) of 1958 Act could not be applied for determining standard rent of an addition, as if that addition was only structure standing on land - Assessing authorities could not determine standard rent of additional structure by taking reasonable cost of construction of additional structure and adding to it market price of land and applying statutory percentage of 7 ½ to aggregate amount - Market price of land could not be added twice over, once while determining standard rent of original structure and again while determining standard rent of additional structure - Once addition was made, formula set out in s. 6 (I)(A)(2)(b) and (I)(B)(2)(b) of 1958 Act could be applied only in relation to premises as whole and where additional structure consists of distinct and separate unit of occupation, standard rent would have to be apportioned in manner indicated by SC in earlier part of this Judgment - These were principles on which rate-able value of different categories of properties was liable to be assessed under 1957 Act - Same principles would a fortiori apply also in relation to assessment of rate-able value under 1911 Act - Since there were number of petitions and appeals before SC and they involve different fact situations SC did not think it would be convenient to dispose them of finally by one single Judgment - SC would therefore direct that these petitions and appeals should be placed on Board on some convenient date so that they could be disposed of in light of principles laid down - Appeal disposed of.
