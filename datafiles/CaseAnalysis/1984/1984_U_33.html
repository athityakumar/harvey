O.P. Singla and Another v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

14 August 1984

Case Analysis

Bench

Y.V. Chandrachud, R.S. Pathak, S. Mukharji

Where Reported

1984 Indlaw SC 318; (1984) 4 SCC 450; (1984) SCC (L&S) 657; AIR 1984 SC 1595; 1985 (50) FLR 257; 1984 LabIC 1659; 1985 (2) LLJ 309; 1984 (2) LLN 893; 1984(2) SCALE 144; [1985] 1 S.C.R. 351; 1984 (3) SLR 34

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Keywords: Delhi Judicial Service Rules, 1970, Bihar Police Service (Recruitment) rules 1953

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Delhi Higher Judicial Service Rules, 1970, r. 7(a) - Question of Seniority - Between promotes and direct recruits - Petition was filed by petitioners to determine seniority between promotes and direct recruits - Hence instant petition - Petitioners contended that seniority between promotees and direct recruits should be determined in accordance with the respective dates of their continuous officiation as Additional District and Sessions Judges and that, direct recruits who were appointed as Additional District and Sessions Judges after the promotees were so appointed, could not rank higher in seniority over the promotes - It was urged that promotees discharged identical functions and bear the same responsibilities as direct recruits and upon their appointments, they constituted one common class - Therefore, to give seniority to the direct recruits who were appointed later in point of time is violative of arts. 14 and 15 of Constitution - In support of these contentions, promotees had filed charts showing what, according to them, was grave and glaring act of injustice done to them in the matter of seniority - These charts showed, indisputably, that promotees who had been functioning as temporary Additional District and Sessions Judges for unbroken period of anything between 8 to 12 years were regarded as juniors to the direct recruits who have been appointed as Additional District and Sessions Judges much later - Few illustrations would help appreciate the grievance of the promotes - One of the respondents to these writ petitions, was appointed directly to the Service on 27-9-1978 - However, he ranked higher in seniority over two others who were appointed as temporary Additional District and Sessions Judges on 7-6-1977 and 1-4-1978 respectively - Respondent No. 5 who was appointed directly as an Additional District and Sessions Judge on 22-4-1980, was regarded as senior to several promotees who were appointed as temporary Additional District and Sessions Judges long before her - Others were all appointed as temporary Additional District and Sessions Judges on 24-3-1972, which was 8 years before respondent No. 5 was appointed as direct recruit from the Bar - Twenty-five other members of the Delhi Judicial Service were appointed as temporary Additional District and Sessions Judges on various dates between 31-8-1973 and 11-12-1979 - Held, one should be hesitant and loath to upset the just expectations of the members of the legal profession who have joined the service and one would be very sorry to do that - Judicial appointments were no longer attractive for any lawyer of any kind of success - One would be hesitant to put further disincentives for those with professional experience to join Judicial service, and therefore be reluctant to interfere with the just expectations of professional entrants who had entered Judicial service at sacrifice of considerable money and position - But the provisions of r. 7(a) of Rules as well as of the Constitution should be given effect to - In the instant case members of the Judicial service, petitioners had made representations to HC in 1977 - Two entrants who would be vitally affected by the re-adjustment of the list would be respondent No. 4 who joined the service on 27-9-1973 and respondent No. 5 who joined the service on probation on 24-4-1980 - At that time challenge in the form of representation to existing seniority before the HC was there - So far as respondent No. 5 was concerned, she was appointed on probation in April, 1980 and got her confirmation during the pendency of petitions - Respondent No. 5 was still young and had long tenure - It was hoped that she would overcome any temporary or momentary loss of seniority and would continue to render her valuable service to the administration of justice - One should insist that govt. should abolish system of making appointments from two different sources in ad-hoc manner - If appointments had to be made from two different sources, then authorities should so plan that the recruits came from two different sources in time and officers from one source was not required to function substantively and effectively in the jobs which were intended to be performed by recruits of other source and face the prospect of being either pushed back or thrown out - Rules nisi were made absolute - Current gradation list of the Judicial Department of the Delhi Administration specially with reference to respondent Nos. 4 and 5 was quashed with direction to the respondent Nos. 1 to 3 to prepare the gradation list of the Delhi Higher Judicial Service on the basis of the principles indicated in judgment - It would not in any way prejudice the claim of seniority of respondent No. 4, on the ground of his being member of Scheduled Caste or Scheduled Tribe - Direction would also not in any way affect the seniority of the promotee officers of the Delhi Higher Judicial Service amongst themselves - That position would be guided by the principles laid down by SC in Joginder Nath and Ors.' case 1974 Indlaw SC 164, relied on - Petitions partly allowed.
