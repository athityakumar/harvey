S. Harcharan Singh v Sajjan Singh and Others

Supreme Court of India

29 November 1984

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Mukharji, S. Murtaza Fazal Ali, A. Varadarajan

Where Reported

1984 Indlaw SC 357; (1985) 1 SCC 370; AIR 1985 SC 236; 1985 (1) CCC 771; 1984(2) SCALE 834; [1985] 2 S.C.R. 159

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Summary: Election - Representation of the People Act, 1951, ss. 100(1)(b) and 123 (3) - Whether respondent 3, his election agent and other person with his consent had appealed to voters of constituency in name of religion, namely Sikh religion for voting in his favour or to refrain from voting in favour of appellant? - Several decisions of this Court have laid down various tests to determine standard of proof required to establish corrupt practice - While insisting on standard of strict proof, Court should not extend or stretch this doctrine to such an extreme extent as to make it well-nigh impossible to prove an allegation of corrupt practice - Such an approach would defeat and frustrate very laudable and sacrosanct object of Act in maintaining purity of electoral process - Evidence naturally is mostly oral - Therefore specially where charge is a grave one, namely corrupt practice which if proved would disentitle candidate to contest election for some time to come, courts must proceed with caution - An election once held ought not to be treated in a light-hearted manner and defeated candidate should not get away with it by filing election petition - Held, respondent 3 was guilty of corrupt practice as mentioned in sub-s. (3) of s. 123 of Act - In result his election is set aside and seat is declared vacant - Appeal allowed.
