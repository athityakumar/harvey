 R. S. Joshi, S.T.O. Gujarat Etc. Etc v Ajit Mills Ltd., Ahmedabad & Anr. Etc. Etc

Supreme Court of India

31 August 1977

Case Analysis

Bench

V.R. Krishna Iyer, M. Hameedullah Beg, Y.V. Chandrachud, P.N. Bhagwati, N.L. Untwalia, S. Murtaza Fazal Ali, P.S. Kailasam

Where Reported

1977 Indlaw SC 98; (1977) 4 SCC 98; (1977) SCC (Tax) 536; AIR 1977 SC 2279; 1977 (6) CTR 354; [1978] 1 S.C.R. 338; 1977 (40) STC 497

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes; Municipalities & Local Governments; Practice & Procedure; Sales Tax

Summary: (Majority Order)

(M. H. Beg, CJ for himself and for Y. V. Chandrachud, P. N. Bhagwati, V. R. Krishna Iyer, N. L. Untwalia and Syed M. Fazal Ali, JJ)

Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Constitution - Bombay Sales Tax Act, 1959, ss. 37, 46 and 63 - Constitution of India, 1950, Entries 54 and 64 - Constitutional validity - Respondents, registered dealers of sales tax, collected from various customers amounts qua sales tax prohibited by s. 46 of the Act - The tax officials discovered this deviance and, acting on the prohibition plus penalty contained in s. 46 r/w. s. 37(1) of the Act, imposed penalties and forfeited the sum collected by the respondents by way of tax in contravention of s. 46 of the Act less amounts shown to have been refunded to the customers as wrong levy of sales tax - On appeal, HC struck down the last limb of the forfeiture provision contained in s. 37(1)(a) as being unconstitutional on the ground that it was not competent for the State Legislature to forfeit to the public exchequer punitively, under entry 54 r/w. entry 64 of List II, sums collected by dealers by way of sales tax which was not excisable under the Act - Hence, the instant appeals - Whether ss. 37(1) (a) and 46(2) of the Bombay Sales Tax Act, 1959 were beyond the legislative power conferred by Entry 54, List II, Schedule VII of the Constitution? - Held, the legal test that divides the constitutional from the unconstitutional was that if all that the legislation means to do was to take over, whatever the verbal veils worn, the collections which were ex-hypothesis not sales tax but were illegal additives as if sales tax were due, then such an expropriation of' the expropriators was beyond entry 54 of the Constitution and, therefore, ultra vires - On the other hand, all real punitive measures, including the dissuasive penalty of confiscating the excess collections, were valid, being within the range of ancillary powers of the legislature competent to exact a sales tax levy - Having regard to the object of s. 37 r/w. s. 46 of the Act forfeiture has a punitive impact - In the instant case, the fact that there was arithmetical identity between the figures of the illegal collections made by the dealers and the amounts forfeited to the State cannot create a conceptual confusion that what was provided was not punishment but a transference of funds - If this view be correcting must be held that it was so the legislature, by inflicting the forfeiture, does not go outside the crease when it hits out against the dealer and deprives him, by the penalty of the law, of the amount illegally gathered from the customers - The contention that s. 37(1) of the Act fastens a heavy liability regardless of fault has no force in depriving the forfeiture of the character of penalty - The notion that a penalty or a punishment cannot be cast in the form of an absolute or no-fault liability but must be preceded by mens rea should be rejected - Section 37(4) of the Act forbids penalty plus prosecution but permits forfeiture plus prosecution - Hence, HC was wrong in denouncing the impugned legislation as exceeding legislative competence or as a colourable device or as supplementary not complementary - Appeals allowed.

(Minority Order)

(P.S. Kailasam, J.)

Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Constitution - Bombay Sales Tax Act, 1959, ss. 37, 46 and 63 - Constitution of India, 1950, Entries 54 and 64 - Constitutional validity - Whether ss. 37(1) (a) and 46(2) of the Bombay Sales Tax Act, 1959 were beyond the legislative power conferred by Entry 54, List II, Schedule VII of the Constitution? - Held, forfeiture was one form of penalty - Forfeiture of property was one of the punishments provided for in the IPC - For contravention of the sales tax law the section provides two forms of punishment i.e. levy of penalty and forfeiture - Therefore, the use of the word 'forfeiture' as distinct from penalty will not make forfeiture any the less a penalty - A combined reading of ss. 37 and 55 makes it clear that it was not obligatory on the part of Commissioner to direct that the entire amount collected by way of tax in contravention of the provisions of the Act be forfeited - Therefore, the contention that the forfeiture was only a device for recovering the unauthorised collection has no force - Moreover, Courts cannot declare that an Act was beyond the legislative competence of the State Legislature on the ground that, while under the Act the amounts erroneously or innocently collected by the assessees were forfeited, an obligation remained with the assessees to refund the amounts to the persons from whom they were collected - The mere fact that in some cases dealers were prejudiced would not affect the validity of the legislation - Section 46(2) of the Act was not unconstitutional - For the enforcement of sales tax law such a provision was absolutely necessary, for, without such prohibition unauthorised collection of tax can never be checked - Sales tax will have to demarcate articles on which tax can be collected and prohibit collection of tax in any manner not authorised by law - Appeals allowed.
