State of Punjab v Pritam Singh and Others

Supreme Court of India

04 August 1977

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Murtaza Fazal Ali, A.C. Gupta

Where Reported

1977 Indlaw SC 312; (1977) 4 SCC 56; (1977) SCC (Cr) 551; AIR 1977 SC 2005; 1977 CRLJ 1575; 1977 CRLR 363

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 148, 149, 302, 307 and 452 - Arms Act, 1959, s. 27 - Explosive Substances Act, 1908, ss. 4 and 5 - Whether the respondents have been falsely implicated due to enmity and the case against them was not proved beyond reasonable doubt as it was replete with inherent improbabilities and consisted of purely partisan evidence? - Held, there were certain important intrinsic circumstances which threw a good deal of doubt on the prosecution case - (i) There was serious enmity between the parties and there was sufficient motive for Pala Singh to have implicated the respondents at whose instance he was implicated in the murder of Toga Singh, equally there was a motive for the respondents also to kill Pala Singh in order to wreck vengeance on him for having caused Gurmej Singh' father's death, when the motives were equally balanced, the Court had to look to surrounding circumstances - (ii) Since, Ajit Singh's near relations had been killed in his presence, was the most material witness to be examined yet he was not examined by the prosecution at all - (iii) The only eye witness Jaswant Kaur who was present at the spot, chose not to disclose the incident or the names of the assailants to Darbara Singh who was the only witness examined to corroborate the version of Pala Singh and Jaswant Kaur - (iv) Darbara Singh had stated that he was informed about the occurrence and the names of the assailants by one Jita Sigh who, however, has not been examined, thus PW5's evidence becomes valueless - (v) Although the accused party consisting of 6 person seems to have come variously armed, excepting the hand-grenade, no other weapon appears to have been used in the offence, even though the hand-grenade missed the main target - (vi) No wads or pellets were recovered from occurrence site and this renders the story of pistol-shots having been fired by Gurmej Singh extremely doubtful - (vii) Why accused made no attempt to kill Pala Singh at all although they were armed with hand-grenades, pistols and Gandasis when they missed the target is not intelligible, having regard to the wholly interested and partisan nature of the testimony of the prosecution witnesses and the complete absence of corroborative evidence, the case is not proved beyond reasonable doubt - Appeal dismissed.
