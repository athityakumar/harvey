 Fatehchand Himmatlal & Others v State Of Maharashtra Etc

Supreme Court of India

28 January 1977

Case Analysis

Bench

V.R. Krishna Iyer, M. Hameedullah Beg, P.N. Bhagwati, S. Murtaza Fazal Ali, A.N. Ray

Where Reported

1977 Indlaw SC 162; (1977) 2 SCC 670; AIR 1977 SC 1825; [1977] 2 S.C.R. 828

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: Assam Land (Requisition and Acquisition) Act 1948

Summary: Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 301, 246 (3) - Maharashtra Debt Relief Act, 1976 - vires - There was legislative incompetency for State Legislature because it had forfeited power to legislate on money-lending where gold loans were involved, since Parliament had occupied field under Entry 52 of List I by enacting Gold Control Act, 1968, and had thereby elbowed out State Legislature from that field - Held, meaningful, yet minimal analysis of 1976 Act, read in the light of times and circumstances which compelled its enactment, would bring out human setting of statute - Bulk of beneficiaries were rural indigents and rest urban workers - These were weaker sections for whom constitutional concern was shown because institutional credit instrumentalities had ignored them - Moneylending might be ancilliary to commercial activity and benignant in its effects, but money-lending might also be ghastly when it facilitates no flow of trade, no movement of commerce, no promotion of intercourse, no servicing of business, but merely stagnates rural economy, strangulates borrowing community and turns malignant in its repercussions - Former might surely be trade, but latter the law might well say was not trade - In this view, SC was more inclined to the view that this narrow, deleterious pattern of moneylending could not be classed as 'trade' - No other question then arises, since petitioners and appellants could not summon art. 301 of Constitution to their service - Act was temporary measure limited to grimy levels of society - Existing debts of some classes of indigents alone were liquidated - If impossible burdens on huge human numbers were not lifted, social orderliness would be threatened and as regulatory measure this limited step was taken by Legislature - Regulation of situation was necessitous might reach limit of prohibition - Disorder might break out if the law does not step in to grant some relief - Trade could not flourish where social orderliness was not secure - Tensions and unrests and violence spawned by desperation of debtors were not dissolved by State action, no moneylending trade could survive - It followed that for the very survival of Trade regulatory measure of relief of indebtedness was required - That form this relief should take was ordinarily for legislature to decide - It was not ordinarily for Court to play the role of Economic Adviser to Administration - Here amelioratory measures were laid down by Legislature so that socio-economic scene might become more contented, just and orderly - Obviously, this was regulatory in interest of Trade itself - This policy decision of the House could not be struck down as perverse by Court - Restrictions under 1976 Act were reasonable - Equally ,clearly, if the steps of liquidation of current debts and moratorium were regulatory, art. 301 of Constitution do not hit them - Art. 246 (3) r/w Entry 30 in List 11, empowered State to make impugned law - State Legislature could make laws regarding money-lending even where gold was involved under Entry 30, List II, even as it could regulate 'gambling in gold' under Entry 34, impose sales tax on gold sales under Entry 54, regulate by municipal law under Entry 5 and by trade restrictions under Entry 26, the type of buildings for gold shops and kind of receipts for purchase or sale of precious metal - Appeals dismissed.
