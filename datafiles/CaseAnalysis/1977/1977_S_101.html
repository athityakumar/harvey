Radhakrishna Agarwal and Others v State of Bihar and Others

Supreme Court of India

17 March 1977

Case Analysis

Bench

M. Hameedullah Beg, A.C. Gupta, P.S. Kailasam

Where Reported

1977 Indlaw SC 282; (1977) 3 SCC 457; AIR 1977 SC 1496; [1977] 3 S.C.R. 249

Case Digest

Subject: Contract & Commercial

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14 and 226 - Breach of Contract - Maintainability of writ petition - Writ petitions were filed against orders of the State Government revising the rate of royalty payable by the appellants under a lease and later challenge to the cancelation of the lease was also included - It was alleged that revision of the rate' of royalty payable by the appellants for the lease to collect and exploit sal seeds from forest area was illegal during the subsistence of the lease, and, thereafter, cancellation of the lease itself was illegal - HC held that that no writ or order can be issued u/art. 226 of the Constitution in such cases 'to compel the authorities to remedy a breach of contract pure and simple' - Present appeals - (A) Whether HC rightly, held that the present cases involved questions of pure alleged breaches of contract? - Held, HC rightly held that present case was the case where the contract entered into between the State and the person aggrieved was non-statutory and purely contractual and the rights and liabilities of the parties were governed by the terms of the contract and the petitioner complains about breach of such contract by the State - Further, in the present case, there was no restriction, under the terms of the contract upon the amount by which the royalty could be increased by a revision after a three years' cycle - Lessee was only entitled, under the contract, to be consulted before a revision; but decision of the governmental authorities to enhance was binding upon him after that - Furthermore, dispute whether there was or there was not a breach of contract should, be determined by ordinary civil courts - (B) Violation of art. 14 of the Constitution - Held, before any adjudication on the question whether art. 14 of the Constitution could possibly be said to have been violated, as between persons governed by similar contracts, they must be properly put in issue and established - In the present case, contracts do not contain any statutory terms or obligations and no statutory power or obligation which could attract the application of art. 14 of the Constitution - Allegations on which a violation of art. 14 could be based were neither properly made nor established - Appeals dismissed.
