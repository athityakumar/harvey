 (1) Prakash Kumar @ Prakash Bhutto; (2) v State of Gujarat

Supreme Court of India

12 January 2005

Case Analysis

Bench

H.K. Sema, R.C. Lahoti, B.N. Agrawal, G.P. Mathur, P.K. Balasubramanyan

Where Reported

2005 Indlaw SC 17; (2005) 2 SCC 409; (2005) SCC (Cr) 518; AIR 2005 SC 1075; 2005 CRLJ 929; JT 2005 (11) SC 209; 2005 (1) RCR(Criminal) 498; 2005(1) SCALE 274; [2005] 1 S.C.R. 408

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: TADA Act, Special Courts Act, 1984

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, ss. 12, 15 - Confession u/s. 15 of the Act - Admissibility - Whether the confessional statement duly recorded u/s. 15 of TADA would continue to remain admissible as for the offences under any other law which were tried along with TADA offences u/s. 12 of the Act, notwithstanding the fact that the accused was acquitted of offences under TADA in the said trial - Held, language employed in ss. 15 and 12 is plain and unambiguous - First limb of s. 15 bars application of provisions of the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 and the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 to a confession made by a person before a police officer not lower in rank than a Superintendent of Police and recorded by him in any of the modes noted in the Section - Second limb of s. 15 makes such a confession admissible, dehors the provisions of the Evidence Act in the trial of such person or co-accused, abettor or conspirator for an offence under the TADA Act or rules made there under provided the co-accused, abettor or conspirator is charged and tried in the same case together with the accused as provided in s. 12 of the Act - However, in the event CrPC and the Evidence Act come in conflict with either recording of a confession of a person by a police officer or its admissibility at the trial, s. 15 of the TADA Act will have a overriding effect over the CrPC and the Evidence Act - Since s. 15 has overriding effect over the Evidence Act and the CrPC, the only procedure to be followed in recording confession is the procedure prescribed under the provisions of s. 15 and Rules framed there under - S. 15 has to be read together with s.12 and a conjoint reading of two sections as a whole, leaves no manner of doubt that one provision is to be construed with reference to the other provision and vice versa so as to make the provision consistent with the object sought to be achieved - Scheme and object of the Act being the admissibility of the confession recorded u/s. 15 of the Act in the trial of a person or co-accused, abettor or conspirator is charged and tried in the same case together with the accused, as provided u/s. 12 of the Act - S. 12(2) expressly empowered the Trial Court to convict the accused person of such other offence and pass any sentence authorised by the Act, however, only embargo imposed on the exercise of the said power is that the offence under the TADA is connected with any other offence being tried together - Legislative intendment underlying s.12(1) and (2) is clearly discernable, to empower the Trial Court to try and convict the accused for offences committed under any other law along with offences committed under the Act, if the offence is connected with such other offence - In State v. Nalini, 1999 INDLAW SC 810, SC laid down correct law and the confessional statement duly recorded u/s. 15 of TADA and Rules framed there under would continue to remain admissible for the offences under any other law which were tried along with TADA offences u/s. 12 of the Act, notwithstanding that the accused was acquitted of offences under TADA in the same trial - Order accordingly.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, ss. 12, 15 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 21 - Conviction of accused under TADA - Discrimination - Whether the rigours of s. 12 is discriminatory and attract arts. 14 and 21 of the Constitution as it empowers the Trial Court to try and convict the accused for the offences committed under any other law along with the offences committed under the TADA thereby depriving the rights available to the accused under the ordinary law - Held, art. 14 prohibits discrimination, however, it allows reasonable classification based on intelligible differentia, having nexus with the object sought to be achieved - Object sought to be achieved by introducing s. 12 is to take care of the offence connected with or incidental to terrorist activities - Persons who are tried for offences specified under the provisions of TADA are a distinct class of persons and the procedure prescribed for trying them for the aggravated and incensed nature of offences are under different classification distinguishable from the ordinary criminals and procedure - Such distinction and classification of grouping of the accused and the offences to be tried under TADA are to achieve the meaningful purpose and object of the Act as reflected from the preamble as well as the statement of objects and reasons - Therefore, TADA is a special provision for special purpose which warranted a departure from the ordinary procedural law, hence plea of discriminatory treatment for want of availability of ordinary procedural law would not be available - Order accordingly.
