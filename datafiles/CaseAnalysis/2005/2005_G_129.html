Basavarajappa v Gurubasamma and others

Supreme Court of India

01 February 2005

Case Analysis

Bench

Ashok Bhan, A.K. Mathur

Where Reported

2005 Indlaw SC 1811; (2005) 12 SCC 290

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Land & Property

Summary: Family & Personal - Land & Property - Hindu Adoptions and Maintenance Act, 1956, ss.12(c) and 13 - Partition - Share of adopted child in ancestral property - Entitled to - Appeal by grant of special leave was directed against the final judgment and decree in appeal passed by HC - Single Judge with some modifications regarding the share to be inherited by the parties had affirmed the judgment and decree passed by the first appellate court in which first appellate court took the view that deceased had become the absolute owner of the properties being the sole surviving male member of the joint Hindu family and the adoption made by him did not divest him of the absolute ownership of the properties - Such view was taken by the court on the interpretation of ss.12(c) and 13 of Act - Whether what would be the share of appellant in the ancestral property - Held, HC as well as the first appellate court erred in holding that the adoption of the appellant did not have the effect of divesting deceased of the properties to the extent of half share - Properties held by deceased were admittedly ancestral - On adoption, the adoptee would get transplanted in the family in which he was adopted with the same rights as that of a natural-born son - Legal effect of giving a child in adoption was to transfer the child from the family of his birth to the family of his adoption - Adoptee would severe all his ties with the family from which he was taken in adoption - Interpreting ss.12(vi) of Act, SC held that the adoptee ceases to have any ties with the family of his birth - Correspondingly, these ties would automatically be replaced by those created by the adoption in the adopted family - Adopted child becomes a coparcener in the joint Hindu family property - Sitabai v. Ramchandra 1969 Indlaw SC 113, relied on - Vew taken by the first appellate court and the HC that deceased even after the adoption continued to be the absolute owner of the property being the sole surviving coparcener was incorrect - On adoption, the appellant/adopted child became a coparcener with deceased and entitled to his coparcenary interest in the ancestral properties held by deceased - Appellant became entitled to half share in the joint Hindu family of his father as a coparcener like a natural son - SC in one such case ahd held that after considering the scheme of ss.11, 12 and 14 of Act that on adoption the adopted child would become a coparcener in the adopted family after severing all his ties with the family from which was adopted - Sitabai v. Ramchandra 1969 Indlaw SC 113, relied on - As the appellant had been validly adopted, he became entitled to half share of the ancestral properties as a coparcener - On the death of deceased in the year 1982, succession opened - Notional partition u/s. 6 of Act took place between deceased and his adopted son of the joint Hindu family properties - Father and son became entitled to half share each - Half share of the property which fell to the share of deceased had to be treated as self-acquired property in terms of s.6 of 1956 Act and liable to be divided in equal shares amongst the adopted son and the daughters i.e. each one of them would get 1/9th share out of the half property which had fallen to the share of deceased which would be equivalent to 1/18th share of the entire property held by deceased - Appellant would be entitled to the ancestral house in which he was living along with his adoptive father during his lifetime unless he chooses to divide the same and after his death, if there was no other coparcener, then the property would revert back to all the heirs of his father i.e. as per Schedule of Act - Female heirs would have no right to claim partition - Deceased had married off all his daughters during his lifetime - Right of the appellant in the dwelling house would be subject to the right of any of the female heirs if she becomes a widow or was separated from her husband - Narashimaha Murthy v. Susheelabai 1996 Indlaw SC 3932, relied on - Appeal allowed.
