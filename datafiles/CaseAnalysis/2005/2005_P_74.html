Pentakota Satyanarayana and others v Pentakota Seetharatnam and others

Supreme Court of India

29 September 2005

Case Analysis

Bench

AR. Lakshmanan, Ruma Pal

Where Reported

2005 Indlaw SC 1262; (2005) 8 SCC 67; AIR 2005 SC 4362; 2005 (6) ALD(SC) 35; 2005 (4) AWC 3548; 2006 (1) JhrCR(SC) 283; JT 2005 (12) SC 258; 2005 (4) RCR(Civil) 615; 2005(7) SCALE 682; [2005] Supp3 S.C.R. 719

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Land & Property - Family & Personal - Practice & Procedure - Hindu Adoption and Maintenance Act, 1956, s. 22 - Will - Proof of - Testator allegedly executed a Will bequeathing the joint family property to appellants (children born out of his 2nd wife) and to 1st respondent (1st wife) - 1st respondent filed a suit denying the execution of the said Will - 1st respondent contended that she was entitled to 1/4th share and 2nd respondent (adopted son of testator) was entitled to 3/4 share of joint family property - Testator and appellants contested the suit - Trial Court decreed the suit in favour of respondents and in the meantime, testator died - Appellants' appeals before the First Appellate Court and HC were dismissed - Hence the present appeal - (A) Whether Will in question is valid and whether it is proved in compliance with the requirement of s. 68 of the Evidence Act - Held, HC and lower Courts in their judgment held that Will had been duly proved - Courts below also held that the testator was physically healthy and in a sound state of mind at the time of execution - Will was a registered document and the scribe had been examined for proving the Will - No evidence was led in by the respondents to show the exercise of any fraud or undue influence at the time of execution of the Will - No evidence was adduced to show that the testator was not in sound state of mind - Evidence adduced by the appellants/propounders were sufficient to satisfy the conscience of the Court of law that the Will was duly executed by the testator - Signatures of the Registering Officer and of the identifying witnesses affixed to the registration endorsement in the Will were sufficient attestation within the meaning of s. 68 of the Evidence Act - Appellants/propounders who were required to remove the suspicious circumstances have led satisfactory evidences - Testator in his pleadings before the Trial Court unequivocally admitted the execution of Will - Therefore, appellants discharged their burden and established that Will in question was executed by testator and the Will was his last Will - Hence, onus is discharged by the appellant/propounder adducing prima facie evidence proving the competence of the testator and execution of the Will in the manner contemplated by law - (B) Whether the 2nd respondent is the adopted son of the testator - Held, respondents had a heavy onus to discharge the burden lies on them to prove the factum of adoption - 2nd respondent seeks to exclude the natural line of succession to the property by alleging adoption, however, alleged adoptive father himself filed a written statement denying adoption - Plaint filed before the Trial Court also was bereft of details which was essential for proving the adoption - Plaintiff witness stated that he did not remember the date or year of adoption - No adoption deed was executed for such an important event - No invitation card is available on record for such a big ceremony of adoption - 1st respondent (1st wife of the testator) raised a dispute for maintenance, however, she did not claim any maintenance for her adopted son (2nd respondent) - Further, 1st respondent had never pleaded that the adopted son was living with her - None of the witnesses have deposed the date of the alleged adoption function - Therefore, alleged adoption is not true and valid and 2nd respondent/alleged adopted son has no right in the suit property and mesne profits - Hence the Will in question is genuine and appellants and 1st respondent would be entitled to the properties respectively allotted to them under the Will - Appeal allowed.
