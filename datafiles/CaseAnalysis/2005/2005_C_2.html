C. Albert Morris v K. Chandrasekaran and Others

Supreme Court of India

26 October 2005

Case Analysis

Bench

AR. Lakshmanan, Altamas Kabir

Where Reported

2005 Indlaw SC 1046; (2006) 1 SCC 228; 2006 (1) ALD(SC) 106; 2006 (1) AWC 229; JT 2005 (9) SC 127; 2005 (4) RCR(Civil) 603; 2005(8) SCALE 788; [2005] Supp4 S.C.R. 779

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property; Petroleum & Gas

Keywords: Consent, Express Or Implied, Tenancy At Will, Lessee Holding Over, Tenancy At Sufferance, Tenant At Will, Pondicherry Buildings (Lease and Rent Control) Act, 1969, Madras Cinemas (Regulations) Act, 1955

Summary: Land & Property - Constitution of India, 1950 - Petroleum Act, 1934 - Transfer of Property Act, 1882 - Pondicherry Buildings (Lease and Rent Control) Act, 1969 - Madras Cinemas (Regulations) Act, 1955 - Petroleum Rules, 1976 - Appellant-C Albert Morris is tenant of first respondent-K Chandrasekaran (landlord) vide a lease deed for ten years culminating in year 1966 - Appellant is dealer of second respondent - Hindustan Petroleum Corporation Ltd - Said dealership is being carried on in leased site belonging to first respondent - On 15/5/1992, landlord issued notice to appellant seeking vacant possession of property - During pendency of application of restoration, landlord again issued a notice of termination of lease - Landlord sought for a writ of mandamus before High Court directing that licence of appellant to carry on petrol bunk shall not be renewed contending that appellant had lost his right to site - Division Bench gave a finding that appellant is not a lawful tenant and that possession was not legal possession and that earlier suit filed by landlord would not be in any manner prevent him from seeking a writ remedy - (A) Whether by reason of first respondent withdrawing suit of eviction and accepting further rent, he has impliedly permitted appellant to continue as a tenant and further affirmed tenancy? - Held, mere acceptance of rent by landlord -

1st respondent herein from tenant in possession after lease has been determined either by afflux of time or by notice to quit would not create a tenancy so as to confer erstwhile tenant status of a tenant or a right to be in possession - (B) Whether appellant is a statutory tenant on basis of recitals of lease agreement? - Further held, it is abundantly clear from recitals in plaint, schedule to notice and to plaint and also of lease deed that word 'leased out' was only a vacant site to put up a petrol bunk with accessory constructions thereon - Mention of a small shed in current lease is undoubtedly belonged to tenant himself and, therefore, building put up by tenant situated in vacant site belongs to landlord cannot be said to be building of landlord in order to attract statutory protection of Rent Control Act - Appeal dismissed.
