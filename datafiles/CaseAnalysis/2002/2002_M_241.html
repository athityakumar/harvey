Mysore Paper Mills Limited v Association and another

Supreme Court of India

08 January 2002

Case Analysis

Bench

Doraiswamy Raju, G.B. Pattanaik

Where Reported

2002 Indlaw SC 7; (2002) 2 SCC 167; (2002) SCC (L&S) 223; AIR 2002 SC 609; [2002] 108 Comp Cas 652; 2002 (2) CompLJ 252; JT 2002 (1) SC 61; 2002 (1) LLJ 1088; 2002 (1) LLN 654; 2002(1) SCALE 52; 2002 (1) SCJ 16; [2002] 1 S.C.R. 37; 2002 (1) SLT 169; 2002 (1) Supreme 59

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Keywords: Malafides, Controlled, Contract, Labour (Regulation And Abolition) Act, 1970, Eviction of Unauthorized Occupants) Act 1974, Jammu and Kashmir Registrations of Societies Act, 1898, Legality, Order of transfer

Summary: Service - Companies Act, 1956, ss. 617, 619 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 12, 226 - Order of transfer - Legality - Respondent no.2 joined services of appellant - By memorandum respondent no.2 was transferred to regional office - On WP filed by respondents, Single Judge granted stay of order of transfer confirming thereby ex-parte interim order of stay earlier granted and rejecting application of appellant for vacating same - DB held that WP filed were not maintainable against appellant, since it was not 'State or other authority ' within meaning of art. 12 of the Constitution - On reference, Full Bench held that entire company was run as part or enterprise of State Govt. and that everyone of schemes of company were also to be approved by govt.

Held, except taking exception to the finding that the company's business in the manufacture of news printing papers no monopoly of State is enjoyed and that the said assumption was wrong, none of the other factual findings were ever shown to be incorrect or unwarranted and without basis. A careful consideration of the principles of law noticed and the factual details not only found illustrated from the memorandum as well as Articles of Association of the appellant but enumerated from the day-to-day running of the business and administration of the company leave no room for any doubt as to the identity of the appellant being 'other authority' and consequently 'the State' within the meaning of art. 12 of the Constitution. Despite best and serious efforts made on behalf of the appellant, the decision under challenge has not been shown to suffer any infirmity whatsoever to call for interference. Appeals dismissed.

Ratio - Words 'any person or authority' used in art. 12 of the Constitution are not to be confined to only statutory authorities and instrumentalities of State, they may cover any other person or body performing public duties and form of body concerned is not very much relevant.
