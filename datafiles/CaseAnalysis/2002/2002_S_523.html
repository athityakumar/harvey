 Laxman v State of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

27 August 2002

Case Analysis

Bench

G.B. Pattanaik, D.M. Dharmadhikari, Doraiswamy Raju, M.B. Shah, S.N. Variava

Where Reported

2002 Indlaw SC 1921; (2002) 6 SCC 710; (2002) SCC (Cr) 1491; AIR 2002 SC 2973; AIR 2002 SCW 3479; 2002 (3) CCR 247; 2002 (4) Crimes 42; 2002 (6) CRJ 520; 2002 CRLJ 4095; 2003 (1) G.L.R. 1; 2002 (3) JCC 1494; JT 2002 (6) SC 313; 2002 (4) RCR(Criminal) 149; 2002(6) SCALE 135; 2002 (5) SLT 49; [2002] Supp1 S.C.R. 697; 2002 (5) Supreme 557

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Dying Declaration - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 32 - Whether dying declaration of deceased can be relied upon if there is no medical certification to this effect? - Held, yes - If deceased was fully conscious and capable of making a statement, dying declaration cannot be ignored merely because no endorsement made by doctor that deceased was in a fit state of mind to make statement - Dying declaration can be oral or in writing and in any adequate method of communication whether by words or by signs or otherwise will suffice provided indication is positive and definite - No requirement of law that a dying declaration must necessarily be made to a Magistrate and when such statement is recorded by a magistrate there is no specified statutory form for such recording - Consequently, what evidential value or weight has to be attached to such statement necessarily depends on facts and circumstances of each particular case - What is essentially required is that person who records a dying declaration must be satisfied that deceased was in a fit state of mind - Where it is proved by testimony of Magistrate that declarant was fit to make statement even without examination by doctor declaration can be acted upon provided court ultimately holds same to be voluntary and truthful - Certification by doctor is essentially a rule of caution and therefore voluntary and truthful nature of declaration can be established otherwise - Order accordingly.
