Sukhbir Singh v State of Haryana

Supreme Court of India

20 February 2002

Case Analysis

Bench

R.P. Sethi, K.G. Balakrishnan

Where Reported

2002 Indlaw SC 1950; (2002) 3 SCC 327; (2002) SCC (Cr) 616; AIR 2002 SC 1168; 2002 (1) CCR 185; 2002 (2) Crimes 60; 2002 (3) CRJ 792; JT 2002 (3) SC 345; 2002 (2) RCR(Criminal) 57; 2002(2) SCALE 244; 2002 (2) SCJ 139; [2002] 1 S.C.R. 1152; 2002 (2) SLT 135; 2002 (2) Supreme 28; 2002 (1) UC 618B

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 141, 149, 300 and 302 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 136 - Exception 4 of s. 300 - Avail of - Held, in an order u/s. 302 r/w s. 149, IPC, High Court not justified in disturbing finding and holding that prosecution had failed to prove sharing of common object of all accused person - The court does not, by special leave, convert itself into a Court to review evidence for a third time,however, where HC is shown to have failed in appreciating true effect and material change in version given by witnesses,it would not be right for this court to affirm such a decision when it occasions a failure of justice - The power u/art. 136 of Constitution is, extraordinary in amplitude and this court goes into action only to avert miscarriage of justice if existence of perversity is shown in impugned judgment - Unless some serious infirmity or grave failure of justice is shown, this court normally refrains from re-appreciating matter on appeal by special leave - Findings of HC have to be judged by yardstick of reason to ascertain whether such findings were erroneous, perverse and resulted in miscarriage of justice - If conclusions of courts below can be supported by acceptable evidence, Supreme Court will not exercise its overriding powers to interfere with such a decision and if two views of an occurrence are possible view taken by one of courts which is favourable to accused should be given credence - Members of unlawful assembly can be held liable u/s. 149 of IPC if it is shown that they knew before hand that offence actually committed was likely to be committed in prosecution of common object and common object does not require prior concert and a common meeting of mind before attack - Further held, in absence of existence of common object Sukhbir Singh committed offence of culpable homicide without pre-meditation in a sudden fight in heat of passion upon a sudden quarrel and did not act in a cruel or unusual manner and his case is covered by Exception 4 of s. 300, IPC punishable u/s. 304 (Part I) of IPC - Appeal No. 257 of 2002 dismissed and Appeal No. 650 of 1992 partly allowed.
