State of Haryana and another v Ram Singh and another

Supreme Court of India

15 January 2002

Case Analysis

Bench

U.C. Banerjee, N. Santosh Hegde

Where Reported

2002 Indlaw SC 24; (2002) SCC (Cr) 350; (2002) 2 SCC 426; AIR 2002 SC 620; 2002 (1) CCR 68; 2002 (1) Crimes 247; 2002 (2) CRJ 319; 2002 CRLJ 987; 2002 (1) JCC 385; JT 2002 (1) SC 273; 2002 (1) RCR(Criminal) 443; 2002(1) SCALE 149; 2002 (1) SCJ 282; [2002] 1 S.C.R. 208; 2002 (1) SLT 219; 2002 (1) Supreme 130; 2002 (1) UC 306

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Murder, Conviction, Ballistics

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 149, 201, 302 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 27 - Murder - Conviction - Medical evidence - Accused/appellants faced charge u/s. 302 of IPC r/w s. 201 of IPC - Trial Court convicted accused no.1 u/s. 302 of IPC and accused no. 2, 3 and 4 were convicted u/ss. 302/149 of IPC - On appeal by accused, HC partly allowed appeal acquitting accused no.3 - Hence, instant appeals - Accused contended that medical evidence as is available on record completely demolished prosecution case.

Held, HC has dealt with the issue that the thumb marked disclosure statement of accused no.3 casts a lot of doubt as to the involvement of accused no.3 since accused no.3 was arrested only on 13-2-1992 as such disclosure statement of 29-1-1992 cannot be had. It is this inconsistency which was noticed by the HC and accused no.3, at whose instance the ring was supposed to have been recovered, stands acquitted on the ground of benefit of doubt. It is the same prosecutor, which has recovered the pieces of bones, had it exhibited but not produced before the postmortem doctor, who would otherwise be able to identify the bones as that of the deceased. Judgment under appeal admittedly does not contain a whisper even pertaining to the contradictions between eye-witnesses' account and the medical evidence. In the contextual facts, medical evidence runs positively counter to the eye-witnesses' account rendering the ocular testimony not being dependable or trustworthy. There is no credible evidence on record. All of accused are under-trial prisoners being involved in a murder trial. The Court thus has to scrutinise its evidence with a little bit of caution and scrutiny so as to judge their veracity. Admittedly all the supposed eye-witnesses are relations of the deceased. As such they fall within a category of interested witnesses. HC fell into a manifest error in coming to a conclusion as reflected in the Judgment under appeal and which thus cannot be sustained. Appeals disposed of.

Ratio - In appeal against conviction for offence of murder SC would be rather slow to intervene in event of there being concurrent finding of fact, in event finding, which suffers from vice of perversity of any fundamental rules or even definite procedural injustice going to root of prosecution case question of SC being slow in intervention would not arise.
