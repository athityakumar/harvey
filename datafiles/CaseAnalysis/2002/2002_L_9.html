Hira Lall and Sons and Others v Lakshmi Commercial Bank

Supreme Court of India

05 August 2002

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Rajendra Babu, P. Venkatarama Reddi

Where Reported

2002 Indlaw SC 1603; (2002) 6 SCC 389; AIR 2002 SC 2873; 2002 (48) ALR 806; 2002 (5) AWC 4177; 2002 (3) BC 190; 2002 (2) CGLJ 205; [2002] 112 Comp Cas 3; 2002 (4) CompLJ 220; 2002 (2) CRC 509; 2002 (3) JhrCR(SC) 86; 2003 (1) JhrCR(SC) 47; JT 2002 (6) SC 94; 2002 (132) PLR 459; 2002 (3) RCR(Civil) 694; 2002 RD 839; 2002(5) SCALE 474; 2002 (125) STC 3; [2002] Supp1 S.C.R. 444; 2002 (5) Supreme 187; 2002 (2) UC 553

Case Digest

Subject: Corporate

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Trade - Constitution - Civil Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art.139 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1973, s.25 - Recovery of Debts to Banks and Financial Institutions Act, 1993, ss.17,18,31 - Contract - Letter of Credit - Maintainability - Petitioners requested for opening Letters of Credit in favour of Singapore to cover payment of consignment of PVC, C & F Bombay - Respondent issued letter of credit in favour of seller and same was transmitted to seller through negotiating bank authorizing him under Letter of Credit and to claim reimbursement by debiting account of respondent-bank - Seller had shipped goods and negotiated documents as required under Letter of Credit with negotiating bank, which made payment on receipt of original documents issuing bank presented them to buyer and asked buyer to pay amount - Respondent filed application before Tribunal - Appellant/petitioner took stand that ship carrying goods had sink, was informed to bank and documents of goods were received by respondent as sent by appellant/petitioner - Respondent/bank lodged formal claims with Insurance Company under copy to appellant/petitioner which was not accepted - Respondent Bank filed suit against appellants/petitioners that leave to defend had been refused and decree was passed - Appeal was filed against order refusing to grant leave - Meanwhile Recovery of Debts to Banks and Financial Institutions Act, 1993 came into force and said matter was transferred to Tribunal - Appellants/Importers claimed that same matters were pending before HC which could be transferred - Hence, instant appeal - Whether said case should be transferred from Tribunal to HC was justifiable - Held, art.139 of Constitution was not attracted to cases where transfer of case from one HC to another HC was sought for and s.25 of CPC would be applicable since transfer of proceeding was not from one State to another State - Therefore, suit based on insurance claim and claim based on Letter of Credit arise out of different causes of action though it might be true that case pending before HC against Insurance Company and application before Tribunal certain common issues might arise - Thus, exact nature of other suits filed by other parties which were stated to be pending in HC and cause for retention on its file was not available on record - Apart from fact, Court noticed that when exclusive jurisdiction had been given to Tribunal under the Act in respect of matters that could be dealt with u/s.17 of the Act, jurisdiction in other courts to entertain and decide such matters for recovery of debts due to banks and financial institutions stood ousted as provided u/s.18 of the Act. - Further s.31of the Act provides transfer of cases from civil courts to Tribunal - Therefore, relied on Union of India & Anr. vs. Delhi High Court Bar Association & Ors. 2002 Indlaw SC 163, after examining entire scheme of the Act , upheld Constitutional validity of same - Hence, it was expedient for ends of justice to direct transfer of case to HC - Appeal dismissed.
