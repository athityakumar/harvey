Bijoy Singh and Another v State of Bihar

Supreme Court of India

17 April 2002

Case Analysis

Bench

R.P. Sethi, D.M. Dharmadhikari

Where Reported

2002 Indlaw SC 1613; (2002) 9 SCC 147; AIR 2002 SC 1949; 2002 (2) AllCriC 124; 2002 (3) AllCriR 2145; 2002 CRLJ 2623; JT 2002 (Supp1) SC 372; 2002 (2) RCR(Criminal) 544; 2002(3) SCALE 592; [2002] 3 S.C.R. 179; 2002 (2) UC 83

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Criminal Procedure Code, 1973, ss. 154, 155, 156 and 157 - First Information Report - Importance of FIR - Contents and value thereof - Requirement of lodging prompt FIR - Reasons therefore - Requirements of s. 157 - Status of co-accused who has not appealed against conviction - Whether entitled to benefit? - Held, FIR is a report giving information of commission of a cognizable crime which may be made by complainant or by any other person knowing about commission of such an offence - Any information relating to commission of a cognizable offence is required to be reduced to writing by officer-in-charge of Police Station which has to be signed by the person giving it and the substance thereof is required to be entered in a book to be kept by such officer in such form as the state Govt. may prescribe in that behalf - Registration of FIR empowers officer in charge of police station to commence investigation with respect to crime reported to him - A copy of FIR is required to be sent forthwith to Magistrate empowered to take cognizance of such offence - After recording FIR, officer in charge of police station is obliged to proceed in person or depute one of his subordinate officers not below such rank as the state Govt. may, by general or special order, prescribe in that behalf, to proceed to the spot to investigate facts and circumstances of case and if necessary to take measures for discovery and arrest of offender - It has been held time and again that FIR is not a substantive piece of evidence and can only used to corroborate statement of maker u/s. 161 of Evidence Act or to contradict him u/s. 145 of that Act - Cannot be used as evidence against maker at trial if he himself becomes an accused nor to corroborate or contradict other witnesses - It is not a requirement of law that minuets details be recorded in FIR lodged immediately after occurrence - Object of insisting upon lodging of FIR is to obtain earliest information regarding circumstance in which crime was committed - Sending copy of special report to Magistrate as required u/s. 157 of CrPC, 1973 is only external check on working of police agency, imposed by law which is required to be strictly followed - Delay in sending copy of FIR may by itself not render whole of case of prosecution as doubtful - Immediate sending of report mentioned in s. 157, CrPC, 1973 is mandate of law - Delay wherever found is required to be explained by prosecution - If delay is reasonably explained, no adverse inference can be drawn but failure to explain delay would require Court to minutely examine prosecution version for ensuring itself as to whether any innocent person has been implicated in crime or not - In instant case copy of report referred to in s. 157, CrPC, 1973, is shown to have been received by Magistrate - Even though there is a mention in FIR that its copy was sent through special messenger, yet no date or time of sending said report is mentioned - Magistrate, receiving copy of report, has also not noted time of its receipt - Magistrates receiving reports u/s. 157, CrPC, 1973, particularly when it relates to commission of heinous crime are required to note not only date but also time of receipt of copy thereof - Prosecution failed to explain delay in sending copy of report in terms of s. 157, CrPC, 1973, to Magistrate of area - This aspect highlighted by learned counsel of appellant to content that many of accused were innocent and wrongly roped in case - Is some substance in such a submission - Further held, Court has set up a judicial precedent that where on evaluation of case if Court reaches conclusion that no conviction of any accused is possible, benefit of that decision must be extended to co-accused, similarly situated, though he has not challenged order by way of an appeal - Appeals partly allowed.
