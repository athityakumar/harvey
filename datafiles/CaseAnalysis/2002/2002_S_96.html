Shabir Ahmad v Sham Lal and Another

Supreme Court of India

08 February 2002

Case Analysis

Bench

S.S.M. Quadri, S.N. Variava

Where Reported

2002 Indlaw SC 1943; (2002) 3 SCC 118; AIR 2002 SC 1036; 2002 (48) ALR 369; JT 2002 (1) SC 646; 2002 (131) PLR 518; 2002 (1) RCR(Rent) 306; 2002(2) SCALE 68; 2002 (1) SCJ 679; [2002] 1 S.C.R. 910; 2002 (1) SLT 757; 2002 (1) Supreme 589

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Rent Control

Keywords: East Punjab Urban Rent Restrictions Act, 1949

Summary: Land & Property - Rent Control - East Punjab Urban Rent Restrictions Act, 1949, ss. 2(g) and 13(3)(a)(i)(a) - Respondents filed a petition for eviction of appellant on ground of bona fide requirement of respondents for residential purposes, u/s. 13(3)(a)(i)(a) - Appellant contested eviction petition on ground that premises let out to him is a non-residential building and, therefore, his eviction cannot be sought under said provision - Whether premises is a residential building as defined in s. 2(g)? - If a building is being used solely for purpose of business or trade, it is a non-residential building and a building other than a non-residential building is a residential building - Approach in interpreting expression 'shop-cum-flat' having regard to dictionary meaning of word 'flat' is not proper - Courts ought not to be unmindful of consequence of too much reliance on dictionaries and Lexicons lest they go astray in interpreting recitals in a deed or document or provisions in a Statute - Held, appellant has been using premises solely for purpose of running a Hair Dressing Saloon from inception of tenancy - From deed of conveyance, letter of allotment of site, plan and agreement of tenancy it is evident that 'shop-flat' is a non-residential building within meaning of Act and premises is a part of a non-residential building and in view of embargo cannot be used for residential purposes - Order accordingly.
