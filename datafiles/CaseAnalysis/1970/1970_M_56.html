National Bell Company and another v Metal Goods Manufacturing Company Private Limited and Another

Supreme Court of India

18 March 1970

Case Analysis

Bench

J.M. Shelat, C.A. Vaidyialingam

Where Reported

1970 Indlaw SC 524; (1970) 3 SCC 665; AIR 1971 SC 898; 22 PTC 586; [1971] 1 S.C.R. 70

Case Digest

Subject: Intellectual Property

Keywords: Prohibition Act

Summary: Intellectual Property - Trade and Merchandise Marks Act, 1958, ss.9,11,32 (c) - Trademark - Disputes - Challenged - Respondent/company filed suit in Trial Court on ground of infringement of Trademarks - Applications for rectification was filed by appellant/defendant wherein Trial Court directed them to file in HC and till then stayed suit proceedings - Appellant/defendant filed application in HC and was allowed by Single Judge by cancelling Trade mark of respondent - Aggrieved respondent filed appeal and was allowed by DB - Hence, instant appeals - Whether judgment of HC was justified - Held, in absence of any evidence regarding manufacture and sale of bells it would not be possible to say with any certainty that respondent company did not come to know about it till 1958 and therefore presumed that appellant had started using word 'Fifty' on its bells in that year - Appellant started production only in 1957 and in 1959 respondent company filed suit against appellant - Similar notice of infringement followed by suit was also taken given to 'X' but suit became in fructuous on account of lack of jurisdiction - This evidence negatives any abandonment of trademarks or letting infringements go unchallenged or misleading other manufacturers that respondent company would not interfere if they were to use the same marks - Rights in trade mark could be abandoned by its owner as long as he remains registered proprietor of trade mark and carried on business to which trade mark was attached and plea of abandonment was difficult to sustain - Though evidence was produced by appellant companies to show that there were other bells in market with 'Fifty' or '50' inscribed on them no evidence was led to show that use of word 'Fifty' or numeral '50' was substantial - Thus it was impossible to sustain contention founded on s. 32 (c) of the 1958 Act - Power to rectify was undoubtedly discretionary and when it was properly exercised Appellate Court would not interfere - Trial Court did not appreciate principle embodied in ss. 32 and 11 Of the 1958 Act - DB was justified in interfering with discretion exercised by Trial Court - Appeals dismissed.
