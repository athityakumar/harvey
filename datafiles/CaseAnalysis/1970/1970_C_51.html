A. K. T. K. M. Vishnudatta Andharjanam v Commissioner of Agricultural Income Tax, Trivandrum

Supreme Court of India

05 May 1970

Case Analysis

Bench

A.N. Grover, J.C. Shah, K.S. Hegde

Where Reported

1970 Indlaw SC 235; (1970) 2 SCC 165; AIR 1970 SC 2055; [1970] 78 ITR 58; 1970 KLT 352; [1971] 1 S.C.R. 535

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Income-tax Act, 1961 - Indian Income Tax Act, 1922, s.10(2A) Sale of teak trees - Balance amount - Agricultural Income - Capital structure - Revenue receipt -Assessee filed a return showing a net agricultural income of Rs.12,558-76 for A/Y 1963-64 - When the matter came up for hearing before Agricultural Income tax Officer another statement showing an amount of Rs. 43,250-00 as income from teak trees was filed - Agricultural Income tax Officer disallowed certain expenses and assessed the income for the year 1963-64 at Rs.62,021 - For A/Y 1964-65 a return was filed declaring a net agricultural income of Rs. 25,733-63 - No income was shown from sale of teak trees - Agricultural Income tax Officer found that teak trees had been sold for a lump sum of Rs.76,500-00 out of which Rs.43,250 had been received in the previous year 1963-64 and he included amount in that year's income - Balance amount of Rs. 33,250 was received in previous year corresponding to A/Y 1964-65 - Assessee filed appeals before Additional Appellate Assistant Commissioner who confirmed assessment and dismissed the appeals - HC not agree with the contention of assesses that amounts received by sale of teak trees constituted capital and were not agricultural income - Whether receipt from sale of teak trees for purpose of planting area with rubber was capital in nature and exempt from Act - Held, that HC in the judgment under appeal was particularly impressed with profit motive of assessee in, planting teak trees although that was done several years ago - But it was overlooked that profit motive was not decisive - Accretion to capita does not become taxable income merely because an asset was acquired in hope that it may be sold at a profit - Trees so long as they are uncut form a part of the land - If they are cut with roots once and for all a part of the assets was disposed of - Sale proceeds on account of their disposal cannot constitute revenue because by removing roots the source, from which fresh growth of trees can take place was also removed - Sale of such trees thus affects capital structure and cannot give rise to a revenue receipt - Judgment of HC was set aside - Appeal allowed.
