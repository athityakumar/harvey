Nainsingh v Koonwarjee and Others

Supreme Court of India

02 April 1970

Case Analysis

Bench

K.S. Hegde, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1970 Indlaw SC 79; (1970) 1 SCC 732; AIR 1970 SC 997; 1970 (2) ALT(SC) 297; [1971] 1 S.C.R. 207

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, ss. 151, 105 (2) and O. 41 r. 23 and O. 43 - Jagir Abolition Act, 1953 - Jagirs abolition - Claim over land - Entitlement of -Appellant/plaintiff was Jagirdar of suit properties - One 'B' was his tenant and said 'B' died in the year 1947 leaving behind no male issues and his wife had predeceased him - 'B' had 2daughters who were living at the time of his death - After 'B's death, defendants Nos. 1 to 5 who were his distant relations took possession of suit properties and changed in their names - Aggrieved by that appellant filed suit - Trial Court decreed suit - On appeal, decree was confirmed - On appeal, HC agreed with Trial Court and Appellate Court and held that in view of abolition of jagirs under 1953 Act appellant/plaintiff had lost his title to the suit properties and therefore he could not get a decree for possession of suit properties - Held, SC opined that HC erred in holding that correctness of remand order was open to review by it - Order in question was made under O. 41 r. 23 of CPC - That order was appealable under O. 43 of CPC - As the same was not appealed against, its correctness was no more open to examination in view of s. 105 (2) of CPC which laid down that where any party aggrieved by an order of remand from which an appeal lies did not appeal therefrom he should thereafter be precluded from disputing its correctness - HC misconceived the scope of its inherent powers - Court could not make use of the special provisions of s. 151 of CPC where a party had his remedy provided ,elsewhere in CPC and he neglected to avail himself of the, same - Further power u/s. 151 of CPC could not be exercised as an appellate power - HC was not right in holding that in view of the abolition of Jagirs, plaintiff had lost all rights in suit properties - It was true that in view of provisions of 1953 Act, the suit properties vested in State - However, it was conceded at the bar that if plaintiff was proved to have been the owner of suit properties on the day 1953 Act came into force, he was entitled to compensation provided in that Act - Therefore appellant/plaintiff was interested in establishing that on the date the Act came into force, he was the full owner of suit properties - Facts of instant case fall within the rule laid down by SC in Himatrao v. Jaikishandas and Ors., 1966 Indlaw SC 345 - Order accordingly.
