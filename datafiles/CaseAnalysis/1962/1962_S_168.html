R. R. Chari v State of Uttar Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

28 March 1962

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo

Where Reported

1962 Indlaw SC 158; AIR 1962 SC 1573; 1962 CRLJ 510; 1962 (2) ILR(All) 904; [1963] 1 S.C.R. 121

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Defence & Security Forces; Land & Property

Keywords: Defence Regulations

Summary: Criminal - Defence of India Rules, 1939 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 161,165,120(B) and 467 - Criminal Procedure Code, 1898, ss. 197, 219 and 206 - The appellant R R Chari was a permanent employee in a gazetted post under Govt. of Assam - In 1941, his services were lent to Govt. of India - First appointment which appellant held under Govt. of India was that of Deputy Director of Metals in Munitions Production Department at Calcutta - Then he came to Delhi on similar work in office of Master-General of Ordnance which was Steel Priority Authority during War period - He was subsequently transferred to Kanpur as Assistant Iron a Steel Controller in 1945 - He became Deputy Iron and Steel Controller, Kanpur Circle which post he held for one month in September, 1945 - Period covered by charges which were eventually formed against appellant and, others is from 01-01-1946 to 20-09-1946 - Appellant proceeded on leave for four months and did not return to' service either under Govt. of India or under Assam Govt. - It appears that while appellant had proceeded on leave Govt. of India wrote to Assam Govt. on 08-02-1947, intimating that it had decided to replace services of appellant at disposal of Assam Govt. on expiry of leave granted to him with effect from September, 21, 1946 - A subsequent notification issued by Central Govt. extended leave up to 13-05-1947 - On this latter date, Central Govt. suspended appellant, and on a warrant issued by District Magistrate, Kanpur, he was arrested on 28-10-1947 - Subsequently, he was released on bail - Thereafter, Govt. of India accorded sanction for prosecution of appellant u/s. 197 of CrPC, 1973 on 31-01-1949 - A Charge-sheet was submitted by prosecution alleging that appellant along with three of his former assistants had committed various acts of conspiracy, corruption and forgery during period 01-01-1946 to 20-09-1946 - HC confirmed appellant's conviction u/ss. 161 and 467 and sentences imposed by Trial Court in that behalf - Whether appellant's case can be said to have been pending before a magistrate at relevant time, and this dispute has to be decided in light of provisions contained in s. 219 of CrPC, 1973? - Held, s. 219 confers power on committing Magistrate to summon and examine supplementary witnesses after commitment and before commencement of trial, and to bind them over in manner here in before provided to appear and give evidence - It is on provisions of this section that appellant's case rests argument is that since committing Magistrate is given power to summon supplementary witnesses even after an order of commitment has been passed, that shows that committing Magistrate still hold jurisdiction over case and in that sense, case must be deemed to be pending before him - Power to summon supplementary witnesses and take their evidence is merely a supplementary power for recording evidence and no more - This supplementary power does not postulate continuance of jurisdiction in committing Magistrate to deal with case - It is significant that this power can be exercised even by a Magistrate other than committing magistrate, provided he is empowered by or u/s. 206 and clearly, case covered by commitment order passed by one magistrate cannot be said to be pending before another Magistrate who may be empowered to summon supplementary witnesses - When s. 10 of Criminal law Amendment Act refer to cases pending before any Magistrate, it obviously refers to cases pending before magistrates who can deal with them on merits in accordance with law and this requirement is plainly not satisfied in regard to any case in which a commitment order had been passed by committing Magistrate - After order of commitment is passed, case cannot be said to be pending before committing Magistrate within meaning of s. 10 - Therefore, HC was right in coming to conclusion that s. 10 did not apply to present case and so, Addl. Sessions Judge had jurisdiction to try case in accordance with provisions of CrPC,1973 - It is true that in dealing with this point, HC has proceeded on consideration that appellant's trial had actually commenced before Addl Sessions Judge even prior to 28-07-1952 - HC has found that at relevant time, appellant continued to be in employment of affairs of Federation and had not reverted to Assam Govt. - This finding of HC is right - It is thus clear that though Govt. of India had originally thought of replacing appellant's services with Assam Govt. at end of leave which was proposed to be granted to him, subsequent events which led to an investigation against appellant and his suspension caused a change in attitude of Govt. of India and it decided to continue him in its employment in order that he should face a trial on charges which were then subject matter of investigation - There is no order reverting him to Assam Govt. passed by Govt. of India and there is no order passed by Assam Govt. at all on this subject - Therefore, conclusion is inescapable that appellant was employed in affairs of Federation at time when sanction was accorded - Regard to fact that at relevant time appellant was employed in connection with affairs of Federation, it was Governor-General alone who was competent to accord sanction - Therefore, sanction granted by Governor-General for prosecution of appellant is valid - But in respect of s. 161, the conviction of the appellant under that section is set aside on the ground that his trial for the said offence was without jurisdiction since his prosecution in that behalf was commenced without a valid sanction as required by s.6 of the prevention of Corruption Act - Appeal disposed of.
