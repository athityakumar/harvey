 Izhar Ahmad Khan v Union of India

Supreme Court of India

16 February 1962

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, A.K. Sarkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, K.C. Das Gupta, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar

Where Reported

1962 Indlaw SC 432; AIR 1962 SC 1052; [1962] 3 S.C.R. 235; [1962] Supp3 S.C.R. 235

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Defence & Security Forces; Foreigners; International

Keywords: Foreign Law

Summary: Foreigners - Citizenship Act, 1955, ss. 9(2) and 18 - Citizenship Rules, 1956, r. 3 of sch. III, rr. 14 and 30(1)(2) - S. 9(2), r. 3, whether constitutionally valid? - (i) Constitutional validity of s. 9(2) on touchstone of arts. 19(1)(e) and 14 - (ii) Constitutional validity of rule making power of Central Govt. u/s. 18 of Act, on touchstone of excessive delegation - (iii) Constitutional validity of r. 3 of sch. III of Rules on ground that such rule provides for prescribing rule of evidence and in garb of it, Central Govt. had in fact prescribed rule of substantive law which was outside purview of delegated authority conferred by s. 9(2)- (i) Art. 19 proceeds on assumption that person who claims rights guaranteed by it is a citizen of India - If basic status of citizenship is validly terminated by a Parliamentary statute, person whose citizenship is terminated has no right to claim fundamental rights u/art. 19 Finding on point (i), challenge to s. 9(2) on ground that it enables rule-making authority to make a rule to deprive citizenship rights of petitioners cannot be sustained (ii) Acquisition of citizenship of foreign country u/s. 9(1) is either by naturalisation or registration and lastly it could be otherwise than these two, i.e. by otherwise voluntarily acquiring citizenship of any foreign country and it is with respect to this last category of acquisition that Rules were made - Basic principle on which Act proceeds and which has been recognised by art. 9 of Constitution itself is that no Indian citizen can claim a dual or plural citizenship - Finding on point (ii), having regard to scheme of Act and principles enunciated in its relevant sections, it cannot be held that in enacting s. 9(2), Legislature has abdicated its essential legislative function in favour of rule making authority - (iii) Whether r. 3 is rule of evidence or rule of substantive law - Elaborate discussion on law of evidence and its genesis - Test propounded by Court: In deciding question as to whether a rule about irrebuttable presumption is a rule of evidence or not, it seems that proper approach to adopt would be to consider whether fact A from proof of which a presumption is required to be drawn about existence of fact B, is inherently relevant in matter of proving fact B and has inherently any probative or persuasive value in that behalf or not - If fact A is inherently relevant in proving existence of fact B and to any rational mind it would bear a probative or persuasive value in matter of proving existence of fact B, then a rule prescribing either a rebuttable presumption or an irrebuttable presumption in that behalf would be a rule of evidence - On other hand, if fact A is inherently not relevant in proving existence of fact B or has no probative value in that behalf and yet a rule is made prescribing of a rebuttable or an irrebuttable presumption in that connection that rule would be a rule of substantive law and not a rule of evidence' - R. 3 examined - Finding on point (iii), r. 3 is a rule of evidence and so within scope of authority conferred on Central Govt. u/s. 9(2)(A) Held, s. 9(2), r. 3, to be constitutionally valid - Petition dismissed.
