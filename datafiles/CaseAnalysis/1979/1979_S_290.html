 Ganga Sugar Company Limited v State of Uttar Pradesh and Others

Supreme Court of India

20 September 1979

Case Analysis

Bench

V.R. Krishna Iyer, Y.V. Chandrachud, N.L. Untwalia, P.N. Shingal, A.D. Koshal

Where Reported

1979 Indlaw SC 68; (1980) SCC (Tax) 90; (1980) 1 SCC 223; AIR 1980 SC 286; [1980] 1 S.C.R. 769; 1980 (45) STC 36

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Indirect Tax; Agriculture & Agri. Products

Summary: Agriculture & Agri. Products - Indirect Tax - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art.14 - Imposition of Tax - Sustainability - Appeals spring from a common demand for tax by the State from a number of sugar mills on the purchase of sugarcane at a rate related by weight, not value, a pragmatic novelty in the sales tax pattern which has provoked an argument about its validity - Whether imposition of Tax on the purchase of sugarcane at a rate related by weight, not value was sustainable - Held, tax on sale or purchase should be on the occurrence of a taxing event of sale transaction - Beyond that was left to the free play of the legislature, subject, of course, to the contra-indications about capricious, arbitrary or irrational features - It was a superstition, cultivated by familiarity, to consider that all sales-tax should necessarily have nexus with the price of the commodity - Price as basis was not only usual but also safe to avoid uneven, unequal burdens, although it was conceivable that a legislature could regard prices which fluctuate frequently, as too impractical to tailor the purchase tax - It might even be, in rare cases, iniquitous to link purchase tax with price, if more sensible bases could be found - Supposing a legislature classified sales-tax on the basis of human categories and reduced the rate or exempts the tax in respect of abject destitutes, or starving flood victims or notoriously hazardous habitations, with respect to necessity of life - Such differentiation could not be castigated as discrimination out of hand - Of course, it was common and commonsense that reliable standard was the price, although in regard to customs duties there were still items levied on the nature of the goods rather than its value in money - It was sufficient to state that the practice had been to impose purchase tax by weight of cane - Also, in weight of cane its sucrose content and its price had a close nexus, although, theoretically, they might appear unconnected - SC had stated that the quantity crushed, the sugar produced and the profits earned, had a substantial linkage - Quality of cane over the whole of Uttar Pradesh varied over a range of 8 to 10 per cent which, if converted to purchase tax, might inflict a trivial difference per quintal - Moreover, for many years past the bulk of the sugar was absorbed by 'levy' by the State and in the costing components the State, as buyer of sugar, had borne the burnt - SC had no facts to hold that arbitrary or various burdens were cast because weight, not price, had been the yardstick for tax - Fine-tuning to attain perfect equality might be a fiscal ideal but, in the rough and tumble of work-a-day economics, the practical was preferred to the ideal, provided glaring caprice of gross disparity did not make the levy arbitrary or frolicsome - Art. 14 of Constitution was not intellectual chess unrelated to actual impact or the wear and tear of life but even-handed justice with some play in the joints - Appeals dismissed.
