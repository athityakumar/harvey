Gujarat Steel Tubes Limited v Gujarat Steel Tubes Mazdoor Sabha

Supreme Court of India

19 November 1979

Case Analysis

Bench

V.R. Krishna Iyer, D.A. Desai, A.D. Koshal

Where Reported

1979 Indlaw SC 37; (1980) SCC (L&S) 197; (1980) 2 SCC 593; AIR 1980 SC 1896; 1980 (40) FLR 152; 1980 LabIC 1004; 1980 (1) LLJ 137; 1980 (1) LLN 230; [1980] 2 S.C.R. 146

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Summary: Majority View

Labour & Industrial Law - Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Act, 1946 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 226 and 227 - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, ss. 11-A, 10-A and 11 - Appellant-management and respondents-workmen had entered into agreement, according to the settlement, it was not open to the workmen to resort to a strike till the expiry of a period of five years ; nor could the management declare a lock-out till then - Any disputes arising between the parties, according to the terms arrived at, were to be sorted out through negotiations or failure that, by recourse to arbitration - Dispute was raised by the Gujarat Steel Tubes Mazdoor Sabha soon thereafter over the implementation of the recommendations of the Central Engineering Wage Board, the payment of bonus for the year 1971 and wages for an earlier lock-out - Respondents-workmen went on a massive strike - Management terminated the workers - Industrial dispute was raised - Both sides agreed to resolve their disagreement by arbitral reference u/s. 10A of the Act - Award was passed - Arbitrator held the action of the management warranted while the HC reversed the award and directed reinstatement - Hence, instant appeals - (A) Whether the discharge of the workmen en masse was on innocuous termination or a disciplinary action? - Held, Model Standing Orders prescribed u/s. 15 of the Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Act, 1946, apply to said factory wherein termination of workers services on giving the prescribed notice or wages in lieu of such notice was provided for; but Clause (4A) required reasons for such termination of service of permanent workmen to be recorded and, if asked for, communicated - Punitive discharge was prohibited unless opportunity to show-cause against charges of misconduct was afforded (Standing Order 25) - In the facts of the instant case, it was established that management did remove workers from service, on punitive grounds - Action was taken under the general law or the Standing Orders, was illegal in the absence of individualised chargesheets, proper hearing and personalised punishment, if found guilty - (B) Whether the evidence before the Arbitrator even if accepted at its face value, established any misconduct of any discharged workman? - Held, passive participation in a strike which was both illegal and unjustified did not ipso facto invite dismissal or punitive discharge - There must be active individual excess such as masterminding the unjustified aspects of the strike - Absent such gravamen in the accusation, the extreme economic penalty of discharge was wrong - Discharge orders, though approved by the Arbitrator, invalid - (C) Scope of s. 11A of the Industrial Disputes Act - Power of Arbitrators - Held, s. 11A did clothe the Arbitrator with similar power as Tribunals, despite the doubt created by the abstruse absence of specific mention of 'arbitrator' in s. 11A - Arbitrator would fall within the summit of the term 'Tribunal' as used in sub-section (2) of s. 11A - (D) Interference by HC u/art. 226/227 of the Constitution - Held, HC acted within the four corners of its jurisdiction u/art. 227 of the Constitution while interfering with the finding of the Arbitrator that the workmen were correctly punished with dismissal if the orders of discharge could be construed as such - Appeals dismissed.

Minority View

Labour & Industrial Law - Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Act, 1946 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 226 and 227 - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, ss. 11-A, 10-A and 11 - (A) Whether the discharge of the workmen en masse was on innocuous termination or a disciplinary action? - Held, under Model Standing Orders 23 and 25 the management had the power to effect termination of the services of an employee by having recourse to either of them - In action taken under Model Standing Orders 23 no element of punishment was involved and the discharge was a discharge simpliciter ; and no opportunity to the concerned employee to show-cause against the termination was provided for - For an order to be ' founded' on misconduct, it must, be intended to have been passed by way of punishment, that is, it must be intended to chastise or cause pain in body or mind or harm or loss in reputation or money to the concerned worker - If such an intention could not be spelled out of the prevailing circumstances, the order of discharge or the reasons for which it was ostensibly passed, it could not be regarded as an order of dismissal - Such would be the case when the employer orders discharge in the interests of the factory or of the general body of workers themselves - In the instant case, orders of discharge of the workmen could not be regarded as orders of their dismissal and were, on the other hand, orders of discharge simpliciter properly passed under Model Standing Orders 23 - (B) Scope of s. 11A of the Industrial Disputes Act - Power of Arbitrators - Held, Arbitrator could not exercise the powers conferred on a Tribunal u/s. 11A of the 1947 Act and could not, therefore, interfere with the punishment awarded by the management to the workmen (even if the discharge could be regarded a punishment) - Powers given to a Tribunal u/s. 11A were not exercisable by an Arbitrator who, therefore, could not interfere with the punishment (awarded by the employer) in case he finds misconduct proved - (C) Interference by HC u/art. 227 of the Constitution - Held, while discharging its functions as envisaged by art. 227, did not sit as a Court of appeal over the award of the Arbitrator but exercises limited jurisdiction which extends only to seeing that the Arbitrator has functioned within the scope of his legal authority - HC exceeded the limits of its jurisdiction in interfering with the said punishment purporting to set in the exercise of its powers u/art. 227 of the Constitution - Judgment of the HC must be reversed and the order of the Arbitrator restored - Order accordingly.
