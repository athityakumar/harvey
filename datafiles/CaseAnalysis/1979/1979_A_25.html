Prakash Chandra v Angadlal and Others

Supreme Court of India

24 January 1979

Case Analysis

Bench

R.S. Pathak, Jaswant Singh, A.P. Sen

Where Reported

1979 Indlaw SC 508; (1979) 4 SCC 393; AIR 1979 SC 1241; 1979 UJ 277

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Possession Of Land, Settlement of declaration suit

Summary: Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Suit for specific performance - Settlement of declaration suit - Legality - Appellant (plaintiff) filed a suit for specific performance - Trial Court refused for specific performance but granted a decreed for Rs.1000 by way of damages and for the refund of amount paid towards the price of the site in question - First appeal was allowed by Lower Appellate Court, against which second appeal was filed and First Appellate Court decree was set aside and Trial Court decree was restored - However, HC observed that if civil suit of 1956 was regarded as decreed in favour of appellant's vendors for declaration of their title and for possession against 'U', they would succeed in obtaining possession from appellant's predecessor-in-interest 'G' because he claimed to be in possession through 'U' and HC held that appellant by executing the document had permitted their vendors to settle the dispute in suit of 1956 by conveying ownership in the land to respondent nos. 1 and 3, who had succeeded to the interest of 'G' - Whether relief of specific performance being a discretionary relief granted in equity should be refused to appellant.

Held, HC has erred in holding that the sale deed amounted to an amicable settlement of the suit, and that it was the settlement contemplated by the appellant when executing the document dt.18-11-1957. The finding of Fist Appellate Court that the sale effected under the deed dt.6-12-1957 amounts to a breach of contract by the vendors is plainly right. The conduct of the appellant has not been such as to disentitle him to the relief of specific performance. He has acted fairly throughout and there is nothing to show that by any act of omission or commission he encouraged his vendor's to enter into the sale with the respondent nos. 1 and 2. There is no evidence that the appellant secured and unfair advantage over his vendors when he entered into the agreement. Nor is there anything to prove that the performance of the contract would involve the respondents in some hardship which they did not foresee. There is no reason why the appellant should not be granted the relief of specific performance. Having regard to the materials, Court was unable to hold that any permanent constructions have been raised on the said land. If the respondent nos. 1 and 2 have in fact raised any constructions on the site, it will be open to them to remove the building material when possession is delivered to the appellant. Appellant is required to deposit Rs.1000, which is the balance of the purchase price, in Trial Court for payment to respondent no. 3 within one month and on such deposit respondent no. 3 will execute a sale deed in favour of appellant in accordance with the terms of the contract and respondent nos. 1 and 2 will join the conveyance. On execution of sale deed, the appellant will be entitled to delivery of possession of land from respondent nos. 1 and 2. Impugned judgment and decree of HC are set aside. Appeal allowed.
