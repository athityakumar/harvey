Municipal Corporation of Delhi v Tek Chand Bhatia

Supreme Court of India

11 October 1979

Case Analysis

Bench

A.P. Sen, S. Murtaza Fazal Ali

Where Reported

1979 Indlaw SC 604; (1980) SCC (Cr) 87; (1980) 1 SCC 158; 1980 (17) ACC 1; AIR 1980 SC 360; 1980 CRLJ 316; [1980] 1 S.C.R. 910

Case Digest

Subject: Consumer Protection; Health & Drug; Socio-Economic

Summary: Health & Drug - Socio-economic - Food adulteration - Prevention of Food Adulteration Act, 1954, ss. 7(1) and 16(1)(a) - High Court found that insect-infested food article, cannot be treated as 'adulterated' within meaning of s. 2(1)(f) of Act unless it is further proved to be 'otherwise unfit for human consumption' - Acquitted the respondent - (A) Validity of acquittal - Held, there is no. proof that the samples were taken from tins bearing the manufacturer's label guaranteeing purity of goods, nor is there any such warranty in the invoice Ext. DW 3/A - It is, however, urged that the tins bore the imprint 'Good' - There is nothing to substantiate this fact, and even if it were so, it is of little consequence - The word 'Good' on which great emphasis is placed merely contains a description of the goods - At the most it amounts to 'puffing of goods' - The word 'Good' is not a warranty as to quality - The respondent is, therefore, not protected u/s. 19 (2) of Act r/w r. 12A of the rules framed under the Act - The order of acquittal of the respondent is set aside, and he is convicted for having committed an offence punishable u/s. 16(1)(a) r/w. s. 7 (1) of the Act - (B) Whether the respondent should be released on probation of good conduct u/s. 4 of the Probation of Offenders Act, 1958? - Held, the provisions of s. 20 AA inserted by Act 34 of 1976 interdicts the court from applying the provisions of Probation of Offenders Act to a prosecution under the Prevention of Food Adulteration Act, 1954 - In the instant case, the respondent is a man aged 75 years - The offence was committed on 01-08-1968 i.e., more than eleven years ago - The order of acquittal was based on the decision of the HC in Dhanraj v. Municipal Corporation of Delhi (ILR (1972) 2 Delhi 681) - The samples were taken from sealed tins - These are all mitigating circumstances - Supreme Court accordingly refrain from passing a substantive sentence of imprisonment and instead of sentence the respondent to the period already undergone and to pay fine of Rs. 2, 000/- or in default to undergo rigorous imprisonment for a period of three months - Appeal allowed.
