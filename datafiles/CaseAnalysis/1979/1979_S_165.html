Yogendra Morarji v State of Gujarat

Supreme Court of India

10 December 1979

Case Analysis

Bench

R.S. Sarkaria, D.A. Desai, O. Chinnappa Reddy

Where Reported

1979 Indlaw SC 330; (1980) 2 SCC 218; (1980) SCC (Cr) 394; AIR 1980 SC 660; 1980 CRLJ 459; 1980 CRLR 219

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 99, 100, 102, 300 and 302 - Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 3, 5 and 105 - Criminal Procedure Code, 1973, s. 313 - Whether the accused had discharged the burden that lay on him u/s. 105, Evidence Act to prove that right of private defence accrued to him and if yes, whether this right extended to voluntary causing of death? - A person who is in imminent and reasonable danger of losing his life or limb may in the exercise of right of self-defence inflict any harm, even extending to death on his assailant either when the assault is attempted or directly threatened; this is subject to the rule that the harm or death inflicted is not substantially disproportionate to and incommensurate with the quality and character of the perilous act or threat intended to be repelled; u/s. 105 of the Evidence Act, the burden of proving the existence of circumstances bringing the case within any of the General Exceptions in the IPC, 1860 or within any special exception or proviso contained in any other part of the Code or in any other law, shall be on the accused person and the Court shall presume the absence of such circumstances; the accused may discharge his burden by establishing a mere balance of probabilities in his favour with regard to the said circumstances; the material before the Court to establish such a preponderance of probability in favour of the defence plea may consist of oral or documentary evidence, admissions appearing in evidence led by the prosecution or elicited from prosecution witnesses in cross-examination, presumptions, and the statement of the accused recorded u/s. 313 CrPC, 1973; admittedly, Ravudan, Rata and their companions resented the haughty manner in which the accused denied them payment and turned them out of the house of Malshi; at that time, Khima was present in the compound of Malshi and was in dire need of money, thus, it would be but natural that Khima also shared with his companions a sense of resentment against the accused; according to the prosecution evidence, while moving towards the place of occurrence all these five persons were discussing among themselves "of getting money from the accused"; the common object of this assembly of five persons in attempting in intercept and stop the vehicle of the accused was to extort money from him by putting him in fear of injury and in the circumstances, as soon as Khima and Kana raised their hands to stop the jeep of the accused and then tried to follow and close in on it, it was not unreasonable for the accused to apprehend some physical harm at their hands, therefore, u/s. 102, IPC, 1860, a right of private defence of the body accrued to the accused; however, the deceased or any of his companions were not carrying any arm; even though the complainant party pelted the vehicle with stones, then also it being a closed station-wagon, the inmates could not reasonably apprehend death or grievous hurt; though the accused should not have fired all the three rounds in quick succession, but mere fact that he did not assess the necessity of firing each successive shot does not negative good faith on his part in the exercise of his right because a person placed in peril is not expected to weigh "in golden scales" what amount of force is necessary to keep within the right; this circumstance can be taken into account in mitigation of the sentence - Held, the accused exceeded the right of private defence available to him u/s. 101; the offence of accused is one u/s. 304, Part II, IPC, 1860 and does not amount to murder under any of the 4 clauses in s. 300; conviction u/s. 304, Part II, is upheld; sentence reduced to 6 months' rigorous imprisonment - Appeal disposed of.
