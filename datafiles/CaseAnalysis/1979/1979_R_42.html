Ramesh Bejoy Sharma and Others v Pashupati Rai and Others

Supreme Court of India

17 July 1979

Case Analysis

Bench

D.A. Desai, R.S. Sarkaria

Where Reported

1979 Indlaw SC 615; (1979) 4 SCC 27; AIR 1979 SC 1769; [1980] 1 S.C.R. 6

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Agriculture & Agri. Products

Summary: Land & Property - Bihar Land Reforms Act, 1950, s. 6(1)(b) - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 1, r. 27 - (A) Whether land was in khas possession of plaintiff who was an intermediary on date of vesting u/s. 6(1)(b) and by operation of s. 6, stood settled by State with plaintiff and he would be entitled to retain possession? - Even if it is held that a tenant at will had no sure interest or estate, yet he is entitled to notice and he cannot be evicted without notice, duration of which would be dependant upon nature of lease and in case of an agricultural lease, notice must expire with end of agricultural year - As a corollary, a tenant-at-will can hold out against his landlord if he is sought to be evicted without due process of law which would imply notice terminating tenancy and can continue to remain in possession against landlord till termination of lease by a proper legal notice, thus, a tenant-at-will is not holding possession on behalf of landlord but he has a vestige of title to it and holds on his own behalf and can set up his possession against landlord till formality prescribed by law is undertaken by landlord and is evicted by due process of law - Further, u/s.2(k), a cultivation of land by tenant-at-will could not be said to be cultivation by landlord himself or by his servants or by hired labourer and accordingly, landlord could not be said to be in khas possession of land in possession of tenant-at-will, therefore, land in possession of a tenant at-will cannot be said to be in khas possession of intermediary for purpose of s. 6 - Held, on 12-04-1953 when notification u/s. 3 was issued, respondents tenants at-will were under a notice to quit which would expire on 12-04-1953, therefore, till 12-04-1953, respondents tenants-at-will were in actual and physical possession of land and till that date could hold against intermediatary landlord, therefore, on date of vesting, intermediary was not in khas possession within meaning of s. 6(1) and not entitled to recover possession, and after vesting of estate in State which event had occurred prior to institution of suit, he could not maintain an action for eviction as he had no more subsisting interest in estate, having vested in State - (B) Whether, if application u/O. 41, r. 27, CPC seeking permission to lead additional evidence would have been permitted, it would tend to establish that land was already settled with plaintiff intermediary by State as envisaged by s. 6? - Further held, if plaintiff has obtained some settlement of land on basis of a decree of Trial Court or First Appellate Court which became interlocutory in view of appeal preferred to High Court and then to this Court, it is not open to plaintiff to take any advantage of such a settlement, if this evidence is allowed to be admitted on record at this stage it might put respondents at a disadvantage, therefore, rejection of application justified - (C) Whether respondent's appeal should have been dismissed by HC in view of non-compliance of its order D/- 12-04-1953? - Further held, firstly, this contention was not taken before HC though it was available to, which could be said to have been abandoned and once it is abandoned it cannot be re-agitated before this Court and apart from this, if Court fixes time for doing a certain thing, it always retains power to extend time - Appeal dismissed.
