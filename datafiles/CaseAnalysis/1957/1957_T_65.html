 Bhatnagars and Company Limited v Union of India (and connected petitions)

Supreme Court of India

21 February 1957

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Sudhi Ranjan Das, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, S.K. Das

Where Reported

1957 Indlaw SC 21; AIR 1957 SC 478; 1983 ECR 1607; 1957 SCJ 546; [1957] 1 S.C.R. 701

Case Digest

Subject: Customs

Keywords: Stores, Sea Customs Act 1878, Import and Export (Control) Act. 1947

Summary: Import and Export (Control) Act 1947, s. 3 - Defence of India Rules, r. 84 (2) - Defence of India Act, 1939 - Sea Customs Act, 1878 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Essential Supplies (Temporary Powers) Act, 1946 - (A) Whether failure of undertaking given by Solicitor General for and on behalf of Central Govt. for disposing revision petition of present petitioner within a specified time, attracts contempt proceedings? - Petitioner is entirely in error in assuming that, on behalf of Union of India, any undertaking was given that his revisional petition would be disposed of within a day or two - Petitioner presumably thinks that Court's order required that his revisional petition should be disposed of by Central Govt. within a month - This assumption is entirely unwarranted - Period of one month which is mentioned in order was period granted to petitioner to move this Court for special leave after decision of his revisional petition by Central Govt. was communicated to him - Held, order is plain and unambiguous and there is no scope for any misunderstanding whatever - If no undertaking was given as assumed by petitioner, it is impossible to understand how any contempt can arise on ground that undertaking had not been complied with - Besides, petitioner has not stopped to consider which person Union of India represents as Respondent No. 1 in his petition - He has also not paused to consider how other respondents could be guilty of contempt - Petition No. 423 of 1956 dismissed - (B) Whether policy enunciated in Press Note dated February 3, 1955 and Public Notice No. 25-ITC (PN)/ 56 dated June 30, 1956 amounts to a monopoly and therby affect petitioner's fundamental right of carrying on his trade and business? - In modern times, export and import policy of any democratic State is bound to be flexible - Needs of country, position of foreign exchange, need to protect national industries and all other relevant considerations have to be examined by Central Govt. from time to time and rules in regard to export and import suitably adjusted - It would, therefore, be idle to suggest that there should be unfettered and unrestricted freedom of export and import or that policy of Govt. in regard to export and import should be fixed and not changed according to requirements of country - Held, it is difficult to entertain argument from present petitioner that alleged monopoly has affected his right to carry on trade - In substance no monopoly has been created and petitioner's application is entirely misconceived - Petition No. 164 of 1956 dismissed - (C) Whether any considerable prejudice was caused to petitioner since he was granted licences which were to be alive for one year from February 13, 1952, and because of illegal seizure of licence and unauthorised confiscation of consignments from him? - Held, authorities have found that, though licences were obtained by petitioner in his name, he has been, trafficking in these licences - The consignments had been ordered by another individual Messrs N Jivanlal and Co. - Said individual holds no licence for import of soda ash and as such consignments received by said individual are liable to be confiscated - If goods have been seized in accordance with law and they have been seized as a result of findings recorded by relevant authorities competent to hold enquiry under Sea Customs Act, it is not open to petitioner to contend that we should ask authorities to exercise discretion in favour of petitioner and allow his licences a further lease of life - Petition No.377 of 1955 dismissed.
