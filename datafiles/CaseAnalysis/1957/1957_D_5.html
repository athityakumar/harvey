Manak Lal v Dr. Prem Chand

Supreme Court of India

06 February 1957

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, S.K. Das

Where Reported

1957 Indlaw SC 159; AIR 1957 SC 425; 1957 SCJ 359; [1957] 1 S.C.R. 575

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Indian Bar Councils Act, 1926, Legal Practitioners Act, 1879, Code of Criminal Procedure 1898

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Legal Practitioners Act, 1879, s. 13 - Bread Act of 1836, ss. 4 and 15 - Indian Bar Councils Act, 1926, s. 10(2) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, s. 145 - Appellant was an advocate - Alleged that appellant was guilty of professional misconduct - Complaint was sent for enquiry to tribunal nominated by Chief Justice of High Court of Rajasthan - Tribunal held that appellant was guilty of professional misconduct - HC agreed with findings made by tribunal - (A) Whether tribunal was improperly constituted and all proceedings taken before tribunal are all invalid? - Test is whether a litigant could reasonably apprehend that a bias attributable to a member of tribunal might have operated against him in final decision of tribunal - When a lawyer is charged for professional misconduct and is given privilege of being tried by a tribunal enquiry before tribunal must leave no room for reasonable apprehension in mind of lawyer that tribunal may have been even indirectly influenced by any bias in mind of any of member of tribunal - Held, constitution of tribunal suffered from a serious infirmity in that Shri Chhangani, who had appeared for Dr. Prem Chand in criminal proceedings in question, was appointed a member of tribunal and in fact acted as its Chairman - (B) Whether it was open to appellant to take this objection for first time before HC? - Waiver cannot always and in every case be inferred merely from failure of party to take objection - Waiver can be inferred only if and after it is shown that party knew about relevant facts and was aware of his right to take objection - Though point of law raised against competence of tribunal be sound, it is still necessary to consider whether appellant was precluded from raising this point before HC by waiver or acquiescence - Held, appellant waived his objection deliberately and cannot now be allowed to raise it - Since appellant knew material facts and must be deemed to have been conscious of his legal rights in that matter, his failure to take present plea at earlier stage of proceedings creates an effective bar of waiver against him - (C) Whether appellant was guilty of professional misconduct? - Procedure adopted by HC in dealing with this matter does not suffer from any serious irregularity as a result of which their final orders should be set aside and a fresh hearing of matter should be ordered - Not at all satisfied with this enquiry and its final decision - Evidence against appellant is substantially circumstantial and there is no doubt that finding against appellant cannot be made on such circumstantial evidence unless evidence is wholly inconsistent with his innocence and leads irresistibly to inference of his guilt - Held, no case has been made out for interference with conclusions of HC - Order passed by HC directing removal of appellant's name from rolls is fully justified - Appeal dismissed.
