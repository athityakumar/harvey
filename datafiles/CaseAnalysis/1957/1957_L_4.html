 Lilavati Bai v State of Bombay

Supreme Court of India

05 March 1957

Case Analysis

Bench

Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, Sudhi Ranjan Das, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, S.K. Das, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar

Where Reported

1957 Indlaw SC 29; AIR 1957 SC 521; 1957 (59) BomLR 934; 1957 SCJ 557; [1957] 1 S.C.R. 721

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property

Keywords: Electricity Act, 1910, Bihar Buildings (Lease, Rent and Eviction) Control Act, Bombay Land Requisition Act, 1948, Bombay Land Requisition (Amendment) Act, 1950

Summary: Land & Property - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Bihar Buildings (Lease, Rent and Eviction) Control Act - Indian Electricity Act, 1910 - Trade Marks Act, 1940 - Constitution Of India, 1950 - Bombay Land Requisition (Amendment) Act, 1950 - Bombay Land Requisition Act, 1948 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Govt. of India Act, 1935 - Indian Electricity Act, 1910 - Petitioner is widow of one Dharamdas Chellaram, who was a tenant of premises in question - Petitioner's grievance is that towards end of January 1954 she found pasted on outer door of premises, an order D/- 27-01-1954, wherein Governor of Bombay, acting u/s. 6(4)(a) of Bombay Land Requisition Act, 1948, had requisitioned said premises for a public purpose - Order was also meant to be served on Dharamdas Chellaram, who was dead at date of order - (A) Whether Act became invalid on 26-01-1950, inasmuch as it was in conflict with art. 31(2) of Constitution and there was non-compliance with requirements of art. 31(3)? - Held, Act is thus covered by saving clause, cl. 5(a), being an existing law other than a law to which provisions of cl. (6) apply - Act, therefore, would be valid even if provisions of art. 31(2) are not in terms fully satisfied, in so far as Act did not before its amendment contain expression 'for a public purpose' - Amending Act only made explicit what had been left to be gathered from whole tenor of Act - Hence, argument that amending Acts required assent of President u/art. 31(3) has, therefore, no force - (B) Whether ss. 5 and 6 of Act quoted above and underlined by us have made certain matters conclusive, so that High Court or even this Court could not go behind order of State Govt. ? - Further held, Act has made a specific provision to effect that determination on questions referred to in ss. 5 and 6 of Act by State Govt. shall be conclusive evidence of declaration so made - But that does not mean that jurisdiction of HC u/art. 226 or of this Court u/art. 32 or on appeal has been impaired - In a proper case, HC or this Court in exercise of its special jurisdiction under Constitution has power to determine how far provisions of statute have or have not been complied with - But special powers aforesaid of this Court or of HC cannot extend to reopening a finding by State Govt. u/s. 5 of Act - Those are not collateral matters which could on proper evidence be reopened by courts of law - (C) Whether impugned order is not enforceable because it was directed against petitioner's husband, who was dead at date of order? - Held, an order like one passed u/s. 6(4)(a) of Act is not in nature of an order in judicial proceedings between Govt. on one hand and other parties named - If proceedings were intended by Act in sense of judicial or quasi-judicial proceedings between named parties, it may have been legitimately argued that an order passed against a dead man is a complete nullity - Petitions dismissed.
