 State of Madras v Gannon Dunkerley and Company, (Madras) Limited

Supreme Court of India

01 April 1958

Case Analysis

Bench

T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Sudhi Ranjan Das, S.K. Das, A.K. Sarkar, Vivian Bose

Where Reported

1958 Indlaw SC 191; AIR 1958 SC 560; 1958 (2) MLJ(SC) 66; 1958 SCJ 696; [1959] 1 S.C.R. 379; 1958 (9) STC 353

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Corporate; Income Tax & Direct Taxes; Land & Property

Summary: Sales Tax - T. N. General Sales Tax Act,1939, ss. 2(h)and 2(i) - Sale of Goods Act, 1930, s. 4 - Contract Act,1872, ss. 73 and 65 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 246 and Sch. 7, List 2, Entry 24 - 'Sale of goods' in Entry 48 is a nomen juris - Claim for quantum meruit- Passing of titles - Building Contracts- (A)Whether under Madras General Sales Tax Act which impose a tax on materials used for building contracts is valid ?- That expression 'sale of goods' in Entry 48 Sch 7, List 2 of Constitution is a nomen juris, its essential ingredients being an agreement to sell movables for a price and property passing therein pursuant to that agreement - In a building contract which is not a sale of goods, and it is not within competence of Provincial Legislature under Entry 48 to impose a tax on supply of materials used in such contract treating it as a sale - Held, hence provisions of Madras General Sales Tax Act which impose a tax on such materials which is used in building contract and not a contract for sale is ultra vires - (A1)Whether mere passing of title amounts to sale of goods? -Further held, transaction property must actually pass in goods - Merely title to goods passes but not as a result of any contract between parties, express or implied, there is no sale - If under contract of sale, title to goods has not passed, then there is an agreement to sell and not a complete sale - (A2) Whether sale of goods without consideration of money amounts to sale of goods mentioned in Sales of Goods Act?- Further held, that also if consideration for transfer was not money but other valuable consideration it may then be an exchange or barter but not sale - (B) Whether imposing of tax is justified in case of contract for sale for future or uncertain goods?- Further held, where contract relates to future or unascertained goods, although in that sense an agreement to sell would, as one of essential ingredients of sale, furnish sufficient nexus for a State to impose a tax, such levy could be made only when transaction is one of sale, and it would be one of sale only when it has resulted in passing of property in goods to purchaser - 'Sale of goods' in Entry 48 cannot be construed in its popular sense but must be interpreted in its legal sense - (C) Whether Provincial legislature is competent to enact laws in respect of matters enumerated in Lists 2 and 3 of Constitution? - Further held, that under Govt. of India Act, Provincial legislature is competent to enact laws in respect of matters enumerated in Lists 2 and 3 - A power to enact a law with respect to tax on sale of goods under Entry will be intra vires, only if will be one relating in fact to sale of goods - (D) Whether work contract constitutes sale of goods?- In a building contract (works contract) agreement between parties is that contractor should construct a building according to specifications contained in agreement and in consideration therefore receive payment as provided therein - In such an agreement there is neither a contract to sell materials used in construction, nor does property pass therein as movables - Further held, that as under law there cannot be an agreement relating to one of property and sale as regards another -(E)What is claim for quantum meruit regarding element constituting sale under building contract? - Further held, that contention that a building contract contains all elements constituting a sale of materials cannot be established by reference to form of action when claim is in quantum meruit - Claim for quantum meruit is a claim for damages for breach of contract and value of materials is a factor relevant only as furnishing a basis for assessing amount of compensation - Claim is not for goods sold and delivered but for damages - Appeal dismissed.
