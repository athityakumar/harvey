G. Venkataswami Naidu and Company v Commissioner of Income Tax

Supreme Court of India

24 November 1958

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, A.K. Sarkar, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar

Where Reported

1958 Indlaw SC 104; AIR 1959 SC 359; [1959] 35 ITR 594; 1959 (2) MLJ(SC) 31; [1959] 1 S.C.R. 646; [1959] Supp1 S.C.R. 646

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Income Tax Act, 1922, ss. 2(4), 10(1) and 66(1) - A/y 1948-49 - Business income - Appellant, a firm acting as managing agents of a limited company, purchased 4 contiguous plots of land for a total consideration of Rs. 8,712/- - After about 5 years said properties were sold by the appellant to 'X' for a sum of Rs. 43,887/- - Income Tax Officer (ITO) treated the said amount of Rs. 43,887 as the income of the appellant and assessed it to income-tax under the head 'business' on the ground that since the lands were adjacent to the 'X', the appellant must have purchased them solely with a view to sell them to the said mills with a profit - ITO also held that it had all the elements of a business transaction and was thus an adventure in the nature of trade - Appellate authority set aside the order of the ITO and held that the amount in question was not assessable as it cannot be held to be income or profit resulting from a profit-making scheme - Tribunal upheld the view taken by the ITO and held that the amount in question was not a capital accretion but a gain made in an adventure in the nature of business in carrying out a scheme of profit making - On appeal, HC held that the transaction in question was an adventure in the nature of trade and so the respondent was justified in taxing the amount in question under the head ' business' - Hence, the instant appeal - Whether HC was right in holding that the transaction in question was an adventure in the nature of trade? - Held, an isolated transaction can satisfy the description of an adventure in the nature of trade - Though judicial decisions which deal with the character of transactions alleged to be in the nature of trade do not purport to lay down any general or universal test - Conduct of the assessee was a relevant factor - If a person invests money in land intending to hold it, enjoys its income for some time, and then sells it at a profit, then it was a case of capital accretion and not profit derived from an adventure in the nature of trade - But where a purchase had been made solely and exclusively with the intention to resell at a profit and the purchaser had no intention of holding the property for himself or otherwise enjoying or using it, there would be a strong presumption that the transaction was an adventure in the nature of trade but this may be rebutted by the other facts or circumstances of the case - In the instant case, appellant purchased the 4 plots during 2 years with the sole intention to sell them to the mills at a profit and this intention raises a strong presumption in favour of the view taken by the tribunal - In regard to the other relevant facts and circumstances in the case, none of them offsets or rebuts the presumption arising from the initial intention on the other hand, most of them corroborate the said presumption - Hence, impugned order of the HC upheld - Appeal dismissed.
