Express Newspapers Private, Limited, and Another v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

19 March 1958

Case Analysis

Bench

Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati

Where Reported

1958 Indlaw SC 3; AIR 1958 SC 578; 1961 (1) LLJ 339; 1958 SCJ 3133; [1959] 1 S.C.R. 12

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Corporate; Labour & Industrial Law; Service

Keywords: Fair Wage

Summary: (A) Constitution - Media & Entertainment - Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 32 - Working Journalists (Conditions of Service) and Miscellaneous Provisions Act, 1955, s. 8 and 9 - Working Journalists Wage Board Rules, 1956 - Central Government constituted a Wage Board u/s. 8 of the Act for fixing rates of wages in respect of working journalists in accordance with the provisions of the Act, consisting of equal representatives of employers in relation to newspaper establishments and working journalists - After considerable discussion, in which the representatives of the newspapers and of working journalists had joint sittings, unanimous decisions were arrived at on (i) classification of newspapers, (ii) classification of centers and (iii) classification of employees, except on one point, namely, classification of group, multiple units and chains on the basis of their total gross revenue - Decision was taken regarding pay scales, dearness allowance, location allowance and retainer allowance - Instant petitions u/art. 32 of the Constitution raise the question as to the vires of the Working Journalists (Conditions of Service) and Miscellaneous Provisions Act, 1955 and the decision of the Wage Board constituted thereunder - (A) Whether decision by a majority was unwarranted by the Act and since there was no provision in the Act, the Rules providing for the same went beyond the Act and were therefore ultra vires? - Held, Working Journalists Wage Board Rules, 1956, which were published on July 31, 1956, were made by the Central Government in exercise of the power conferred upon it by s. 20 of the Act - That section empowered the Central Government to make rules to carry out the purposes of the Act, in particular to provide for the procedure to be followed by the Board in fixing rates of wages - After the publication of these rules, they became a part of the Act itself and any decision thereafter reached by the Wage Board by a majority as prescribed therein was therefore lawful and could not be impeached in the manner suggested - (B) Whether decision of Wage Board was invalid, because (a) no reasons were given, (b) nor did it disclose what considerations prevailed with the Board in arriving at its decision? - Held, Act made no provision in this behalf and the Board was perfectly within its rights if it chose not to give any reasons for its decision - Fact that no reasons were thus given, however, would not vitiate the decision in any manner - (C) Whether classification on the basis of gross revenue was illegal and unauthorised by the Act? - Held, what the Act authorised it to do was to fix the rates of wages for working journalists having regard to the principles laid down in s. 9(1) of the Act - In fixing the wage structure the Wage Board constituted under the Act was perfectly at liberty if it thought necessary to classify the newspaper establishments in any manner it thought proper provided of course that such classification was not irrational - (D) Whether grouping into chains or multiple units was unauthorised by the Act? - Held, Wage Board was authorised to fix the wage structure for working journalists who were employed in various newspaper establishments all over the country - If the chains or multiple units existed in the country the newspaper establishments which formed such chains or multiple units were well within the purview of the inquiry before the Wage Board and if the Wage Board thus chose to group them together in that manner such grouping by itself could not be open to attack - (E) Whether Board was not authorised by the Act to fix the salaries of journalists except in relation to a particular industrial establishment and not on an All-India basis of all newspapers taken together? - Held, there was nothing in the Act to prohibit the treating of several newspaper establishments producing or publishing one or more newspapers though in different parts of the country as one newspaper establishment for the purpose of fixing the rates of wages - (F) Whether Board had authority to render a decision which was retrospective in operation? - Held, Wage Board had the jurisdiction and authority to pronounce a decision which could be retrospective in effect from the date of its appointment and there was no legal flaw in the Wage Board prescribing that its decision should be retrospective in operation in the manner indicated by it - (G) Whether decision was bad as it did not disclose that the capacity to pay any particular establishment was ever taken into consideration? - Held, essential pre-requisite of deciding the wage structure was to consider the capacity of the industry to pay and this, introduced a fatal infirmity in the decision of the Board - Hence, Board had contravened the mandatory requirement of s. 9 as an essential condition for the fixation of wage structure was ignored and therefore decision was ultra vires the Act itself - Decision could not be sustained and must be set aside - Petitioners would be entitled to an order declaring that s. 5(1)(a)(iii) of the Working Journalists (Conditions of Service) and Miscellaneous Provisions Act, 1955, was ultra vires the Constitution of India and that the decision of the Wage Board was illegal and void - Petitions allowed.

(B) Constitution - Media & Entertainment - Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 32 - Working Journalists (Conditions of Service) and Miscellaneous Provisions Act, 1955 - Arts. 19(1)(a) and 19(2) - Freedom of speech and expression - Whether impugned Act violated the fundamental rights of the petitioners ? - Held, impugned Act was enacted to regulate certain conditions of service of working journalists and other persons employed in newspaper establishments and in the very forefront of the Act, the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, was by s. 3 made applicable to working journalists with certain modification in connection with the application of s. 25F of that Act - Neither the intention nor the effect and operation of the impugned Act was to take away or abridge the right of freedom of speech and expression enjoyed by the petitioners - Petitions allowed.

(C) Constitution - Media & Entertainment - Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 32 - Working Journalists (Conditions of Service) and Miscellaneous Provisions Act, 1955 - Violation of Art. 19(1)(g) - Right to carry on any occupation, trade or business - (A) Whether impugned Act imposed unreasonable restrictions on the freedom to carry on business in empowering fixation of wages, grant of gratuity and compensation without making it incumbent on the Board to consider the major factor of the capacity of the industry to pay? - Petitioners contended that the relevant criteria for the fixation of rates of wages were not laid down in s. 9 of the Act - Held, it was not made incumbent on the Board to consider the major factor of the capacity of the industry to pay as an essential circumstance in fixing the rates of wages - S. 9(1) did not eschew the consideration of this essential circumstance, viz., the capacity of the industry to pay and it was not only open but incumbent upon the Wage Board to consider that essential circumstance in order to arrive at the fixation of the rates of wages of the working journalists - (B) Whether impugned Act imposed unreasonable restrictions on the freedom to carry on business in providing for a procedure which did not compel the Board to conform to the rules under the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, thus permitting the Board to follow any arbitrary procedure violating the principle of audi alteram partem? - It was contended that the procedure to be followed by the Board for fixing the rates of wages was not laid down and it was open to the Board to follow any arbitrary procedure violating the principle of audi alteram partem and as such this also was unreasonable - Held, even though the Board was not bound to exercise the same powers and follow the same procedure as an industrial tribunal constituted under the Industrial Disputes Act, the Board was, in any event, not entitled to adopt any arbitrary procedure violating the principles of natural justice - Wage Board was not empowered or authorized to adopt any arbitrary procedure and flout the principles of natural justice - Petitions allowed.

(D) Constitution - Media & Entertainment - Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19(6) - Working Journalists (Conditions of Service) and Miscellaneous Provisions Act, 1955 - Violation of art. 19(6) - It was contended that the restrictions imposed on newspaper establishments under the terms of the impugned Act were unreasonable in so far as they would have the effect of destroying the business of the petitioners and would therefore exceed the bounds of permissible legislation u/art. 19(6) - It was urged that the right to impose reasonable restrictions on the petitioners' right to carry on business did not empower the legislature to destroy the business itself - (A) Whether inclusion of proof reader in the definition of 'working journalist' imposed upon the newspaper establishments an unreasonable burden far in excess of what they were expected to bear? - Held, proof-readers occupy a very important position in the editorial staff of a newspaper establishment - By inclusion of proof reader in the definition of 'working journalist', they would be entitled to higher wages by reason of the fixation of rates of wages by the Wage Board but that would by itself be no ground for holding the inclusion of proof-readers within the definition of working journalist an unreasonable burden on newspaper establishments - (B) Period of notice for the termination of service - Held, period of notice for the termination of services should be based on the length of the service rendered and the nature of the appointment - There could be no hard and fast rule as to what the notice period should be - Period of six months, in the case of an editor, and three months, in the case of any other working journalists prescribed u/s. 3(2) of the impugned Act was therefore was not open to objection - (C) Whether payment of gratuity in cases where a working journalist who had been in continuous service for not less than three years voluntarily resigned from service from a newspaper establishment was correct? - Held, provision in regard to the payment of gratuity to working journalists who voluntarily resigned from service from newspaper establishments was not reasonable - Where an employee voluntarily resigns from service of the employer after a period of only three years, there would be no justification whatever for awarding him a gratuity and any such provision of the type which was made in s. 5(1)(a)(iii) of the Act would be unreasonable - Therefore this provision imposed an unreasonable restriction on the petitioners' right to carry on business and was liable to be struck down as unconstitutional - (D) Whether prescribing 144 hours only during any period of four consecutive weeks was unreasonable? - Held, provision in regard to the hours of work could not be considered unreasonable having regard to the nature and quality of the work to be done by working journalists - Petitions allowed.

(E) Constitution - Media & Entertainment - Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 14 - Working Journalists (Conditions of Service) and Miscellaneous Provisions Act, 1955 - Violation of Art. 14 - (A) Whether impugned Act selected the working journalists for favoured treatment by giving them a statutory guarantee of gratuity, hours of work and leave which other persons in similar or comparable employment had not got and in providing for the fixation of their salaries without following the normal procedure envisaged in the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947? - Held, working journalists were a group by themselves and could be classified as such apart from the other employees of newspaper establishments and if the Legislature embarked upon a legislation for the purpose of ameliorating their conditions of service there was nothing discriminatory about it - They could be singled out thus for preferential treatment against the other employees of newspaper establishments - Classification of this type could not come within the ban of Art. 14 - Further, what was contemplated by the provisions of the impugned Act however, was a general fixation of rates of wages of working journalists which would ameliorate the conditions of their service and the constitution of a wage board for this purpose was one of the established modes of achieving that object - (B) Whether provisions contained in s. 17 of the Act in regard to the recovery of money due from the employers in the same manner as an arrear of land revenue was discriminatory?- Held, no - Aim and object of such provision was to provide the working journalists who were a group by themselves from amongst employees employed in the newspaper establishments with a remedy for the recovery of the monies due to them in the same manner as the workmen under the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 - It was not discriminatory in making such a provision for the recovery of monies due by the employers to these working journalists - (C) Discrimination between Press industry employers on the one hand and the other industrial employers on the other - Held, classification was based on an intelligible differentia which distinguished the working journalists from other employees of newspaper establishments and that differentia had a rational relation to the object sought to be achieved, viz., the amelioration of the conditions of service of working journalists - Petitions allowed.

(F) Constitution - Media & Entertainment - Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 32 - Working Journalists (Conditions of Service) and Miscellaneous Provisions Act, 1955 - Violation of Art. 32 - Held, impugned Act did not say that the Wage Board shall not give any reason for its decision - It was left to the discretion of the Wage Board whether it should give the reasons for its decision or not - In the absence of any such prohibition, it could not be held that the fundamental right conferred upon the petitioners u/art. 32 was in any manner whatever sought to be infringed - Act could not be challenged as violative of the fundamental right enshrined in Art. 32 of the Constitution - Petitions allowed.
