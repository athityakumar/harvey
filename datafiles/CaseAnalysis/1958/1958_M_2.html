M. K. Venkatachalam, Income Tax Officer, and v Bombay Dyeing and Manufacturing Company Limited

Supreme Court of India

28 April 1958

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, A.K. Sarkar, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar

Where Reported

1958 Indlaw SC 201; AIR 1958 SC 875; [1958] 34 ITR 143; 1958 (2) MLJ(SC) 182; 1958 SCJ 1054; [1959] 1 S.C.R. 703

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Keywords: Indian Income Tax (Amendment) Act, 1953

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Income Tax Act, 1961, ss.18A(5), 23 and 35 - Income Tax (Amendment) Act, 1953 - A/y 1952-53 - Rectification of mistake apparent from record - The Income Tax Officer (ITO) assessed assessee and gave him credit for Rs. 50,603,150/- as representing interest at 2% on tax paid in advance u/s. 18A(5) of the Act - Subsequently, the Amendment Act came into force and as a result of which assessee was entitled to get interest at 2% not on whole of advance amount of tax paid by him as before but only on difference between payment made and amount at which assessee was assessed to tax under regular assessment u/s. 23 of Act - After Amendment Act was passed, ITO exercises his power u/s. 35 of Act and purported to rectify mistake apparent from record in regard to credit for Rs.50,603,150/- allowed by him to assessee - A demand notice issued against assessee for sum of Rs.29,44,690/- on ground that assessee had been given credit for this excess amount through mistake - Assessee filed writ petition against said order - HC held that ITO was not entitled to rectify the mistake in question u/s. 35 of the Act - Hence, the instant appeal - Whether an order which was proper and valid when it was made can be said to disclose a mistake apparent from the record if the said order would be erroneous in view of a subsequent amendment made by the Amendment Act when the Amendment Act is intended to operate retrospectively ? - Held, the legal fiction about the retrospective operation of the Amendment Act must be read as forming part of s. 18A(5) of the Act and consequently the order of the ITO was inconsistent with the provisions of the proviso and suffered from a mistake apparent from the record - Hence, ITO was justified in exercising his power u/s. 35 of the Act and rectifying the said mistakes - Impugned order of the HC set aside - Revenue's appeal allowed.
