Messrs Lakshmichand Baijnath v Commissioner of Income Tax, West Bengal

Supreme Court of India

13 November 1958

Case Analysis

Bench

T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, A.K. Sarkar

Where Reported

1958 Indlaw SC 86; AIR 1959 SC 341; [1959] 35 ITR 416; [1959] 1 S.C.R. 415; [1959] Supp1 S.C.R. 415

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Income Tax & Direct taxes - Indian Income Tax Act, 1922 ,ss. 23, 25(a) - Concealed income - Excess profits tax - Hindu undivided family - Taxable income - Appellant was a Hindu undivided family carrying business of piece goods merchants - While assessment appellant filed a petition under s. 25-A of Income tax Act, 1922 claiming there was a partition in family and Income tax Officer enquired matter of partition and quantum of income charge able to tax and pronounced orders - Income assessable u/s. 23 of Act, 1922, dispute related to six sums aggregating to Rs. 2, 30,346 shown in accounts as sale proceeds of ornaments appellant stated sums was at partition jewels of family was sold in six lots and invested in business and Income-tax Officer declined explanation - Appellant stated jewels in question had come down to family through several generations was not pure - Income tax Officer rejected explanation and held weight which was actually deducted for impurities in accounts of purchaser was almost negligible and what was sold was pure gold and not gold in old family jewels - Appellant took orders in appeal to Appellate Assistant Commissioner and observed appellant had been changing his version as to true character of sales from time to time and confirmed the findings of Income-tax Officer and dismissed appeals - Appellant appealed to Appellate Tribunal and sought to rely on a certain proceedings book as showing that family jewels were really broken up and was sold to 'A' and gold was separated - Appellant applied to Tribunal to receive proceedings book in evidence for support of application and Income-tax Officer not considered it - Whether in concealed income, levy of excess profits tax could be made without finding that they represented business income - Held, in concealed income no levy of excess profits tax can be made without a further finding if they represented business income and when an amount was credited in business books it is not an unreasonable inference to draw it is a receipt from business - If assessed fails to prove satisfactorily source and nature of certain amounts of cash received during accounting year Income-tax Officer was entitled to draw inference that receipts are of an assessable nature - Appeals dismissed.
