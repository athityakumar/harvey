 Shri Ram Krishna Dalmia v Shri Justice S. R. Tendolkar and Others

Supreme Court of India

28 March 1958

Case Analysis

Bench

Sudhi Ranjan Das, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, S.K. Das, A.K. Sarkar

Where Reported

1958 Indlaw SC 34; AIR 1958 SC 538; 1959 (61) BomLR 192; 1959 (1) MLJ 67; 1959 (1) MLJ(SC) 67; [1959] 1 S.C.R. 279

Case Digest

Subject: Corporate

Keywords: Code of Criminal Procedure 1898, misa

Summary: Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14 and 246 - Commissions of Enquiry Act, 1952, s. 3 - Legislation empowering Government to appoint Commission - Constitutionality - The Central Government in exercise of powers conferred upon it by s. 3 of the 1952 Act, by notification dt. 11.12.1956 appointed a Commission of Inquiry to inquire into and submitted a report in respect of certain companies mentioned in the Schedule attached to the notification - By the subsequent notification, the Central Government fixed period of two years from that date as the time within which the Commission was to exercise its function and to make its report - Petitioners filed applications u/art. 226 of the Constitution before the HC challenging the validity of the 1952 Act and the notifications issued under said Act - HC dismissed said applications and held that the said notification was legal and valid except as to the last part of clause (10) thereof from the words "and the action" to the words "in future cases" and directed the Commission not to proceed with the inquiry to the extent that it related to the aforesaid last part of clause (10) of the said notification - Petitioners filed appeal against said order contending that the 1952 Act was ultra vires the Constitution as it was beyond the legislative competency of Parliament to enact a law conferring such a wide sweep of powers - Whether the Commissions of Enquiry Act, 1952 was ultra vires the Constitution and notification issued said Act could be set aside? - Held, Parliament enacted the Act in exercise of the legislative powers conferred on it by art. 246 of the Constitution r/w. entry 94 in List I and entry 45 in List III of, the Seventh Schedule to the Constitution which relate to inquiries for the purposes of any of the matters in List I and in Lists II and III respectively - Neither Parliament nor the Government had itself undertaken any inquiry at all - Parliament had made a law with respect to inquiry and had left it to the appropriate Government to set up a Commission of Inquiry under certain circumstances referred to in s. 3 of the Act - The Central Government, in its turn, has, in exercise of the powers conferred on it by the Act, set up this Commission - It was, therefore, not correct to say that Parliament or the Government itself has undertaken to hold any inquiry - The discretion given to the Government to set up a Commission of Inquiry was guided by the policy laid down in the Act that the executive action was to be taken only when there exists a definite matter of public importance into which an inquiry was necessary - Moreover, the last portion of clause (10) was bad because it signifies that Parliament or the Government had usurped the functions of the judiciary, with respect, to be inconsistent with the conclusion arrived at in a later part of the judgment that as the Commission can only make recommendations which are not enforceable proprio vigore there can be no question of usurpation of judicial functions - Hence, the Commissions of Enquiry Act, 1952 was not ultra vires the Constitution and the said notifications was well within the powers conferred on the Government by s. 3 of the Act and did not go beyond the provisions of the Act except the use of some words, which went beyond the Act - Impugned order of the HC upheld - Appeals dismissed.
