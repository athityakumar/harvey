 Mohd. Hanif Quareshi and Others v State of Bihar

Supreme Court of India

23 April 1958

Case Analysis

Bench

S.K. Das, Sudhi Ranjan Das, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Vivian Bose

Where Reported

1958 Indlaw SC 48; AIR 1958 SC 731; 1958 SCJ 975; [1959] 1 S.C.R. 629

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Environment, Wildlife & Animal

Keywords: Constitutionality, Preventive Detention Act, 1950, Bombay Animal Preservation Act, 1954, Bombay Prohibition Act 1949, Bihar Preservation And Improvement Of Animals Act, 1956, Central Provinces and Berar Animal Preservation Act, 1949

Summary: Constitution - Bihar Preservation and Improvement of Animals Act, 1956, ss. 2(2) and 3 - U P Prevention of Cow Slaughter Act, 1955, ss. 2(2) and 3 - Central Provinces and Berar Animal Preservation Act, 1949, ss. 3, 4 - Constitution Of India, 1950, arts. 14, 19, 48, 246, 245, 19(1)(g), 13(2) and 25(1), sch 7 List 2, Entry 15 - Supreme Court Rules, O. XLI, r. 2 (A) Slaughter of cows - Art. 48 - Directive principles of State policy - Held, protection recommended by directive in art. 48 is confined only to cows and calves and to those animals which are presently or potentially capable of yielding milk or of doing work as draught cattle but does not, from very nature of purpose for which it is obviously recommended, extend to cattle which at one time were milch or draught cattle but which have ceased to be such - Directive principles of State policy set out in Part IV of Constitution have to conform to and run as subsidiary to fundamental rights - Further held, ban on slaughter of cows even on slaughter day did not violate fundamental rights of petitioners u/art. 25 as it had not been established that sacrifice of a cow on that day was an obligatory overt act for a Mussalman to exhibit his religious belief and idea (1) Whether law which permits such discrimination as even between butchers who kill cattle and butchers who kill buffaloes must be struck down as violative of salutary provisions of art. 14 of Constitution? - Further held, impugned Acts did not offend art. 14 of Constitution - Different categories of animals being susceptible of classification into separate groups on basis of their usefulness to society, butchers who kill each category may also be placed in distinct classes according to effect produced on society by carrying on of their respective occupations - This classification is based on an intelligible differentia which places petitioners in a well defined class and distinguishes them from those who slaughter sheep or goats and this differentia has a close connection with object sought to be achieved by impugned Acts, namely, preservation, protection and improvement of livestock - In determining question of reasonableness of restrictions imposed on fundamental rights conferred by art. 19(1)(g) Court cannot proceed on a general notion of what is reasonable in abstract or even on consideration of what is reasonable from point of view of person or persons on whom restrictions are imposed - What Court has to do is to consider whether restrictions imposed are reasonable in interests of general public - Test of reasonableness has been laid down in State of Madras v I G Row, [1952] S C R 597 at 602 - It should also be remembered that legislature is best judge of what is good for community - Though a constitutional question cannot be decided on grounds of sentiment of a section of people, it has to be taken into consideration, though only as one of elements, in arriving at a judicial verdict as to reasonableness of restrictions - Effect of impugned Acts on fundamental rights of petitioners u/art. 19(1)(g) is direct and instantaneous as soon as Acts are brought into force, and it has to be determined whether they can be justified under cl. (6) of art. 19 - Country is in short supply of milch cattle, breeding bulls and working bullocks, and a total ban on slaughter of these which are essential to national economy for supply of milk, agricultural working power and manure is a reasonable restriction in interests of general public - But a total ban on slaughter of useless cattle, which involves a wasteful drain on nation's cattle feed which is itself in short supply and which would deprive useful cattle of much needed nourishment, cannot be justified as being in interests of general public - (B) Whether several legislative enactments banning slaughter of certain animals passed by States of Bihar, Uttar Pradesh and Madhya Pradesh respectively are constitutionally valid ?- Further held, (i) that a total ban on slaughter of cows of all ages and calves of cows and of she-buffaloes, male and female, was quite reasonable and valid; (ii)that a total ban on slaughter of she-buffaloes or breeding bulls or working bullocks (cattle as well as buffaloes), as long as they were capable of being used as milch or draught cattle, was also reasonable and valid; and (iii) that a total ban on slaughter of she-buffaloes, bulls and bullocks (cattle or buffalo) after they ceased to be capable of yielding milk or of breeding or working as draught animals was not in interests of general public - Further held, Bihar Act in so far as it prohibits slaughter of cows of all ages and calves of cows and calves of buffaloes, male and female, is constitutionally valid and in so far as it totally prohibits slaughter of she-buffaloes, breeding bulls and working bullocks (cattle and buffalo), without prescribing any test or requirement as to their age or usefulness it infringes rights of petitioners u/art. 19 (1) (g) and is to that extent void - Further held, Uttar Pradesh Act is constitutionally valid in so far as it prohibits slaughter of cows of all ages and calves of cows, male and female, but so far as it purports to totally prohibit slaughter of breeding bulls and working bullocks without prescribing any test or requirement as to their age or usefulness it offends against art. 19 (1) (g) and is to that extent void - Further, Madhya Pradesh Act is constitutionally valid in so far as it prohibits slaughter of cows of all ages and calves of cows, male and female, but is void in so far as it totally prohibits slaughter of breeding bulls and working-bullocks without prescribing any test or requirement as to their age or usefulness Act is valid in so far as it regulates slaughter of other animals under certificates granted by authorities mentioned therein - Petitions partly allowed.
