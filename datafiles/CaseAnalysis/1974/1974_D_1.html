Collector of Customs, Madras and Others v D. Bhoormall

Supreme Court of India

03 April 1974

Case Analysis

Bench

R.S. Sarkaria, M. Hameedullah Beg

Where Reported

1974 Indlaw SC 1; (1974) 2 SCC 544; (1974) SCC (Cr) 784; AIR 1974 SC 859; 1975 CRLJ 545; 1974 (3) CTR 175; 1985 ECR 2284; 1983 (13) E.L.T. 1546; 1974 (2) MLJ(SC) 49; [1974] 3 S.C.R. 833

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Customs; Indirect Tax

Keywords: Person Concerned, Collector Of Customs, Importation, Any Person, Evidence Act, Smuggled Goods, Concerned In, Solicitors

Summary: Customs - Customs Act, 1962, s. 131 and 171A - Sea Customs Act, 1878, ss. 19 and 167(8) - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 106 - Imports And Exports Control Act, 1947, ss. 3(1) and 3(2) - Confiscation - Goods illegally imported - Burden of proof - The Preventive Officers of the Department recovered 10 packages containing imported goods from the shop of respondent - Said packets were lying as if they had been freshly delivered or were ready for despatch to a further destination - Collector of Customs on consideration of the fact that the goods were admittedly foreign goods and respondent failed to give satisfactorily explanation regarding the same, concluded that the goods were acquired from the illegally acquired stocks and ordered the confiscation of the goods u/s. 167(8) of 1878 Act - Respondent filed writ petition against said order which was dismissed by the Single Judge- However, DB allowed the appeal filed by respondent and held that the Customs Department had not discharged its burden of proof that the goods were smuggled goods - Whether order of the DB could be upheld and department failed to discharged onus of proving that the goods are smuggled goods? - Held, law requires prosecution to establish such a degree of probability that a prudent man may, on its basis, believe in existence of fact in issue - In the present case, although no direct evidence of the illicit importation of goods was adduced by the Department the fact that the goods were of foreign origin coupled with the inference arising from the dubious conduct of respondent could reasonably lead to the conclusion drawn by the Collector that the goods were smuggled goods - Hence, department established said degree of probability - DB was in error in reversing the judgment of the Single Judge and in quashing order of Collector of Customs - Revenue's appeal allowed.
