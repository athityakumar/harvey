Manoj Sharma v State

Supreme Court of India

16 October 2008

Case Analysis

Bench

Altamas Kabir, Markandey Katju

Where Reported

2008 Indlaw SC 2064; (2008) 16 SCC 1; 2009 (9) ADJ 566; AIR 2008 SC (Supp) 1171; 2009 ALL MR (Cri) 267; 2009 (1) JCC 60; JT 2008 (11) SC 674; 2008 (4) KLT 417; 2008 (4) RCR(Criminal) 827; 2008(14) SCALE 44; [2008] 14 S.C.R. 539

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Power Of The Court

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120-B, 302, 326, 34, 406, 420, 468, 471, 498-A - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 320, 320(1), 320(9), 482 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Compromise - Quashing of FIR - Maintainability - Appellant forged documents in respect of vehicle and FIR was lodged - Complainant and accused had compromised and settled matter between themselves - Appellant filed writ petition before HC for quashing of FIR which was dismissed - Hence, instant appeal - Whether First Information Report could be quashed u/s. 482 of 1973 Act or u/art. 226 of 1950 - Held, SC relied on B.S. Joshi and others v State of Haryana and another 2003 Indlaw SC 230 and that criminal proceedings deserve to be quashed, question might have to be decided in some subsequent decision or decisions as to which non-compoundable cases could be quashed u/s. 482 CrPC or art. 226 of Constitution on basis that parties had entered into a compromise - Further, there could be no doubt that a case u/s. 302 IPC or other serious offences like those u/ss. 395, 307 or 304B IPC could not be compounded and hence proceedings in those provisions could not be quashed by HC in exercise of its power u/s. 482 CrPC or in writ jurisdiction on basis of compromise - However, if civil cases were there then proceedings could be quashed by HC even if parties had come to an amicable settlement even though provisions were not compoundable - Therefore, some guidelines should have to be evolved in this connection and matter could not be left at sole unguided discretion of Judges, otherwise there might be conflicting decisions and judicial anarchy - Thus, judicial discretion had to be exercised on some objective guiding principles and criteria, and not on whims and fancies of individual Judges - Appeal allowed.
