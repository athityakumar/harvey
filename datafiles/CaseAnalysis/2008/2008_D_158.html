Sooraram Pratap Reddy and others v District Collector and others

Supreme Court of India

05 September 2008

Case Analysis

Bench

C.K. Thakker, D.K. Jain

Where Reported

2008 Indlaw SC 2410; (2008) 9 SCC 552; 2008 (6) ALD(SC) 19; 2008 (4) AWC 3875; JT 2008 (9) SC 622; 2008(12) SCALE 367; [2008] 13 S.C.R. 126

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Bombay Land Requisition Act, 1948, Land Acquisition (Amendment) Act, 1923, Andhra Pradesh Infrastructure Development Enabling Act, 2001, Andhra Pradesh Urban Area Development Act, 1975, District of Columbia Redevelopment Act, 1945, Infrastructure Development Act, Land Acquisition (Amendment and Vallidation) Ordinance, 1967

Summary: (A) Practice & Procedure - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - Land acquisition - 'public purpose' - Interpretation of - Held, expression 'public purpose' is a term of very wide amplitude and is illustrative and not exhaustive - Inclusive definition of the said term does not restrict its ambit and scope - Said expression is incapable of precise and comprehensive definition - Term 'public purpose' is used in a generic sense of including any purpose wherein even a fraction of the community may be interested or by which it may be benefited - Appeals dismissed.(B) Land & Property - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 5A, 17 - Land acquisition - 'public purpose' - Respondents issued notification for acquisition of appellants land for an infrastructure development project - Respondents invoked 'urgency clause' u/s. 17 of the Act - Appellants challenged the notification before HC by way of writ proceedings - Appellants contended that respondents had acquired the land not for public purpose but to transfer the same to a foreign company with mala fide intention and oblique motive - HC upheld the notification, however, set aside the invocation of urgency clause and directed the respondents to proceed to hear objections of the owners/interested person by following procedure u/s. 5A - Appellants filed writ appeal - DB dismissed the appeal - (a) Whether selection of a nodal agency by the respondents for implementation of the project would vitiate the very purpose of acquisition - Held, respondents developed the land through a nodal agency which would facilitate socio-economic progress of the State by generating revenues, weeding out unemployment and bringing new avenues and opportunities for public at large - Development of infrastructure is legal and legitimate 'public purpose' for exercising power of eminent domain - Simply because a nodal agency has been chosen for fulfillment of public purpose does not mean that the larger public interest has been sacrificed, ignored or disregarded and it would make exercise of power bad, mala fide or for collateral purpose vitiating the proceedings - (b) Whether joint venture mechanism of nodal agency with a foreign company for executing the project would vitiate the object of acquisition - Held, infrastructure development project conceived by the State and executed under the auspices of its instrumentality, i.e., nodal agency, is one covered by the Act - Joint venture mechanism for implementing the policy, executing the project and achieving lawful public purpose for realizing the goal of larger public good would neither destroy the object nor vitiate the exercise of power of public purpose for development of infrastructure - Appeals dismissed.

(C) Land & Property - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - Land acquisition for integrated projects - Involvement of public interest - Extent of - Held, in the case of integrated and indivisible projects, the project has to be taken as a whole and must be judged whether it is in the larger public interest - Integrated projects cannot be split into different components and to consider whether each and every component would serve public good - If the project taken as a whole is an attempt in the direction of bringing foreign exchange, generating employment opportunities and securing economic benefits to the State and the public at large, it would serve public purpose - Appeals dismissed.

(D) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32, 226, 136 - 'public purpose' - Judicial review - Scope of - Held, a writ court, while exercising powers u/arts. 32, 226 or 136 of the Constitution, cannot substitute its own judgment for the judgment of the Government as to what constitutes 'public purpose' - Question of whether there exists public purpose or not, to be decided by the State, however, the decision of the State in that regard is not beyond the judicial scrutiny - Writ-court can interfere if the State exercise power in mala fide or for collateral purposes or the purported action is de hors the Act, irrational or otherwise unreasonable or the so-called purpose is 'no public purpose' at all and fraud on statute is apparent - Appeals dismissed.
