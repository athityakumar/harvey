Daya Nand v State of Haryana

Supreme Court of India

03 April 2008

Case Analysis

Bench

Arijit Pasayat, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2008 Indlaw SC 2330; (2008) 15 SCC 717; AIR 2008 SC 1823; 2008 CRLJ 2975; 2008 (2) JCC 1283; JT 2008 (5) SC 336; 2008(6) SCALE 62; [2008] 5 S.C.R. 1043

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1960, s. 299(b), 300(3), 302 and 304 Part II - Distinction between culpable homicide and Murder - Appropriate provision to be applied - Appellant convicted for offence punishable u/s. 302, IPC - Conviction upheld by HC - Hence, present appeal - Held, distinction lies between a bodily injury likely to cause death and a bodily injury sufficient in the ordinary course of nature to cause death - Difference is one of the degree of probability of death resulting from the intended bodily injury - Clause (4) of s. 300 would be applicable where the knowledge of the offender as to the probability of death of a person or persons being caused from his imminently dangerous act, approximates to a practical certainty - Such knowledge on the part of the offender must be of the highest degree of probability, the act having been committed by the offender without any excuse for incurring the risk of causing death or such injury - According to the rule laid down in Virsa Singh v. State of Punjab 1958 INDLAW SC 82 even if the intention of accused was limited to the infliction of a bodily injury sufficient to cause death in the ordinary course of nature, and did not extend to the intention of causing death, the offence would not be murder - In present case, considering the evidence on record in the background of the principles of law, appropriate conviction would be u/s. 304 Part II IPC - Conviction accordingly altered - Appeal disposed of.
