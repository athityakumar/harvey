Noor Aga v State of Punjab and Another

Supreme Court of India

09 July 2008

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, V.S. Sirpurkar

Where Reported

2008 Indlaw SC 2605; (2008) 16 SCC 417; AIR 2009 SC (Supp) 852; 2008 (3) JCC (Narcotics) 135; JT 2008 (7) SC 409; 2008 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 2181; 2008 (3) RCR(Criminal) 633; 2008(9) SCALE 681; [2008] 10 S.C.R. 379

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Customs; Health & Drug

Keywords: Samples, Power Of The Court, Human Rights Act, 1998, Constitutionality, Immigration and Asylum Act, 1999, Immigration Act, 1971, Forgery and Counterfeiting Act, 1981, Misuse of Drugs Act, 1971, Misuse of Drugs Act, 1975, Road Traffic Act, 1956, Dangerous Drugs Act, 1986

Summary: (A) Criminal - Customs - Health & Drugs - Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985, ss. 22, 23 - Customs Act, 1962 - Appellant allegedly having possession of contraband articles - Trial Court convicted appellant u/s. 22 and 23 - Appellant filed appeal before HC - HC dismissed the appeal - Whether conviction by Trial Court is sustainable? - Held, material objects seized by the investigating officer was not produced in the Trial Court being allegedly missing and no convincing explanation was rendered in that behalf - Prosecution failed to produce the samples taken from the bulk quantity - HC proceeded on the basis that non-production of physical evidence is not fatal to the prosecution case but the discrepancies in physical evidence dents the credibility of the prosecution - Retracted confession of the appellant cannot be taken as evidence against him - Available court records showed a number of discrepancies with reference to the Forensic Report - No independent witnesses were examined with regard to the seizure - Contradictions could be seen in the statements of official witnesses - Since the investigation was not fair, impugned judgment of conviction cannot be sustained and hence the same is set aside - Appeal allowed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985, ss. 39, 37 - Suspension of sentence u/s. 39 - Power of the Court - Constitutionality - Held, restrictions on the power of the court to suspend the sentence as envisaged in s. 39 of the Act was held to be unconstitutional in Dadu @ Tulsidas v. State of Maharashtra, (2000) 8 SCC 437, subject to the restrictions for grant of bail as contained in s. 37 of the Act - Appeal allowed.

(C) Human Rights - Criminal - Constitution - International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, art. 14(2) - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 20, 21 - Presumption of innocence - Fundamental Rights under the Constitution - Comparison of - Held, presumption of innocence is a human right as envisaged u/art. 14(2) of the International Covenant, however, it cannot per se be equated with the fundamental right and liberty adumbrated in art. 21 of the Constitution of India and to that extent, it cannot militate against other statutory provisions, which subject to the arts. 20 and 21 of the Constitution - Appeal allowed.

(D) Criminal - Constitution - Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985, s. 35 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Offences under the Act - Burden of proof on accused u/s. 35 - Constitutionality - Held, Act was enacted having regard to the mandate contained in International Conventions on Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances - Only because the burden of proof under certain circumstances is placed on the accused, the same, by itself would not render the impugned provisions unconstitutional - A right to be presumed innocent, subject to the establishment of certain foundational facts and burden of proof, to a certain extent, can be placed on an accused and such burden must be construed having regard to the other international conventions and hence it is constitutional - Appeal allowed.

(E) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985, s. 35 - Burden of proof on accused u/s. 35 - Standard of proof - Held, an initial burden of proof with regard to the guilt of accused exists upon the prosecution and only when it stands satisfied, the legal burden would shift to the accused for proving his innocence - Standard of proof required for the accused to prove his innocence is not as high as that of the prosecution - Standard of proof required to prove the guilt of accused on the prosecution is 'beyond all reasonable doubt' but it is 'preponderance of probability' on the accused - If the prosecution fails to prove the foundational facts so as to attract the rigours of s. 35 of the Act, the actus reus which is possession of contraband by the accused cannot be said to have been established - Appeal allowed.

(F) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Burden of proof on accused - Nature of - Held, whether the burden on the accused is a legal burden or an evidentiary burden would depend on the statute in question and its purport and object thereof - Such provisions must pass the test of doctrine of proportionality - Difficulties faced by the prosecution in certain cases may be held to be sufficient to arrive at an opinion that the burden on the accused is an evidentiary burden and not merely a legal burden, however, the trial must be fair and the accused must be provided with opportunities to effectively defend himself - Appeal allowed.

(G) Customs - Practice & Procedure - Customs Act, 1962, s. 108 - Smuggling of goods - Enquiry u/s. 109 - Nature of - Held, leading of evidence, production of document or any other thing in an enquiry u/s. 108 of the Act in connection of smuggling of goods would be a judicial proceeding within the meaning of ss. 193 and 228 of the Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Therefore, enquiry contemplated u/s. 108 is for the purpose of 1962 Act and not for the purpose of convicting an accused under any other statute including the provisions of the Act - Appeal allowed.

(H) Customs - Practice & Procedure - Customs Act, 1962, s. 108 - Retracted confession - Probative value of - Held, any confession made u/s. 108 of the Act is subjected to art. 20(3) of the Constitution of India where it provides that no accused should be compelled to be a witness against himself - A retracted confessional statement may be relied upon but it is made voluntary - Burden of proving that such a confession was made voluntarily would, thus, be on the prosecution - Appeal allowed.

(I) Customs - Customs Act, 1962, s. 53 - Confession to customs officers - Applicability of s. 25 of Indian Evidence Act, 1872 - Whether customs officers under the Act are police officers - Held, customs officers are clothed with the powers of police officers under the Act and therefore, the Act is not only dealt with matter of imposition of penalty or an order of confiscation of the properties under the Act but also with the offences having serious consequences - Further, s. 53 of the Act empowers the customs officers with the powers of the Station House Officers - Therefore, an officer invested with the power of a police officer by reason of Customs Act in terms of s. 53(2) would, thus, be deemed to be police officers and for the said purposes of s. 25 of the Indian Evidence Act should be applicable - Appeal allowed.
