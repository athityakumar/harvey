Shaikh Ali Hossain And Ors. v Sh. Showkat Ali And Anr.

Supreme Court of India

14 May 2008

Case Analysis

Bench

R.V. Raveendran, Tarun Chatterjee

Where Reported

2008 Indlaw SC 2677; (2008) 8 SCC 180; AIR 2008 SC (Supp) 251; JT 2008 (7) SC 265; 2009 (107) RD 332; 2008(9) SCALE 128; [2008] 8 S.C.R. 867

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Tarun Chatterjee, J.

Constitution - Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 226 and 227 - Interference by SC - Whether SC can interfere with the order of the HC u/art. 136 of the Constitution? - Held, SC would not interfere with the order of the HC u/art. 136 of the Constitution if the following conditions exist: (a) where two views are possible and the view taken by the HC is a plausible one; (b) where the order of the HC rendered substantial justice to the parties or did not prejudice either of the parties; (c) Where there is nothing illegal in the reasoning of the order of the HC and the conclusions arrived at by it appear to be well merited and quite in accordance with the rule of interpretation; (d) where the order of the HC is based on the ground that the concurrent orders of the courts below were wrong or incorrect on the face of record; (e) where no special circumstance is shown to exist and the justice of the case on facts does not require interference - Matter referred to Larger Bench.

Constitution - Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 226 and 227 - Interference by HC - Grant or refusal of injunction - Respondents filed suit against the appellants for a decree for declaration that the appellants had no right, title and interest in the suit properties and for permanent injunction - In the suit, the respondents filed an application for injunction restraining the appellants from disturbing, alienating, dealing, encroaching and/or interfering with the possession of the respondents in respect of the suit properties - Respondents alleged that in view of an earlier order of SC, the respondents were entitled to an order of injunction as prayed for - Both the courts below on the interpretation of the order of SC rejected the application for injunction of the respondents - Respondents filed application before the HC u/art. 227 of the Constitution which was allowed - HC set aside the concurrent orders of the courts below rejecting the application for injunction and directed the parties to maintain status quo in respect of the suit properties till the disposal of the suit - Hence, present appeal - Whether HC in exercise of supervisory jurisdiction u/art. 227 was justified in setting aside the concurrent orders of the courts below by giving a different interpretation given to the order of SC? - Held, it cannot be said that the HC, u/art. 226 or 227 of the Constitution, would not at all be entitled to interfere with the concurrent orders of the courts below, more so when the HC finds that the courts below had proceeded on a wrong interpretation or that the orders of the courts below were incorrect on the face of record and accordingly, if the HC replaces its own view on the basis of its interpretation of the order of SC, SC would not interfere with the order of the HC, if the view taken by the HC is a plausible one, in the exercise of discretionary power u/art. 136 of the Constitution - Therefore, where different interpretations of the order of SC are available and the interpretation given by the HC cannot be said to be totally incorrect, in that situation, the parties should be directed to maintain status quo in respect of the suit properties till the suit is decided - It would not be just and proper to interfere with the impugned order of the HC in the exercise of our discretionary power u/art. 136 of the Constitution - Matter referred to Larger Bench.

R. V. Raveendran, J.

Constitution - Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 226 and 227 - Interference by HC - Grant or refusal of injunction - Held, grant or refusal of an injunction is within the judicial discretion of the Trial Court - In SC, the Trial Court had considered the facts in detail and found that the respondents were not in a position to make out a prima facie case and rejected the application; that order was affirmed in appeal - In the circumstances, the HC in exercise of supervisory jurisdiction u/art. 227, was not justified in interfering with the order - On the facts and on proper interpretation of the order of SC, the view taken by the HC was not warranted and calls for interference - Impugned order set aside - Matter referred to Larger Bench.
