Dhanajaya Reddy v State Of Karnataka

Supreme Court of India

14 March 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

R.P. Sethi, K.T. Thomas

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 19893; (2001) 4 SCC 9; (2001) SCC (Cr) 652; AIR 2001 SC 1512; 2001 (2) CCR 26; 2001 (2) CGLJ 59; 2001 (2) Crimes 15; 2001 CRLJ 1712; 2001 (1) JCC 246; JT 2001 (3) SC 395; 2001 (2) RCR(Criminal) 196; 2001(2) SCALE 452; 2001 (2) SCJ 514; [2001] 2 S.C.R. 399; 2001 (2) SLT 725; 2001 (2) Supreme 348

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Defence & Security Forces

Keywords: Criminal Procedure Code, 1963

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34 and 302 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 164 - Extramarital relations between wife and her paramour put an end to life of husband - (A) Circumstantial Evidence - Held, in a case based upon circumstantial evidence, prosecution is under a legal obligation to prove, firstly, on facts existence of such circumstances and secondly, that circumstances form a complete chain which lead to irresistible conclusion that accused are guilty and such circumstances are inconsistent with their innocence - On proof of aforesaid conditions, Court can convict accused of charges framed against them - Evidence on record in this case fully established circumstances against A-2 and A-3 - (B) Whether a voluntary confessional statement can be used as extrajudicial confession when it does not satisfy statutory requirements? - Further held, No - Confessions in criminal law have been categorised to be either judicial or extrajudicial - As extrajudicial confession cannot be treated as judicial confession, similarly an alleged judicial confession proved to have not been legally recorded cannot be used as extrajudicial confession - (C) Conviction of A-1 - Moral conviction regarding involvement of an accused in commission of crime cannot be a substitute for a legal verdict based upon facts and law - Further held, there is no evidence to hold that A-1 has been proved to be guilty beyond any reasonable doubt - Doubt and suspicion of her involvement in ghastly crime cannot be ruled out but suspicion, however strong it may be, cannot take place of proof - Lapses in investigation, failure of judicial officer to record confessional statement in accordance with law and absence of corroborative evidence have to give A-1 benefit of doubt - Conviction and sentenced of A-1 for commission of offence of murder her husband set aside - Order accordingly.
