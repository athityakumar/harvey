Suryanarayana v State of Karnataka

Supreme Court of India

03 January 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

R.P. Sethi, K.T. Thomas

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 20708; (2001) 9 SCC 129; (2002) SCC (Cr) 413; AIR 2001 SC 482; AIR 2001 SCW 81; 2001 (1) CCR 65; 2001 (1) Crimes 99; 2001 CRLJ 705; 2001 (1) JCC 74; JT 2001 (1) SC 230; 2001 (1) RCR(Criminal) 602; 2001(1) SCALE 7; 2001 (1) SCJ 7; [2001] 1 S.C.R. 1; 2001 (1) SLT 278; 2001 (1) Supreme 1; 2001 (1) UC 375

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Appreciation of evidence - Child witness - Whether evidence of a child witness can be made sole basis for conviction? - Admissibility and principles to be considered, reiterated - Held, nothing favouring defence could be extracted out of her in cross-examination - The Trial Court as well as High Court accepted her testimony as no inherent defect was pointed out by defence - The mere fact that her mother had told that she did not know any other language except Malayalam and that words spoken to by her were not in that language cannot be used as a ground to reject her testimony - There is no ground of doubting veracity of testimony of this child witness as her name is mentioned in FIR which is proved to have been recorded immediately after occurrence - There is sufficient corroboration to rule out possibility witness being tutored or used for ulterior purposes by some alleged interested persons - Evidence of child witness cannot be rejected per se, but court, as a rule of prudence, is required to consider such evidence with close scrutiny and only on being convinced about quality of statements and its reliability, base conviction by accepting statement of child witness - The fact of being her being a child witness would require court to scrutinise her evidence with care and caution - If child witness is shown to have stood test of cross-examination and there is no infirmity in her evidence, prosecution can rightly claim a conviction based upon her testimony alone - Corroboration of testimony of a child witness is not a rule but a measure of caution and prudence - Some discrepancies in statement of a child witness cannot be made basis for discarding testimony - Discrepancies in deposition, if not in material particulars, would lend credence to testimony of a child witness who, under normal circumstances, would like to mix up what witness saw with what he or she is likely to imagine to have seen - While appreciating evidence of child witness, courts are required to rule out possibility of child being tutored - In absence of any allegation regarding tutoring or using child witness for ulterior purposes of prosecution, courts have no option but to rely upon confidence inspiring testimony of such witness for purposes of holding accused guilty or not - Under circumstances of cases and relying upon testimony of child witness which is found to be not suffering from any infirmity and is corroborated in all material particulars - Appeal dismissed.
