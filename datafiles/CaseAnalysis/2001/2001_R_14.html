Muninanjappa and Others v R. Manual and Another

Supreme Court of India

11 April 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

A.P. Misra, U.C. Banerjee

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 20174; (2001) 5 SCC 363; AIR 2001 SC 1754; 2002 (48) ALR 179; 2001 (2) CGLJ 368; 2001 (4) RCR(Civil) 684; 2002 (93) RD 616; 2001(3) SCALE 321; [2001] 2 S.C.R. 1113; 2001 (3) SLT 546; 2001 (3) Supreme 315

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal

Keywords: In Lieu Of Maintenance, Hindu Woman's Right to Property Act

Summary: Family & Personal - Hindu Succession Act, 1956, s. 14 (1) - Indian Succession Act, 1925 - Hindu Woman's Right to Property Act, 1937 - Interpretation of will - True intentions of testator - Whether right given to G, under will was a limited right? - If G had a limited right, whether his widow S could get absolute right u/s. 14(1) of Hindu Succession Act, 1956 to execute impugned sale deed in favour of respondents 1 and 2? - Held, language in will is clear that testatrix intended to give limited right to both plaintiff and G and absolute right only to sons born to them - Limited right of G cannot be interpreted by any stretch of language that testatrix intended to give absolute right to G or to his widow - They were to hold property for delivery to son, in case, born out of their wedlock - In no case S right over property would mature into absolute right by virtue of s. 14(1) of Hindu Succession Act, 1956 - Her right could only mature as such, if her claim could be based on any of her pre - existing rights including right in lieu of maintenance out of her husband's property - But in no case it would mature where property is held by her husband either in trust for benefit of others or as limited and restricted owner with no right to alienate - Hence even if S continued to enjoy property after death of her husband, she held property at most, in same capacity as her husband but not to claim it towards her right of maintenance - If husband had any other property apart from what was gifted by P, she could claim her above right u/s. 14(1) but not over property given to her husband G as a limited owner - HC fell into error while construing s. 14(1) of Hindu Succession Act by extending its width so wide which spills over its permissible boundary when it held, a Hindu wife will acquire absolute right in property of her husband and then applying it to facts of this case - It seems High Court was not apprised of settled law, in respect of field of s. 14(1) as declared by this court as far back as in V Tulasamma v Sesha Reddy (1977 Indlaw SC 281) and also reiterated in Velamuri Venkata Sivaprasad v Kothuri Venkateswarlu (1999 Indlaw SC 846) which holds that benefit to a female could be given u/s. 14(1) where her claim is based on her pre - existing right over her husband's property - S. 14(2) is in nature of a proviso to s. 14(1) - S. 14(1) applies to property granted to a female Hindu by virtue of a pre - existing right of maintenance - Decision while carrying out field of s. 14(2) held that sub - section (2) must be confined to cases where property is acquired by a Hindu female for first time as a grant, without any pre - existing right, terms of which prescribe a restricted estate in property - Where, however, property is acquired by a Hindu female at a partition or in lieu of maintenance, it is in virtue of a pre - existing right and such an acquisition would not be within scope of sub - s. (2), but within scope of sub - s. (1) - Thus will D/- 01-06-1942, grants G limited and restrictive right in no case to mature into full right - As a consequence of this court answer second question by holding that S did not inherit suit property from her husband nor possessed it in lieu of maintenance hence question of it maturing into full right u/s. 14(1) of Hindu Succession Act does not arise - Set aside findings and judgment of both trial court and HC and decree suit of plaintiff - Order accordingly.
