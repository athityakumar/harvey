Hameed Joharan (D) and others v Abdul Salam(D) By Lrs. and others

Supreme Court of India

13 August 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

U.C. Banerjee, A.P. Misra

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 21137; (1996) 3 SCC 318; AIR 1996 SC 1543; 2001 (5) ALD(SC) 111; 1996 (83) E.L.T. 255; JT 1996 (3) SC 242; 1996(2) SCALE 626; 2001(5) SCALE 163; [1996] 2 S.C.R. 1134; 1996 (2) Supreme 670

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Summary: Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, ss. 2(15), 2(2), 28, 35, 44(1), 48, 67(3) - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Indian Stamp Act, 1899 - Limitation Act, 1963 - Suit for partition - Execution - Validity - In a suit for partition final decree was passed - Decree-holder did not furnish any stamp paper to draft final decree as such no decree was drafted or finalized - Original decree-holder died and that application was filed by legal representatives of decree- holder to implead themselves as additional plaintiffs and same was ordered and execution application was presented before District Court - Revision Petition was filed before HC against order of impleadment but same was dismissed - Execution petition was dismissed with finding that since same was filed beyond twelve years, execution petition was barred by limitation - Subsequently, Revision Petition was filed and HC however set aside order of executing court and directed that question of limitation should be considered afresh - Trial Court held that Execution Petition was not barred by limitation - Revision Petition was filed before HC by legal heirs of defendant no. 1 challenging said finding - Single Judge allowed revision petition and set aside order of Trial court - Consequently, execution petition also stood dismissed - Hence, instant appeal - Whether executive petition was time barred - Held, limitations starts by reason of statutory provisions as prescribed in statute - Time did not stop running at instance of any individual unless, of course, same had statutory sanction being conditional, as more fully noticed - In Bholanath Karmakar and Ors. v. Madanmohan Karmakar, 1987 Indlaw CAL 130 had completely misread and misapplied law for reasons noted above and thus could not but be said to be not correctly decided and thus stands overruled - Undoubtedly, judgment of HC had been a very learned judgment but appreciation of legislative intent had not been effected in manner apposite to intent rather had quick shift therefrom by reason wherefor, Special Bench came to a manifest error in recording that period of limitation for execution of a partition decree should not begin to run until decree was engrossed on requisite stamp paper - Appeal dismissed.
