A.C. Arulappan v Smt. Ahalya Naik

Supreme Court of India

13 August 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

U.C. Banerjee, A.P. Misra

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 21399; (2001) 6 SCC 600; AIR 2001 SC 2783; 2001 (5) ALD(SC) 90; 2001 (44) ALR 853; 2001 (3) AWC 2456; JT 2001 (6) SC 394; 2001(5) SCALE 154; 2001 (3) SCJ 570; 2001 (5) SLT 739; [2001] Supp1 S.C.R. 425; 2001 (5) Supreme 730; 2001 (2) UC 553B

Case Digest

Subject: Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Indian Independence Adoptation of Central Acts and Ordinance Order, 1948, Government of India Adoptation of Indian Laws Order, 1937, Rent Act, 1977

Summary: Practice & Procedure - Limitation Act, 1963, art. 136 - Indian Stamp Act 1899, ss. 2(15) and 35 - Availability of plea of limitation in matter of execution of decree - In a suit for partition a final decree thereon was passed - Suit being a suit for partition, the parties were under an obligation to furnish the stamp paper for drafting of the final decree - Decree-holder did not furnish any stamp paper by reason wherefor, no decree was drafted or finalised - Meanwhile, original decree-holder died and legal representatives of the original plaintiff were impleaded - Thereafter, execution application was presented before the District Court - However, execution petition was dismissed with a finding that since the same was filed beyond twelve years, the execution petition was barred by limitation - Subsequently, on being directed by HC question of limitation was considered afresh and it was held that Execution Petition was not barred by limitation - Revision Petition filed thereagainst was allowed - Consequently, the execution petition also stood dismissed - Hence, present Special Leave Petition - Appellant submitted that question as to when a decree for partition becomes enforceable cannot be decided without reference to relevant provisions of Stamp Act, since a decree for partition was also an instrument of partition in terms of s. 2(15) of the Stamp Act - It was further contended that period of limitation, in respect of the partition decree cannot begin to run till it was engrossed on requisite stamp paper - Held, whole purport of the Indian Stamp Act is to make available certain dues and to collect revenue but it does not mean and imply, overriding the effect over another statute operating on a completely different sphere - Limitation starts by reason of the statutory provisions as prescribed in the statute - Prescription of a twelve year period cannot possibly be obliterated by an enactment wholly unconnected therewith - Legislative mandate as sanctioned u/art. 136 of Limitation Act cannot be kept in abeyance unless the self same legislation makes a provision therefore - Time does not stop running at the instance of any individual unless, of course, the same has a statutory sanction being conditional - Appeal dismissed.
