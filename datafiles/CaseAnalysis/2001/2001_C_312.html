Oxford University Press, Etc v Commissioner of Income Tax

Supreme Court of India

24 January 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

S.P. Bharucha

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 240; (2001) 3 SCC 359; AIR 2001 SC 886; 2001 (165) CTR 629; [2001] 247 ITR 658; JT 2001 (2) SC 203; 2001(1) SCALE 390; 2001 (2) SCJ 141; [2001] 1 S.C.R. 574; 2001 (1) SLT 834; 2001 (1) Supreme 357; [2001] 115 TAXMAN 69

Case Digest

Subject: Education; Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Income Tax Act, 1961, ss. 148 and 144B - University Grants Commission Act, 1956, ss. 2(f) and 52(2) - Income Tax Act, 1922, ss. 16(3), 24(2), 10(22), 256(1) and 10(22A) - Whether on facts and in circumstances of case, tribunal was justified in holding that Oxford University Press Bombay, which is part of Oxford University, is exempt u/s. 10(22) of IT Act, 1961? - Main basis for claim for exemption from tax is that Oxford University Press is a part of Oxford University in U.K. and, therefore, its income is exempted from tax u/s. 10(22) of Act - High Court held even assuming that Oxford University Press is a part of Oxford University, what is existing in India is only 'Oxford University Press' which is assessee and in such a situation assessee cannot be regarded as a university or an educational institution existing solely for educational purposes and not for purpose of profit, therefore, assessee is not entitled to exemption provided u/s. 10(22) of Act - Held, in using expression 'existing solely for educational purposes and not for purposes of profit' legislature had made it clear that it intends to exempt income of institutions established solely for educational purposes and not for commercial activities - A provision of exemption from tax in a fiscal statute is to be strictly construed - Interpretation of such a statutory provision which does not stand test of rationality and will lead to absurd result cannot be accepted - Giving a purposeful interpretation of provision it will be reasonable to hold that in order to be eligible to claim exemption from tax u/s. 10(22) of Act assessee has to establish that it is engaged in some educational activity in India and its existence in this country is not for profit only - This interpretation of s. 10(22) neither causes violence to language of provision nor does it amount to re-writing same - On other hand, it only gives a harmonious construction of provision which subserves object and purpose for which provision in intended to serve - In facts and circumstances of case HC rightly held that assessee, Oxford University Press, was not entitled to exemption from tax u/s. 10(22) of Act - Appeals dismissed.
