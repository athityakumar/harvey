Dr. Surajmani Stella Kujur v Durga Charan Hansdah and Another

Supreme Court of India

14 February 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

R.P. Sethi, K.T. Thomas

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 19898; (2001) 3 SCC 13; (2001) SCC (Cr) 1305; AIR 2001 SC 938; 2001 (1) AllCriC 1089; 2001 (42) ALR 847; 2001 (2) AWC 969; 2001 (1) Bom.C.R. 609; 2001 (1) CCR 208; 2001 (1) Crimes 263; 2001 (89) DLT 836; 2001 (2) G.L.R. 929; 2001 (1) JCC 164; JT 2001 (2) SC 631; 2001 (1) RCR(Civil) 766; 2001 (1) RCR(Criminal) 851; 2001(2) SCALE 21; 2001 (2) SCJ 281; [2001] 1 S.C.R. 1028; 2001 (2) SLT 86; 2001 (1) Supreme 681; 2001 (1) UC 449

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Family & Personal

Keywords: Constitution (Scheduled Tribes) Order, 1950

Summary: Family & Personal - Hindu Marriage Act, 1955, ss. 10 and 13 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13(3), 20 and 366 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 494 - General Clauses Act, 1897, s. 3(37) - Constitution of India (Scheduled Tribes) Order, 1950, s. 2 - Tribals - Oraon and Santhal - Deemed to be Hindus - Whether respondent who is a tribal professing Hinduism, but his marriage being governed only by Santhal customs and usage can be punished for bigamy ? - Held, s. 2 of Act specifies persons to whom Act is applicable - Applicability of Act is therefore comprehensive and applicable to all persons domiciled in territory of India who are not Muslims, Christians, Parsis or Jews by religion - It is conceded even by appellant that parties to petition are two tribals, who otherwise profess Hinduism, but their marriage being out of purview of Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 in light of s. 2(2) of Act, are thus governed only by their Santhal customs and usage - Appellant relying upon an alleged custom in tribe which mandates monogamy as a rule has contended that as respondent has solemnised a second marriage during subsistence of first marriage with appellant, second marriage being void, respondent is liable to be prosecuted for offence punishable u/s. 494 of IPC - No custom can create an offence as it essentially deals with civil rights of parties and no person can be convicted of any offence except for violation of law in force at time of commission of act charged - For custom to have colour of a rule or law, it is necessary for party claiming it, to plead and thereafter prove that such custom is ancient, certain and reasonable - Custom being in derogation of general rule is required to be construed strictly - Party relying upon a custom is obliged to establish it by clear and unambiguous evidence - Mere pleading of a custom stressing for monogamy by itself was not sufficient unless it was further pleaded that second marriage was void by reason of its taking place during life of such husband or wife - In order to prove second marriage void, appellant was under an obligation to show existence of a custom which made such marriage null, ineffectual, having no force of law or binding effect, incapable of being enforced in law or non est - Fact of second marriage being void is a sine qua non for applicability of s. 494 IPC - It is settled position of law that for fastening criminal liability, prosecution or complainant is obliged to prove existence of all ingredients constituting crime which are normally and usually defined by a statute - Appellant herself appears to be not clear in her stand inasmuch as in her statement in court recorded on 24-10-1992 she has stated that 'I am a Hindu by religion' - Complaint was dismissed by trial court holding, ''there is no mention of any such custom in complaint nor is there evidence of such custom'' - In absence of pleadings and evidence reference to book alone is not sufficient - Therefore trial Magistrate and High Court have rightly dismissed complaint of appellant - Appeal dismissed.
