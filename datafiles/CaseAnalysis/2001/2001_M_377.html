Union of India v Messrs Popular Construction Company

Supreme Court of India

05 October 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

Ruma Pal, G.B. Pattanaik

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 26; (2001) 8 SCC 470; AIR 2001 SC 4010; 2001 (6) ALD(SC) 52; 2002 AllCJ 668; 2001 (45) ALR 531; 2001 (44) ARBLR 345; 2001 (4) AWC 3132; 2002 (2) Bom.C.R. 123; 2002 (1) CGLJ 76; 2001 (2) CLC 1536; 2002 (1) CompLJ 46; 2001 (3) JhrCR(SC) 330; JT 2001 (8) SC 271; 2001 (3) RAJ 163; 2002 (1) RCR(Civil) 124; 2001(6) SCALE 657; 2001 (4) SCJ 571; 2001 (7) SLT 104; 2001 (Supp) MLJ(SC) 22; [2001] Supp3 S.C.R. 619; 2001 (7) Supreme 354

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Practice & Procedure - Limitation Act, 1963, s. 5 - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, s. 34, 34(1),(2),(3) - Proviso s. 5 of the 1963 Act - Applicability to the 1996 Act - Arbitrator passed the award, which was filed before HC for decree - Appellant filed application challenging the award u/s. 30 r/w s. 16 of the 1940 Act - Application was amended by inserting the words 'Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996' in place of 'Arbitration Act 1940' - The application was dismissed by Single Judge on the ground that it was barred by limitation u/s. 34 of the 1996 Act - DB rejected the appeal and upheld the findings of the Single Judge - Hence instant appeal - Whether the provisions of s. 5 of the 1963 Act are applicable to an application challenging an award, u/s. 34 of the 1996 Act.

Held, apart from the language, 'express exclusion' may follow from the scheme and object of the special or local law. Here the history and scheme of the 1996 Act support the conclusion that the time limit prescribed u/s. 34 to challenge an Award is absolute and un-extendable by Court u/s. 5 of the Act. The Arbitration and Conciliation Bill, 1995 which preceded the 1996 Act stated as one of its main objectives the need 'to minimise the supervisory role of courts in the arbitral process'. The 'Part' referred to in s. 5 is Part I of the 1996 Act which deals with domestic arbitrations. S. 34 is contained in Part I and is therefore subject to the sweep of the prohibition contained in s. 5 of the 1996 Act. Furthermore, s. 34(1) itself provides that recourse to a court against an arbitral award may be made only by an application for setting aside such award 'in accordance with' s. 34(2) and 34(3). S. 34(2) relates to grounds for setting aside an award and is not relevant for our purposes. But an application filed beyond the period mentioned in s. 34(3), would not be an application 'in accordance with' that sub section. Consequently by virtue of s. 34 (1), recourse to the court against an arbitral award cannot be made beyond the period prescribed. The importance of the period fixed u/s. 34 is emphasised by the provisions of s. 36 which provide that 'where the time for making an application to set aside the arbitral award u/s. 34 has expired the award shall be enforced and the CPC in the same manner as if it were a decree of a court'. This is a significant departure from the provisions of the 1940 Act. Under the 1940 Act, after the time to set aside the award expired, the court was required to 'proceed to pronounce judgment according to the award and upon the judgment so pronounced a decree shall follow'. Now the consequence of the time expiring u/s. 34 of the 1996 Act is that the award becomes immediately enforceable without any further act of the Court. If there were any residual doubt on the interpretation of the language used in s. 34, the scheme of the 1996 Act would resolve the issue in favour of curtailment of the Court's powers by the exclusion of the operation of s. 5 of the 1963 Act. Appeal dismissed.
