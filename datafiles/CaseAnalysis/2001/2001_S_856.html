Muraleedharan v State of Kerala

Supreme Court of India

18 April 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

K.T. Thomas, R.P. Sethi

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 20165; (2001) 4 SCC 638; (2001) SCC (Cr) 795; AIR 2001 SC 1699; 2001 (2) CCR 155; 2001 CRLJ 2187; JT 2001 (5) SC 84; 2001 (2) KLT 355; 2001(3) SCALE 440; [2001] 3 S.C.R. 57; 2001 (3) SLT 533; 2001 (3) Supreme 551B

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Criminal Procedure Code, 1963

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Kerala Abkari Act, 1077, s.8 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s.438 - Anticipatory bail - Reversed - Legality - Appellant who was described by the investigating agency as one of the kingpins in a series of grave crimes including the offence u/s. 8 of Act found it easy to secure orders of anticipatory bail in all those cases from the Sessions Judge - But HC within a month, reversed those orders of the Sessions Judge as per an order passed by Single Judge which was sought to be impugned in SC - Whether HC rightly reversed order of anticipatory bail granted by Sessions Court - Held, SC had held, time and again, that no person who was involved in an offence under that Act should be released on bail in contravention of the conditions laid down in the said Section - If the position was thus in regard to an accused even after arrest, it was incomprehensible how the position would be less when he approaches the court for pre-arrest bail knowing that he would also be implicated as an accused - Union of India vs. Ram Samujh and anr. 1999 Indlaw SC 680, relied on - Custodial interrogation of such accused was indispensably necessary for the investigating agency to unearth all the links involved in the criminal conspiracies committed by the persons which ultimately led to the capital tragedy - SC expressed our reprobation at the supercilious manner in which the Sessions Judge decided to think that no material could be collected by the investigating agency to connect the petitioner with the crime except the confessional statement of the co-accused - Such a wayward thinking emanating from a Sessions Judge deserved judicial condemnation - No court could afford to presume that the investigating agency would fail to trace out more materials to prove the accusation against an accused - SC was at a loss to understand what would have prompted the Sessions Judge to conclude that the investigating agency would not be able to collect any material to connect the appellant with the crime - Order of the Sessions Judge, blessing the appellant with a pre-arrest bail order, would have remained as a bugbear of how the discretion conferred on Sessions Judges u/s. 438 of the CrPC would have been misused - It was heartening that HC did not allow such an order to remain in force for long - Appeals dismissed.
