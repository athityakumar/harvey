Raju Ram v State of Bihar

Supreme Court of India

14 August 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

K.T. Thomas, K.G. Balakrishnan

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 21415; (2005) 10 SCC 571

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34, 302, 387 - Code of Criminal Procedure ,1973 - Murder - Conviction - Eye-witness - Accused persons had entered into shop of deceased ‘X’ with revolvers & dagger and demanded money - ‘X’ was not able to pay said demand accused no. 1 had plunged dagger into abdomen of ‘X’ on which he died - Police complaint was lodged against accused persons - Accused was charged for offence u/ss. 34, 302, 387 of IPC - Trial Court found guilty against accused had passed conviction order, same was confirmed by HC - Accused no. 3/appellant had challenged said orders of Lower Courts - Hence instant appeal.

Held, appellant made bold endeavour to contend that maximum which present appellant would have intended along with his co-assailants was to commit extortion and nothing more. Court applied its mind seriously to various facets of said contention. Appellant first submitted that it is possible that two persons who held victim with revolvers would not have intended to murder him because if they had so intended they would certainly have used revolver. Second is alternative contention that they would have carried and shown only toy revolvers. Either of said alternatives is not enough to dispel possibility of three persons entertaining common intention to commit murder of deceased. Court’s reasons are if two persons carried only toy revolvers that would explain why those two persons could not kill deceased. If revolvers were genuine, why one of them at least did not use it need not be countenanced by Court because third one had plunged his dagger which itself was lethal weapon. If three persons had decided to go for committing extortion they would have thought of what should be done in case they failed to extract money. It is unthinkable for Court that three would have decided to walk back coolly on failure of their mission to commit extortion. State invited Court’s attention to situation and time, in order to emphasise his point that there would not have been possibility of three persons walking back without achieving their purpose and without inflicting any harm to ‘X’. Incident happened at 6.30 p.m. on day in month of January. Evidently, it would have been dark then. Be that as it may, Court finds it extremely difficult to decipher intention of accused no.3 from that of other two persons for purpose of de-escalating penal liability to be fastened on him. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - It is well settled salutary principle that accused is liable to convict when prosecution proved case beyond reasonable doubt.
