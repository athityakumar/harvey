International Woollen Mills v Standard Wool (U. K.) Limited

Supreme Court of India

25 April 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

S.N. Variava, V.N. Khare

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 21136; (2001) 5 SCC 265; AIR 2001 SC 2134; 2001 (44) ALR 354; JT 2001 (5) SC 147; 2001 (3) MLJ(SC) 99; 2001 (3) RCR(Civil) 158; [2001] 3 S.C.R. 166

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Keywords: Foreign Court, Foreign Judgment

Summary: Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, ss. 11, 13, 13(a), 13(b), 3, 38, 39, 40, 44A, 47 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 114 - Constructive Resjudicata - Execution of Foreign Decree - Merits - Appellant filed application for dismissal of execution application as it was filed without following procedure prescribed u/ss. 38, 39 and 40 CPC - In reply to this Respondent contended that execution was under s. 44-A of CPC and as such there was no requirement to observe provisions of ss. 38, 39 and 40 CPC - Appellants filed another Application stating that decree was not on merits and as per provisions of s. 44(A) r/w s. 13(b) CPC and Court had to refuse to execute decree - Both Applications were heard by Civil Judge and both applications were dismissed - Civil Revision was filed by appellant which was dismissed by HC - By this Judgment HC found that decree was not on merits but it still dismissed Revision on ground that second Application was barred by principles of constructive res-judicata - Hence instant appeals - Whether HC was right in holding that second Application was barred on principles of constructive res-judicata - Held, both applications were heard together - In other words second Application was filed and heard before any decision was given in first Application - There was thus no question of their being decision finally deciding right or claim between parties - Explanation IV to s. 11 CPC would have come into play only if some decision had been finally given before second Application was filed - Further held, that appellants could had, instead of filing second Application, amended their first Application and taken these pleas in that Application itself - Had they amended first Application there would be no bar of res-judicata or constructive res judicata - To be remembered that Orders were passed after hearing arguments on both the Applications - Under such circumstances no question arises of their being any res-judicata or constructive res-judicata - (B) Whether above mentioned decree of English Court could be executed in India - Held that if decree had not been given on merits of case then foreign judgment was not conclusive between parties and same could not be executed in India - s. 114 of Evidence Act merely raises presumption, under illustration (e) thereof, that judicial acts have been regularly performed - To say that decree was passed regularly was completely different from saying that decree had been passed on merits - An ex-parte decree passed without consideration of merits may be decree passed regular if permitted by rules of that Court - Such a decree would be valid in that country in which it was passed unless set aside by Court of Appeal - Further, held that Judgment and decree does not indicate whether any documents were looked into and/or whether merits of case was at all considered - It merely grants to respondent decree for amounts mentioned - Appellant had replied to notice of respondent that goods were of inferior quality and not as per contract - Court has not applied its mind or dealt with this aspect - It had not examined points at controversy between parties - It was given ex-parte as appellant did not appear at hearing of Suit - Therefore, it was not judgment on merits - Hence, it was clear that such decree could not be said to be decree on merits and such decree could not be enforced in India - Appeals disposed of.
