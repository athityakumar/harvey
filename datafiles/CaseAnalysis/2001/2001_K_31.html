K. S. Bhoir v State of Maharashtra and Others

Supreme Court of India

12 December 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

V.N. Khare, B.N. Agrawal

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 21262; (2001) 10 SCC 264; AIR 2002 SC 444; JT 2001 (10) SC 445; 2001(8) SCALE 456; 2002 (2) SLR 765; [2001] Supp5 S.C.R. 593; 2002 (1) UPLBEC 559

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Health & Drug

Keywords: Establishment of New Medical Colleges, Opening of Higher Courses of Study and Increase of Admission Capacity in Medical Colleges Regulation, 1993

Summary: Education - Administrative - Dentists Act, 1948, ss. 10A & B(3) - Indian Medical Council Act, 1956, ss. 10, 33 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Entrance Test - Revised Merit List - Refusal to Increase Admission Capacity - Justifiability - Respondent issued revised merit list as per direction of HC, in petition filed by students who had appeared for entrance and seeking admission in medical colleges - Appellants were displaced due to revised merit list - Hence, instant appeals - Appellants contended that , Central Govt. acted illegally in refusing to grant permission for one time increase in admissions capacity in various Medical Colleges - Whether direction issued by HC was justifiable - Held, s. 10(A)(1) of the 1948 Act was substantive provision in itself and begins with non-obstante clause i.e. there was a prohibition in matter of increase in admission capacity in medical college unless previous permission of Central Govt. was obtained in accordance with recommendation of Medical Council of India - Object being to achieve highest standard of medical education, which could be achieved only by ensuring that medical college had requisite infrastructure to impart medical education - Thus, Central Govt. was fully justified in rejecting request of respondent for grant of permission for one time increase in admission capacity in medicine and Dentistry courses in various medical colleges - In instant case, observation made by HC were in context of extraordinary and difficult situation that had arisen due to revision of merit list - It was no doubt true that large number of students who were already admitted in colleges incurred lot of expenditure in taking admissions were to be dislodged by issue of revised merit list - One could sympathise with plight of such students - Further compassion and sympathy had no role to play where a rule of law was required to be enforced - Adjusting equities in exercise of extraordinary jurisdiction u/art. 226 was one thing, and HC assuming role of Central Govt. and Medical Council u/s. 10A of the 1956 Act was a different thing - It was not permissible for HC to direct an authority under the 1956 Act to act contrary to statutory provisions - Thus, HC was justified in issuing various direction and affirmed judgment of HC - Appeals dismissed.
