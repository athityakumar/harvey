Firozuddin Basheeruddin and Others v State of Kerala

Supreme Court of India

20 August 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

D.P. Mohapatra, K.T. Thomas

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 20017; (2001) 7 SCC 596; (2001) SCC (Cr) 1341; AIR 2001 SC 3488; 2001 (3) CCR 188; 2001 (4) Crimes 162; 2001 CRLJ 4215; JT 2001 (6) SC 547; 2001 (3) KLT 189; 2001 (4) RCR(Criminal) 20; 2001(5) SCALE 335; 2001 (5) SLT 880; 2001 (6) Supreme 247

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Defence & Security Forces

Keywords: Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (as amended by Act No.25 of 2005 & Act No.2 of 2006)

Summary: Criminal - Conspiracy - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34, 109, 120B, 143, 148, 194, 201, 302 and 511 - Arms Act, 1959, ss. 27 and 25 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 10 - (A) Principles for criminal conspiracy and degree of proof required laid down - Held, conspiracy criminalizes an agreement to commit a crime - Agreement constitutes act, and intention to achieve unlawful objective of that agreement constitutes required mental state - All conspirators are liable for crimes committed in furtherance of conspiracy by any member of group, regardless of whether liability would be established by law of complicity - Conspirators may be tried and punished for both conspiracy and completed crime - (B) Determination of scope of a conspiracy - Further held, conspiracy is not only a substantive crime, it also serves as a basis for holding one person liable for crimes of others in cases where application of usual doctrines of complicity would not render that person liable - (C) Regarding admissibility of evidence - Further held, loosened standards prevail in a conspiracy trial because a conspiracy is hatched in secrecy and it may be difficult to adduce direct evidence of same - Conspirators are liable on an agency theory for statements of co - conspirators, just as they are for overt acts and crimes committed by their confreres - Conspiracy can be undoubtedly proved by such evidence direct or circumstantial - (D) Conspiracy - Further held, most important ingredient of offence of conspiracy is agreement between two or more persons to do an illegal act - Illegal act may or may not be done in pursuance of agreement, but very agreement is an offence and is punishable - Court must enquire whether two persons are independently pursuing same end or they have come together in pursuit of unlawful object - Former does not render them conspirators, but latter does - Evidence as to transmission of thoughts sharing unlawful design may be sufficient - To establish a charge of conspiracy knowledge about indulgence in either an illegal act or a legal act by illegal means is necessary - Further held, prosecution has not to establish that a particular unlawful use was intended, so long as goods or service in question could not be put to any lawful use - Appeals dismissed.
