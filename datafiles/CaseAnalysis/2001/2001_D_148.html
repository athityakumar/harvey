State (Delhi Administration) v Dharampal

Supreme Court of India

19 October 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

S.N. Variava, K.T. Thomas

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 20714; (2001) 10 SCC 372; (2003) SCC (Cr) 1012; AIR 2001 SC 2924; AIR 2001 SCW 4385; 2002 (46) ALR 579; 2001 (4) CCR 215; 2002 (1) Crimes 136; 2001 CRLJ 4748; 2001 (2) JCC 312; JT 2001 (9) SC 136; 2001 (4) RCR(Criminal) 550; 2001(7) SCALE 414; 2001 (5) SCJ 391; 2001 (7) SLT 474; [2001] Supp4 S.C.R. 448; 2001 (8) Supreme 181

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Limitation Act, 1963, art. 114 - Prevention of Food Adulteration Act, 1954, ss. 12(2), 13(2), 20 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Food adulteration - Conviction - Sustainability - Respondent was found guilty u/s. 20 of the 1954 Act on report of Public analyst, which found that sample to be non- confirming to prescribed standards - Complaint was filed in Trial Court - Respondent exercised his right u/s. 13(2) of the 1954 Act - Director, Central Food Laboratory also found adulterating material, starches and colouring material in sample - Respondent was convicted by Trial Court - Respondent preferred appeal before Sessions Court - Trial Court acquitted respondent only on ground that Trial Court, while recording statement of respondent u/s. 313 CrPC, did not read out contents of certificate of Director to accused - As against this acquittal appellants filed appeal to HC - HC dismissed appeals - Hence, instant appeals - (A) Whether HC was justified in acquitting accused - Held, both Trial Court and HC were wrong in concluding that omission to put contents of certificate of Director, could only result in accused being acquitted - Accused had to show that some prejudice was caused to him by report not being put to him - Even otherwise, it was duty of Trial Court or HC that if they found that some vital circumstance had not been put to accused, to put those questions to counsel for accused and get the answers of accused - If accused could not give any plausible or reasonable explanation it would have to be assumed that there was no explanation - Both Trial Court and HC had overlooked this position of law and failed to perform their duties and thereby wrongly acquitted accused - (B) Whether appeals filed were barred by limitation - Held appeals by State Govt. or Central Govt. continue to be governed by art. 114(a) of the 1963 Act - In other words, those appeals should be filed within 90 days from the date of order appealed from - Needless to state if there was a delay in filing an appeal by State Govt. or Central Govt. it would be open to them to file an application u/s. 5 of the 1963 Act for condonation of such delay - HC was thus wrong in concluding that appeals had to be filed within 60 days as provided in s. 378(5) CrPC - Appeals disposed of.
