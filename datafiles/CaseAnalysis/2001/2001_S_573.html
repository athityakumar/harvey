Arvind Singh v State of Bihar

Supreme Court of India

26 April 2001

Case Analysis

Bench

U.C. Banerjee, K.G. Balakrishnan

Where Reported

2001 Indlaw SC 20096; (2001) 6 SCC 407; (2001) SCC (Cr) 1148; AIR 2001 SC 2124; AIR 2001 SCW 1913; 2001 (2) CCR 186; 2 Crimes 242; 2002 (1) CRJ 768; 2001 CRLJ 2556; 2001 (1) JCC 341; JT 2001 (5) SC 127; 2001 (2) RCR(Criminal) 631; 2001(3) SCALE 549; 2001 (4) SCJ 444; [2001] 3 S.C.R. 218; 2001 (3) SLT 771; 2001 (3) Supreme 570; 2001 (2) UC 23

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 (as amended by Act No.25 of 2005 & Act No.2 of 2006)

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 498-A and 304-B - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 32 - Dowry death - Appreciation of evidence - (A) Cruelty - Meaning and Scope of - Held, requirement of s. 498-A is acts of cruelty by husband of a woman or any relative of husband - Word cruelty in common English acceptation denotes a state of conduct which is painful and distressing to another - The legislative intent thus is clear enough to indicate that in event of there being a state of conduct by husband to wife or by any relative of husband which can be attributed to be painful or distressing, same would be within meaning of section - In instant case there is no evidence whatsoever, therefore conviction u/s. 498A is not sustainable - (B) Dying declaration - Reliability of - Held, dying declaration herein has not been effected before any doctor or any independent witness but to mother who is said to have arrived at place only in morning - Mother admittedly is an interested witness - Though that by itself would not discredit evidence tendered in court but fact remains that doctors evidence considering nature of burns posed a considerable doubt as to whether such a statement could be made half-an-hour before death of accused - Severity of burn injury and its impact on body speaks volumes by reason of death of deceased - It is reliance on such a dying declaration by High Court which shall thus have to be scrutinized with a certain degree of caution - Evidence of this declaration depicts that just before a few minutes of her death, deceased would make a declaration quietly to mother naming therein all three relations along with husband who poured kerosene to burn her alive - Having regard to declaration being made to mother only, this is not acceptable - In any event, it is not conceivable that husband along with father-in-law, mother-in-law, brother-in-law would start pouring kerosene together on to girl - As if each was prepared with a can of kerosene to pour simultaneously - This not only would lead to an absurdity but reliance on such a vague statement would be opposed to basic tenets of law - Dying declarations shall have to be dealt with care and caution and corroboration thereof though not essential as such, but is otherwise expedient to have same in order to strengthen evidentiary value of declaration Independent witnesses may not be available but there should be proper care and caution in matter of acceptance of such a statement as trustworthy evidence - Question of dying declaration to mother is not worth acceptance and HC thus clearly fell into an error in such an acceptance - Appeal allowed.
