 K. Damodarasamy Naidu and Bros. and Others v State of Tamil Nadu and Another

Supreme Court of India

12 October 1999

Case Analysis

Bench

S.P. Bharucha, B.N. Kirpal, D.P. Mohapatra, N. Santosh Hegde, V.N. Khare

Where Reported

1999 Indlaw SC 2006; (2000) 1 SCC 521; AIR 1999 SC 3909; JT 1999 (8) SC 309; 2000 (117) STC 1; [1999] Supp3 S.C.R. 597

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Practice & Procedure; Sales Tax

Keywords: Intention And Attempt

Summary: Sales Tax - Constitution (Forty - Sixth Amendment) Act, 1982 - Uttar Pradesh Sales Tax Act, 1948 - Tamil Nadu General Sales Tax Act - Tamil Nadu General (Sales Tax) Fourth Amendment Act, 1984 - (A) Whether mere amendment of definitions of 'sale' and 'dealer' was not enough to entitle State of Tamil Nadu to levy tax prior to 1997? - Held, once definition of 'sale' in said Tamil Nadu Act was amended to include supply of food and drink, supply of food and drink fell within purview of charging section thereof and became exigible to tax thereunder? - That State Legislature had earlier chosen specifically to incorporate 3-A and 3-B to tax transfer of right to use goods and transfer of goods involved in work contracts respectively does not lead to conclusion that, therefore, it had not intended to tax supply of food and drink until s. 3-D was inserted in 1997 - (B) Whether tax on food served in restaurants could not be levied on sum total of price charged to customer? - Further held, art. 366 (29-A)(f) permits States to impose a tax on supply of food and drink - Tax is on supply of food or drink and it is not of relevance that supply is by way of a service or as part of a service - In our view, therefore, price that customer pays for supply of food in a restaurant cannot be split up - (C) Whether tax could not be levied on composite charge for boarding and lodging unless State made rules which set down formulae for determining that component of composite charge which was exigible to tax on food and drink? - Further held, it is in practical terms impossible for Sales Tax Authorities to make assessments upon basis of facts relevant to each individual customer in each individual hotel - Generalisations are, therefore, inevitable and there is every likelihood that basis of generalisation made by one Sales Tax Officer would differ from basis of generalisation made by another, leading to unacceptable arbitrariness - Court direct that State of Maharashtra shall henceforth not make assessments of said tax until it frames rules that set out formulae for such assessment which take account of fact that residential hotels may provide lodging and full or part-board as set out above - Appeals dismissed.
