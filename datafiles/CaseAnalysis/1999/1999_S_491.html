Rajesh Govind Jagesha and Others v State of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

02 November 1999

Case Analysis

Bench

R.P. Sethi, G.B. Pattanaik

Where Reported

1999 Indlaw SC 492; (1999) 8 SCC 428; (1999) SCC (Cr) 1452; AIR 2000 SC 160; 2000 (5) Bom.C.R. 549; 2000 CRLJ 380; JT 1999 (9) SC 1; [1999] Supp4 S.C.R. 277

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34, 149 and 302 - Murder - Test identification parade - Appellants came on spot with deadly weapons and inflicted injuries to deceased intention of appellants was causing death of deceased - Whether conviction is justified? - Where direct evidence regarding commission of offence is worthy of credence and can be believed, question of motive becomes, more or less academic - 'Motive' may be relevant in a case based upon circumstantial evidence only, being one of circumstances - Ss. 34 and 149 are distinct and distinguishable - No premeditation or previous meeting of mind is necessary for applicability of s. 34 IPC, 1860 - Existence of common intention can be inferred from attending circumstances of case and conduct of parties - No direct evidence of common intention is necessary - For purposes of common intention even participation in commission of offence need not be proved in all cases - Common intention can develop even during course of an occurrence - Held, circumstances of present case clearly show that accused persons had come on spot with deadly weapons and inflicted injuries on deceased, obviously with intention of causing his death - No error in appreciation of evidence and upholding conviction - Order accordingly.
