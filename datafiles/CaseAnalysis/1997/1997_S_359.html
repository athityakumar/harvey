Kalpnath Rai and Others v State Through C.B.I

Supreme Court of India

06 November 1997

Case Analysis

Bench

K.T. Thomas, M.K. Mukherjee

Where Reported

1997 Indlaw SC 3281; (1997) 8 SCC 732; AIR 1998 SC 201; AIR 1997 SCW 4166; 1998 (5) Bom.C.R. 450; 1998 CRLJ 369; JT 1997 (9) SC 18; 1997(2) SCALE 50; 1997 (2) Supreme 149; 1997 (1) UJ 483

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Socio-Economic

Summary: Criminal - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 20 - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987 (TADA), ss. 2, 3, 5, 12 and 20 - Criminal Procedure Code, 1973, ss. 100, 313 and 465 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 52-A, 136, 157 and 312 - Arms Act, 1959, ss. 5 and 25 - Appellants convicted by designated court - Some of the appellants were found to be members of a terrorists gang called 'Dawood Ibrahim Group' - (A) Requirement in s. 20A(1) of TADA, 1987 - Whether prior approval envisaged in s. 20-A(1) of TADA, 1987 should necessarily be in writing? - Held, nothing in sub-section to indicate that prior approval of District Superintendent of Police should be in writing - What is necessary is fact of approval which is sine qua non for recording information about commission of offence under TADA, 1987 - Provision is intended to operate as a check against police officials of lower ranks commencing investigation into offences under TADA, 1987 because of serious consequences which such action befalls accused - Approval can be obtained even orally if such an exigency arises in a particular situation - So oral approval by itself is not illegal and would not vitiate further proceedings - One of offences included is s. 5 of Explosive Substances Act, 1908 - No legal requirement to obtain prior permission from District Superintendent of Police to register a case for that offence - So FIR as such was not vitiated even otherwise - (B) Mens rea u/s. 3(4) of TADA, 1987 - Unless statute clearly excludes mens rea in commission of an offence same must be treated as essential ingredient of criminal act to become punishable - If s. 3(4) is understood as imposing harsh punishment on a person who gives shelter to a terrorist without knowing that he was a terrorist such an understanding would lead to calamitous consequences - Many an innocent person, habituated to offer hospitality to friends and relatives or disposed to zeal of charity, giving accommodation and shelter to others without knowing that their guests were involved in terrorist acts, would then be exposed to incarceration for a long period - Mens rea is an essential ingredient for offence envisaged in s. 3(4) of TADA, 1987 - (C) Designated Court relied on two letters which he had received presumably from A-7 while accused was in jail during pre-trial period - Whether justified? - It was illegal on part Designated Court to have used any part of said letters, especially when those letters were not adduced as evidence in case through any procedure known to law - S. 313 of CrPC, 1973 is intended to afford opportunity to an accused 'to explain any circumstance appearing in evidence against him' - S. 313 not intended to be used as an interrogation - (D) S. 15 of TADA, 1987 - Held, confession made admissible u/s. 15 of TADA, 1987 can be used as against a co-accused only in same manner and subject to same conditions as stipulated in s. 30 of Evidence Act - (E) Evidence of police officers - There can be no legal proposition that evidence of police officers, unless supported by independent witnesses, is unworthy of acceptance - Non-examination of independent witness or even presence of such witness during police raid would cast an added duty on court to adopt greater care while scrutinising evidence of police officers - If evidence of police officer is found acceptable it would be an erroneous proposition that court must reject prosecution version solely on ground that no independent witness was examined - Order accordingly.
