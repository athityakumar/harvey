 L. Chandra Kumar v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

18 March 1997

Case Analysis

Bench

A.M. Ahmadi, K. Ramaswamy, K.T. Thomas, K. Venkataswami, S.P. Bharucha, S. Saghir Ahmad, M.M. Punchhi

Where Reported

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution; Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Constitution - Administrative Tribunals Act, 1985, ss. 5 and 28 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 16(1), 32, 136, 226, 227, 309, 323A and 323B - Provisions u/arts. 323A and 323B excluding jurisdiction of "all courts" except that of Supreme Court were challenged - Issues raised are - (A) Whether Central Administrative Tribunal constituted under Act has authority and jurisdiction to strike down a rule framed by President of India under provisos to art. 309 of Constitution as being violative of arts. 14 and 16(1) of Constitution - Held, Administrative Tribunal being a substitute of High Court had necessary jurisdiction, power and authority to adjudicate upon all disputes relating to service matters including power to deal with all questions pertaining to constitutional validity or otherwise of such laws as offending arts. 14 and 16(1) of Constitution - (B) Whether a single bench can decide a constitutional question in CAT? - Held, that Chairman should keep in view nature of litigation and where questions of law and interpretation of constitutional provisions are involved they should not be assigned to a single member - It would be open to either party appearing before a single member to suggest to member hearing matter that it should go to a bench of two members - S. 5(6) of Act will have to be struck down as a single member bench of a tribunal cannot be considered to be a substitute for exercise of power of a HC u/art. 226 of Constitution - (C) Whether CAT has power of Judicial review? - Held, that constitutional courts alone are competent to exercise power of judicial review to pronounce upon constitutional validity of statutory provisions and rules - (D) Whether Parliament can exclude Jurisdiction of HC u/art. 226? - Held, that Parliament cannot, in exercise of its constituent power confer power on Parliament and State Legislatures to exclude constitutional jurisdiction conferred on HC - These provisions violate basic structure of Constitution in so far as they take away power of judicial review vested in Supreme Court u/art. 32 of Constitution and HC u/arts. 226 and 227 of Constitution - While tribunals constituted u/arts. 323A and 323B can be vested with power of judicial review over administrative action, power of judicial review of legislative action cannot be conferred upon them - (E) Difference b/w HC and tribunal - Held, (1) HC enjoy vast powers as a consequence of their being courts of record u/art. 215 of Constitution and also possess power to issue certificates of appeal u/arts. 132 and 133 of Constitution in cases where they feel that a decision of this court is required - This is not so for tribunals (2) qualifications for appointment of a HC judge and constitutional safeguards provided ensure independence of and efficiency of judges who man HCs conditions prescribed for members of tribunals are not comparable (3) while jurisdiction of HCs is constitutionally protected, a tribunal can be abolished by simply repealing its parent statute;(4) while expenditure of HCs is charged to consolidated fund of states, Tribunals are dependent upon appropriate Govt. for grant of funds for meeting their expenses - Tribunals, being deprived of constitutional safeguards for ensuring their independence, are incapable of being effective substitutes for HCs - (F) Power of judicial review - Held, power of judicial review vested in Supreme Court u/art. 32 and HC u/art. 226 is part of basic structure of Constitution - Power of judicial review of legislative enactments cannot in any event be conferred in any other court or Tribunal - HC would continue to have jurisdiction to decide vires of an Act even in area of service disputes and would, therefore, perform a supervisory role over tribunals in respect of matters involving constitutional questions - Further held, arts. 323A and 323B do not seek to exclude supervisory jurisdiction of HCs over all Tribunals situated within their territorial jurisdiction - Viewed from this perspective, HCs would still be vested with constitutional powers to exercise corrective or supervisory jurisdiction - (G) Whether litigant can directly approach Supreme Court in cases where question of ultra vires of statute is involved? - Held, tribunals will act like courts of first instance in respect of areas of law for which they have been constituted - It will not be open for litigants to directly approach HCs even in cases where they question vires of statutory legislations (except where legislation which creates particular Tribunal is challenged) by overlooking jurisdiction of concerned tribunal - S. 5(6) of Act is valid and constitutional and is to be interpreted in manner Supreme Court have indicated - All decision of tribunal will be subject to Division Bench of HC - Appeal allowed.
