Sri Adi Visheshwara Of Kashi Vishwanath Temple, Varanasi And Ors. Etc. v State Of U.P. And Ors

Supreme Court of India

14 March 1997

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Ramaswamy, K. Venkataswami, G.B. Pattanaik

Where Reported

1997 Indlaw SC 3333; (1997) 4 SCC 606; JT 1997 (4) SC 124; 1997(3) SCALE 1; [1997] 2 S.C.R. 1086

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Trusts & Associations

Keywords: Operation, Religious Denomination, A.P. Charitable and Hindu Religious Institutions and Endowments Act, 1987, Uttar Pradesh Sri Kashi Vishwanath Temple Act, 1983, Civil Rights Protection Act, Auroville (Emergency Provisions) Act, 1980, Dargah Khwaja Saheb Act, 1955, Sri Jagannath Temple Act, 1954, Rajasthan Public Trust Act, U.P. Sri Kashi Vishwanath Temple (Second) Ordinance, 1983

Summary: Constitution - Trusts & Associations - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 24, 25 and 26 - Uttar Pradesh Sri Kashi Vishwanath Temple Act, 1983 - Constitutionality of Uttar Pradesh Sri Kashi Vishwanath Temple Act, 1983 made for management of Temple of renowned Lord Vishwanath, otherwise known as Sri Adi Visheshwara of Kashi - Writ petitions were filed challenging the Act on ground that it infringes appellants' fundamental rights enshrined in arts. 25(1) and 26(b) and (d) of Constitution - One of Judges of Bench held that though Temple of Sri Kashi Vishwanath was a common man's Temple but it was a denominational Temple of Shivaites of Hindu community - Another Judge held that, it was not a denominational one - However, both judges held that Legislature was competent to enact law for management of Temple along with its properties - Present appeals - (A) Whether Sri Kashi Vishwanath temple was a denominational Temple? - Held, right to establish and maintain institutions for religious and charitable purposes or to administer property of such institutions in accordance with law was protected only in respect of such religious denomination or any section thereof which appears to extend help equally to all arid religious practice peculiar to such small or specified group or section thereof as part of main religion from which they got separated - Further, right to observe and practice rituals and right to manage in matters of religion are protected u/arts. 25 and 26 of Constitution; but right to manage Temple or endowment is not integral to religion or religious practice or religion as such which is amenable to statutory control - Every Hindu whether a believer of Shaiva form of worship or of Pancharatna form of worship, has a right of entry into Hindu Temple and worship Deity - Therefore, Hindu believers of Shaiva form of worship were not denominational worshippers - They were part of Hindu religious form of worship - Furthermore, State is required under Act to protect religious practices of Hindu form of worship of Lord Vishwanath, be it in any form, in accordance with Hindu Sastras, customs or usage obtained in temple; It is not restricted to any particular denomination or sect - Believers of Shaiva form of worship are not a denominational sect or section of Hindus but they are Hindus as such and entitled to protection u/arts. 25 and 26 of Constitution - However, they are not entitled to protection, in particular, of clauses (b) and (d) of Art. 26 as a religious denomination in matter of management, administration and governance of temples under Act - Act, therefore, not ultra vires arts. 25 and 26 of Constitution - (B) Whether regulation of rendering service and prohibition to receive offerings, though may affect livelihood of a pre-existing archaka? - Held, s. 22 takes care of any service being rendered as archaka (priest) - "Archaka" was defined in s. 4(2) to mean any person who performs or conducts any worship, service, rituals in Temple and includes a Pujari, if he was doing same on appointed date - Further, right to receive offerings from pilgrims in incidental to service rendered by Archaka (priest) - When service on customary basis is abolished, concomitantly right to receive offerings given by pilgrims stands extinguished and prohibited and is vested in deity, Lord Shiva - Therefore, regulation of rendering service and prohibition to receive offerings, though may affect livelihood of a pre-existing archaka, it being a regulatory measure, it is sequel or consequential to abolition - Hence, it is not an acquisition of their right but it has only incidental and consequential effect - Rights of persons in service as archakas is not affected; on other hand, s. 22 is subject to regulation and extends right to earn livelihood guaranteed by art. 21 - Contention that some of persons have customary and hereditary rights as archakas and that Act extinguishes their rights and so violative of arts. 25 and 26 (b) and (d) of Constitution, untenable and devoid of substance - (C) Whether in constitution of committees or Board of Trustees appellants entitled to be nominated as members of Board? - It was contended that absence of any provision in Act in that behalf is violative of their right to be members of Board - Held, it is for appropriate Government to consider whether or not any of them would be eligible to be considered for nomination as one of eight non-official members of Board at relevant time - (D) Whether Act overboard and vitiated with vice of discretionary power without any supervision or guidelines and ultra vires? - Held, Act ensures and enjoins Board, Executive Committee and Chief Executive Officer assisted by all staff, to ensure due and proper performance of worship, services, rituals and ceremonies, daily or periodical, general of special, of Sri Kashi Vishwanath and other deities in Temple in accordance with Hindu Shastra, scriptures and usage - Act does not invest Government with any power to interfere with religious part of management or day-to-day administration of Temple or its endowments - Act carefully formulated different principles, applied same in matter of nomination of members of Board, appointment of Executive Committee, staff and proper and efficient management of Temple - Even discretionary powers are well within parameters laid under Act - Act cannot be declared as ultra virus - (E) Whether Act infringe rights conferred u/art. 26(b) and (d)? - Held, various regulatory measures devised under Act aim only at proper and better management and administration of Temple, endowments and all matters incidental to or connected with management thereof - Act itself has drawn a distinction between religious affairs and secular control - Act itself has demarcated and drawn distinction between secular part and religious parts of activities in Temple; former have been entrusted to competent priests well-versed in performance of rituals and ceremonies and services according to Hindu Sastras, customs, usages and practices as applicable and prevailing in Temple - Secular part has been entrusted to Board, Executive Committee and chief Executive Officer etc. appointed as per Act - Management of Temple by Mahant/Panda/Archaka is not their property - Act merely changed management from Pandas to Board - Only right of management in Pandas has been extinguished from appointed day and placed in Board for better and proper management - It is not vested in State nor State acquired it for itself - This entrustment of management cannot be said to constitute acquisition of property or extinguishment of right to property - Hence, as clear demarcation came to be made between temporal and secular aspects of management of Temple - Religious management is entrusted to eminent personalities professing Hindu religion, well-versed in religious and administrative facets of management and, therefore, Act does not infringe rights conferred u/arts. 26(b) and (d) - Appeals dismissed.
