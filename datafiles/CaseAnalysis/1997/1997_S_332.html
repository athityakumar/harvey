Vishaka And Others. v State Of Rajasthan And Others

Supreme Court of India

13 August 1997

Case Analysis

Bench

J.S. Verma, Sujata V. Manohar, B.N. Kirpal

Where Reported

1997 Indlaw SC 2304; (1997) 6 SCC 241; (1997) SCC (Cr) 932; AIR 1997 SC 3011; 1997 (3) AWC 2316; 1997 (3) CCR 126; 1997 (3) Crimes 188; 1997 (77) FLR 297; JT 1997 (7) SC 384; 2013 (3) KarLJ 546; 1997 LabIC 2890; 1998 (2) LLN 965; 1997(5) SCALE 453; [1997] Supp3 S.C.R. 404; 1997 (7) Supreme 323

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law; Women & Children

Keywords: Safety, Woman, Sexual Harassment, Industrial Employment (Standing Order ) Act, 1946

Summary: (A) Women & Children – Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 19, 21 - Sexual harassment in work place – Absence of domestic law – Effect - Petitioners filed instant writ petitions seeking to find suitable methods for realization of true concept of gender equality and to prevent sexual harassment of working women in all work places through judicial process, to fill the vacuum in existing legislation – Whether international conventions can be considered while construing domestic law.

Held, the meaning and content of the fundamental rights guaranteed in the Constitution are of sufficient amplitude to compass all the facets of gender equality including prevention of sexual harassment or abuse. Independence of Judiciary forms a part of constitutional scheme. The international conventions and norms are to be read into them in the absence of enacted domestic law occupying the fields when there is no inconsistency between them. It is now an accepted rule of judicial construction that regard must be had to international conventions and norms for construing domestic law when there is no inconsistency between them and there is a void in the domestic law. Petitions disposed of.

(B) Women & Children – Protection of Human Rights Act, 1993 - Sexual harassment in work place – Guidelines laid down by SC.

Held, since present civil and penal laws in India do not adequately provide for specific protection of women from sexual harassment in work places and that enactment of such legislation will take considerable time, SC has laid down instant guidelines: (i) It shall be duty of the employer or other responsible persons in work places or other institutions to prevent or deter the commission of acts of sexual harassment and to provide the procedures for the resolution, settlement or prosecution of acts of sexual harassment by taking all steps required; (ii) All employers or persons in charge of work place whether in the public or private sector should take appropriate steps to prevent sexual harassment; (iii) Where such conduct amounts to a specific offence under the IPC or under any other law the employer shall initiate appropriate action in accordance with law by making a complaint with the appropriate authority; (iv) Where such conduct amounts to mis-conduct in employment as defined by the relevant service rules, appropriate disciplinary action should be initiated by the employer in accordance with those rules; (v) Whether or not such conduct constitutes an offence under law or a breach of the service rules, an appropriate complaint mechanism should be created in the employers organization for redress of the complaint made by the victim. Such complaint mechanism should ensure time bound treatment of complaints; (vi) Complaints Committee should be adequate to provide, where necessary, a Complaints Committee, a special counsellor or other support service, including the maintenance of confidentiality. The Complaints Committee should be headed by a woman and not less than half of its member should be women; (vii) Employees should be allowed to raise issues of sexual harassment at workers meeting and in other appropriate forum and it should be affirmatively discussed in Employer-Employee Meetings; (viii) Awareness of the rights of female employees in this regard should be created in particular by prominently notifying the guidelines (and appropriate legislation when enacted on the subject) in suitable manner; (ix) Where sexual harassment occurs as a result of an act or omission by any third party or outsider, the employer and person in charge will take all steps necessary and reasonable to assist the affected person in terms of support and preventive action; (x) The Central/State Governments are requested to consider adopting suitable measures including legislation to ensure that the guidelines laid down by this order are also observed by the employers in Private Sector; (xi) These guidelines will not prejudice any rights available under the Act. Further, the SC directs that the said guidelines and norms would be strictly observed in all work places for the preservation and enforcement of the right to gender equality of the working women. Those directions would be binding and enforceable in law until suitable legislation was enacted to occupy the field. Petitions disposed of.
