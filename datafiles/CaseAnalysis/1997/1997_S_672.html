Tvl K.A.K. Anwar and Company v State of Tamil Nadu

Supreme Court of India

27 November 1997

Case Analysis

Bench

B.N. Kirpal, K.T. Thomas, S.C. Sen

Where Reported

1997 Indlaw SC 1721; (1998) 1 SCC 437; AIR 1998 SC 518; JT 1997 (9) SC 384; 1997(7) SCALE 261; 1998 (1) SLT 190; 1998 (108) STC 258; [1997] Supp5 S.C.R. 551; 1997 (10) Supreme 458

Case Digest

Subject: Sales Tax

Keywords: Declared Goods, Inter-State Sale, Last Purchase, Levy Of Tax

Summary: Sales Tax - Central Sales Tax Act, 1956, ss. 14, 14(iii), 15, 15(d) - Tamil Nadu General Sales Tax Act, 1959 - Tamil Nadu General Sales Tax (3rd Amendment) Act, 1987 s. 3 - Inter-State sale - Levy of tax - Legality - Appellants (delars/traders) purchased raw hides and skins and after dressing they sold in course of inter-State trade - Appellants before Assessing Authority contended that at the time of purchase of raw hides and skins, goods suffered tax and could not be taxed against at the time of inter-State sale - But Assessing Authority held that restrictions contemplated u/s. 15 of 1956 Act were not applicable - Aggrieved appellants appeals before Tribunal, which were dismissed - Aggrieved appellants filed appeals before HC - HC by impugned judgment held that State had legislative competence to tax inter-State sale of dressed hides and skins even though tax was paid on purchase of raw hides and skins - Hence, instant appeals - Appellants contended that raw hides and skins and dressed hides and skins could not be considered as commercially separate commodities, difference being only in form and that hides and skins could be taxed at only one stage in State with the result that if they were subjected to tax at raw state under local Act, then s. 15 of 1956 Act would have the effect of preventing tax being levied on dressed hides and skins - Whether dressed hides and skins and raw hides and tanned skins were different commodities and whether s. 14(iii) of 1956 Act regards them as a single commodity - Held, raw hides and skins and dressed hides and skins were not one and the same commodity - Thus, contention raised by appellants in instant case could not be accepted - There was nothing in language of s. 14 of 1956 Act which could lead Court to conclusion that dressed hides and skins commodities were to be regarded as constituting a single commodity for purpose of taxation - Ss. 14 and 15 of 1956 Act have to be read together as they constitute a scheme relating to taxation of goods of special importance in inter-State trade or commerce - S. 14 of 1956 Act enumerated items which were regarded as goods of special importance in inter-State trade or commerce, it was s. 15 which imposed restriction and conditions in regard to tax on sale or purchase of declared goods within a State - S. 14 of 1956 Act was not a taxing provision but it merely classified different commodities under the same species under one entry - Merely because different goods or commodities were listed together in same sub-heading or sub-item in s. 14 of 1956 Act could not mean that they were regarded as one and same item - Whenever legislature wanted different goods placed in same entry to be regarded as a single commodity it expressly provided for same - S. 14(iii) of 1956 Act clearly indicated that legislature recognised that raw hides and skins was an item different from dressed hides and skins - In instant case, dressed hides and skins was a separate commercial commodity which emerges after raw hides and skins has been subjected to manufacturing process and, thus, s. 14(iii) of 1956 Act deals with 2 different types of goods which unlike the case of pulses referred to in s. 15(d) of 1956 At, was not regarded by 1956 Act as one and same commodity - when appellants purchased raw hides and skins on payment of tax they would be liable to pay sales tax in respect of dressed hides and skins and such levy would not fall foul of s. 15 of 1956 Act as 2 goods were different taxable commodities - HC rightly held that sales tax authorities could levy sales tax on sale of dressed hides and skins and that provisions of s. 3 1987 Act were not ultra vires - Appeals dismissed.
