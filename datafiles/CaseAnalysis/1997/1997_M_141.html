Rajkot Municipal Corporation v Manjulben Jayantilal Nakum and Ors.

Supreme Court of India

17 January 1997

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Ramaswamy, G.B. Pattanaik

Where Reported

1997 Indlaw SC 3330; (1997) 9 SCC 552; 1997 (2) CLT 396; 1997 (67) DLT 413; JT 1997 (1) SC 580; 1997(1) SCALE 370; [1997] 1 S.C.R. 304; 1997 (2) Supreme 294

Case Digest

Subject: Tort

Keywords: Attorney-General, Decreed, Suit for damages

Summary: Tort - Bombay Provincial Municipal Corporations Act, 1949, ss. 202, 66 - Suit for damages - Decreed - Legality - One deceased was walking on footpath on way to his office, a road-side tree suddenly fell on him as a result of which he sustained injuries on his head and other parts of body and died - Respondents were wife and children of deceased - Respondents filed suit for damages before Trial Court from appellant Corporation - Trial Court decreed the suit for sum of Rs. 45,000/- - On appeal before DB of HC - DB confirmed decree of Trial Court - Hence instant appeal.

Held, plaintiff is entitled to sue for tort of negligence. Authority will be liable to pay damages for omission or negligence in performance of duty. The plaintiff is entitled to lay suit for tort of negligence. But in a situation like present one where victim being not aware of decease/decay, tree suddenly falls in a still weather condition, no one can anticipate and its is difficult to foresee that a tree would fall suddenly and thereby a person who would be passing by on road-side, would suffer injury or would die in consequence. Corporation or authority is not liable to be sued for tort of negligence since causation is too remote. Novus act us inconveniences snaps the link and, therefore, it is difficult to establish lack of care resulting in damage and foresee ability of damage. Case in hand falls in this category. Jayantilal was admittedly passing on roadside to attend to his office duty. The tree suddenly fell and he sustained injury and consequently died. It was difficult to foresee that a tree would fall on him. Conditions in India have not developed to such an extent that a Corporation can keep constant vigil by testing healthy condition of trees in public places, road-side, highway frequented by passers-by. There is no duty to maintain regular supervision thereof, though local authority/other authority/owner of a property is under a duty to plant and maintain the tree. The causation for accident is too remote. Consequently, there would be no Common Law right to file suit for tort of negligence. It would not be just and proper to fasten duty of care and liability for omission thereof. It would be difficult to conclude that appellant has been negligent in maintenance of tees planted by it on road-sides. Judgment and decree of Trial Court, as affirmed by HC, stands set aside. SC direct that amount of Rs. 45,000/- may not be recovered from respondents though they are not entitled in law to same, since they are to poor and amount must have already been spent out. Corporation is directed to pay him a further sum of Rs. 5,000/-. Appeal allowed.
