Sajjan Singh v The State Of Punjab

Supreme Court of India

28 August 1963

Case Analysis

Bench

K.C. Das Gupta, Mohammad Hidayatullah, S.K. Das

Where Reported

1963 Indlaw SC 318; AIR 1964 SC 464; 1964 CRLJ 310; [1964] 4 S.C.R. 630

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Constitution of India, 1950 - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1947 - Presumption - Fundamental Right - Accused joined the service of the Punjab Govt. as an Overseer in the Irrigation Department - Later he became a Sub-divisional Officer in the Department - Accused by illegal and corrupt means and by abusing their position as public servants, dishonestly and fraudulently, obtained illegal gratification from the contractors - Case u/s. 45(2) was registered on the basis of this complaint, which was treated as a first information report and after sanction of the Govt. of Punjab had been obtained for the prosecution of the accused u/s. 5(2) of the 1947 Act and u/ss. 161/165 of the IPC, he was tried by Special Judge - Learned Special judge convicted him u/s. 5(2) of the 1947 Act and sentenced him to rigorous imprisonment for one year and a fine of Rs. 5000/ in default of payment of fine, he was directed to undergo rigorous imprisonment for six months - Present appeal was by the accused against his conviction and sentence u/s. 5(2) of the 1947 Act by special leave of this Court - Whether on the evidence on the record a presumption under s. 5(3) of the Prevention of Corruption Act arose - Held, the Court had set aside the conviction of the appellant on the basis of the presumption under s. 5(3) - Though the accused had been charged with having committed the offence of criminal misconduct in the discharge of his duty by doing the acts mentioned in cl. (c) of sub-s. 1 of s. 5, the Special Judge and the HC convicted him by invoking the rule of presumption laid down in sub-s. 3 of s. 5, of an offence u/cl. (d) of s. 5(l)- Court held that it was not open to the Courts to do so - This case was however no authority for the proposition that the courts couldn't have convicted the accused for an offence u/s. 5 (1) (c) for which he had been charged - After pointing out that the charge against the appellant was that he has dishonestly and fraudulently misappropriated or otherwise converted for his own use property entrusted to him - It was not open to the learned Special Judge to have convicted the appellant of that offence by invoking the rule of presumption laid down in sub-s. (3)- He acquitted the appellant on that charge - Learned Counsel had submitted that by calling in aid the rule of presumption in sub-s. 3 the appellant could not be found guilty of any other type of criminal misconduct referred to in cls. (a), (b) or (d) of sub-s. (1) in respect of which there was no charge against the appellant - Therefore, the facts proved in this case raise a presumption under s. 5(3) of the Prevention of Corruption Act and the appellant's conviction of the offence with which he was charged must be maintained on the basis of that presumption - In this view of the matter we do not propose to consider whether the HC was right in basing its conclusion also on the other evidence adduced in the case to prove the actual payment of illegal gratification by the partners of the firm - Appeal dismissed.
