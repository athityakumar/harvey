Mirza Raja Shri Pushavathi Viziaram Gajapathi Raj v Shri Pushavathi Visweswar Gajapathi Raj and Others

Supreme Court of India

19 March 1963

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Mohammad Hidayatullah, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1963 Indlaw SC 384; AIR 1964 SC 118; [1964] 2 S.C.R. 403

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Apportionment Of Compensation, Survivorship, Remainder, Impartible Property, Zamindar

Summary: Family & Personal - Custom - Land & Property - Practice and Procedure - Evidence Act, 1872, s. 13 - Tamil Nadu Estates (Abolition and Conversion into Ryotwari) Act, 1948, s. 18(4) - Madras Impartible Estates Acts,s. 2 of 1902,s. 2 of 1903 and s. 2 of 1904 - (A) Whether an estate which is impartible by custom can be said to be exclusive property of holder?- Held, impartible estate is not an exclusive property of holder - If holder has got estate as an ancestral estate and he has succeeded to it by primogeniture, it will be a part of joint estate of undivided Hindu family - (B) Whether right to partition exist in impartible estate ?- Further held, only right of survivorship exist in such case - In case of an ordinary joint family property, members of family can claim four rights : (1) right of partition; (2) right to restrain alienations by head of family except for necessity; (3) right of maintenance; and (4) right of survivorship - Right of survivorship, remains and it is by reference to this right that property, though impartible, has, in eyes of law, to be regarded as joint family property - (C) Whether concept of spes successionis and survivorship are same? - Further held, right of survivorship which can be claimed by members of undivided family which owns impartible estate should not be confused with a mere spes successionis- Unlike spes successionis, right of survivorship can be renounced or surrendered - (D) What is difference between blending of property and incorporation of property to impartible estate? - Further held, incorporation is a matter of intention - Effect of incorporation in such cases is reverse of effect of blending self-acquired property with joint family property where property loses its character as separate acquisition and merges in joint family property, with result that devolution in respect of that property is then governed by survivorship and not by succession - If holder of an impartible estate acquires property and incorporates it with impartible estate, he makes it a part of impartible estate with result that acquisition ceases to be partible and becomes impartible - (E) Whether incorporation theory applies to movable property?- Further held, none of considerations which are relevant in respect of immovable property would apply to movable property, and so, theory of incorporation cannot apply to such movable property - It is not to say that movable property cannot be treated as impartible - (F) In what way can an impartiable estate be alienated ?- Further held, a holder of an impartible estate can alienate estate by gift inter vivos, or even by a will, though family is undivided; only limitation on this power would flow from a family custom to contrary or from condition of tenure which has same effect - (G) Whether a share in building which is impartiable can be asked u/s. 18(4) of Madras Estates (Abolition and Conversion into Ryotwari) Act, 1948 by members of a family?- Further held, they cannot - Junior members of joint Hindu family are not entitled to claim a share in buildings which have been incorporated with impartible estate and which fall u/s. 18 (4) of that Act - Appeal dismissed.
