Badat and Company v East India Trading Company

Supreme Court of India

10 May 1963

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Subba Rao, J.R. Madholkar, Raghubar Dayal

Where Reported

1963 Indlaw SC 275; AIR 1964 SC 538; [1964] 4 S.C.R. 19

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR; Civil Procedure

Keywords: Foreign Award

Summary: (A) Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration (Protocol and Convention) Act, 1937 - Arbitration Act, 1940, ss. 26, 30, 36 - Arbitration Protocol and Convention Act, 1937 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 - Breach of contract - Merger - Counter-Claim - Foreign - Appellants a firm were formerly carrying on business - Respondents were a private limited company incorporated under the laws of the State of New York in the United States of America and having their registered office in the State of New York - Respondents instituted a suit against the appellants in the HC for the recovery of a certain amount with interest thereon - It was alleged in the plaint that by correspondence, the details whereof were given in the plaint, the appellants agreed to do business with the respondents on the terms of the American Spice Trade Association contract - By subsequent correspondence the parties entered into two different contracts under which the appellants agreed to sell to the respondents different quantities of Allepey Turmeric Fingers on agreed terms - Dispute was referred to arbitration and two awards were made in due course - Respondents initiated proceedings in the SC of the State of New York to have the said awards confirmed and a judgment entered thereon in the said Court - On those allegations a suit was filed in the HC of Bombay for recovery of amounts payable under the said two awards by the appellants to the respondents - Whether the awards merged in the judgment of the SC of the State of New York for all purposes - Held, since the foreign judgment constitutes a simple contract debt only, there is no merger of the original cause of action, and it is therefore open to the plaintiff to sue either on the foreign judgment or on the original cause of action on which it is based, unless the foreign judgment has been satisfied - In order to prevent misconception, it appeared desirable to add that it was not pleaded or contended at any stage of the proceedings that the award had merged in the English judgment, and accordingly their Lordships do not deal with that point - Whatever may be the origin, the doctrine of non-merger of the original cause of action with the foreign judgment has now been well established in spite of the fact that some text-book writers were not able to discover a logical basis for the doctrine - In 'Smith's Leading Cases', the learned author says: 'Foreign judgments certainly do not occasion a merger of the original ground of action' - Appeal allowed.

(B) Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration (Protocol and Convention) Act, 1937 - Arbitration Act, 1940, ss. 26, 30, 36 - Arbitration Protocol and Convention Act, 1937 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 - Breach of contract - Merger - Counter-Claim - Foreign - Whether the award could furnish a valid cause of action in a suit - Held, the award had no finality till the entire procedure was gone through and that the award was such that it could never be enforced - An award can furnish a fresh cause of action - But the award must be final - If the law of the country in which it was made gives finality to judgment based upon an award and not to the award itself, the award can furnish no cause of action for a suit in India - In these circumstances, the conclusion was that though the HC of Bombay had jurisdiction to enforce a final award made in a foreign country in pursuance of a submission made within the limits of its original jurisdiction, the awards in question being not final, could not furnish a valid cause of action for the suit - Appeal allowed.
