Buckingham and Carnatic Company Limited v Venkatiah and Another

Supreme Court of India

02 August 1963

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, K.C. Das Gupta, Kailas Nath Wanchoo

Where Reported

1963 Indlaw SC 105; AIR 1964 SC 1272; 1963 (7) FLR 343; 1963 (2) LLJ 638; [1964] 4 S.C.R. 265

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Keywords: Sickness Benefit

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 - Employees State Insurance Act, 1948, s. 73(1) - Workman - Unauthorised absence - Dismissal - Legality - Respondent/ workman went on leave for six days but remained absent even after expiry of leave period - Appellant/firm refused to take back respondent in its employment for remaining absent without leave and without sending any communication to appellant for extending his leave - Union raised industrial dispute and matter was referred to Labour Court which held that decision of appellant not to take back respondent was inconsistent with s. 73 of the 1948 Act - Labour Court directed management of appellant to reinstate respondent within two weeks after its award came into force - Appellant filed petition in HC which allowed the same on ground that s. 73 of the 1948 Act was inapplicable to instant case - on appeal, DB restored Labour Court's award on ground that action of appellant amounted to contravention of s. 73 of the 1948 Act - Hence instant appeal - Whether order of DB could be justified - Held, s. 73(1) of the 1948 Act prohibited employer from dismissing, discharging or otherwise punishing employee - This seemed to suggest that what was prohibited was some positive act on part of employer, such as order passed by him either dismissing, discharging employee - Where termination of employee's services followed automatically either from contract or from Standing Order by virtue of employee's absence without leave for specified period, such termination was not result of any positive act or order on part of employer, and so to such termination prohibition contained in s. 73(1) of the 1948 Act would be inapplicable - Word 'discharge' in s. 73(1) of the 1948 Act should, therefore, in the context, be taken to be discharge which was result of decision of employer embodied in order passed by him - It might also include case of discharge where discharge was provided for by Standing Order - In such case, it might be said that discharge flowing from Standing Order was, in substance, discharge brought about by employer with assistance of Standing Order - Even so, it should not cover case of abandonment of service by employee which was inferred under Standing Order 8(ii) - Therefore, HC was not justified in taking the view that termination of respondent under Standing Order 8(ii) to which appellant gave effect by refusing to take him back, contravened provisions of s. 73(1) of the 1948 Act - Appeal allowed.
