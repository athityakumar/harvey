Crystal Developers and another v Asha Lata Ghosh (Dead) and another

Supreme Court of India

05 October 2004

Case Analysis

Bench

S.H. Kapadia, Ashok Bhan

Where Reported

2004 Indlaw SC 1686; (2005) 9 SCC 375; AIR 2004 SC 4980; 2005 (1) ALD(SC) 1; 2005 (1) CalLT(SC) 45; JT 2004 (9) SC 6; 2004 (4) MLJ(SC) 172; 2004 (4) RCR(Civil) 453; 2004(8) SCALE 390; 2005 (Supp) RD 734; [2004] Supp5 S.C.R. 31

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - Civil Procedure - Indian Succession Act, 1925, ss. 211, 212, 213, 227, 263 and 273 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Deceased had three wives - On 21.9.1981, partition suit was filed - Third wife of Deceased was defendant no.1 and her son 'A' was defendant no.2 in the said suit - 'N' the second wife of Deceased was the defendant no. 3 - 'P', the son from the first wife, was defendant no.4 - 'S', son of Deceased from the second wife, was the defendant no. 5 - Parties to the suit claimed 1/11th undivided share each in the suit premises - In the written statement, defendants no.1 and 2, relied on registered will made by Deceased, probate; consent decree dated 3.8.1981 in suit as also the conveyance in favour of defendant no.14 (Developers) - Also relied on the order passed by the District Judge to show that defendant no. 3 had knowledge of the registered will of Deceased and of the appointment of defendant no.2 as the executor under the said will - In 1993, the plaint was amended and defendant no.14 was brought on record - It was alleged that on inspection of assessment record of the municipality on 22.6.1993 and 22.8.1993, the plaintiffs came to know of the impugned transfer - Trial Court held that defendant no.14 was not a bona fide purchaser - Trial Court concluded that defendant no.2 had practised fraud upon the Probate Court in collusion with defendant no.14 and in the circumstances, same were not binding on the other heirs of Deceased and decreed the partition suit - On appeal, HC concluded that defendant no.14 was a privy to the fraudulent acts of defendant no.2 and was, therefore, not a bona fide purchaser - Hence, present appeals - (A) Effect of revocation of the probate on the disposition(s) during the pendency of the probate - Held, u/s. 263, grant of probate or letters of administration is liable to be revoked on any of five grounds mentioned therein - One of the grounds is failure on the part of the grantee to exhibit/file an inventory or statement of account - Similarly, the probate or letter or administration is liable to be revoked if the grant is obtained fraudulently - Further, if the intermediate act of the executor is not for the purpose of administration of the estate or if the act is performed in breach of trust then such act(s) is not protected - However, acts which are in consonance with the testator's intention and which are compatible with the administration of the estate are protected - Therefore, on reading ss. 211, 227 along with s. 263, it is clear that revocation of the grant shall operate prospectively and such revocation shall not invalidate the bona fide intermediate acts performed by the grantee during the pendency of the probate - Further, s. 273 refers to conclusiveness of the probate as to the representative title - It establishes the factum of the will and the legal character of the executor and all the property of the deceased testator from the date of the death of the testator, as long as the grant stands - (B) Whether disposition during the pendency of the probate was founded on fraud or collusion between the executor and the developers? - Held, grant of probate establishes the genuineness of the will and the person in whose favour the probate is granted is entitled to convey the title arising out of the will probated by the Court - It may happen that the propounder did not take appropriate steps, by mistake, to notify the other heirs before obtaining probate - But the third party who acts bona fide and deals with the grantee cannot be made answerable to the fraud or mistakes committed by the propounder - In the present case, applying the above tests to the evidence on record, it was found that Courts below erred on the basis of expedition of sale was fictitious and based on collusion between 'A' and defendant no.14 - (C) Whether defendant no.14 was bona fide purchaser for value without notice? - Held, suit for partition was filed on 21.9.1981 - Defendant no. 3 was aware of the will as early as 21.8.1982 - She did not apply for letters of administration nor challenged the will - Between 21.9.1981 to 22.6.1993 (when the plaint was amended) the developers demolished the old building and they constructed a multi-storey building - In the circumstances, it cannot be said that the other heirs of Deceased had no knowledge of the aforestated events - Hence, defendant no.14 was bona fide purchaser for value and the alienation effected by defendant no.14 in favour of defendants no.15 to 20 was valid - Appeals disposed of.
