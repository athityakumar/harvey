Mannulal Sahu and another v State of Madhya Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

18 August 2004

Case Analysis

Bench

B.N. Agrawal, H.K. Sema

Where Reported

2004 Indlaw SC 1740; (2005) 10 SCC 259

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 304-B, 316, 498-A - Dowry death - Conviction - Validity of dying declaration - Appellants were charged for causing death of ‘X’/deceased - Trial Court convicted, relying on dying declaration of ‘X’ convicted appellant No.1 u/ss. 304-B, 498-A of IPC and appellant No.2 and his brother/’A’ were convicted u/ss. 304-B, 498-A, 316 of IPC - HC confirmed convictions and sentences awarded against appellants and confirmed conviction of ‘A’, but reduced sentence awarded against ‘A’ u/ss. 304-B, 316 of IPC from 10 years to 7 years - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, there was no direct evidence. There were three dying declarations before Court. First dying declaration was recorded by Executive Magistrate on date of occurrence, in which case of accidental fire had been disclosed. Second dying declaration was recorded on next day by treating doctor, which also disclosed case of accidental fire. Third dying declaration was recorded by Tahsildar, which ran into more than two pages and wherein entire history from date of marriage till date of occurrence had given in great detail and it was stated that accused persons poured kerosene oil on victim and set her on fire. Third dying declaration recorded by Tahsildar was highly doubtful and it was an afterthought especially in view of fact that according to earlier two dying declarations, it was a case of accidental fire and no case for torture was made out. It was not safe to place reliance upon dying declaration recorded by Tahsildar, as such prosecution had failed to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt. Convictions and sentences of appellants were set aside and they were acquitted of all charges. Appeal allowed.

Ratio - When dying declaration relied by the prosecution is highly doubtful and does not implicate the accused then such dying declaration could not be made basis for convicting accused and benefit of doubt could be granted.
