Voltas Limited v State of Andhra Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

25 March 2004

Case Analysis

Bench

S.N. Variava, H.K. Sema

Where Reported

2004 Indlaw SC 880; (2004) 11 SCC 569; [2004] 121 Comp Cas 267; 2005(9) SCALE 576; 2004 (136) STC 15

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Indirect Tax; Sales Tax

Summary: Sales Tax - Administrative - Andhra Pradesh General Sales Tax Act, 1957 - Sick company - Negotiations - Sales tax deferral - Charging of interest - Waiver of statutory liability - Company/appellant suffered huge losses and became sick company and matter was taken up before Board for Industrial and Financial Reconstruction (BIFR) - Further, negotiations took place and memorandum of understanding was entered into and scheme was placed before BIFR which was further sanctioned - Govt. issued order that sales tax deferral would be up to maximum of 50 percent of fresh monies - Appellants made reference to Govt. - Govt. directed appellants to move BIFR for obtaining specific order to extend concession of sales tax deferral without charging any interest - BIFR refused to interfere - Appellants filed appeal before Appellate Board which was dismissed - Appellants filed writ petition in HC which was dismissed - Hence, instant Appeal - Whether there was implied waiver of statutory liability - Held, under provisions of the Act, assessee had to obtain licence, file returns and pay tax at time returns were filed - Time for payment could be extended - If extension was granted statutory liability to pay interest at rate of 18 per cent arose which was statutory liability - If statutory liability had to be waived then there must be express waiver of same - Fact that scheme was silent about such waiver showed that there was no waiver which became clear from cl. 13(b)(3) of the scheme - Such provision had to be made as statutory liability would have been to pay interest at rate of 18 per cent - In cl. 13(b)(4) of the scheme, there was no express waiver of or reduction in rate of interest and payment - HC in impugned judgment concluded that there could be no implied waiver of statutory liability - Hence, there being no express waiver of interest, statutory provision could prevail and there was no infirmity in reasoning of HC - Appeal dismissed.
