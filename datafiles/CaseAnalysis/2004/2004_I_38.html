H. K. N. Swami v Irshad Basith (Dead) By Lrs.

Supreme Court of India

22 July 2004

Case Analysis

Bench

Ashok Bhan, S.H. Kapadia

Where Reported

2004 Indlaw SC 1786; (2005) 10 SCC 243

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - Title suit - Permanent injunction - Validity of evidence - Respondent/plaintiff filed suit for permanent injunction restraining appellant defendant from interfering with his possession in suit property - Trial Court dismissed suit on ground that plaintiff had failed to prove his title as also possession over suit property which was reversed by HC on appeal - Appellant contended that finding as to title of suit property was not arrived at by HC on appreciation of evidence and filed appeal before SC - Hence instant appeal.

Held, first appeal has to be decided on facts as well as on law. In first appeal parties have right to be heard both on questions of law as also on facts and First Appellate Court is required to address itself to all issues and decide case by giving reasons. Unfortunately, HC has not recorded any finding either on facts or on law. Sitting as First Appellate Court it was duty of HC to deal with all issues and evidence led by parties before recording finding regarding title. Order of HC is cryptic and same is without assigning any reason. Without recording finding that respondent was in possession of property as also title to property abruptly concluded that title of property is found to be in favour of respondent. For aforesaid reasons, Court accepts appeal, set aside judgment of HC and remit matter back to HC to decide it afresh. Registry is directed to remit record to HC forthwith. Since first appeal pertains to year 1990, Court would request HC to dispose of appeal as expeditiously as possible. Interim stay granted by HC during pendency of first appeal shall continue till disposal of appeal subject to its variation on application being made by either of parties. Appeal allowed.

Ratio - At time of claiming possession of property, said person should prove its title beyond reasonable doubt arise on property.
