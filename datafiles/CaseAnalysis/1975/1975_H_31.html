Hukam Chand Shyam Lal v Union Of India And Ors

Supreme Court of India

17 December 1975

Case Analysis

Bench

R.S. Sarkaria, A.N. Ray, M. Hameedullah Beg, P.N. Shingal

Where Reported

1975 Indlaw SC 304; (1976) 2 SCC 128; AIR 1976 SC 789; [1976] 2 S.C.R. 1060

Case Digest

Subject: Telecom

Summary: Telecom - Telegraph Act, 1885, s. 5 (1) - CPC,1908, s. 115 - Telegraph Rules, 1951, r. 422 - 'public emergency' - Meaning of - Whether disconnection of telephone lines u/s. 5(1) of Indian telegraph Act r/w. r. 422 of Telegraph Rules without giving ample notice and was reasonable? - Held, s. 5(1), if properly construed, does not confer unguided and unbridled power on Central Govt/State Govt/Specially Authorised Officer to take possession of any telegraph - Firstly, occurrence of a 'public emergency' is sine qua non for exercise of power under this section - As a preliminary step to exercise of further jurisdiction under this section, Govt. or authority concerned must record its satisfaction as to existence of such an emergency - Further, existence of emergency which is a pre-requisite for exercise of power under this section, must be a 'public emergency' and not any other kind of emergency - Expression 'public emergency' has not been defined in statute, but contours broadly delineating its scope and features are discernible from section which has to read as a whole - In sub-s. (1) phrase 'occurrence of any public emergency' is connected with and is immediately followed by phrase 'or in interests of public safety' - These two phrases appear to take colour from each other - In first part of sub-s. (2) these two phrases again occur in association with each other, and context further clarifies, with amplification, that a 'public emergency' within contemplation of this section is one which raises problems concerning interest of public safety, sovereignty and integrity of India, security of State, friendly relations with foreign States or public order or prevention of incitement to commission of an offence - It is in context of these matters that appropriate authority has to form an opinion with regard to occurrence of a 'public emergency' with a view to taking further action under this section - It is well settled that where a power is required to be exercised by a certain authority in a certain way, it should be exercised in that manner or not at all, and all other modes of performances are necessarily forbidden - It is all more necessary to observe this rule where power is of a drastic nature and its exercise in a mode other than one provided, will be violative of fundamental principles of natural justice - If telephones of appellants were to be disconnected on ground of misuse, then they had to give, in consonance with principles of natural justice, opportunity to appellants to explain their conduct before taking action u/r. 427 r/w. rr. 416 and 421 - Resort to wrong and more drastic course provided in r. 422, on a ground which was not germane to an action under that Rule, vitiates impugned order, particularly when it is manifest that in making impugned order, General Manager was influenced more by this ground and less, if at all, by existence of 'public emergency' certified by Delhi Administration - Order to quash impugned orders and direct respondents to restore telephone connections to each of these appellants - Appeals allowed.
