 Sukhdev Singh & Ors v Bagatram Sardar Singh Raghuvanshi And Anr.

Supreme Court of India

21 February 1975

Case Analysis

Bench

A.N. Ray, Kuttyil Kurien Mathew, Y.V. Chandrachud, A. Alagiriswami, A.C. Gupta

Where Reported

1975 Indlaw SC 107; (1975) SCC (L&S) 101; (1975) 1 SCC 421; AIR 1975 SC 1331; [1975] 45 Comp Cas 285; 1975 (30) FLR 283; 1975 LabIC 881; 1975 (1) LLJ 399; 1975 (30) PLR 283; [1975] 3 S.C.R. 619; 1975 SLJ 39; 1975 (1) SLR 605

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Oil and Natural Gas Commission, Cost And Works Accountants Act, 1959, National Co-Operative Development Corporation Act, 1962, Oil and Natural Gas Commission Act, 1959, Marine Products Export Development Authority Act, 1972, International Airports Authority Act, 1971, Khadi and Village Industries Commission Act, 1956, All India Institute of Medical Sciences Act, 1956, Industrial Employment (Standing Order ) Act, 1946, Coir Industry Act, 1953, Central Silk Board Act 1948, Central Silk Board Act, 1948, Faridabad Development Corporation Act, 1956

Summary: A. N. Ray, C.J., Y. V. Chandrachud, A. C. Gupta, JJ.

(A) Administrative - Statutory Corporations - Control of Government - Extent - Whether the Oil and Natural Gas Commission (ONGC), the Industrial Finance Corporation (IFC) and the Life Insurance Corporation (LIC) were under the control of Government - Held, ONGC, IFC and LIC were created by statutes - ONGC was owned by the Government, the management was by the Government and it could be dissolved only by the Government - Provisions of the Oil and Natural Gas Commission Act, 1954, showing that it acted as an agency of the Central Government - Provisions of the Industrial Finance Corporation Act, 1948, creating the IFC showing that the Corporation was under the complete control and management of the Central Government - Provisions of the Life Insurance Act, 1956, which created the LIC showing that Corporation was also an agency of the Government carrying on the exclusive business of Life Insurance - LIC was owned and managed by the Government and it could be dissolved only by the Government - All the three statutes constituting the three statutory corporations enabled them to make regulations which provide, inter alia, for the terms and conditions of employment and services of their employees - Order accordingly.

(B) Administrative - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 162 - Regulations framed by the Statutory Corporations - Nature of - Whether Regulations framed by the statutory corporations had the force of law - Held, regulations had the force of law and the employees were entitled to the declaration of being in employment when their dismissal or removal was in contravention of the regulations - Regulations under an Act were framed in exercise of a specific power conferred by the statue to make regulations - Authority making the regulations should specify the sources of its specific power - Essence of law was that it was made by the law makers in exercise of specific authority - Vires of such of law was capable of being Challenged if the power was absent or had been exceeded by the authority making rules or regulations - Manner and procedure adopted in making the regulations in the instant case, by the 'three statutory corporations, have that characteristic of law - Another characteristic of law was its content - Law was a rule of general conduct while administrative instructions related to a particular person - Broadly stated the distinction between rules and regulations on one hand, and administrative instructions on the other, was that rules and regulations could be made only after reciting the source of power whereas administrative instructions were not issued after reciting the source of power - However, no substantial difference was subsisting between rules and regulations inasmuch as 'both were subordinate legislation under powers conferred by the statute - A regulation framed under statute applied uniform treatment to everyone or to all members of some group of class - Regulations in the instant case were, inter alia, for the purpose of defining the duties, conduct and conditions of service of officers and other employees - Regulations contained the terms and conditions of appointment which were imperative - Form and content of the contract with a particular employee was prescriptive and statutory - Notable feature was that those statutory bodies had no free hand in framing the conditions and terms of service of their employees -Statutory bodies were bound to apply the terms and conditions as laid down in the regulations - Regulations were not only binding on the authority but also on the public imposing obligations on the statutory authorities - Statutory authorities could not deviate from the conditions of service - Any deviation would be enforced by legal sanction of courts to invalidate actions in violation of rules and regulations - Existence of rules and regulations under the statute was to ensure regular conduct with a distinctive attitude to that conduct as a standard -Statutory regulations in the instant case gave the employees a statutory status and imposed restrictions on the employer and the employee with no option to vary the conditions -An ordinary individual, in the case of master and servant contractual relationship, enforced breach of contract, the remedy being damages because personal service was not capable of enforcement - In the case of statutory bodies, however, there was no personal element whatsoever because of the impersonal character of the bodies - In their case the element of public employment and service and the support of statute required observance of rules and regulations - Failure to observe requirements of the regulation by statutory bodies was enforced by courts by declaring the dismissal in violation of rules and regulations to be void - Further the executive power of a 'state' was not authorized to frame rules u/art. 162 of the Constitution - A statutory body when it made rules and regulations did so under the powers conferred by statute creating it - Further, terms and conditions laid down in a contract of employment not being contractual imposed by one kind of subordinate legislation, viz., regulations made in exercise of the power conferred by the statute which constituted the corporation - Terms of the regulation were not terms of contract - A corporation had no power to alter or modify or rescind the provisions of the regulations at it's discretion which it could do in respect of the terms of contract that it may wish to enter with its employees independent of these regulations - So far as the terms of the regulations were concerned the actions of the corporation were controlled by the Central Government - Order accordingly.

(C) Administrative - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 12 - Whether the statutory bodies were 'State' within the meaning of art. 12 of the Constitution - Held, statutory bodies were authorities within the meaning of art. 12 of the Constitution though their employees were not the servants of the Union or of a State - State undertook commercial functions in combination with governmental functions in a welfare State - It should be able to impose decisions by or under law with authority - Rules and regulations were authoritative because these rules and regulations directed and controlled not only the exercise of the powers by the corporation but also of persons who dealt with these corporations - State itself was envisaged under art. 298 as having the right to carry on trade and business - State as defined in art. 12 comprehended bodies created for the purpose of promoting economic interests of the people -Circumstance that the statutory bodies required to carry on some activities of the nature of trade or commerce did not indicate that it should be excluded from the scope of the word 'State' - A public authority was a body which had public or statutory duties to perform and which performed those duties and carried out its transactions for the benefit of the public and not for private profit - Such an authority was not precluded from making a profit for the public benefit - Power to give directions, the disobedience of which would be punishable as criminal offences would furnish one of the reasons for characterizing the body as an authority within the meaning of art. 12 - Fact that a statutory corporation was not granted immunity from taxation and therefore was under liability to be taxed would not indicate that the corporation was not a state authority - Order accordingly.

Mathew J.

(D) Administrative - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 12 - Whether the statutory bodies were 'State' within the meaning of art. 12 of the Constitution - Held, State could only act through the instrumentality or agency of natural or juridical persons - Nothing strange in the notion of the State acting through a Corporation and making it an agency or instrumentality of the State - With the advent of a welfare State the framework of civil service administration became increasingly insufficient for handling the new tasks which were often of a specialised and highly technical character - Distrust of Government by civil service was a powerful factor in the development of a policy of public administration through separate Corporations which would operate largely according to business principles and be separately accountable - Public Corporation, therefore, became a third arm of the Government -Employees of public Corporation were not civil servants in so far as public corporations fulfilled public tasks on behalf of government, they were public authorities and as such, subject to control by Government, public Corporation being a creation of the State was subject to the constitutional imitation as the State itself - Governing power wherever located should be subject to the fundamental constitutional limitations - A finding of State financial support plus an unusual degree of control over the management and policies might lead one to characterize an operation as state action - Another factor which might be considered was whether the operation was an important public function - It was clear from these provisions of the statutes in question thatthe Central Government had contributed the original capital of the Corporation that part of the profit of the Corporation went to that Government, that the Central Government exercised control over the policy of the Corporation that, the Corporation carried on a business having great public importance and that it enjoyed a monopoly in the business - Corporations in questions were agencies or instrumentalities of the 'state' and were, therefore, 'state' within the meaning of art. 12 of the Constitution - Order accordingly.

(E) Administrative - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 12 - Whether statutory corporation was an agency or instrumentality of the government for carrying on a business for the benefit of the public - Held, on liquidation of the Corporation, its assets should be divided among the shareholders, namely' the Central and State Governments and others, if any, the implication was clear that the benefit of the accumulated income would go to the Central and State governments - Fact that the agent was subject to the direction of the principal did not mean that he had no legal personality of his own - Likewise, merely because a corporation had legal personality of its own, it did not follow that the Corporation could not be an agent or instrumentality of the state, if, it was subject to control of government in all important matters of policy - Public corporation was a new type of institution which had sprung from the new social and economic functions of government and that it therefore did not neatly fit into old legal categories - Order accordingly.

(F) Administrative - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 12 - Whether the employees under the service of statutory corporations were entitled to the declaration of being in employment when their dismissal or removal was in contravention of statutory provisions - Held, since the employment under these corporations was public employment an employee would get a status which would enable him to obtain declaration for cent nuance in service if he was dismissed or discharged contrary to the regulations - If a job was regarded as analogous to property, it ought to be recognized that a man was entitled to a particular job just as the courts of Equity acknowledged his right to a particular piece of property - Where a public authority was concerned, that could be implemented by a declaration - Court should, therefore, adopt the attitude that declaration was the normal remedy for a wrongful dismissal in case of public employees which would only be refused in exceptional circumstances - Remedy of declaration should be a ready-made instrument to provide reinstatement in public sector - Once it was accepted that a man's job was like his property of which he could be deprived of for specific reasons, that remedy became the primary one though it would need to be reinforced where private individuals were being sued - Law of master and servant had not kept pace with the modern conditions and the mandate of equality embodied in the Constitution - Law still attached to the servant a status of inferiority and subjection to his master - Though fundamental reforms could only emanate from the legislature, the principles fashioned by public law if applied to master servant relationship could bring about a change in law to accord with the social conditions of the 20th century - Order accordingly.

Alagiriswami, J. (DISSENTING)

(G) Administrative - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 12 - Whether the employees under the service of statutory corporations were entitled to the declaration of being in employment when their dismissal or removal was in contravention of statutory provisions - Held, in order that an Institution should be an 'authority' it should exercise part of the sovereign power or authority of the State - Port Trust was given the power to make regulations and to provide that breach of its regulations would be punishable - In such a case, it was undoubtedly exercising part of the power of the State - Whole purpose of the Part III of the Constitution was to confer fundamental rights on the citizen, as against the power of the State or those exercising the power of the State - In the instant case none of the Corporations exercised the power of the State and, therefore, should not be the State or Authority - Regulations framed by the Corporations had no force of law - Employees of statutory bodies had no statutory status and they were not entitled to declaration of being in employment when their dismissal or removal was in contravention of statutory provisions - Under the Indian Legislative practice Governments made the rules and regulations were made by any institution or Organization established by a Statute and where it was intended that the regulation should have effect as law, the Statute itself said so - Administrative instructions were not necessarily in relation to the particular persons and may relate to a whole class of persons even as rules and regulations did - An institution like the LIC the country had necessarily got to have a standard set of conditions of service for its various classes of employees - It was not correct to say that the statutory bodies had no free hand in framing the conditions and terms of service of their employees - Statutory bodies were the authorities to make the regulations and, therefore, could make any regulations regarding the conditions and terms of service of their employees and also change them as they please - It should not, therefore, be said that they were bound by the terms and conditions of service - No fallacy in equating-rules and regulations of a Company with rules and regulations framed by a statutory body - Where an institution or Organization was established by a Statute or under a Statute in principle there was no difference between their powers - While rules were generally made by the Government, the regulations were made by a body which was a creature of the statute itself with its powers limited by the statute - While rules applied to all matters covered by the statute, the scope of the regulations was narrower being usually confined to internal matters of the statutory body such as the conditions of service of its employees - When regulations standardize the conditions of service of the employees or purport to formulate them, their character was further diluted by the nature of the subject matter - Service or employment was basically a contract which was deeply rooted in private law - A mere standardisation or enumeration of the terms of a service contract was not, therefore, ordinarily sufficient to convert it into a statutory status - Order accordingly.
