Narayan Ganesh Dastane v Sucheta Narayan Dastane

Supreme Court of India

19 March 1975

Case Analysis

Bench

Y.V. Chandrachud, P.K. Goswami, N.L. Untwalia

Where Reported

1975 Indlaw SC 159; (1975) 2 SCC 326; AIR 1975 SC 1534; 1981 (1) DMC 293; 1975 HLR 111; [1975] 3 S.C.R. 967

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal

Keywords: Matrimonial Offence

Summary: Family & Personal - Hindu Marriage Act, 1955, ss.10(1)(b), 23, 23(1)(a) - Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 3, 101, 102, 103 and 104 - Judicial separation - Cruelty - Appellant filed as petition for annulment of his marriage by a decree of nullity u/s. 12(1)(c) of the Hindu Marriage Act on the ground that his consent to the marriage was obtained by fraud - Alternatively, appellant asked for divorce u/s. 13(1)(ii) on the ground of unsoundness of mind - Also, alternatively, the appellant asked for judicial separation u/s. 10(1)(b) on the ground that the respondent had treated him with such cruelty - Spouses possess high academic qualifications and each one claimed a measure of social respectability and cultural sophistry - Trial Court held that the respondent was guilty of cruelty and on that ground it passed a decree for judicial separation - On appeal, District Court dismissed the appellant's appeal and allowed the respondent's, with the result that the petition filed by the appellant stood wholly dismissed - Second appeal filed thereagainst was dismissed - Hence, present appeal - (A) Judicial separation on ground of cruelty - Held, neither s. 10 of the Act which enumerates the grounds on which a petition for judicial separation may be presented nor s. 23 which governs the jurisdiction of the court to pass a decree in any proceeding under the Act requires that the petitioner must prove his case beyond a reasonable doubt - S. 23 confers on the court the power to pass a decree if it is 'satisfied' on matters mentioned in Clauses (a) to (e) of the section - Considering that proceedings under the Act are essentially of a civil nature, the word 'satisfied' must mean 'satisfied on a preponderance of probabilities' and not 'satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt' - S. 23 does not alter the standard of proof in civil cases - Under that provision, the relevant consideration is to see whether the conduct is such as to cause a reasonable apprehension in the mind of the petitioner that it will be harmful or injurious for him to live with the respondent - In the instant case, threat by respondent she would put an end of her own life or that she would set the house on fire, the threat that she would make him lose his job and have the matter published in newspapers and the persistent abuses and insults hurled at the appellant and his parents were all of so grave an order as to imperil the appellant's sense of personal safety, mental happiness, job satisfaction and reputation - Hence, conduct of the respondent clearly amounted to cruelty within the meaning of s. 10(1)(b) of the Act - (B) Whether the appellant had at any time condoned the respondent's cruelty u/s. 23(1)(b) of the Act, in any proceeding under the Act? - Held, condonation means forgiveness of the matrimonial offence and the restoration of offending spouse to the same position as he or she occupied before the offence was committed - To constitute condonation there must be, therefore, two things : forgiveness and restoration - Evidence showing that the spouses led a normal sexual life even after a series of acts of cruelty by one spouse was proof that the other spouse condoned that cruelty - Further, condonation is conditional forgiveness but the grant of such forgiveness does not give to the condoning spouse a charter to malign the other spouse - In the instant case, evidence of condonation consisted was the fact that the spouses led a normal sexual life despite the respondent's acts of cruelty - Evidence of condonation was strong and satisfactory as the evidence of cruelty - Hence, respondent was guilty of cruelty but the appellant condoned it and the subsequent conduct of the respondent was not such as to amount to a revival of the original cause of action - Appeal dismissed.
