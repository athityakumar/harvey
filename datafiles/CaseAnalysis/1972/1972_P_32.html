Pushpavathi and Others v Chandraraja Kadamba and Others

Supreme Court of India

23 August 1972

Case Analysis

Bench

D.G. Palekar, A.N. Grover

Where Reported

1972 Indlaw SC 506; (1973) 3 SCC 291; AIR 1972 SC 2492

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Land & Property

Keywords: Suit For Possession, Validity of Will

Summary: Land & Property - Family & Personal - Suit for possession - Validity of Will - Legality - Plaintiffs filed a suit for possession of immovable properties and mesne profits, alleging that deceased 'S' had made no Will and had died intestate and that Will which was put forward by the defendants was a forgery having been brought into existence after death 'S' by the defendants with the help of the scribe and the attesting witnesses - Trial Court held that Will must have come into existence after the death of 'S' and the forged Will must have been brought into existence by defendant no. 1 with the help of the other defendants, the scribe and attesting witnesses - On appeal HC reversed the finding of Trial Court by impugned judgment - Hence, instant appeal - Whether the alleged Will was valid Will executed by 'S'.

Held, all the defendants were close to 'S' and at one time or the other lived with him. At the time of his death defendant no. 4 and 5 were living with him in his house. The HC has observed that the plaintiff's family judged from the Aliyasanthana concept of inheritance would never have been the heirs of deceased testator. On the other hand, defendant no. 1 was a member of the testator's 'Kutumba' or family and the old man, if he still believed in the concept of inheritance, was more likely to leave the bulk of his property to defendant No. 1 than to anybody else. Perusal of the Will which is a short one would go to show that the several disposition were not injudicious. Defendant no. 1 has been given all the lands of 'I' village which come under pattas 1 and 83. He is also entrusted with the management of some of his other lands in 'I' village coming under pattas 15, 58 and 81, the object of the entrustment being that he should perform all the customary religious viniyogs (performances) out of the income derived from those lands. These customary religious performances were to be carried out in both the villages 'I' and 'M'. Patta No. 91 of 'P' village which contains only small lands is also left to him with the condition that the entire produce from the lands covered by Patta No. 91 should be handed over to defendant no. 3 during her life-time. Defendant no. 1 is further enjoined to meet the entire household expenses of the second and third defendants from the income of these lands. HC is right in concluding that the dispositions under the Will were neither unnatural nor unjust. The Will was executed in a room in the Kolke house belonging to the testator who, it appears, had casually asked the two attesting witnesses the previous day to come to his house at noon on 10-3-1958. He had not told them what the business was. That he wanted to keep the whole matter a very well guarded secret is clear from the fact that the scribe and the two attesting witnesses came to know that a Will was being executed only when it was being actually dictated by the testator. No other member of the family living in the house was in the room. The teacher 'V' had come to the house, as usual, at noon and after he had taken his meal he was called inside the room. When the admitted signatures were shown in the witness-box, 'R', handwriting expert on behalf of plaintiffs, obviously called for additional signatures for further comparison, the implication of which was that he could not make up his mind by a mere look at the admitted signatures in the witness-box whether the disputed signature was made by the person who had made the admitted signatures. Naturally 'R' evidence was of little help. It was not possible to disagree with the view taken by the HC and conclusion arrived at by the HC regarding the genuineness of the Will is correct. Appeal dismissed.
