Turner Morrison and Company, Limited v Hungerford Investment Trust Limited

Supreme Court of India

09 March 1972

Case Analysis

Bench

K.S. Hegde, Kuttyil Kurien Mathew

Where Reported

1972 Indlaw SC 190; (1972) 1 SCC 857; AIR 1972 SC 1311; [1972] 42 Comp Cas 512; [1972] 85 ITR 607; [1972] 3 S.C.R. 711; 1973 TAXLR 398

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: (A)Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Limitation Act, 1963 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Indian Income Tax Act, 1922 - Payment of Tax - liability - Plaintiff/appellant/Turner Morrison filed a suit before Trial Court and claimed decree for sum of Rs. 1,27,67,052.16 Ps - Claim was made on ground that plaintiff had paid either as an agent or on behalf of defendant/Hungerford Investment Trust Ltd a sum of Rs. 79,70,802/- as super-tax which it was entitled to be reimbursed - Rs. 47,96,250.16 Ps was added as interest in shape of damages - Trial Court dismissed plaintiff's suit holding that claim in question was barred by estoppel, waiver or acquiescence - On appeal before Appellate Court - Appellate Court affirmed some of findings of Trial Court - Aggrieved by said order plaintiff filed appeal before HC - HC affirmed decision of Trial Court and passed order - Hence instant appeal - Whether claim made by plaintiff was barred by rule of estoppel, or waiver or abandonment - Held, judgments of Trial Court and Appellate Court have not made any distinction between estoppel, waiver and abandonment - Distinction between those three concepts was fine but real - There was no plea of any release u/s. 63 of Contract Act - Hence argument of plaintiff on scope of that section was irrelevant and should not go into same - Essential question to be considered was whether facts established in this case support plea of estoppel put forward by respondent - If answer to that question was in affirmative then there was no need to examine whether there was any waiver or abandonment as pleaded by respondent - 'Estoppel' was a rule of equity - That rule had gained new dimensions in recent years - A new class of estoppel - Full implication of 'promissory estoppel' was yet to be spelled out - It was not denied that year after year from 1941 to 1954 plaintiff passed resolutions undertaking to discharge tax liability of respondent - In pursuance of those resolutions taxes due from respondent paid - Amounts claimed in present suit except those in respect of assessment for assessment year 1955-56 were all admittedly paid before 15-11-1962 - Hence they were prima facie barred by limitation - So far as payments made in respect of assessment for assessment year 1955-56 was concerned, plaintiff could have no, claim against respondent because under amended s. 23-A of 1922 Act, that liability was that of plaintiff itself - But it was urged on behalf of plaintiff that in view of s. 15 (5) of 1963 Act claim made, leaving aside claim made in respect of assessment for assessment year 1955-56, was not barred - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Limitation Act, 1963 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Indian Income Tax Act, 1922 - Claim made - Limitation - Justifiability - Whether claim made in, suit or any portion thereof barred by limitation - Held, s. 15(5) of 1963 Act could be viewed in one of two ways i.e., that provision does not apply to incorporated companies at all or alternatively that incorporated companies must be held to-reside in places where they carry on their activities and thus being present in all those places - Respondent was an Investment company - It had invested large sums of monies in plaintiff's company - It was attending general meeting of shareholders of Plaintiff - Under these circumstances, it must have been residing in India and consequently was not absent from this country - Hence s. 15(5) could not afford any assistance to plaintiff to save bar of limitation - It was clear that there was no justification for bringing suit - There was no justification to make plaintiff in which 'M' had only 49% shares to bear costs - Appeal dismissed.
