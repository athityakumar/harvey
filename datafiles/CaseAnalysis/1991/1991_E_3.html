Union Of India & Ors. v E.G. Nambudiri

Supreme Court of India

23 April 1991

Case Analysis

Bench

K.N. Singh, P.B. Sawant

Where Reported

1991 Indlaw SC 266; (1991) 3 SCC 38; (1991) SCC (L&S) 813; AIR 1991 SC 1216; 1991 (17) ATC 104; 1991 (1) CLR 812; 1991 (62) FLR 850; JT 1991 (2) SC 285; 1991 LabIC 1256; 1991 (2) LLJ 594; 1991 (2) LLN 48; 1991(1) SCALE 783; [1991] 2 S.C.R. 451; 1991 (2) SLR 675; 1991 (2) UJ 303

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution of India, 1950, art.309 - Remarks - Representation against - Rejection - Legality of - Whether in considering and deciding representation against report, authorities are duty bound to record reasons, or to communicate same to person concerned? - Held, on a careful scrutiny of the two remarks, it would appear that observation contained in Item No. 5 'that nothing adverse has come to notice regarding your integrity' is not adverse to the respondent's work and conduct - These remarks are neutral in nature, and they do not adversely comment upon the respondent's work, conduct or character, though they are no commendatory in nature - As regards the remarks at Serial No. 6, they are self-explanatory, which show that inspite of oral and written warnings the respondent the respondent did not improve - If the superior authority was not satisfied with the explanation of the respondent as contained in his representation, what reasons could be stated, except that the authority was not satisfied with the explanation - The superior authority was not obliged to write detail judgment or order giving detais of the warnings or the material on which he formed opinion - There is no dispute that there is no rule or administrative order for recording reasons in rejecting a representation - In the absence of any statutory rule or statutory instructions requiring the competent authority to record reasons in rejecting a representation made by a Govt. servant against the adverse entries the competent authority is not under any obligation to record reason - But the competent authority has no licence to act arbitrarily, he must act in a fair and just manner - Competent authority is required to consider the questions raised by the Govt. servant and examine the same, in the light of the comments made by the office awarding the adverse entries and the officer counter-signing the same - Every administrative authority is not under any legal obligation to record reasons for its decision, although, it is always desirable to record reasons to avoid any suspicion - If any challenge is made to validity of an order on ground of it being arbitrary or mala fide it is always open to authority concerned to place reasons before Court which may have persuaded it to pass orders - Such reasons must already exist on records as it is not permissible to support order by reasons not contained in records - If representation is rejected after its consideration order of rejection would not be rendered illegal merely on ground of absence of reasons - Appeal allowed.
