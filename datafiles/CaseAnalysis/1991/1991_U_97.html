 K. Veeraswami v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

25 July 1991

Case Analysis

Bench

B.C. Ray, K. Jagannatha Shetty, L.M. Sharma, M.N. Venkatachaliah, J.S. Verma

Where Reported

1991 Indlaw SC 711; (1991) 3 SCC 655; (1991) SCC (Cr) 734; 1992 (1) CCR 178; 1991 CRLR 677; 1991 CrLR(SC) 677; JT 1991 (3) SC 198; 1992 (2) LLJ 53; 1991(2) SCALE 150; [1991] 3 S.C.R. 189

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: Judges (Inquiry) Act, 1968, Judges (Inquiry) Rules 1969

Summary: (A) Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950 - Criminal proceedings - Prosecution of Judge - Legality - Appellant/former Chief Justice filed instant appeal against Full Bench decision of HC refusing to quash criminal proceedings taken against him - Whether Parliament was proper authority for granting sanction for prosecution of a Judge - Held, composition of Parliament consisting of President and two Houses makes it unsuitable to task - Further, nature of transacting business or proceeding in each House renders it impracticable - However, only exception made in Constitution for discussion on conduct of a Judge was when motion was taken up for his removal - On no other occasion conduct of a Judge in discharge of duties could be subject matter of discussion in two Houses of Parliament - Without discussion, it would be difficult for Parliament to make an objective judgment with regard to grant of sanction for prosecution - Hence, Parliament could not be proper authority for granting sanction for prosecution of a Judge - Thus, President had authority competent to give previous sanction for prosecution of a Judge of SC and HC - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 124(4) - Competent authority - Hierarchy - Maintainability -Whether authority competent to give sanction should be vertically superior in hierarchy - Held, President should be considered as authority to grant sanction for prosecution of a Judge since order of President for removal of a Judge was mandatory - Further, motion passed by each House of Parliament with special procedure prescribed u/art. 124(4) of Constitution would not propriovigore operate against judge - Thus, all that was required was that authority should be in a position to appreciate material collected against public servant to judge whether prosecution contemplated was frivolous or speculative Under enactment power was conferred on authority competent to remove public servant - Appeal dismissed.

(C) Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 124(4) - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1947, ss. 1, 5(1) - Disciplinary and criminal proceedings - Concurrent - Implementation - Validity - Whether it was necessary to exhaust disciplinary proceedings before initiating criminal proceedings - Held, since 'misbehavior' u/art. 124(4) of Constitution and 'criminal misconduct' u/s. 5(1)of the Act being synonymous, Constitutional process for removal of Judge should be gone through first and only after his removal prosecution if need be recommended in same process - However, proved 'misbehavior' which was basis for removal of a Judge u/art. 124(4) of Constitution might in certain cases involve an offence of criminal misconduct u/s. 1 of the Act - But that was no ground for withholding criminal prosecution till Judge was removed by Parliament - Both were mutually exclusive - Thus, competent authority might either allow prosecution to go on in a Court of law or subject him to a departmental enquiry or subject him to both concurrently or consecutively - Hence, it was not objectionable to initiate criminal proceedings against public servant before exhausting disciplinary proceedings and a fortiori, prosecution of a Judge for criminal misconduct before his removal by Parliament for proved misbehavior was unobjectionable - Appeal dismissed.

(D) Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 361(2) - Criminal prosecution - Immunity - Legality -Whether there was any immunity u/art. 361 (2) of Constitution conferred on Judges from criminal prosecution - Held, no law providing protection for Judges from Criminal prosecution - Art. 361(2) of Constitution confers immunity from criminal prosecution only to President and Governors of States and to no others - Even that immunity was limited during their term of office - Thus, Judges were liable to be dealt with just same way as any other person in respect of criminal offence - However, it was only in taking of bribes or with regard to offence of corruption sanction for criminal prosecution was required - Appeal dismissed.

(E) Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Judges (Inquiry) Act, 1968, s. 173(2) - Constitution of India, 1950 - Charge sheet filed - Maintainability - Whether charge-sheet filed in instant case was valid - Held, charge sheet contains all requirements of s. 173(2) of the Act - However, appellant as Chief Justice of HC was in possession of pecuniary resources and property in his own name and in name of his wife and two sons etc., which were disproportionate by Rs.6,41,416.36 to known sources of income over same period and could not satisfactorily account for such disproportionate pecuniary resources and property - Thus, details of properties and pecuniary resources of appellant also was set out in clear terms - Appeal dismissed.
