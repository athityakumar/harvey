Spences Hotel Private Limited and Another v State of West Bengal and Others

Supreme Court of India

15 February 1991

Case Analysis

Bench

K.N. Saikia, M.M. Punchhi

Where Reported

1991 Indlaw SC 511; (1991) 2 SCC 154; AIR 1991 SCW 757; 1991 (1) CompLJ 276; 1991 (93) CTR 60; JT 1991 (3) SC 479; 1991(1) SCALE 225; [1991] 1 S.C.R. 429; 1991 (2) UJ 9

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Keywords: Luxury, Bombay Motor Vehicles Tax Act, 1958, West Bengal Entertainments and Luxuries (Hotels and Restaurants) Tax Act, 1972

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Media & Entertainment - Luxury Tax - West Bengal Entertainments and Luxuries (Hotels and Restaurants) Tax Act, 1972, ss. 2 and 4 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 14 - Whether s. 4 of W.B. Luxuries Tax Act, 1972 can be held to be ultra vires art. 14 of Constitution for failure to make required classification and for imposition of luxury tax on flat or fixed rate only on basis of air-conditioned floor space? - Equal protection cannot be said to be denied by a statute which operates alike on all persons and property similarly situated, or by proceedings for assessment and collection of taxes which follows course usually pursued in State; it prohibits any person or class of persons from being singled out as special subject for discrimination and hostile legislation, but it does not require equal rates of taxation on different classes of property, nor does it prohibit unequal taxation so long as inequality is not based upon arbitrary classification - Act requires luxury tax to be proportional to air-conditioned space and requires tax to be uniform upon same class of owners of air-conditioned hotels and restaurants; it does not suffer from lack of classification but instead impliedly authorizes it by leaving out non-air-conditioned hotel and restaurants - Measure or unit and rate of taxation are uniform for all within group subjected to tax and further classification within group was not considered necessary by legislature which had wide latitude in matter of classification keeping in view nature of taxable event - Held, luxury tax charged u/s. 4 of Act could not be said to be discriminatory - Appeal dismissed.
