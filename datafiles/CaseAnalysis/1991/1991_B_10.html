B. Viswanathiah and Company and Others v State of Karnataka and Others

Supreme Court of India

11 February 1991

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Ranganathan, Kuldip Singh, N.M. Kasliwal

Where Reported

1991 Indlaw SC 862; (1991) 3 SCC 358; JT 1991 (1) SC 386; 1991(1) SCALE 174; [1991] 1 S.C.R. 305; 1991 (2) UJ 3

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Central Silk Board Act, 1948

Summary: Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1) and 301 to 304(g) - Mysore Silkworm Seed and Cocoon (Regulation of Production, Supply and Distribution) Act, 1959 - Mysore Act 29 of 1969 - Karnataka Act 33 of 1979 - Industries (Development And Regulation) Act, 1951, s. 2 - Central Silk Boards Act, 1948, ss. 2, 4, 8, 10, 13 - Whether amendments to Mysore Silkworm Act by 1979 Karnataka Act have to be struck down as State legislature was not competent to enact same after enactment by Parliament, of Central Silk Boards Act? - When industry is also a controlled industry, legislation in regard to products of industry would be permissible by both Central and State legislatures by virtue of Entry 33 of List III; though Central Silk Board Act purports to control raw silk industry in territory of India, control of industry vested in Parliament was only restricted to aspect of production and manufacture of silk yarn or silk and it did not obviously take in earlier stages of industry, namely, supply of raw materials; though production and manufacture of raw silk cannot be legislated upon by State legislature in view of provisions of Central Act and declaration in s. 2 thereof, that declaration and Entry 52 do not in any way limit powers of State legislature to legislate in respect of goods produced by silk industry and limitation contained in Entry 52 does not affect validity of present legislation - Held, pith and substance of legislation is constitution of a silk Board for research into scientific technological and economic aspects of industry and it does not have anything to do with aspects covered by Entry 33 in List III, therefore, there is no infirmity in legislation under consideration - Appeals dismissed.
