Rajeswar Prosad Misra v State of West Bengal and Another

Supreme Court of India

06 May 1965

Case Analysis

Bench

Mohammad Hidayatullah, A.K. Sarkar, Vaidynathier Ramaswami

Where Reported

1965 Indlaw SC 456; AIR 1965 SC 1887; 1965 CRLJ 817; [1966] 1 S.C.R. 178

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Croiminal Procedure, 1898, ss. 428, 423 and 417(3) - (A) What is difference between appeal from a conviction and an appeal from an order of acquittal? - Held, no difference as per Code - Except that in conviction, appeal is as of right and lies to courts of different jurisdictions depending on nature of sentence, kind of trial and court in which it was held - Whereas in an appeal in case of acquittal State Govt. or by a complainant (where case is started on a complaint) can appeal to High Court with special leave of HC - Such appeals lie on a matter of fact as well as a matter of law (except in trials by Jury) - Although procedure for dealing with two kinds of appeals is identical - S. 428 clearly applies to appeals against acquittal u/s. 417(3) - (B) What are powers of appellate court? - Further held, if trial already held is found to be unsatisfactory or leads to a failure of justice, a retrial may be ordered after setting aside conviction or acquittal (as case may be) - Take additional evidence, which, for reasons to be recorded, it considers necessary - Additional evidence must be necessary - Because there would be failure of justice without it - Power has to be exercised sparingly and only in suitable cases - Once such action is justified, there is no restriction on kind of evidence which may be received - It may be formal or substantial - Although it must not be received in such a way as to cause prejudice to accused ie, change nature of trial or as a disguise for a retrial - Order must not ordinarily be made if prosecution has had a fair opportunity and has not availed of it unless requirements of justice dictate otherwise - Appeal dismissed.
