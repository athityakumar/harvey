Calcutta Dock Labour Board v Jaffar Imam and Others

Supreme Court of India

22 March 1965

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, Vaidynathier Ramaswami

Where Reported

1965 Indlaw SC 119; AIR 1966 SC 282; 1966 CRLJ 189; 1965 (11) FLR 72; 1965 (2) LLJ 112; [1965] 3 S.C.R. 453

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Preventive Detention Act, 1950, s. 3(1)(a)(ii) - Detention - Termination - Legality - Respondents/Dock workers were working under appellant/Dock Labour Board and on 12-8-1955, Commissioner of Police, passed an order u/s. 3(1)(a)(ii) of the 1950, Act, directing respondents should be detained, as they were guilty of violent and riotous behavior and their detention was confirmed by State Govt - After they were released appellant commenced disciplinary proceedings against them and appellant's Deputy Chairman terminated their services, which were confirmed by Chairman on 4-4-1957 - Aggrieved respondents filed petitions before HC, which was dismissed - Aggrieved respondents appealed before DB who allowed appeals and had issued writ directing that impugned orders of termination by appellant should be quashed - Hence, instant appeals - Whether order passed by DB was justified - Held, SC, therefore, satisfied that Court of Appeal was right in taking view that in departmental enquiry which appellant held against respondents it was not open to appellant to act on suspicion, and inasmuch as appellant's decision was clearly based upon detention orders and nothing else - Even in regard to its employees who might had detained under the 1950, Act, if after their release appellant wanted to take disciplinary action against them on ground that they were guilty of misconduct, it was absolutely essential that appellant should have held proper enquiry - At that enquiry, reasonable opportunity should had given to respondents to show cause and before reaching its conclusion, appellant was bound to lead evidence against respondents, give them reasonable chance to test said evidence, allowed them liberty to lead evidence in defense, and then come to decision of its own - Such enquiry was prescribed by requirements of natural justice and obligation to hold such enquiry was also imposed on appellant by cl. 36(3) of the 1951 Scheme and cl. 45(6) of the 1956 Scheme - It appears that in instant enquiry, respondents were not given notice of any specific allegations made against them, and record clearly shows that no evidence was led in enquiry at all - It was only detention orders that were apparently produced and it was on detention orders alone that whole proceedings rest and impugned orders were founded - That being so, SC feel no hesitation in holding that Court of Appeal was perfectly right in setting aside respective orders passed by leaned single Judges when they dismissed writ petitions - Circumstance that respondents happened to be detained could afford no justification for not complying with relevant statutory provision and not following principles of natural justice - Any attempt to short-circuit procedure based on considerations of natural justice might, SC think, be discouraged if rule of law had to prevail, and in dealing with question of liberty and livelihood of citizen, considerations of expediency which were not permitted by law could have no relevance whatever - Appeals dismissed.
