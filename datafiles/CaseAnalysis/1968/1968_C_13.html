Narbada Prasad v Chhagan Lal and Others

Supreme Court of India

30 July 1968

Case Analysis

Bench

Mohammad Hidayatullah, A.N. Grover

Where Reported

1968 Indlaw SC 115; AIR 1969 SC 395; 1969 ALJ 462; 1969 BLJR 466; 1969 JabLJ 441; 1969 MPLJ 459; [1969] 1 S.C.R. 499

Case Digest

Subject: Election; Parliament & Legislature; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Election - Parliament & Legislature - Practice & Procedure - Representation of the People Act, 1951, ss. 33(5), 123(2)(ii) - Legislative election - Corrupt practice - Legality - Election petition was filed by elector to Legislative Assembly Constituency, in array of respondents in HC new person was also joined because his nomination paper was rejected by returning officer - HC allowed petition on 2 grounds that nomination paper was wrongly rejected and that corrupt practice attributed to appellate/candidate and his election agent was established - Hence, instant appeal - Whether impugned order of HC was justified - Held, there was no compliance with provisions of s. 33(5) of the Act and there was no power in Court to dispense with this requirement - It was well understood rule of law that if thing was to be done in particular manner it should be done in that manner or not at all, other modes of compliance were excluded - Even certificate of Tehsildar was not certified or true copy of entry, it only gave gist of entry taken from affidavit, SC was satisfied that Single Judge erred in holding that nomination paper was wrongly rejected and it was rightly rejected - Further, it was not necessary to enlarge upon fact that cow was venerated in our country by vast majority of people and that they believe not only in its utility but its holiness, it was also believed that of cardinal sins was that of gohatya - Therefore, it was quite obvious that to remind voters that they would be committing sin of gohatya would be to remind them that they would be objects of divine displeasure or spiritual censure - Appellant went beyond permitted limits of canvassing and exhortation when he added to legitimate manifesto of his party this observation that by voting for congress or congress candidate voters would be objects of divine displeasure or spiritual censure - Thus, both spiritual censure and divine displeasure were implicit in speeches as made case, therefore, fell clearly within s. 123(2)(ii) of the Act - SC was accordingly satisfied that returned candidate was guilty of corrupt practice and HC was right in holding that election of returned candidate should be avoided - Appeal dismissed.
