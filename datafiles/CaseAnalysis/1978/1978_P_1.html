K. M. Mani and another v P. J. Antony And Others

Supreme Court of India

12 September 1978

Case Analysis

Bench

P.N. Shingal, S. Murtaza Fazal Ali

Where Reported

1978 Indlaw SC 114; (1979) 2 SCC 221; AIR 1979 SC 234; [1979] 1 S.C.R. 701

Case Digest

Subject: Election; Practice & Procedure

Summary: : Election - Representation of the People Act, 1951, ss. 123(1), (2), (3), (4) and (7) and 129(2) - Assembly elections - Corrupt practices - Legality - Polling at election was held on 19-3-1977, and result was declared on 20-3-1977 - Appellant obtained 39,664 votes and 'J', who was nearest rival at election, obtained 24,807 votes and other defeated candidate C obtained 521 votes - 'J' challenged the election of appellant with allegations that he had committed corrupt practices u/s. 123(1), (2), (3), (4) and (7) of the Act - Trial Court found that appellant had committed a corrupt practice u/s. 123(7) of the Act and, as stated, it named him and 'J' as persons who had proved at Trial to be guilty of that corrupt practice - Appellant preferred appeal before HC - HC held that appellant obtained and procured the services of 'J', a police officer, in furtherance of prospects of his election and a corrupt practice was committed u/s. 123 (7) of the Act - Hence, instant appeals - Whether the election of appellant was vitiated by all or any of corrupt practices alleged in petition - Held, Trial Court had taken 'J' into consideration, along with facts that he was a police officer, he went and addressed a meeting, and 'openly' canvassed for appellant who was a Minister of State Govt. at that time - HC had noticed the further fact that 'J' was senior most Superintendent of Police and he should not have unaware that his conduct amounted to an offence u/s. 129(2) of the Act - HC had built up its finding that as 'J' took a very great risk, and declared that he was prepared to lose his job, normal conclusion should be that 'in all probability PW6 faced risk out of fear or favour, and either of which would had emanated only from respondent No. 1 because it was respondent No. 1 alone who was benefited by impugned activities of PW6' - Trial Court was not justified in reaching conclusion that 'J' intentionally took great risk of committing an offence u/s. 129(2) and of losing his job out of fear or favour of appellant - But even if all premises set up by Trial Court in connection were accepted as correct, it should not follow, as an inevitable conclusion, that they should establish a nexus between two, for it might well be that 'J' did all that at instance of someone else, or out of his own desire to curry favour with appellant in hope of some future advantage some time - Appellant was not new to that contest and had won elections on 3 earlier occasions - It was difficult to uphold argument that he was so driven by prospect of defeat as to seek assistance of a police officer openly, on pain of losing his success at hands of any 'elector' who might charge him of commission of that corrupt practice - There was no direct evidence to prove that 'J' went to attend the meeting at the instance of appellant and spoke there at his instance, and as circumstantial evidence was inadequate to reach that conclusion - Impugned orders passed by Trial Court and HC were set aside, and corrupt practice set out in s. 123(7) of the Act was established beyond any doubt - Appeals allowed.
