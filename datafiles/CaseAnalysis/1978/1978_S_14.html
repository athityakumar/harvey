State (Delhi Administration) v Sanjay Gandhi

Supreme Court of India

05 May 1978

Case Analysis

Bench

Y.V. Chandrachud, S. Murtaza Fazal Ali, P.N. Shingal

Where Reported

1978 Indlaw SC 416; (1978) SCC (Cr) 223; (1978) 2 SCC 411; AIR 1978 SC 961; 1993 (2) Crimes 84; 1978 CRLJ 952; 1978 CRLR 309; 1978 MLJ 500; [1978] 3 S.C.R. 950; 1978 UJ 534

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Approver

Summary: Criminal - Criminal Procedure Code, 1974, ss. 437, 439(2), 437(5) and 306 - Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 3, 101, 102 and 103 - Cancellation of bail - (A) Power of Supreme Court to take additional evidence - In appropriate cases, the Supreme Court has the power to take additional evidence but power has to be exercised sparingly, especially in appeals u/art. 136 - Held, relying new evidences which came into existence after High Court judgment would be unfair to the respondent to make use of that material without giving him an adequate opportunity to meet it - State cannot rely on any new material which was not available to the HC - (B) Observation of Supreme Court in the incidental proceedings - Contention of such observation will influence the course of pending trial - Held, no comment made on the veracity of witnesses - No subtle guidance offered to unravel why the witnesses have turned hostile - Presently, matters within the domain of Sessions Judge, not of the Supreme Court and Session Judge is free to assess and evaluate the evidence - (C) Cancellation of the bail - Propriety of - Held, rejection of bail is easier than cancellation of bail in a non-bailable offence - Cancellation of bail only if it is not conducive to allow the accused to retain his trial during the trial - Turning witness hostile cannot be inferred that the accused has won him over - The objective fact that witnesses have turned hostile must be shown to bear a causal connection with the subjective involvement therein of the respondent - Bail once granted cannot be cancelled on the off chance or on supposition - It is necessary for the prosecution to show some act or conduct on the part of the respondent from which a reasonable inference may arise that the witnesses have gone back on their statements - (D) Cancellation of bail - Proof of prosecution - Nature of - In criminal cases, every incidental matter need not be proved beyond the doubt - Preponderance of probabilities that the accused has attempted to tamper with the witness is sufficient - Proving by the test of balance of probabilities that the accused has abused his liberty or that there is a reasonable apprehension that he will interfere with the course of justice is all that is necessary for the prosecution to do in order to succeed in an application for cancellation of bail - Held, the respondent has misused the facility afforded to him by the HC by granting anticipatory bail to him - Rejection of affidavits of Y and S by HC as 'defective' is unreasonable - (E) Power of the HC u/s. 439(2) of CrPC, 1973, for any person be arrested and committed to custody - Held, such power has to be exercised with care and circumspection, but to be exercised in appropriate cases - Refusal to exercise such power makes the court to be a silent spectators to the subversion of the judicial process - Cancel the bail of respondent - Appeal partly allowed.
