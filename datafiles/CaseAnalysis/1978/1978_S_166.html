Tehal Singh and Others v State of Punjab

Supreme Court of India

17 October 1978

Case Analysis

Bench

O. Chinnappa Reddy, Jaswant Singh

Where Reported

1978 Indlaw SC 379; (1979) SCC (Cr) 722; (1980) Supp SCC 400; AIR 1979 SC 1347; 1979 CRLJ 1031; 1979 CRLR 660

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Evidence Act, 1872, s. 32 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34, 120-B and 302 - Arms Act, 1959, s. 27 - (A) Whether the dying declaration was a later fabrication in view of the wealth of detail contained therein indicating that it was not the statement of the dying man and that it was not recorded in the words of the deceased and was not made in the expectation of death? - From the certificate appended at the foot of the statement of deceased Harmel Singh, it appears that Harmel Singh was in a position to make the statement attributed to him - S. 32 of the Evidence Act, 1872, does not require that a statement should be made in expectation of death and it is clear from the evidence that the condition of Harmel Singh was serious at that time; Head Constable Kulwant Singh had stated in his evidence that he put questions to Harmel Singh and recorded his answers and that he recorded what Harmel Singh stated "In his own way" but it does not mean that he recorded something other than what Harmel Singh stated - Further, the details contained in any statement depend upon the capacity for observance of the person making the statement, the condition of the person at the time of making the statement, his anxiety to mention details and the manner in which questions are put and answers elicited - The very wealth of detail in a statement attributed to a dying man may arouse suspicion in certain cases but this cannot necessarily lead to the inference that the statement is a fabricated one - Held, there is no infirmity attached to the dying declaration and was recorded at the time when it purports to have been recorded - (B) Whether evidence of witness (i) Hardip Singh could not be accepted without corroboration since he was a child witness and was related to the deceased and interested in implicating the accused (ii) Chotta Singh was entirely unworthy of credit? - (i) Hardip Singh is a lad of 13 years; the learned Sessions Judge who had the opportunity of seeing Hardip Singh in the witness-box did not consider it necessary to treat him as a child witness; perusal of his evidence also shows that he has certainly attained a measure of mature understanding and his evidence does not suffer from any infirmity; the mere circumstance that he is the son of one of the deceased persons does not justify looking at his evidence with any suspicion; the dying declaration refers to his presence and there was no reason for him to be influenced by anyone else to implicate the accused, nor was there any reason for anyone else to influence him to implicate the accused; (ii) Chotta Singh's evidence regarding the main occurrence remained unshaken in cross-examination; his name was also mentioned in the dying declaration as present at the time of the occurrence and PW 18 also spoke as to his presence - Held, the opinion of the learned Sessions Judge and the High Court about the weight to be attached to the evidence of Hardip Singh is endorsed and also in the circumstances of the case, Chotta Singh's evidence also can be safely accepted in regard to the attack on the three deceased persons - (C) Whether Darshan Singh and Gurmel Singh could not be convicted u/s. 302 r/w s. 34, IPC, 1860 since they did not share commom intention with Tehal Singh to cause death of the deceased persons as the injuries inflicted by them were not only simple but appeared to have been given with light force or a light weapon? - The 3 accused persons came together, one of them was armed with a gun, the other two with iron rods; the person armed with the gun opened fire and after the deceased fell down the other two accused were not content but went forward to inflict injuries on the deceased person with the iron rods and thereafter all of them went away together; merely because injuries inflicted by the two were not serious injuries, it cannot be said that they were not actuated by the common intention to cause the death of the deceased - Held, the irresistible inference is that the three of them were actuated by the common intention to cause death of the deceased; in order to attract the applicability of s. 34 r/w s. 302, it is not necessary that each and everyone of the accused persons must have inflicted a serious injury - (D) Whether Tehal Singh should not be awarded the extreme penalty since there was some provocation on the part of Pirthi Singh? - The action of the deceased Pirthi Singh which might be said to have given rise to some provocation was not part of the same incident; the provocation, if any, was to Darshan Singh and not to Tehal Singh and after the earlier incident Darshan Singh went away; it was a few hours thereafter that the 3 accused persons came armed with gun and iron rods and attacked the deceased; Mithu Singh and Gurmel Singh had offered no provocation at all and their only sin was that they attempted to intervene when Pirthi Singh was shot at - Held, no justifiction to interfere with the sentence awarded - Appeal dismissed.
