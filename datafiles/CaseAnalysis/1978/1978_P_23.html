 Prag Ice and Oil Mills and Another v Union Of India

Supreme Court of India

21 February 1978

Case Analysis

Bench

M. Hameedullah Beg, P.N. Bhagwati, P.N. Shingal, S. Murtaza Fazal Ali, Y.V. Chandrachud, D.A. Desai, Jaswant Singh

Where Reported

1978 Indlaw SC 472; (1978) 3 SCC 459; AIR 1978 SC 1296; 1978 CRLJ 1281; [1978] 3 S.C.R. 293

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Probable Consequence

Summary: Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950 - Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951 - Essential Commodities Act, 1955 - Legislative powers - Fundamental right - Statutory rule - Right of Property - An order was passed by the Central Govt. u/s. 3 of the 1955 Act fixing the fair price for mustard oil at Rs.10 per kilogram - This order was challenged by the petitioners alleging that the order denied the right to property u/art. 19(1)(f) and the right to carry on their trade and business guaranteed by art. 19(1)(g) of the Constitution - Also that the petitioners were denied the benefits of art. 14 of the Constitution - Petitioners also stated as the order was beyond the scope of s. 3 of the 1955 Act, and thus it was not protected by the Ninth Schedule of the Constitution - The SC in 1977 had passed an order holding that the fair price fixed was reasonable - A large number of petitions requested for delivery of reasons in support of the order passed by the SC - Hence, the instant application combining all the petitions to answer the question based on law as to the validity and reasons for the order - Whether the order fixing the fair price was violative of arts. 14 and 19 of the Constitution and whether the order passed by the Central Govt. was beyond the scope of s.3 of the 1955 Act - Held, s. 3(1) of the 1955 Act empowered the Central Govt. to fix the prices of essential commodities if it was of the opinion that it was necessary or expedient to do so for maintaining or increasing supplies of any essential commodity or for securing their equitable distribution and availability at a fair price - Dominant purpose of these provisions was to ensure the availability of essential commodities to the consumers at a fair price - Though patent injustice to the producer was not to be encouraged, a reasonable return on investment or a reasonable rate of profit was not the sine qua non of the validity of action taken in furtherance of the powers conferred by s. 3(1) and s. 3(2)(c) of the 1955 Act - Interest of the consumer had to be kept in the forefront and the prime consideration that an essential commodity was to be made available to the common man at a fair price - Hence, the price control order was not beyond the scope of s. 3 of the 1955 Act - Moreover the Price Control Order was not unreasonable for it to be held constitutionally invalid under arts. 14 and 19, and therefore, it did not violate the petitioner's right to property or their right to do trade or business - Also the Govt. was entitled to make pragmatic adjustments which may be called for by particular circumstances and the price control could be declared unconstitutional only if it was patently arbitrary, discriminatory or demonstrably irrelevant to the policy which the legislature was free to adopt - The interest of the producer and the investor was only one of the variables in the 'constitutional calculus of reasonableness' and Courts ought not to interfere so long as the exercise of Govt.al power to fix fair prices was broadly within a 'zone of reasonableness' as these powers were exercised for the benefit of public interest in general - Petitions dismissed.
