P. N. Easwara Iyer v P. N. Venkatasubramania Iyer and Others

Supreme Court of India

05 May 1978

Case Analysis

Bench

R.S. Pathak

Where Reported

1978 Indlaw SC 415; (1978) 3 SCC 373

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal

Summary: Family & Personal - Hindu law - Joint Hindu family consisting of various branches - Registered partition deed - Properties partitioned between branches -Difference between first defendant and eldest son - Suits for partition of coparcenary properties belonging to father and his branch - Plaintiff claimed several items to be coparcenary properties including houses gifted to daughters - Whether properties in question were coparcenary properties liable to inclusion for partition? - Held, findings of both courts below that house properties gifted by registered gift deeds to daughters by father should be regarded as falling outside partition suits must be upheld - Regarding properties at items 327, 328 and 330, evidence shows that properties at items 327 and 328 were purchased by second defendant at a court auction - Item 330 was subject to a usufructuary mortgage and second defendant purchased it at a sale effected by Official Receiver - Therefore these properties are self-acquisitions of second defendant - Property at item 341 was purchased at a public auction by fourth defendant and property must be regarded as his self-acquisition - Further, if father made a gift of money to one of sons and property was acquired from that money, property must continue to be regarded as separate property of son - It was never acquired by him for family, and all that can be said is that father is liable to account for money so gifted - As regards claim of plaintiff in regard to two fixed deposits of Rs. 50, 000, matter should be investigated by court of first instance in order to determine whether they can be directly related to original fixed deposits standing in name of first defendant at time of division in status of family - Properties at items Nos 210 to 282, which were originally in nature of coparcenary property, remained coparcenary property in hands of first defendant and were liable to partition between him and his sons - Hence they must be regarded as coparcenary property liable to partition - Property at item 336 was purchased from coparcenary funds - Hence it constitutes coparcenary property available for partition between members of family- Appeals dismissed.
