Pattam Khader Khan v Pattam Sardar Khan And Anr.

Supreme Court of India

09 July 1996

Case Analysis

Bench

M.M. Punchhi, Sujata V. Manohar

Where Reported

1996 Indlaw SC 2980; (1996) 5 SCC 48; 1996 (5) AD(SC) 381; 1996 (3) AWC 1340; 1996 (2) BC 275; 1996 (3) CCC 100; 1996 (3) CLT 125; JT 1996 (6) SC 201; 1996 (2) MLJ(SC) 142; 1996(5) SCALE 63B; [1996] Supp3 S.C.R. 320; 1996 (5) Supreme 358

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Civil Procedure; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Civil Procedure - Practice & Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 21, r. 95, s. 65 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908, art. 180 - Limitation Act, 1963, s. 134 - When sale become absolute? - There is nothing in r. 95 to make it incumbent for purchaser to file certificate along with application - On sale becoming absolute, it is obligatory on court to issue certificate - Whether there be failure to issue certificate or delay of action on behalf of court or inaction of purchaser in completing legal requirements and formalities, are factors which have no bearing on limitation prescribed for application under art. 134? - Purchaser cannot seek to extend limitation on ground that certificate has not been issued - Starting point of limitation for application being date when sale becomes absolute i.e., date on which title passed, evidence of title, in form of sale certificate, due from court, could always be supplied later to court to satisfy requirements of O. 21, r.95 - Title of court auction-purchaser becomes complete on confirmation of sale under O. 21, r. 92, and by virtue of thrust of s. 65 CPC, property vests in purchaser from date of sale - Certificate of sale, by itself, not creating any title but merely evidence thereof - Sale certificate rather is a formal acknowledgment of a fact already accomplished, stating as to what stood sold - Held, High Court erred in holding that it is only from date when a sale certificate is issued, that limitation starts running - Such view of HC would not only cause violence to clear provisions of art. 134 of Limitation Act but have effect of unsettling law already settled - Impugned orders of HC set aside restoring that of first court - Appeal allowed.
