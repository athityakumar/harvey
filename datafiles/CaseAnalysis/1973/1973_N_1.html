N. L. Devender Singh & Ors. v Syed Khaja

Supreme Court of India

03 August 1973

Case Analysis

Bench

M. Hameedullah Beg, Kuttyil Kurien Mathew

Where Reported

1973 Indlaw SC 154; (1973) 2 SCC 515; AIR 1973 SC 2457; 1974 (1) MLJ(SC) 65; [1974] 1 S.C.R. 312

Case Digest

Subject: Contract & Commercial

Summary: Civil Procedure - Land & Property - Specific Relief Act, 1877 ss. 12, 22 - Specific Relief Act, 1963 s. 20 - Agreement to sell - Specific performance - Defendant no.1 /appellant agreed to sell his house for Rs. 60,000 to plaintiff/respondent and entered into agreement on 27-9-1962 and paid Rs. 10,000 as earnest money through cheque - Because of cancellation of cheque another agreement was entered into on 9-10-1962 and paid Rs. 20,000 - Plaintiff made requests to defendant no.1 for registration of property which was ignored and it is learnt that defendant no.1had sold said property to some other persons - Plaintiff filed suit before Trial court for specific performance of contract which held plaintiff was not entitled to specific performance of agreement but awarded decree for return of Rs.20,000 with penalty of Rs. 20,000 and interest - On appeal HC decreed for specific performance - Hence, instant appeal - Whether specific performance to convey property ought to be granted.

Held, presumption contained in the explanation u/s. 12 of 1877 Act was not rebutted here. The HC had rightly decreed the suit for specific performance of the contract and the discretion, as laid down u/s. 22 of 1877 Act corresponding to s. 20 of Act of 1963, is not to be exercised arbitrarily but on sound and reasonable grounds guided by judicial principles so that it is capable of correction by a court of appeal. Therefore, HC court had rightly held that Trial court had gone completely astray in the exercise of its discretion on the footing that the plaintiff enjoyed an "unfair advantage" over the defendant no.1, whereas, on the facts and circumstances of the case, it was defendant no.1 who was placed in a position to exploit the need of the plaintiff and the plaintiffs allegedly insecure position under the first agreement. Thus, it is clear that the plaintiff had dealt very fairly and squarely with defendant no.1 and Trial court's error in the exercise of its discretion on an utterly untenable, fanciful and unsound ground was rightly corrected by the HC. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - Equity helps honest plaintiffs against defendants who break solemnly given undertakings.
