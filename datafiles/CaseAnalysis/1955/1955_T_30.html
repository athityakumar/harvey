 Thakur Amar Singhji v State Of Rajasthan(And Other Petitions)

Supreme Court of India

15 April 1955

Case Analysis

Bench

T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Syed Jaffer Imam

Where Reported

1955 Indlaw SC 58; AIR 1955 SC 504; 1955 SCJ 523; [1955] 2 S.C.R. 303

Case Digest

Subject: Contract & Commercial

Keywords: court of Wards Act, Bombay Prohibition Act 1949, Rajasthan Land Reforms & Resumption of Jagirs Act, 1952, Marwar Land Revenue Act, Marwar Tenancy Act, 1949, Orissa Estates Abolition Act

Summary: Land & Property - Bombay Prohibition Act, 1949 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Court of Wards Act - Marwar Land Revenue Act - Marwar Tenancy Act, 1949 - Oriss Estates Abolition Act - Rajasthan Land Reforms and Resumption of Jagirs Act, 1952 - Constitution Of India, 1950 - Government of India Act, 1935 - Indian Contract Act, 1872 - (A) Whether Rajpramukh had no competence to enact law, and Rajasthan Land Reforms Act is therefore not a valid piece of legislation? - Held, before formation of United State, covenanting rulers enjoyed sovereign rights of legislation in their respective territories; and u/art. VI(2)(a), they agreed to surrender those rights and vest them in United State - It was therefore plainly intended that State of Rajasthan should have plenary legislative authority such as was formerly exercised by rulers - Rajpramukh was by reason of art. X(3) of Covenant 'the authority functioning immediately before commencement of Constitution as Legislature' of Rajasthan, and that he could u/art. 385 exercise powers which Legislature of State could - (B) Whether Bill was not prepared by Rajpramukh as required by art. 212-A(2), and therefore law was not validly enacted? - Held, when Bill has not been prepared by Rajpramukh, he has to firstly, if he thinks fit, adopt it before he could pass on to second stage and reserve Bill for consideration of President; and very purpose of his endorsing his approval on Bill is to show that he has thought fit to adopt it - There is no provision in art. 212-A(2) for Rajpramukh approving of a Bill, and in context, therefore, an endorsement of approval on Bill must signify its adoption by him - And when Bill is produced with an endorsement of approval under his signature, question must be held to be concluded, and any further discussion about legislative or executive state of mind of Rajpramukh must be ruled out as inadmissible - (C) Whether Act is ultra vires powers of State as resumption is not one of topics of legislation enumerated either in State List or in Concurrent List in Seventh Schedule of Constitution? - Held, reading provisions of Act as a whole, it is abundantly plain that what was meant by resumption was only acquisition - Indeed, if Act purported to be one for acquisition of jagirs, its provisions could not have been different from what they are - Resumption for which Act provides is something different from resumption which is authorised by art. VII(3) - It was a resumption made not in enforcement of rights which rulers had as grantors but in exercise of sovereign rights of eminent domain possessed by State - Such being true character of legislation, not much significance could be attached to use of word 'resumption' in Act - In view of peculiar relationship between jagirdar and States, it cannot be said that word 'resumption' is inadmissible to signify acquisition - (D) Whether legislation is not saved by art. 31-A? - Held, object of art. 31-A was to save legislation which was directed to abolition of intermediaries so as to establish direct relationship between States and tillers of soil, and construing word in that sense which would achieve that object in a full measure, jagir was meant to cover all grant under which grantees had only rights in respect of revenue and were not tillers of soil - Maintenance grants in favour of persons who were not cultivators such as members of ruling family would be jagirs for purposes of art. 31-A - Petitions dismissed.
