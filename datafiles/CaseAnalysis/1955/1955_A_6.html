Aher Raja Khima v State of Saurashtra

Supreme Court of India

22 December 1955

Case Analysis

Bench

Vivian Bose, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, N. Chandrasekhara Aiyar

Where Reported

1955 Indlaw SC 41; AIR 1956 SC 217; 1956 CRLJ 426; 1956 (1) MLJ 135; 1956 (1) MLJ(SC) 135; 1956 SCJ 243; [1955] 2 S.C.R. 1285

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Indian Evidence Act, Police Act, Code of Criminal Procedure 1898

Summary: Criminal - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, s. 342 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302 and 447 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 - Constitution Of India, 1950, arts. 132, 134 and 136 - Appellant prosecuted for murder - High Court based its conviction on a retracted confession plus certain circumstances regarded as corroborative - Whether HC erred in allowing appeal against acquittal of appellant by taking a different view of evidence? - Appellant sent to Magistrate for recording confession - But not recorded till 10 days later - Judicial lock-up is in charge of a Police Guard under control of Sub Inspector who had conducted investigation - Appellant repudiated his confession at earliest opportunity and on examination stated that confession made under threat from police - Two important factors in every criminal trial that weigh heavily in favour of an accused person - Firstly, the accused is entitled to benefit of every reasonable doubt - Secondly, when an accused person offers a reasonable explanation of his conduct, then, even though he cannot prove his assertions, they should ordinarily be accepted unless circumstances indicate that they are false - What appellant said in this case is not impossible - Moreover police absent themselves from witness box - Thus reasonable apprehension that confession was not voluntary - Only reason that HC Judges give for displacing this conclusion is that in circumstances it would have been difficult for police to threaten him - Held, HC has not shown compelling reasons for holding Sessions Judge wrong - Sessions Judge's hesitation to convict Appellant is grounded on well established judicial principles - Appeal allowed.
