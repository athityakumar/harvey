Pritam Singh and Another v State of Punjab

Supreme Court of India

04 November 1955

Case Analysis

Bench

Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha

Where Reported

1955 Indlaw SC 111; AIR 1956 SC 415; 1956 CRLJ 805

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, ss. 342, 539 B, 87 and 88 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 - Arms Act, s. 19 (f) - Appellants charged with murder - Conviction by Addl. Sessions Judge and confirmed by High Court - (A) Whether revolvers and rifle could be relied upon as corroborative evidence? - Position of two weapons stood on different footing as rifle had been found with one of appellants and revolvers had been recovered pursuant to information supplied by other - Evidence of two witnesses in whose presence rifle was recovered was scrutinised both by Addl. Sessions Judge and also by the High Court and both these Courts came to the conclusion that they could rely upon same - Held, Court not prepared to differ from that appreciation of evidence by Courts below - Recovery of revolvers not considered enough to establish his guilt by Addl. Sessions Judge - Acquittal of one of appellants of that charge was tantamount to a finding that prosecution had failed to establish possession of revolver by him - Maxim 'res judicata pro veritate accipitur' is applicable to criminal proceedings - HC held that appellant having been acquitted at first trial on charge of having ammunition in his possession, prosecution was bound to accept correctness of that verdict and was precluded from taking any steps to challenge it at second trial - HC was right in rejecting evidence relating to revolvers - (B) Whether identification of accused was flawed? - 95 percent of witnesses failed to identify the first appellant - However both Courts below relied upon evidence of two witnesses as sufficient to establish identity of accused - Held, Appellant was satisfactorily identified by both of them and that piece of evidence is available to prosecution against him - Identification of second appellant was similarly criticised as unsatisfactory - Witnesses failed to remember he limped - Absence of any mention of this limp, by these witnesses is not of much consequence - Identification of both stands established - (C) Whether examination of one of Appellants by Addl. Session Judge u/s. 342 CrPC to identify limp is valid? - If Appellant had refused to accede to suggestion of Addl. Sessions Judge he would have been justified in doing so and no provision in law by which this demonstration could have been made - Accused, however agreed to do so - Held, this was occular demonstration and result of such occular demonstration could certainly be taken into account - (C) Whether facial identification of both accused by learned Addl. Sessions Judge on a comparison of description in F.I.R. was valid? - It was not quite legitimate for Addl. Sessions Judge to do so because it would be substituting his view and observations in place of evidence which could be tested by cross-examination - Held, it was not open to Addl. Sessions Judge to incorporate these observations of his in judgement and base his conclusions on same - However abundant evidence on record against Appellants - Appeal dismissed.
