M.V. Elisabeth And Ors. v Harwan Investment And Trading Pvt. Ltd., Hanoekarhouse, Swat

Supreme Court of India

26 February 1992

Case Analysis

Bench

T.K. Thommen, R.M. Sahai

Where Reported

1992 Indlaw SC 902; (1993) Supp2 SCC 433; AIR 1993 SC 1014; AIR 1993 SCW 177; 1993 (1) BankCLR 407; JT 1992 (2) SC 65; 1992(1) SCALE 490; [1992] 1 S.C.R. 1003

Case Digest

Subject: Carriers & Transportation; Constitution

Keywords: Court Of Record, Indian Independence Act, 1947, Andhra State Act, 1953, Colonial Courts Of Admiralty Act, 1890, Colonial Courts of Admiralty (India) Act, 1891

Summary: Carriers & Transportation - Constitution - International - Colonial Courts of Admiralty (India) Act, 1891 - Admiralty Court Act, 1861 - Admiralty jurisdiction - Place of residence or business - Action in rem - Planitiff was a private limited company having its registered office in Goa - Defendants acted in breach of duty by leaving the port of Marmagao and delivering the goods to the consignee in breach of the plaintiff's directions to the contrary, thereby committing conversion of the goods entrusted with them - Suit was instituted in Andhra Pradesh HC invoking its admiralty jurisdiction by means of an action in rem - Vessel was arrested when it entered the Port of Vishakhapatnam after returning from foreign ports - Owner of the vessel entered appearance and provided security by furnishing a Bank Guarantee under protest in the sum of Rs. 14,25,000 - Vessel was released from detention - Defendants moved an application in the HC raising a preliminary objection to the jurisdiction of that Court - Defendants contended that plaintiff's suit against a foreign ship owned by a foreign company not having a place of residence or business in India was not liable to be proceeded against on the admiralty side of the HC by an action in rem in respect of a cause of action alleged to have arisen by reason of a tort or a breach of obligation arising from the carriage of goods from a port in India to a foreign port - Plaintiff alleged that every person had a right to approach the Court of the land for appropriate remedy in respect of claims against a foreign ship and its owner, and to deny him that right and to compel him to pursue remedy in a foreign country according to an unfamiliar system of law and practice in strange and uncertain conditions, and consequently incurring high expenses with all the uncertainties of such a pursuit, was unjust and uncalled - Whether the HC had admiralty jurisdiction over claims relating to inward and outward cargo - Held, that Admiralty jurisdiction was an essential aspect of judicial sovereignty which under the Constitution and the laws was exercised by the HC as a superior court of record administering justice in relation to persons and things within its jurisdiction - Power to enforce claims against foreign ships was an essential attribute of admiralty jurisdiction and it was assumed over such ships while they were within the jurisdiction of the HC by arresting and detaining them - Once a foreign ship arrested in Indian waters by an order of the HC, in exercise of the admiralty jurisdiction vested in it by statute, or inherent in it as a court of record, in respect of any maritime claim against its owner, wherever the cause of action may have arisen, and whether or not the ship was subsequently released by the owner furnishing security, proceeding must continue against the owner as in any other suit - The arrest of the vessel while in Indian waters attracted the jurisdiction of the HC, exercising unlimited jurisdiction to proceed with the trial - All foreign ships entering Indian waters were presumed to know that they fell within the jurisdiction of that country during their stay - The vessel in question was lying in the Port of Vishakhapatnam when she was arrested in respect of a cause of action relating to cargo - Jurisdiction over the ship in this case had to be exercised by its arrest and detention by means of an action in rem and the reason for this wide exercise of jurisdiction was that the foreign owner being not available within jurisdiction, and the stay of the foreign ship in the waters of the coastal state being necessarily brief - Hence, HC rightly assumed jurisdiction by the arrest of the vessel while it was lying in the port of Vishakhapatanam - Appeal dismissed.
