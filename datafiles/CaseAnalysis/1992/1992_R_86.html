Mahesh Chandra v Regional Manager, U.P. Financial Corporation And Ors.

Supreme Court of India

12 February 1992

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Ramaswamy, R.M. Sahai

Where Reported

1992 Indlaw SC 1069; (1993) 2 SCC 279; AIR 1993 SC 935; AIR 1992 SCW 3629; 1992 ALJ 1202; 1993 (1) BankCLR 51; [1993] 78 Comp Cas 1; 1992 (2) CompLJ 89; 1993 (2) GLH 337; JT 1992 (2) SC 326; 1992(3) SCALE 388; [1992] 1 S.C.R. 616; 1992 (2) UPLBEC 1333

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Land & Property

Keywords: Default in payments, Auction of property

Summary: Banking & Finance - Land & Property - Uttar Pradesh State Financial Corporations Act, 1951, ss. 24, 25(1)(g), 29 - Default in payments - Auction of property - Justifiability - Appellant (petitioner) was Managing Partner of a Mill, owned 2 plots and was constructed by partnership firm - Said plot was hypothecated respondent (Corporation) and a loan was sanctioned, which was repayable in 11 annual installments - Non-cooperation of other partners and lack of working capital, due to failure to release the balance loan, landed the running mill into rough weather and defaults in payment were committed - Ultimately, Corporation took possession of hypothecated plot published for sale inviting tenders - Respondent nos. 3 to 5 submitted tender and was accepted - Before accepting the tender no notice nor an opportunity in that regard was given to appellant - Aggrieved appellant, filed a petition before HC, which was dismissed by impugned judgment - Hence, instant appeal - Whether impugned judgment passed by HC was justified - Held, Corporations deal with public money for public benefit - Approach has to be public oriented, helpful to loanee, without loss to Corporation - U/s. 24 of the Act itself required the Board 'to discharge its function on business principles, due regard being had to the interest of industry, commerce and general public' - In instant case, release of plot for private sale along with other unencumbered 2 plots would have fetched the necessary amount to pay off the debt - Even the offer to receive Rs.5 lacs in full quids would have salvaged the problem - Any prudent businessman with least acumen would have agreed to proposal of the release of plot for sake of recovering its debts - Instead of agreeing to receive 5 lacks in lump sum, it opted for 2 lacs fifty thousands, that too in 4 yearly installments - It was neither business principle, nor in the interest of commerce and industry, nor good of general public - Any reasonable approach, which of course was not only desirable but necessary, while dealing with such matters, would have immediately demonstrated that the Corporation by such step of releasing the plot, which was of no consequence to it, was going to gain and perpetuated the objectives of the Act - Attitude of Corporation was contrary to spirit and scheme of s. 24 of the Act - In spite of reminding the Corporation that due to lack of working capital, appellant was unable to run the mill - Corporation did not release the balance loan and no explanation came forth - Corporation was guilty of not acting in accordance with law either at the stage of takeover or in transferring the unit - Admittedly the entire loan was not disbursed - Need of capital in the last stages could not be doubted - If Corporation refused to release the amount at a time when the unit was nearing completion or was ready to start functioning, then it falls short of capital and it was bound to land itself in trouble - That was what happened in instant case - Corporation or its officers or servants as trustee were bound to exercise their power in good faith in selling or dealing with property of debtor as an ordinary prudent man would exercise in the management of his own affairs to preserve and protect his own estate - Thus, acts of officer or servant of corporation should be reasonable, just and fair which should meet the eye and offer accepted should be of competitive and every attempt should be made to secure as maximum price as possible to liquidate the liabilities incurred by industrial concern or the debtor under the Act - Though tenders were invited respondent no. 3 alone had given the tender for a sum of Rs.2 lacs - On negotiation it was said to been raised to Rs.2,55,000 - But deferred payments, on initial deposit of 25% and balance payment within 4 years of half yearly installments, were given - That solicitous attitude, at the expense of appellant, appear to be unjust and unfair and no reasonable prudent owner would accept such an offer - Appellant himself, long prior to sale, offered to pay Rs.5 lacs and odd in full quids - S. 29 of the Act did not exclude the application of principles of natural justice - It was not a strait jacket formula - It depend on facts in each case - Nothing prevented the Corporation to have given the appellant a chance for payment thereof at reasonable installments with interest thereon - No bona fide actions were took or attempted by Corporation - Thus acts smacked of bona fides or responsibility or reasonableness as an ordinary prudent businessman/trustee/owner acting in or dealing with such trust - Thus, sale of property was vitiated by unjust and unreasonable act on the part of Corporation or its officers or employees and was liable to be set aside - Possession given to respondent nos. 3 to 5 was illegal and immediately be resumed by Corporation - Appellant was not bound by sale or subsequent acts of purchasers/persons claiming through them - Appeal allowed.
