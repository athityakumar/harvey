 Offshore Holdings Private Limited v Bangalore Development Authority and others

Supreme Court of India

18 January 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, S.H. Kapadia, Mukundakam Sharma, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan, A.R. Dave

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 52; (2011) 3 SCC 139; 2011 (4) Bom.C.R. 212; 2011 (1) CLT(SC) 158; JT 2011 (1) SC 384; 2011 (3) MLJ(SC) 678; 2011(1) SCALE 533; [2011] 1 S.C.R. 453

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Land & Property

Keywords: Distribution Of Legislative Powers, Doctrine Of Pith And Substance, Constitutionality, Severability, Madras Prohibition Act, 1937, Constitution Act, Mysore Land Acquisition Act, 1894, City of Bangalore Improvement Act, 1945, Industrial Development and Regulation Act, 1951, U.P. Sugar Undertaking (Acquisition) Act, 1971

Summary: Constitution - Land & Property - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 246 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - Bangalore Development Authority Act, 1976 - (A) Whether Entry 42 in List III of Schedule VII denudes the power of the State Legislature to the extent that in an enactment within its legislative competence, it cannot incidentally refer/enact in regard to the subject matter falling in the Concurrent List? - It was contended that BDA Act is a law relatable exclusively to Entry 42 of List III of Schedule VII and is beyond the legislative competence of the State legislature - Held, very fact that the subject falls in the Concurrent List means that both the legislative constituents, i.e. the Parliament and the State legislatures, have legislative competence to legislate on that subject - Land Acquisition Act relates to Entry 42 of List III while the BDA Act is relatable to Entries 5 and 18 of List II of Schedule VII - State legislature is competent to enact such a law and it is referable to power and field contained in art. 246(2) of the Constitution read with Entries 5 and 18 of List II of Schedule VII - Such legislation may incidentally refer to Land Acquisition Act for attaining its own object - Hence, BDA Act is relatable to the Entries which squarely fall into a field assigned to the State legislature and, thus, would be a matter within the legislative competence of the State - For that matter State legislature is equally competent to enact a law even with relation to matters enumerated in List III provided it is not a covered field - BDA Act relates to planned development under the scheme and it has been enacted with that legislative object and intent - Further, an ancillary point thereto or reference to certain other provisions which will help in achieving the purpose of the State law, without really coming in conflict with the Central law, is a matter on which a State can enact according to the principle of incidental encroachment - Hence, contentions that the BDA Act is a law relatable exclusively to Entry 42 of List III of Schedule VII and is beyond the legislative competence of the State legislature not accepted - (B) Whether the BDA Act is a complete code in itself? - Held, BDA Act is a self-contained code with distinct and predominant purpose of carrying out planned development under the finalized schemes in accordance with the provisions of the Act - Further, acquisition of land is not its primary purpose but, acquisition of some land may become necessary to achieve its object which is to be specified at the outset of formation of schemes in terms of s. 16 of the BDA Act - Thus, acquisition of land is nothing but incidental to the main object of the State law - Hence, BDA Act has a self-contained scheme dealing with all the situations arising from the formation of the scheme for planned development to its execution - (C) Whether there is any contravention between the Land Acquisition Act and BDA Act? - Held, BDA Act is a self-contained scheme - To the limited extent of acquisition of land and payment of compensation, the provisions of the Land Acquisition Act would be applicable for the reason that they are neither in conflict with the State law nor do such provisions exist in that Act - Provisions of the Land Acquisition Act relating thereto would fit into the scheme of the BDA Act - Both the Acts, therefore, can co-exist and operate without conflict - There appears to be no direct conflict between the provisions of the Land Acquisition Act and the BDA Act - Both these laws cover different fields of legislation and do not relate to the same List - Further, acquisition being merely an incident of planned development, the Court will have to ignore it even if there was some encroachment or overlapping - (D) Whether there being no contravention between the two laws, they can be harmoniously applied and s. 11A of the Land Acquisition Act can be read into the BDA Act without disturbing its scheme? - Hence, provisions introduced in the Land Acquisition Act by Central Act, limited to the extent of acquisition of land, payment of compensation and recourse to legal remedies provided under the said Act, can be read into an acquisition controlled by the provisions of the BDA Act but with a specific exception that the provisions of the Land Acquisition Act in so far as they provide different time frames and consequences of default thereof, including lapsing of acquisition proceedings ,cannot be read into the BDA Act - S. 11A of the Land Acquisition Act being one of such provisions cannot be applied to the acquisitions under the provisions of the BDA Act - Reference answered.
