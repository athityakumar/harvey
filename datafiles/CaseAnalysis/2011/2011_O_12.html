Oriental Bank Of Commerce And Another v R.K. Uppal

Supreme Court of India

11 August 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Lodha, Aftab Alam

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 488; (2011) 8 SCC 695; 2011 (6) AWC 5681; 2011 (131) FLR 56; JT 2011 (9) SC 1; 2011 (4) LLJ 1; 2012 (1) MLJ(SC) 664; 2011(8) SCALE 634; [2011] 10 S.C.R. 218; 2011 (6) SLR 721

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Keywords: Imports and Exports (Control) Act, 1947, State Bank of India (Supervising Staff) Service Rules, Railway Servants (Discipline & Appeal) Rules, 1968, State Bank of Patiala (Officers) Service Regulations, 1979, SEEUY Scheme, Oriental Bank of Commerce Officer Employees (Discipline and Appeal) Regulations, 1982

Summary: Service - Oriental Bank of Commerce Officer Employees (Discipline and Appeal) Regulations, 1982, regns. 12, 17 - Disciplinary proceedings - Department appeal - Failure to grant opportunity of hearing - Respondent (delinquent) was served with an article of charges contained four charges - Delinquent submitted his reply and denied the charges - Inquiry authority held that charges 1 and 2 were partly proved while charge 3 and 4 were proved - Findings and report of the inquiring authority were sent to the delinquent who in response submitted his representation - Disciplinary authority concurred with the findings of the inquiring authority and keeping in view the seriousness of charges and gravity of the proved conduct, it imposed the penalty of dismissal - Delinquent preferred appeal u/regn. 17 assailing his dismissal order on diverse grounds and also requested for grant of personal hearing - Appellate authority rejected the delinquent's request for personal hearing and dismissed his appeal - Delinquent challenged the order of penalty and also the order of the appellate authority before the HC - HC allowed the delinquent's writ petition partly and set aside the order of the appellate authority and remitted the matter back to it with a direction to pass a reasoned order after giving an opportunity of hearing to the petitioner - (A) Whether in terms of regn. 17 of the Regulations, the appellate authority was required to accord personal hearing to the respondent in a departmental appeal - Held, regn. 17 contemplated a provision afforded to an employee right of appeal against an order imposing upon him any of the penalties specified in regn. 4 or against the order of suspension referred to in regn. 12 - As per regn. 17, the appeal should be addressed to the appellate authority and submitted to the authority whose order was appealed against - Authority whose order was appealed against was required to forward the appeal together with its comments and also the record of the case to the appellate authority - Appellate authority then proceeded with the consideration of the appeal and considered whether the findings were justified; whether the penalty was excessive or inadequate and passed appropriate order confirming, enhancing, reducing or setting aside the penalty or remitting the case to the authority that imposed the penalty or to any other authority with such direction as it may deem fit in the circumstances of the case - Appeal provision in regn. 17 of the Regulations did not expressly provide for personal hearing to the appellant - In the absence of personal hearing to the appellant, it could not be said that the very right of appeal was defeated - (B) Whether the order passed by the appellate authority in the appeal preferred by the respondent delinquent u/regn.17 suffered from infirmity for want of reasons - Held, appellate authority's order should not be labelled as a non-speaking order and it did not suffer from the vice of non- application of mind - Appellate authority had addressed the points raised in the appeal and critical to the decision, albeit briefly - It was true that the appellate authority should record the reasons in support of its order to indicate that it had applied its mind to the grounds raised but it was not the requirement of law that an order of affirmation by the appellate authority should be elaborate and extensive - Brief reasons which indicated due application of mind in decision making process may suffice - In the instant case, appellate authority concurred with the view of the disciplinary authority and found no justification to interfere with the penalty awarded by the disciplinary authority - Order of the appellate authority, by no stretch of imagination could be said to suffer from the vice of lack of reasons - Impugned HC order was set aside - Appeal allowed.
