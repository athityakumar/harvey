Municipal Corporation Of Delhi, Delhi v Association Of Victims Of Uphaar Tragedy And Others

Supreme Court of India

13 October 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

R.V. Raveendran, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 746; (2011) 14 SCC 481; 2011 (4) ACC 382; 2012 ACJ 48; AIR 2012 SC 100; 2012 (2) AWC 1731; 2011 (4) CLT(SC) 204; 2011 (4) CPJ(Sc) 74; 2011 (8) MLJ(SC) 513; 2012 (165) PLR 672; 2012 (3) RCR(Civil) 203; [2011] 16 S.C.R. 1

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Human Rights; Media & Entertainment; Tort

Keywords: Human Rights Act, 1998, Delhi Cinematography Rules, 1981, Electricity Rules, Delhi Cinematograph Rules, 1953, Cinematograph Rules, Disaster Management Act, 2005, Prisons Act, 1952, Cinematograph Amendment Rules, 1994, Prison Rules, 1964

Summary: Constitution - Human Rights - Media & Entertainment - Tort - Disaster Management Act, 2005, s. 1(3) - Cinematograph Act, 1952 - Co-operative Societies Act, 1912 - Human Rights Act, 1998, s. 6 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 21, 32, 226 - Prisons Act, 1952 - Electricity Act, 2003 - Companies Act, 1956 - Cinematograph Amendment Rules, 1994 - Cinematograph Rules - Delhi Cinematograph Rules, 1953 - Delhi Cinematography Rules, 1981 - Electricity Rules - Prison Rules, 1964 - Fire - Liability - Compensation - Challenged - Fire at Cinema Theatre resulted in death and injury to patrons - Respondent no. 1, association of victims filed petition before HC and sought adequate compensation for victims of tragedy and punitive damages against theatre owner, Delhi Vidyut Board (DVB), Municipal Corporation of Delhi (MCD), Fire Force and Licensing Authority for showing callous disregard to statutory obligations and to fundamental and indefeasible rights guaranteed u/art. 21 of Constitution, of theatre going public, in failing to provide safe premises, free from reasonably foreseeable hazards - HC allowed petition and held theatre owner, DVB, MCD and Licensing Authority were responsible for fire tragedy - HC exonerated Delhi Fire Force - DVB accepted judgment - Theatre owner, Delhi Police and MCD denied any liability - Licensing Authority contended that theatre owners should be made liable for payment of entire compensation - Theatre owner contended that fixed rates of compensation and their share of liability were excessive - Hence, instant appeal - (A) Whether MCD and Licensing Authority could be made liable to pay compensation to victims and what should be apportionment of liability - Held, Licensing Authority and MCD discharged their statutory functions by granting licence and submitting inspection report or issuing no-objection certificate - Merely on ground that Licensing Authority and MCD could have performed their duties better or more efficiently, they could not be made liable to pay compensation to victims of tragedy - There was no close or direct proximity to acts of Licensing Authority and MCD and fire accident and death/injuries of victims - Therefore, HC committed serious error in making licensing authority and MCD liable to pay compensation to victims jointly and severally - Relevant part of order of HC was set aside - (B) Whether compensation awarded was excessive and was award of punitive damages against theatre owner justified - Held, what could be awarded as compensation was not only restricted to nominal palliative amount, but could be by way of making monetary amounts for wrong done or by way of exemplary damages, exclusive of any amount recoverable in civil action based on tortuous liability - But while awarding compensation to large group of persons, it would be unsafe to use high income as determinative factor - Thus, uniform amount was awarded keeping in view principles relating to award of compensation in public law remedy cases reserving liberty to legal heirs of deceased victims to claim additional amount wherever they were not satisfied with amount awarded - Any increase was to be borne by theatre owner exclusively - As far as injured were concerned if they were not satisfied with sum awarded it was open to them to approach Civil Court for claiming higher compensation - In regard to punitive damages awarded, it was pointed out that theatre owner had installed additional seats illegally which caused death and injuries as they slowed down exiting - Gravity of breach required deterrent to prevent similar further breaches - Therefore, theatre owner was liable to pay compensation for death and injuries and was also denied profits/benefits out of illegal acts which were in utter disregard of safety of patrons of theatre - Liability of theatre owner to return and reimburse profits from illegally installed seats was upheld, but reduced - Appeals disposed of.

Obiter dictum

Constitution - Tort - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 21, 32, 226 - Powers of Constitutional Courts - Violation of statutory provisions - Liability - Compensation - Punitive damages - (Obiter) In case where Right to life and personal liberty guaranteed u/art. 21 of Constitution was violated absence of any statutory provision for compensation in Statute was of no consequence in awarding compensation - Constitutional Court could award monetary compensation against State and its officials for its failure to safeguard fundamental rights of citizens but there was no system or method to measure damages caused in such situations - Compensatory damages were intended to provide claimant with a monetary amount necessary to recoup/replace what was lost, since damages in tort were generally awarded to place claimant in position he would have been in, had tort not taken place which were generally quantified under heads of general damages and special damages - In addition, Constitutional Courts' shall strive to deter future constitutional misconduct without proper excuse or with some collateral or improper motive - For this, Constitutional courts could in appropriate cases of serious violation of life and liberty of individuals award punitive damages - However, it generally required presence of malicious intent on side of wrong doer - Several factors may gauge on constitutional court in determining punitive damages such as contumacious conduct of wrong doer, nature of statute, gravity of fault committed, circumstances etc - Punitive damages could be awarded when wrongdoers' conduct 'shocks conscience' or is 'outrageous' or there is a willful and 'wanton disregard' for safety requirements - Normally, there should be direct connection between wrongdoer's conduct and victim's injury - Need for a comprehensive legislation dealing with tortious liability of State and its instrumentalities were highlighted on various occasions - Unfortunately, no legislation had been enacted to deal with such situations - It was hoped and trusted that utmost attention would be given by legislature for bringing in appropriate legislation to deal with claims in Public Law for violation of fundamental rights, guaranteed to citizens at hands of State and its officials - Appeals disposed of.
