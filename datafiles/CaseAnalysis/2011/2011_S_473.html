Mangluram Dewangan v Surendra Singh & Ors

Supreme Court of India

04 July 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

R.V. Raveendran, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 413; (2011) 12 SCC 773; 2011(5) ALL MR 365; 2011 (88) ALR 215; 2011 (5) AWC 5088; 2011 (6) CTC 214; JT 2011 (12) SC 6; 2011 (8) MLJ(SC) 100; 2011 (3) RCR(Civil) 879; 2011 (114) RD 276; 2011(7) SCALE 60; [2011] 8 S.C.R. 129; 2012 (1) WLN(SC) 40

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Summary: Civil Procedure - Practice & Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, ss. 96, 104, O. 22 r. 3 - Order dismissing impleadment petition - Appeal against - Maintainability - Original plaintiff filed a suit for declaration, possession and damages against defendant respondents, however, he died during the pendency of suit - Appellant (third party) filed an impleadment petition under O. 22 r. 3 CPC substituting him as legal representative of the original plaintiff on the ground that latter executed a Will in his favour - Respondent defendants contested appellant's application - Trial Court dismissed appellant's application and consequently, in the absence of any legal heir of the original plaintiff, dismissed the suit - Appellant filed appeal - First Appellate Court reversed the Trial Court order holding that appellant had proved the Will - First Appellate Court permitted the appellant to be brought on record and remanded the matter to Trial Court for fresh disposal - Respondents filed appeal - HC reversed the First Appellate Court holding that the Trial Court order did not amount to a decree and therefore, appellant's appeal before the First Appellate Court was not maintainable - HC further held that the remedy available to the appellant was to file a revision and not appeal - Whether an order of the Trial Court rejecting an application filed under O. 22 r. 3 of the Code, by a person claiming to be the legatee under the Will of the plaintiff and consequently dismissing the suit in the absence of any legal heir, was an appealable decree - Held, s. 2(2) CPC contemplated three essential requirements to be fulfilled for an order to be treated as a 'decree': (i) there should be an adjudication in a suit; (ii) the adjudication should result in a formal expression which was conclusive so far as the court expressing it; (iii) the adjudication should determine the rights of parties with regard to all or any of the matters in controversy in the suit; and (iv) the adjudication should be one from which an appeal did not lie as an appeal from an order (u/s. 104 and O. 43 r. 1 of the Code) nor should it be an order dismissing the suit for default - Trial Court order in the instant case complied with all the requirements specified in s. 2(2) of the Code - Applicant in an application under O. 22 r. 3 was not a party to the suit - An application under O. 22 r. 3 was by a non- party requesting the court to make him a party as the legal representative of the deceased plaintiff - Necessarily unless the applicant in the application under O. 22 r. 3 allowed and the applicant was permitted to come on record as the legal representative of the deceased, he would continue to be a non-party to the suit - When such an application by a non-party was dismissed after a determination of the question whether he was a legal representative of the deceased plaintiff, there was no adjudication determining the rights of parties to the suit with regard to all or any of the matters in controversy in the suit - It was determination of a collateral issue as to whether the applicant, who was not a party, should be permitted to come on record as the legal representative of the deceased - Hence an order dismissing an application under O. 22 r. 3 after an enquiry u/r. 5 and consequently dismissing the suit, was not a decree - Trial Court order in the instant case was neither a 'decree' appealable u/s. 96 of the Code nor an order appealable u/s. 104 and O. 43 r. 1, the remedy of the applicant under O. 22 r. 3, was to file a revision - HC was therefore, right in its view that the adjudication of the question whether an applicant in an application under O. 22 r. 3 was a legatee under a valid Will executed by the deceased plaintiff in his favour, was not a decree and therefore the remedy of the applicant was to file a revision - (B) Whether the HC was justified in upholding the decision of the Trial Court that the Will was not proved and rejecting the application under O. 22 r. 3 of the Code - Held, First Appellate Court concluded that the evidence of the attesting witness when read with statement/admission of the deceased plaintiff himself, established due execution of the Will and that the appellant was the legatee under the Will of plaintiff - Thus, the First Appellate Court had given cogent reasons for accepting the appellant to be the legal representative of the deceased plaintiff, in pursuance of the Will - HC, after holding that the appeal filed by appellant u/s. 96 of the Code before the First Appellate Court was not maintainable, should not have proceeded to consider the matter on merits - However, HC chose to examine the merits of the matter, in a brief and casual manner and held that the finding of the Trial Court was preferable and finding of the First Appellate Court was erroneous - HC failed to consider all the facts and circumstances considered by the First Appellate Court - Having held that the First Appellate Court could not have entertained the appeal, the HC was not required to examine the matter on merits and If it chose to do so, it ought to have done in thoroughly, which it did not - Finding of the HC that the Will was not proved and therefore, the appellant was not a legal representative was set aside - However, finding of the HC that the Trial Court order, was not appealable was upheld - As a consequence, it would be open to the appellant to challenge the Trial Court order in a revision petition before the HC - Appeal partly allowed.
