Pankaj Mahajan v Dimple @ Kajal

Supreme Court of India

30 September 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Sathasivam, Balbir Singh Chauhan

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 836; (2011) 12 SCC 1; 2012 (1) AWC 983; 2012 (3) CTC 75; 2012 (1) MLJ(SC) 146; 2011 (4) RCR(Civil) 534; [2011] 14 S.C.R. 945

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal

Summary: Family & Personal - Hindu Marriage Act, 1955, s. 13 - Application for divorce by husband - Sustainability - Appeal was directed against the final judgment and order passed by the HC whereby HC allowed the appeal filed by the respondent and set aside the judgment and decree passed by Trial Court - Whether application for divorce filed by husband could be allowed - Held, it was well settled that giving repeated threats to commit suicide amounted to cruelty - When such thing was repeated in the form of sign or gesture, no spouse could live peacefully - In the case on hand, appellant/husband had placed adequate materials to show that the respondent/wife used to give repeated threats to commit suicide and once even tried to commit suicide by jumping from the terrace - Cruelty postulated treatment of spouse with such cruelty as to create reasonable apprehension in his mind that it would be harmful or injurious for him to live with the other party - Acts of the respondent/wife were of such quality or magnitude and consequence as to cause pain, agony and suffering to the appellant/husband which amounted to cruelty in matrimonial law - From the pleadings and evidence, the following instances of cruelty were specifically pleaded and stated - They were

i. Giving repeated threats to commit suicide and even trying to commit suicide on one occasion by jumping from the terrace - ii. Pushing the appellant from the staircase resulting into fracture of his right forearm - iii. Slapping the appellant and assaulting him - iv. Misbehaving with the colleagues and relatives of the appellant causing humiliation and embarrassment to him - v. Not attending to household chores and not even making food for the appellant, leaving him to fend for himself - vi. Not taking care of the baby - vii. Insulting the parents of the appellant and misbehaving with them - viii. Forcing the appellant to live separately from his parents - ix. Causing nuisance to the landlord's family of the appellant, causing the said landlord to force the appellant to vacate the premises - x. Repeated fits of insanity, abnormal behaviour causing great mental tension to the appellant - xi. Always quarreling with the appellant and abusing him - xii. Always behaving in abnormal manner and doing weird acts causing great mental cruelty to the appellant - All these factual details culled out from the pleadings and evidence of both the parties clearly showed the conduct of the respondent/wife towards the appellant-husband - With these acceptable facts and details, it could not be concluded that the appellant/husband could not make out case of cruelty at the hands of respondent/wife - SC was satisfied that the appellant/husband had placed ample evidence on record that the respondent/wife was suffering from mental disorder and due to her acts and conduct, she caused grave mental cruelty to him and it was not possible for the parties to live with each other, therefore, decree of divorce deserved to be granted in favour of the appellant/husband - In addition to the same, it was also brought to SC's notice that because of the abovementioned reasons, both appellant/husband and the respondent/wife were living separately for the last more than nine years - There was no possibility to unite the chain of marital life between appellant/husband and the respondent/wife - Impugned order of HC resulted in grave miscarriage of justice to the appellant/husband, more particularly, the HC failed to consider the relevant material aspects from the pleadings and the evidence, ultimate conclusion could not be sustained - Appellant/husband established and proved both grounds in terms of s. 13 of the Act - Appeal allowed.
