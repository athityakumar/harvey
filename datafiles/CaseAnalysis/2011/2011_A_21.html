Rajeev Hitendra Pathak and another v Achyut Kashinath Karekar and another

Supreme Court of India

19 August 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Dalveer Bhandari, Mukundakam Sharma, A.R. Dave

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 546; (2011) 9 SCC 541; 2012 (2) ALD(SC) 146; 2011(5) ALL MR 951; 2011 (89) ALR 267; 2011 (5) AWC 5302; 2012 (1) Bom.C.R. 160; 2011 (4) CPJ(Sc) 35; JT 2011 (9) SC 407; 2012 (2) MahLJ 1; 2012 (1) MLJ(SC) 684; 2012(1) M.P.H.T. 1; 2012 (1) MPLJ 494; 2011 (2) OLR 942; 2011 (164) PLR 274; 2011 (4) RCR(Civil) 175; 2011(9) SCALE 287; [2011] 10 S.C.R. 513

Case Digest

Subject: Consumer Protection; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Consumer Protection - Practice & Procedure - Consumer Protection Act, 1986, ss. 22, 22A - Ex parte order - Power to recall - Scope - Deceased died due to post-surgery complications - Respondents (legal heirs of deceased) filed complaint before the State Consumer Commission alleging deficiency of service by appellant doctor - State Commission dismissed complaint for want of prosecution - Respondent complainants filed an application for recalling the order of dismissal - State Commission allowed the application and restored the complaint - Appellant challenged the restoration order before National Consumer Commission - National Commission dismissed appellant's revision - Hence the instant appeal - Whether the District Consumer Forums and the State Commissions had the power to set aside their own ex parte orders or in other words had the power to recall or review their own orders - Held, Tribunals were creatures of the Statute and derived their power from the express provisions of the Statute - District Forums and the State Commissions had not been given any power to set aside ex parte orders and power of review and the powers which had not been expressly given by the Statute should not be exercised - Legislature chose to give the National Commission power to review its ex parte orders - Before the amendment of the Act in the year 2002, against dismissal of any case by the Commission, the consumer had to rush to SC - Amendment in s. 22 and introduction of s. 22-A were done for the convenience of the consumers - After the amendment in s. 22 and introduction of s. 22A by which the power of review or recall had vested with the National Commission only and not extended to the District Forums or the State Commissions - SC decision in Jyotsana Arvind Kumar Shah & Others v. Bombay Hospital Trust, 1999 INDLAW SC 933, laid down the correct law and the view taken in the later decision of SC in New India Assurance Co. Ltd. v. R. Srinivasan, 2000 INDLAW SC 183, was untenable and could not be sustained - Hence, findings of the National Commission were set aside as far as it had held that the State Commission could review its own orders - Appeals disposed of.
