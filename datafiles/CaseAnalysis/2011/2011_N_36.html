National Textile Corporation Limited v Nareshkumar Badrikumar Jagad and others

Supreme Court of India

05 September 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 561; (2011) 12 SCC 695; 2012 (1) Bom.C.R. 399; JT 2011 (10) SC 414; 2012 (2) MLJ(SC) 296; 2011 (2) RCR(Rent) 293; 2011(10) SCALE 28; [2011] 14 S.C.R. 472

Case Digest

Subject: Practice & Procedure; Rent Control

Keywords: Food Corporations Act, 1964, Textile Undertakings (Nationalisation) Act, 1995, Textile Undertakings (Taking Over of Management) Act, 1983

Summary: Rent Control - Practice & Procedure - Textile Undertakings (Nationalisation) Act, 1995 - Bombay Rents, Hotel and Lodging House Rates Control Act, 1947 - Maharashtra Rent Control Act, 1999, s. 3(1)(a), 3(1)(b) - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 8 r. 2 - Tenant taken over by the Government - Termination of tenancy - Effect - Respondent trust executed a lease in favour of appellant Textile Corporation - Appellant textile was taken over by the Central Government by the virtue of the provisions of the 1995 Act - After the expiry of lease, respondent trust send a legal notice terminating its tenancy qua the suit premises - Since appellant failed to vacate the premises, respondent trust filed an eviction suit against the appellant under the provisions of the 1947 Act, however, 1947 Act stood repealed by the 1999 Act in the meantime - Respondent trust after withdrawal of the suit filed under the 1947 Act, filed a fresh suit in the Small Causes Court under 1995 Act seeking eviction of appellant and for a decree of mesne profits - Suit was decreed in favour of the respondents by which the appellant was directed to hand over vacant and peaceful possession of the suit premises to the respondents within four months - Appellant filed appeal before the DB of Small Causes Court - DB of Small Causes Court affirmed the Trial Court order - Appellant preferred civil revision before the HC, which had been dismissed by the HC vide impugned judgment and order - Appellant contended that, after the advent of 1995 Act, appellant became the statutory tenant and appellant had stepped into the shoes of the Central Government merely as an agent, thus, the Central Government remained the tenant, therefore, Central Government continued to be a tenant in the suit premises and thus, would be protected in terms of s. 3(1) (a) of the Act 1999 being premises let out to the Government - Held, appellant was neither the government nor the department of the government, but a Government Company - Appellant should not identify itself with the Central Government - Appellant's contention was that the appellant was merely an agent of the Central Government was not worth consideration at all for the simple reason that rights vested in the appellant stood crystallised after being transferred by the Central Government - Appellant was being controlled by the provisions of the 1995 Act and not by the Central Government, however, an agent was merely an extended hand of the principal and cannot claim independent rights - It was not permissible for the appellant to canvass that the Central Government had any concern so far as the tenancy rights were concerned - Right vested in the Central Government stood transferred and vested in the appellant, however, both were separate legal entities and were not synonymous - Appellant being neither the government nor government department could not agitate that as it had been substituted in place of the Central Government, and acted merely as an agent of the Central Government, thus protection of the Act 1999 was available to it - Further, appellant had not taken plea of agent before either of the courts below - In view of the provisions of O. 8 r. 2 CPC, the appellant was under an obligation to take a specific plea to show that the suit was not maintainable which it failed to do so - Vague plea to the extent that the suit was bad for non-joinder and, thus, was not maintainable, did not meet the requirement of law - Appellant ought to have taken a plea in the written statement that it was merely an 'agent' of the Central Government, thus the suit against it was not maintainable - Appellant miserably failed to take the required pleadings for the purpose - Hence, appellant was not entitled for exemption u/s. 3(1)(a) or 3(1)(b) of the 1999 Act nor can it claim the status of an 'agent' of the Central Government - Facts and circumstances of the case did not warrant review of the impugned judgment - Appeal dismissed.
