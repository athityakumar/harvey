Vishram Singh Raghubanshi v State Of U.P.

Supreme Court of India

15 June 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, Swatanter Kumar

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 386; (2011) 7 SCC 776; AIR 2011 SC 2275; 2011 ALL MR (Cri) 2328; JT 2011 (6) SC 629; 2012 (1) MLJ(Crl) 419; 2011 (3) RCR(Criminal) 716; 2011(6) SCALE 534; [2011] 8 S.C.R. 105

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Bar Council of India Rules, 1975

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Practice & Procedure - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971, s. 15 - Contempt of court - Tender of apology - Acceptance - Appellant advocate allegedly misbehaved with District Judge and used abusive language - District Judge made a reference to HC for initiating contempt proceedings against him u/s. 15 of the Act - HC considered the matter and issued show cause notice to appellant - Appellant tendered an apology in the form of an affidavit, however, HC held appellant guilty of committing contempt and sentence him to imprisonment - Whether appellant was held to be punished for contempt of court - Held, appellant was given full opportunity to defend himself or lead evidence in support of his case, however, he had not chosen to defend himself on merit before the HC, rather he merely tendered apology thrice - Superior courts had a duty to protect the reputation of judicial officers of subordinate courts, taking note of the growing tendency of maligning the reputation of judicial officers by unscrupulous practising advocates who either failed to secure desired orders or did not succeed in browbeating for achieving ulterior purpose - Any violation of the principles of professional ethics by an Advocate was unfortunate and unacceptable - Further, an apology should not be a defence, a justification, or an appropriate punishment for an act which was in contempt of court - Apology could not be accepted in case it was hollow; there was no remorse; no regret; no repentance, or if it was only a device to escape the rigour of the law - Use of insulting language did not absolve the contemnor on any count whatsoever - If the words were calculated and clearly intended to cause any insult, an apology if tendered and lack penitence, regret or contrition, did not deserve to be accepted - In the instant case, appellant had tendered the apology only after receiving the show cause notice from the HC as to why the proceedings for criminal contempt be not initiated against him - HC considered the case elaborately examining every issue microscopically and held that there was no reason to disbelieve the facts stated by the judicial officer against the contemnor/appellant, the facts were acceptable, and it was clearly proved that the contemnor was guilty of gross criminal contempt - Appellant intimidated the presiding officer of the court hurling filthiest abuses and lowered the authority of the Court, which was tantamount to interfere with the due course of judicial proceedings - Charge which stood proved against the appellant could not be taken lightly and in such a fact-situation the apology tendered by him, being not bona fide, was not acceptable - Apology had been tendered under pressure only after framing of the charges by the HC in the criminal contempt when appellant realised that he could be punished - Apology was not tendered at the earliest opportunity, rather tendered belatedly just to escape the punishment for the grossest criminal contempt committed by him - Language used by the Advocate for a judicial officer where he practiced regularly and earned his livelihood was such that any apology would fall short to meet the requirement of the statutory provisions - No repent or remorse was there on the part of the appellant at an initial stage - Had it been so, instead of making grossest and scandalous allegations against the judicial officer, writing complaint against him to the Administrative Judge in the HC, the appellant could have gone to the concerned judicial officer and tendered apology in open court - Appellant instead of yielding to the court honestly and unconditionally, advanced a well guarded defence by referring to all the facts that led to the incident - Apology tendered by the appellant gave an impression that the same was in the alternative and not a complete surrender before the law and such attitude had a direct impact on the court's independence, dignity and decorum - In order to protect the administration of public justice, appellant's conduct and utterances should not be ignored or pardoned and he had no business to overawe the court - Apology tendered by the appellant had neither been sincere nor bona fide and thus, not worth acceptance - Appeal dismissed.
