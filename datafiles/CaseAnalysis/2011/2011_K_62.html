Rangammal v Kuppuswami & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

13 May 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Gyan Sudha Misra, J.M. Panchal

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 351; (2011) 12 SCC 220; AIR 2011 SC 2344; 2011 (5) ALD(SC) 38; 2011 (87) ALR 246; 2011 (5) AWC 4692; 2011 (4) Bom.C.R. 32; 2011 (2) CLT(SC) 346; 2011 (4) CTC 769; JT 2011 (6) SC 457; 2011 (5) MLJ(SC) 903; 2011 (2) OLR 278; 2011 (4) RCR(Civil) 251; 2011 (113) RD 744; 2011(6) SCALE 161; [2011] 6 S.C.R. 835

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Summary: Civil Procedure - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 101 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Suit for partition was filed by plaintiff-respondent No. 1 against his brother for partition and separate possession, but the plaintiff-respondent No. 1 also included the property of the appellant in the schedule to the plaint without including her as a party to the suit - It was pleaded by the plaintiff-respondent No. 1 that the share which originally belonged to the appellant was transferred to their predecessors, by way of a sale deed dated 24.2.1951 executed in their favour by 'K' the legal guardian of the appellant when the appellant was a minor - Sale deed was claimed to have been executed for legal necessity in order to discharge the debt of the deceased mother of the appellant in the year 1951 which according to the case of the plaintiff-respondent No. 1 had been transferred to their branch by virtue of the aforesaid sale deed executed by the guardian of the appellant - Subsequently, appellant was impleaded as second defendant - Suit was finally decreed in favour of plaintiff-respondent No. 1 - First appeal filed thereagainst was dismissed - On second appeal, HC cast the burden completely on the appellant to prove that the property shown in the sale deed which fell into the share of the appellant, was not for the purpose of discharge of the liability of her deceased mother who according to her case was not owing any debt to anyone including 'K' - Second appeal was dismissed - Instant appeal - (A) Whether in a partition suit filed by the plaintiff-respondent No. 1, the courts below could shift the burden of proof on the appellant regarding the validity of a sale deed, which was executed when the appellant was a minor, contrary to the pleading in the plaint filed in a suit for partition, who claimed title to the suit land on the basis of the alleged sale deed? - Held, Evidence Act clearly laid down that the burden of proving fact always lies upon the person who asserts - Until such burden was discharged, the other party was not required to be called upon to prove his case - In the instant case, when the plaintiff-respondent No. 1 pleaded that the disputed property fell into the share of the plaintiff-respondent No. 1, then it was clearly for the plaintiff-respondent No. 1 to prove that it was executed for legal necessity of the appellant while she was a minor - Courts below thus have illegally and erroneously failed not to cast this burden on the plaintiff-respondent No. 1 by clearly misconstruing the whole case and thus resulted into recording of findings which were perverse and even against the admitted case of the parties - (B) Whether the question of limitation could arise against the appellant shifting the burden on her to challenge the sale deed, when the story of execution of the alleged sale deed was set up by the plaintiff-respondent No. 1 in the plaint for the first time when he filed partition suit against his brother, without impleading the appellant, but claimed benefit of title to the suit land on the basis of the alleged sale deed? - Held, if there was a dispute about the genuineness and veracity of the sale deed and the appellant was in occupation of her share, then it was the plaintiff-respondent No. 1 who should have filed a suit claiming title on the basis of the sale deed which was claimed to have been executed in their favour by the de facto guardian of appellant when she was a minor before this property could be included in the suit for partition between the brothers excluding the appellant and the consequence of not doing so or delay in this regard, obviously will have to be attributed to the plaintiff-respondent No. 1 - HC erred when it observed that the suit was barred by limitation as it had been filed after 31 years of the execution of the sale deed which was factually incorrect - Judgment and order of the HC partly set aside in so far as the share of the appellant was concerned and consequently the decree passed by the Trial Court, upheld by the first appellate court and the HC which had been illegally decreed including the share of the appellant which had not devolved on the family of the plaintiff-respondent No. 1 and respondent No.2, but was claimed on the basis of a sale deed which could not be proved either by evidence or law, was fit to be set aside - Decree in so far as partition between plaintiff-respondent No. 1 and respondent No.2 was concerned, would remain intact but the said decree would exclude the property which had fallen into the share of appellant but was claimed to have been transferred to the branch of the plaintiff-respondent No. 1 - Appeal allowed.
