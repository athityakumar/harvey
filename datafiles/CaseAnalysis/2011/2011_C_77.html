Commissioner of Cental Excise, Belapur, Mumbai v RDC Concrete (India) Private Limited

Supreme Court of India

09 August 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Anil R. Dave, Mukundakam Sharma

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 497; (2011) 12 SCC 166; 2011 (270) E.L.T. 625; JT 2011 (9) SC 114; 2012 (5) RCR(Civil) 848; 2011(8) SCALE 314; [2011] 9 S.C.R. 982

Case Digest

Subject: Excise; Indirect Tax

Summary: Excise - Indirect Tax - Central Excise Act, 1944, s. 35C(2) - Jurisdiction - Rectification of mistake - Petitioner filed appeal against order of the Tribunal submitting that under the grad of rectification, the tribunal had modified its order dt. 4.11.2008 in such a way as if the respondent had filed an appeal against the said order and the tribunal had virtually allowed the appeal against its own order - Petitioner further submitted that by virtue of the final order, the Tribunal had upheld the demand of duty of Rs.90,89,480.56 together with interest and equivalent penalty of Rs.90,89,480.56 but the order imposing penalty of Rs.25,00,000/- had been set aside - However, in pursuance of the application submitted by the respondent for rectification, the Tribunal modified the original final order to such an extent that the entire demand of duty had been quashed and set aside and as a consequence thereof the penalty imposed upon the respondent had also been set aside - Whether appeal filed by the petitioner could be allowed and order of the tribunal dt. 23.11.2009 rectifying its earlier order could be quashed? - Held, SC in T.S. Balram vs. Volkart Brothers 1971 INDLAW SC 190, held that a 'mistake apparent from the record' cannot be something which can be established by a long drawn process of reasoning on points on which there may conceivably be two opinions - It had been also held that a decision on a debatable point of law cannot be a mistake apparent from the record - In the instant case, upon perusal of both the orders viz. earlier order dt. 4.11.2008 and order dt. 23.11.2009 passed in pursuance of the rectification application, Tribunal exceeded its powers given to it under the provisions of s. 35C(2) of the Act and tried to re-appreciate the evidence and it reconsidered its legal view taken earlier in pursuance of a rectification application - The Tribunal could not have done so while exercising its powers u/s. 35C(2) of the Act, and, therefore, the impugned order passed in pursuance of the rectification application was bad in law - Impugned order passed by the tribunal set aside - Appeal allowed.
