Bhajan Singh and others v State Of Haryana

Supreme Court of India

04 July 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, Swatanter Kumar

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 524; (2011) 7 SCC 421; AIR 2011 SC 2552; JT 2011 (7) SC 141; 2011 (4) MLJ(Crl) 1035; 2011 (3) RCR(Criminal) 641; 2011(6) SCALE 630; [2011] 7 S.C.R. 1

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 149, 302 and 307 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellants/accused allegedly caused death of 2 deceased - Trial Court found accused guilty and convicted u/ss. 302, 307 IPC - Appellants/accused filed appeal before DB - DB upheld conviction of appellants/accused and reversed sentence of remaining accused - Hence, instant appeals - Appellants/accused contended that there was delay in lodging FIR and sending copy of it to Court - DB had place a very heavy reliance on evidence of PWs.9 and 10 and no independent witness was examined - DB committed error in interfering with order of acquittal as far as 3 appellants were concerned - Whether HC was justified in reversing Trial Court order of acquittal in to conviction of appellants - Held, delay in dispatch of FIR by itself was not a circumstance which could throw out prosecution's case in its entirely, particularly when prosecution furnishes a cogent explanation for delay in dispatch of report or prosecution case itself was proved by leading unimpeachable evidence - Thus, there was no delay in lodging FIR or in sending copy of same to Magistrate - It was settled legal proposition that while appreciating evidence of a witness, minor discrepancies on trivial matters, which did not affect core of prosecution's case would not prompt Court to reject evidence in its entirely - Thus , no major contradiction either in evidence of witness or any conflict in medical or ocular evidence was found in instant case, which could tilt balance in favour of appellants - There had been minor improvement, embellishment etc., which remain insignificant and had to be ignored - With regard to interference with order of acquittal was concerned, it was permissible only in 'exceptional circumstances' for 'compelling reasons' - Appellate Court was entitled to consider whether in arriving at finding of fact, Trial Court had failed to take into consideration admissible evidence and/or had taken into consideration evidence brought on record contrary to law - Similarly, wrong placing of burden of proof would also be subject matter of scrutiny by Appellate Court - Thus, where it was possible to take only one view i.e. prosecution points to guilt of accused and judgments was on face of it perverse, Appellate Court could interfere with order of acquittal - In instant case, DB had correctly concluded that judgment of Trial Court was perverse and which was a clear cut case of common object - Appeals dismissed.
