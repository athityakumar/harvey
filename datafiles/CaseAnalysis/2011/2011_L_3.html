Lafarge Umiam Mining Private Limited and another v Union Of India And Others

Supreme Court of India

06 July 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

S.H. Kapadia, Aftab Alam, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 430; (2011) 7 SCC 338; AIR 2011 SC 2781; JT 2011 (7) SC 346; 2011(7) SCALE 242; [2011] 7 S.C.R. 954

Case Digest

Subject: Environment, Wildlife & Animal; Mines & Minerals

Keywords: Forest (Conservation) Rules, 1981, United Khasi - Jaintia Hills Autonomous District (Management and Control of Forests) Act, 1958, United Khasi-Jaintia Hills Autonomous District (Management and Control of Forests) Act, 1985, Forest Conservation Rules, 2003

Summary: Environment, Wildlife & Animal - Mines & Minerals - National Forest Policy, 1988 - Environment (Protection) Act, 1986, ss. 3(1), 3(2)(v), 3(3) - Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980, ss. 2, 3 - Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, 1957, s. 5(1) - Environment (Protection) Rules, 1986, r. 5(3)(d) - Forest (conservation) Rules, 1981 - Mining of lime - Notification - Amendment - Forest Conservation - Clearance certificate - LSCL had set up a cross-border cement manufacturing project, which inter-alia has a captive limestone mine of 100Ha - Mine was leased out in favour of LUMPL - Entire produce of the said mine was used for production of cement at the manufacturing plant under the agreement/arrangement between Govt. of India and Govt. of Bangladesh - 1988 Policy, stood enunciated pursuant to Resolution dt. 12-5-1952 of GOI to be followed in the management of State Forests in India - Said Policy stood enunciated because over the years forests in India had suffered serious depletion due to relentless pressures arising from ever increasing demand for fuel wood, fodder and timber; inadequacy of protection measures; diversion of forest lands to non-forest uses without ensuring compensatory afforestation and essential environmental safeguards; and the tendency to look upon forests as revenue earning resource - Thus, there was a need to review the situation and to evolve, for the future, a strategy of forest conservation including preservation, maintenance, sustainable utilisation, restoration and enhancement of the natural environment - It was need which led to the enunciation of 1988 Policy - Principal aim of the Policy was to ensure environmental stability and maintenance of ecological balance - In exercise of the powers conferred by s. 3(1) r/w s. 3(2)(v) of the 1986 Act r/w r. 5(3)(d) 1986 Rules the Central Govt. issued Environmental Impact Assessment Notification whereby it directed that on and from the date of publication of the said Notification in the official gazette expansion or modernization of any activity or a new project listed in Schedule-I shall not be undertaken in India unless it has been accorded environmental clearance by the Central Govt. in accordance with the procedure specified in the Notification - Thus, site clearance would be granted for a sanctioned capacity and should be valid for five years for commencing construction, operation or mining -

LMMPL made an application for granting environmental clearance for limestone mining project - Application was made under EIA Notification, 1994 - However, the said proposal was returned by MoEF, reason being that MoEF had amended the EIA Notification of 1994 making public hearing mandatory for the development projects listed in Schedule-I of the Notification - By reason of the said Notification dt. 10-4-1997 the then project proponent (M/s. LMMPL) was asked to seek Site Clearance as well as Project Clearance separately - Site Clearance proposal was called for through the State level agency dealing with the mines - Chief Conservator of Forests (C) had visited Limestone Mining Project of M/s. L when it was found that project had completed developmental works and opening of mine benches had also been accomplished for 7Ha of the mining lease land - Rapid EIA report submitted by the project proponent described the land as wasteland though the visit of the Chief Conservator found it to be otherwise - Consequently, by the said letter the Chief Conservator of Forests (C) informed the MoEF that the project proponent may be directed to obtain forest clearance under the 1980 Act and not to proceed with the mining activities till such clearance - Hence instant petition - Whether ex post facto environmental and forest clearances dt. 19-4-2010 and 22-4-2010 respectively stood vitiated by alleged suppression by M/s. Lafarge regarding the nature of the land -

Held, universal human dependence on the use of environmental resources for the most basic needs renders it impossible to refrain from altering environment - As a result, environmental conflicts were ineradicable and environmental protection was always a matter of degree, inescapably requiring choices as to the appropriate level of environmental protection and the risks which were to be regulated - that aspect was recognized by the concept of 'sustainable development' - Making these choices necessitates decisions, not only about how risks should be regulated, how much protection was enough, and whether ends served by environmental protection could be pursued more effectively by diverting resources to other uses - Since the nature and degree of environmental risk posed by different activities varies, the implementation of environmental rights and duties require proper decision making based on informed reasons about the ends which may ultimately be pursued, as much as about the means for attaining them - Setting the standards of environmental protection involve mediating conflicting visions of what is of value in human life - M/s. L proceeded on the basis that there was no forest - That, both the said clearances stood vitiated by suppression of material fact of existence of forest by M/s. L and as a sequel the subsequent revised environmental clearance dt. 19-4-2010 and forest clearance (Stage - I) dt. 22-4-2010 stood vitiated - In this connection, it was submitted that having regard to Para 4.3.1 of the 1988 Policy, tropical rain/ moist forest was required to be totally safeguarded; that, the project was located in a tropical moist forest and no forest clearance ought to have been granted by MoEF because of the special ecological significance recognized by the 1988 policy - According to SAC, the fact that tropical moist forest existed in the area and continues to exist was known to M/s. L at all material times as could be seen from the NEHU Report of 1997 in which it has been categorically stated that the vegetation at the study site was a mixed moist deciduous forest composed of deciduous and evergreen tree elements; that, in the same Report it has been further stated that the vegetation of the area is a tropical semi- evergreen forest composed of deciduous and evergreen elements which was further corroborated by the assessment of Floral Diversity prepared by NEHU dt. June, 2010 in which it was stated that the forest in the study area was tropical moist deciduous forest, tropical semi-evergreen forest, savanna, sub-tropical broad leaves forest, forest garden, orchards and riparian forest; that, the vegetation in the unbroken area is tropical moist deciduous type with variable canopy cover mostly sparse - Thus, according to SAC and CEC, the undisputed position emerging from the record that the subject area was covered by a tropical moist forest deserving highest degree of ecological protection ought to have been taken into account by MoEF which was not done at the time of initial clearances dt. 18-6-1999 and 9-8-2001 - SC was in full agreement with the legal framework suggested by the SAC - There was no dispute on that point -

Court should review the decision-making process to ensure that the decision of MoEF was fair and fully informed, based on the correct principles, and free from any bias or restraint - Once that was ensured, then the doctrine of 'margin of appreciation' in favour of the decision-maker would come into play - Word 'development' was a relative term - One could not assume that the tribals were not aware of principles of conservation of forest - In instant case, SC satisfied that limestone mining was going on for centuries in the area and that it was an activity which was intertwined with the culture and the unique land holding and tenure system of the Nongtrai Village - SC was satisfied with due diligence exercise undertaken by MoEF in the matter of forest diversion - Therefore SC see no reason to interfere with the decision of MoEF granting site clearance dt. 18-6-1999, EIA clearance dt. 9-8-2001 r/w revised environmental clearance dt.19-4-2010 and Stage-I forest clearance dt. 22-4-2010 - Words 'environment' and 'sustainable development' have various facets - At times in respect of a few of these facets data was not available - Care for environment an ongoing process - Time has come for instant Court to declare and we hereby declare that the National Forest Policy, 1988 which lays down far-reaching principles should necessarily govern the grant of permissions u/s. 2 of the 1980 Act as the same provide the road map to ecological protection and improvement under the 1986 Act - Principles/ guidelines mentioned in the National Forest Policy, 1988 should be read as part of the provisions of the 1986 Act read together with the 1980 Act - Said direction was required to be given because there was no machinery even today established for implementation of the said National Forest Policy, 1988 r/w the 1980 Act - S. 3 of the 1986 Act conferred a power coupled with duty and, thus, it was incumbent on the Central Govt., as hereinafter indicated, to appoint an Appropriate Authority, preferably in the form of Regulator, at the State and at the Centre level for ensuring implementation of the National Forest Policy, 1988 - Difference between a regulator and a court should be kept in mind - Court/Tribunal was basically an authority which react to a given situation brought to its notice whereas a regulator was a pro-active body with the power conferred upon it to frame statutory Rules and Regulations - Regulatory mechanism warrants open discussion, public participation, circulation of the Draft Paper inviting suggestions - Basic objectives of the National Forest Policy, 1988 include positive and pro-active steps to be taken - It include maintenance of environmental stability through preservation, restoration of ecological balance that has been adversely disturbed by serious depletion of forest, conservation of natural heritage of the country by preserving the remaining natural forests with the vast variety of flora and fauna, checking soil erosion and denudation in the catchment areas, checking the extension of sand-dunes, increasing the forest/ tree cover in the country and encouraging efficient utilization of forest produce and maximizing substitution of wood - Thus, SC was of the view that u/s. 3(3) of the 1986 Act, the Central Govt. should appoint a National Regulator for appraising projects, enforcing environmental conditions for approvals and to impose penalties on polluters - There was one more reason for having a regulatory mechanism in place - Identification of an area as forest area was solely based on the Declaration to be filed by the User Agency (project proponent) - Project proponent under the existing dispensation was required to undertake EIA by an expert body/ institution - In many cases, the Court was not made aware of the terms of reference - In several cases, the Court was not made aware of the study area undertaken by the expert body - Consequently, the MoEF/ State Govt. acts on the report (Rapid EIA) undertaken by the Institutions who though accredited submit answers according to the Terms of Reference propounded by the project proponent - SC did not wish to cast any doubt on the credibility of these Institutions - However, at times the court faced with conflicting reports - Similarly, the Govt. was also faced with a fait accompli kind situation which in the ultimate analysis leads to grant of ex facto clearance - To obviate these difficulties, SC was of the view that a regulatory mechanism should be put in place and till the time such mechanism was put in place, the MoEF should prepare a Panel of Accredited Institutions from which alone the project proponent should obtain the Rapid EIA and that too on the Terms of Reference to be formulated by the MoEF - Part II of instant order gives guidelines to be followed by the Central Govt., State Govt. and the various authorities under the 1980 Act and the 1986 Act - Guidelines were to be implemented in all future cases - Guidelines were required to be given so that fait accompli situations do not recur - SC issued these guidelines in the light of our experience in the last couple of years - Guidelines would operate in all future cases of environmental and forest clearances till a regulatory mechanism was put in place - Petition disposed of.
