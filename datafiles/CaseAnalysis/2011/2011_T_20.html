Himani Alloys Ltd. v Tata Steel Ltd.

Supreme Court of India

05 July 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

R.V. Raveendran, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 464; (2011) 15 SCC 273; 2011 (87) ALR 875; 2011 (5) AWC 5100; JT 2011 (11) SC 222; 2011 (7) MLJ(SC) 843; 2011 (3) RCR(Civil) 729; 2011 (114) RD 215; 2011(7) SCALE 566; [2011] 7 S.C.R. 60

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Summary: Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 12 r. 6 - Judgment on the basis of admission of a party - Scope - Respondent company filed a suit in the HC against the appellant company for recovery of a sum of in regard to supply of steel - In the said Suit, the respondent filed an application praying for a decree upon admission for the sum alleging that the appellant had admitted liability for such sum, as per minutes of the meeting held between representatives of respondent and appellant - Respondent's application was resisted by the appellant contending that there was no such admission or any other date and pointing out that what transpired was only a tentative agreement to have the accounts verified and not a final settlement or admission of liability - Single Judge granted a judgment on admission under O. 12 r. 6 CPC for a sum in favour of the respondent, subject to respondent furnishing a bank guarantee for a sum in favour of the Registrar of the HC - Intra court appeal filed by the appellant was dismissed by the DB - Hence the instant appeal - Whether there was any admission on the part of respondent which led to passing of the impugned HC judgment - Held, when the minutes merely noted certain figures and stated that they were tentative and both parties would verify the same and said that the final figure would be arrived at the next meeting, after discussions, the same could not be termed as an 'admission' for the purpose of O. 12 r. 6 of the CPC - A judgment can be given on an 'admission' contained in the minutes of a meeting, however, the admission should be categorical - Admission should be a conscious and deliberate act of the party making it, showing an intention to be bound by it - O. 12 r. 6 being an enabling provision, it was neither mandatory nor peremptory but discretionary - Court, on examination of the facts and circumstances, had to exercise its judicial discretion, keeping in mind that a judgment on admission was a judgment without trial which permanently denied any remedy to the defendant, by way of an appeal on merits - Hence, unless the admission was clear, unambiguous and unconditional, the discretion of the Court should not be exercised to deny the valuable right of a defendant to contest the claim - In short the discretion should be used only when there was a clear 'admission' which could be acted upon - Impugned HC orders were set aside - Appeal allowed.
