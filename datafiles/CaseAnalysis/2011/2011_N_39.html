Shaw Wallace And Company Limited (Now United Spirits Limited) v Nepal Food Corporation And Another

Supreme Court of India

13 October 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

R.V. Raveendran, H.L. Gokhale

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 745; (2011) 15 SCC 56; 2012 (1) ACC 353; AIR 2012 SC 72; 2012 (1) CLT 224; 2012 (2) MLJ(SC) 269; 2011(12) SCALE 346; [2011] 15 S.C.R. 1181

Case Digest

Subject: Carriers & Transportation; Practice & Procedure; Contract & Commercial

Summary: Carriers & Transportation - Contract & Commercial - Practice & Procedure - Indian Carriage of Goods By Sea Act, 1925, s. 2 - Contract - Negligence - Breach of obligation - Evidence - Relief - Appellant-defendant no. 2 filed appeal by special leave against judgment of HC - Contract was entered by parties for sale of parboiled rice - As per contract, payment was to be made by buyer by establishing letter of credit confirmed by Rashtriya Banijya Bank, Kathmandu in favour of seller and payment 100% invoice value should be made seller's bank on presentation of 'on board Bills of Lading' - Bangkok Bank Ltd buyer's bankers, issued an irrecoverable letter of credit in favor of sale - Appellant acted as agent to owner of vessel - Appellant-defendant no. 2 supplied blank forms of bills of lading to plaintiff/ respondent no. 1 for being filled and returned - Plaintiff's agent delivered mate's receipts and duly filled forms of bills of lading to appellant with a request to sign and issue bills of ladings - Plaintiff's agent, further sent message appellant regretting that bills of lading had not been delivered to them, and that therefore, appellant would be responsible for all delays and damages - Hence suit was filed by owner of vessel - During pendency of said suit, plaintiff-respondent also filed a suit against owner in HC of Singapore for recovery of certain amount of money being value of goods supplied - Hence, instant appeal - A) Whether suit was not maintainable as against appellant/defendant no.2 on grounds as stated in written statement - Held, appellant was liable to pay damages to plaintiff-first respondent as Act of withholding bills of lading by him was wrongful and in violation of statutory duty imposed by art. III, r. 3 of the 1925 Act - Further, in an attempt to assist owner, appellant had committed Breach of legal duty amounting to a wrongful act and negligence by wrongfully refusing to make over to plaintiff/respondent who suffered losses - Both owner and appellant acted inconsistently in respect of said bills of lading which amounted to conversion of bills thereby causing damages and injury to plaintiff - Hence, suit was maintainable against appellant-defendant no. 2 - (B) Whether any breach of contract exist on part of appellant/defendant no.2 - Held, DB affirmed appellant was guilty of breach of statutory duty and breach of a legal duty which amounted to negligence - Rejected contention of appellant that it was duty of master of ship who took charge of goods to issue bill of lading - HC made appellant liable in to extent of committing breach of statutory duty and breach of a legal duty and consequently liable to pay to plaintiff value of goods by way of damages - (C) Whether any negligence or breach of obligation on part of appellant/ defendant no.2, as alleged in paras 15 and 16 of plaint - Held, DB appellant had obligation to issue bills of lading within validity period of letter of credit, failure of which resulted in loss of value of goods - Had delivery been taken by buyer even without bills of lading would not have caused loss to seller if it had been issued bills of lading entitled to without delay - Hence breach of obligation and negligence was proved on part of defendant no.2 due to delay caused in releasing bills - (D) To what relief, if any, was plaintiff entitled as against defendant no.2 - Held, demand for bills of lading was being prior to 15-1-1979 and therefore, appellant was liable to pay damages equal to value of goods - DB affirmed said findings - Appeals disposed of.
