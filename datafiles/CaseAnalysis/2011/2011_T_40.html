Sanchit Bansal & Anr. v The Joint Admission Board (Jab) & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

11 October 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

R.V. Raveendran, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 842; (2012) 1 SCC 157; 2012 (1) MLJ(SC) 130; [2011] 15 S.C.R. 1057

Case Digest

Subject: Education

Keywords: Examination, Cut off marks

Summary: Education - Examination - Cut off marks - Malafide - Challenged - Right to Information Act, 2005 - Appellant no. 1 appeared in IIT-JEE 2006 examination as a general category candidate - Board/respondents had fixed cut off marks for admission as appellant no. 1 did not secure minimum marks in chemistry he was not qualified, even though his aggregate in other 3 subjects was very high - Aggrieved, appellant no. 1 filed writ petition challenging his non-selection, due to a defective, erroneous and malafide process adopted for cut-off determination before HC - Single Judge dismissed petition - On appeal, DB dismissed appeal upholding order of Single Judge - Hence, instant appeal - Whether procedure adopted by Board to arrive at cut off marks for JEE 2006 was arbitrary and mala fide and whether HC ought to have interfered in matter - Held, procedure adopted in JEE 2006 might not be best of procedures, nor as sound and effective as present procedures - However, action taken by appellants in challenging procedure for JEE 2006, their attempts to bring in transparency in procedure by various RTI applications, and debate generated by several views of experts during course of writ proceedings, had helped in making merit ranking process more transparent and accurate - IITs and candidates who participate in examinations should, to a certain extent, thank appellants for their effort in bringing such transparency and accuracy in ranking procedure - But there was no ground for Courts to interfere with procedure, even if it was not accurate or efficient, in absence of malafides or arbitrariness or violation of law - Further, it was true that if in JEE 2006, a different or better process had been adopted, or process in vogue had been adopted, results would have been different and appellant no. 1 might have obtained a seat - But on that ground it was not possible to impute malafides or arbitrariness, or grant any relief to appellant no. 1 - Therefore, appellant would have to be satisfied in being one of many unsung heroes who helped in improving system - Thus, no reason to interfere with findings of HC and same was upheld - Appeal dismissed.
