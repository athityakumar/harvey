State of Uttar Pradesh v Chhoteylal

Supreme Court of India

14 January 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Lodha, Aftab Alam

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 40; (2011) 2 SCC 550; 2011 (1) ADJ 738; AIR 2011 SC 697; 2011 (1) JCC 449; JT 2011 (1) SC 228; 2011 (3) MLJ(Crl) 556; 2011 (1) RCR(Criminal) 443; 2011(1) SCALE 454; [2011] 1 S.C.R. 406

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 363, 366, 368, 375 and 376 - Kidnapping - Rape - Conviction by Trial Court - Reversal by HC - Challenged - A-1 was charge sheeted for the offences punishable u/ss. 363, 366, 368 and 376 of the IPC - On appeal, HC reversed the judgment of the Trial Court and acquitted A-1 on ground that there was delay in lodging the FIR, and that there was no internal or external injury was found on her body - Present appeal - (A) Word 'consent' in s. 375 of IPC - Held, expressions 'against her will' and 'without her consent' may overlap sometimes but the two expressions in clause First and clause Secondly have different connotation and dimension - Expression 'against her will' would ordinarily mean that the intercourse was done by a man with a woman despite her resistance and opposition; expression 'without her consent' would comprehend an act of reason accompanied by deliberation - (B) Evidence of prosecutrix - Held, in examining the evidence of the prosecutrix the courts must be alive to the conditions prevalent in the Indian society and must not be swayed by beliefs in other countries - Courts must be sensitive and responsive to the plight of the female victim of sexual assault - Also, absence of injuries on the person of the prosecutrix is not sufficient to discredit her evidence; she was a helpless victim - In the present case, although there were certain contradictions and omissions in testimony of prosecutrix, but such omissions and contradictions were minor and on material aspects, her evidence was consistent - Hence, evidence of the prosecutrix was reliable and was rightly been acted upon by the Trial Court - (C) Delay in registration of FIR - Held, delay in registration of the FIR was, reasonably explained - HC erred in concluding that there was no reasonable and plausible explanation for the belated FIR and that it was lodged after consultation and due deliberation and that creates doubt about the case - (D) Whether judgment of HC was justified? - Held, judgment of the HC was vitiated by non-consideration of the material evidence and relevant factors eloquently emerging from the prosecution evidence - HC was not at all justified in taking a different view from the Trial Court - Judgment of acquittal passed by HC set aside - Appeal allowed.
