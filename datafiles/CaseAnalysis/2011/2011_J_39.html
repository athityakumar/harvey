Inspector Of Police, Tamil Nadu v John David

Supreme Court of India

20 April 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Mukundakam Sharma, Dalveer Bhandari

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 285; (2011) 5 SCC 509; 2011 CRLJ 3366; 2011 (3) CTC 104; JT 2011 (5) SC 1; 2011 (3) MLJ(Crl) 725; 2011 (3) RCR(Criminal) 272; 2011(4) SCALE 878; [2011] 7 S.C.R. 354

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 201, 302, 342 and 364 - Reversal of judgment of conviction by HC - Respondent-accused was studying in the senior first year course of MBBS and the deceased was studying in the junior first year course of MBBS - PW-1 (father of deceased) made a missing complaint for his son - On completion of investigation, the police submitted a charge sheet against the respondent-accused guilty u/ss. 302, 201, 364 and 342 of IPC - Sessions Judge held that there was enough circumstantial evidence and motive on the part of the respondent-accused for committing such a crime and held the respondent-accused guilty - On appeal, HC held that the prosecution had failed to prove the guilt of the respondent-accused and acquitted the respondent-accused of all the charges - Hence, instant appeal - Whether the prosecution was able to establish a chain of circumstances? - Held, each and every incriminating circumstance must be clearly established by reliable and clinching evidence and the circumstances so proved must form a chain of events from which the only irresistible conclusion that could be drawn was the guilt of the accused and that no other hypothesis against the guilt was possible - In the instant case, prosecution succeeded in proving its case on circumstantial evidence - All the witnesses were independent and respectable eye-witnesses and from the evidence of the several witnesses, it was clear that the respondent-accused nurtured ill feeling against the deceased; that the deceased was last seen with the respondent-accused; that the conduct of the respondent-accused was very strange and the bags/suitcases kept by him also produced stinking smell; the recovery of skull from canal water, material objects, like, note books of deceased, gold chain, blood stained bags, knifes etc.,; and also the evidence of PW-66, PW-65 and PW-77 who have categorically stated that the skull, torso and limbs recovered were of the deceased only - Therefore prosecution established its case on the basis of strong and cogent circumstantial evidence and that on the basis of the circumstances proved, there could not be any other possible or plausible view favouring the respondent-accused - HC erred in reversing the order of conviction recorded by the Trial Court - Judgment and order of the HC set aside - Judgment and decision of the Trial Court restored - Appeal allowed.
