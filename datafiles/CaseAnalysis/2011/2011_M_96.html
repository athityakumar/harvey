Nitinbhai Saevatilal Shah and another v Manubhai Manjibhai Panchal and another

Supreme Court of India

01 September 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

J.M. Panchal, H.L. Gokhale

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 559; (2011) 9 SCC 638; 2011 (5) Bom.C.R. 786; 2012 (2) MLJ(Crl) 388; 2011 (2) OLR 891; 2012 (165) PLR 108; 2011 (4) RCR(Criminal) 148; 2012 (1) RLW 161; 2011(9) SCALE 583; [2011] 10 S.C.R. 804

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Banking & Finance - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, s.138 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 326, 357, 465 - 1st appellant Director of 2nd appellant company borrowed a loan from 1st respondent and issued cheque in favour of him - 1st respondent presented the cheque for encashment, however, cheque was dishounoured due to insufficiency of funds - 1st respondent served statutory notice, however, appellant failed to pay the amount within the notice period - 1st respondent filed complaint before the Magistrate - Appellant and respondent examined the witnesses and produced the documents - However, in the meantime, Magistrate came to be transferred and he was succeeded by another Magistrate - Parties filed pursis before the new Magistrate declaring that the parties had no objection to proceed with the matter on the basis of evidence recorded by predecessor in office of him in terms of s. 326 of the Code - Magistrate subsequently continued the proceedings and convicted and sentenced the appellants to suffer simple imprisonment for three months with fine - Appellants filed appeal - Sessions Court upheld the conviction but set aside the sentence of imprisonment to 2nd appellant since it was a company - Appellants appealed to HC wherein HC upheld the conviction, however, set aside the final order of sentence imposed upon the appellants and remanded the matter to the Trial Court for passing appropriate order of sentence and compensation, if any payable u/s. 357 of the Code, within three months, after giving to the parties reasonable opportunity of being heard - (A) Whether the succeeding Magistrate was right in proceeding to the trial on the basis of the evidence recorded by his predecessor - Held, HC relied on the pursis submitted by the appellants before the successor Magistrate declaring that they had no objection if matter was decided after taking into consideration the evidence recorded by his predecessor- in-office, was misconceived - Mandatory language in which s. 326 (3) was couched, left no manner of doubt that when a case was tried as a summary case a Magistrate, who succeeded the Magistrate who had recorded the part or whole of the evidence, should not act on the evidence so recorded by his predecessor - In summary proceedings, the successor Judge or Magistrate had no authority to proceed with the trial from a stage at which his predecessor had left it - Reason why the provisions of sub-s. (1) and (2) of s. 326 of the Code had not been made applicable to summary trials was that in summary trials only substance of evidence had to be recorded - Court did not record the entire statement of witness - Therefore, the Judge or the Magistrate who had recorded such substance of evidence was in a position to appreciate the evidence led before him and the successor Judge or Magistrate should not appreciate the evidence only on the basis of evidence recorded by his predecessor - S. 326 (3) of the Code did not permit the Magistrate to act upon the substance of the evidence recorded by his predecessor, the obvious reason being that if succeeding Judge was permitted to rely upon the substance of the evidence recorded by his predecessor, there would be a serious prejudice to the accused and indeed, it would be difficult for a succeeding Magistrate himself to decide the matter effectively and to do substantial justice - (B) What stage the successor Magistrate should proceed with the trial de novo - Held, proceedings held by a Magistrate, to the extent that he was not empowered by law, would be void and void proceedings should not be validated u/s. 465 of the Code and that defect was not a mere irregularity and the conviction of the appellants should not, even if sustainable on the evidence, be upheld u/s. 465 of the Code - Apart from s. 326 (1) and 326 (2) which were not applicable to the instant case in view of s. 326 (3), the Code did not conceive of such a trial - Hence, s. 465 of the Code had no application and it should not be called in aid to make what was incompetent, competent and there had been no proper trial of the case and there should be one - Impugned judgment for conviction was set aside and matter remanded to the Magistrate for retrial in accordance with law - Appeal disposed of.
