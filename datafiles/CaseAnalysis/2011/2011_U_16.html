Om Prakash and another v Union Of India & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

30 September 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Altamas Kabir, Cyriac Joseph, Surinder Singh Nijjar

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 839; (2011) 14 SCC 1; 2012 CRLJ 779; 2011 (272) E.L.T. 321; 2012 (1) MLJ(Crl) 104; 2012 (2) RCR(Criminal) 496; [2011] 14 S.C.R. 240; 2011 (24) S.T.R. 257

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Indirect Tax

Keywords: Excise Act

Summary: Criminal - Indirect Tax - Customs Act, 1962, s. 104(3) - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss.9, 13 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s.2 - Criminal Procedure Code, 1973 - Nature of offences - There were two sets of matters, one relating to provisions of 1962 Act and other involving provisions of 1944 Act - However issue in both sets of matters was same - Hence, instant Petition - Whether all offences under 1944 Act and 1962 Act were non-cognizable, were such offences bailable - Held, definition of 'non-cognizable offence' in s. 2(l) of IPC made it clear that non-cognizable offence was offence for which police officer had no authority to arrest without warrant - Expression 'cognizable offence' in s. 2(c) of IPC meant offence for which police officer could, in accordance with First Schedule or under any other law for time being in force, arrest without warrant - Offences under 1944 Act could not be equated with offences under IPC which was made non-cognizable and non-bailable - Provisions of s. 104(3) of 1962 Act and s. 13 of 1944 Act vested Customs Officers and Excise Officers with same powers as that of Police Officer in charge of Police Station, which include power to release on bail upon arrest in respect of offences committed under two enactments which were uniformly non-cognizable - Both s.9A of 1944 Act and s.104(4) of 1962 Act provided that notwithstanding anything in CrPC, offences under both Acts would be non-cognizable and offences under 1962 Act could be held to be bailable - Petitions allowed.
