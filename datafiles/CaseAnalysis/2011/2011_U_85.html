Indian Medical Association and others v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

12 May 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

B. SUDARSHAN REDDY, Surinder Singh Nijjar

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 379; (2011) 7 SCC 179; AIR 2011 SC 2365; JT 2011 (6) SC 505; 2011 (7) MLJ(SC) 981; 2011(6) SCALE 86; [2011] 6 S.C.R. 599

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution; Education

Keywords: Constitutional Law, Fixation, Constitution (Ninety Third Amendment) Act, 2005, Constitution (One Hundred and Fourth Amendment) Bill, 2005, German Constitution

Summary: (A) Education - Administrative - Guru Gobind Singh Indraprastha University Act, 1998 - Delhi Professional Colleges or Institutions (Prohibition of Capitation Fee, Regulation of Admission, Fixation of Non-Exploitative Fee and Other Measures to Ensure Equity and Excellence) Act, 2007, ss. 12(1), 12(2), 14, 18 - Private educational institution, started and managed by the Army Welfare Education Society (AWES), named Army College of Medical Sciences (ACMS), located in the National Capital Territory of Delhi, sought to admit only students who were wards or children of current and former army personnel and widows of army personnel - ACMS based its admission policy on certain exemptions granted by the State Government exempting ACMS admissions from the operation of provisions of 2007 Act with respect to allocations, as between Delhi and non-Delhi students, reservations as mandated in s. 12(2) of the 2007 Act, and the requirement that all admissions, in such reserved categories and with respect to remaining seats, be based on inter-se merit as determined by marks secured in the common entrance test - Exemption had been allegedly granted by the State Government in exercise of powers provided in s. 12(1)(b) of the 2007 Act - Exemption specifically allowed ACMS to admit only wards of army personnel in accordance with ACMS's admission policy - Admission policy of ACMS was challenged by students who otherwise would be eligible to be considered for admission to ACMS and by Indian Medical Association - Petitioners in the writ petition contending that TMA Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka, 2002 INDLAW SC 1375, as further explained in P.A. Inamdar v. State of Maharashtra, 2005 INDLAW SC 463, specifically mandated that all admissions to private unaided non-minority professional institutions be only based on merit, which was to be taken as inter-se ranking of all the students who had taken the common entrance test - Writ petitioner further contended that even according to the rules and regulations of affiliated university (GGSIU) or the 2007 Act, they would have secured an admission in ACMS if it had followed the principle of inter-se ranking, based on marks secured in the common entrance test, of all the students applying to ACMS if ACMS had not proscribed all non-wards of army personnel from applying - Writ petitioners further added that in fact ACMS was an aided educational institution, in as much as it had received massive aid from the State, in the form of expensive land and access to Army Base Hospital in Delhi to meet the curricular requirements of clinical training in a general hospital that was required by every medical college, per regulations of the Medical Council of India - Single Judge found that the claimed power to exempt, by the State Government, s. 12 (1)(b) of 2007 Act to be applicable as regards only the 15% of seats remaining after the seats allocated to management quota - Single Judge further found that 79% of the seats could be filled by wards of Army personnel, and the remaining 21% by students belonging to the general category - DB, in appeal, held that enactment of 2007 Act, implied that Ordinance 30 of GGSIU had lost its relevance which mandated reservation for socially backward and weaker section in aided educational institutions - DB further held that there was nothing in s. 12 of 1997 Act that prohibited ACMS and AWES to admit only wards of army personnel in all its seats and hence it upheld the admission policy of ACMS - Further, DB also over-ruled the finding of Single Judge that the ratio of TMA Pai as explained in P.A. Inamdar, implied that ACMS ought to admit a 'sprinkling' of students from the general category - Whether the exemptions granted by State Government were valid - Held, by permitting ACMS to allocate all its seats to wards of army personnel, albeit ones who had taken and qualified the common entrance test, the State Government effectively suspended the operation of the provisions of the 2007 Act with regard to selection of students solely on merit from the general category, and also the provisions that mandated allotment and reservation of seats to various constitutionally permissible classes, including but not limited to Scheduled Classes and Scheduled Tribes - S. 12(2) contemplated that not only were reservation of seats, for instance with respect to Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes, to be made with respect to Delhi students, non-Delhi students, and also with respect to all students admitted under the management quota - Further, by permitting ACMS to admit only students of wards of army personnel, notwithstanding the fact there could be others who had taken the common entrance test, and had secured more marks than the wards of army personnel, the exemptions granted by State Government also set at naught the legislative intent to ensure excellence by mandating that all admissions be made on the basis of inter-se merit within each of the categories of students - General category would comprise of all students who had taken the common entrance test, and other wise satisfied the conditions of s. 12(1) of 2007 Act, after the seats reserved pursuant to s. 12 (2) were reserved i.e., allocated for the described constitutionally permissible categories therein - Grant of permission to ACMS to admit students who may have scored lower marks than others, both within the general category and also in the reserved categories, resulted in defeat of the aims, objects and purposes of the 2007 Act, and the entire fabric and scheme of the Act got frustrated - Nowhere in the Act granted any powers to the Government not to implement the Act - Nor did the Act state anywhere that the Government could suspend the implementation of the provisions with respect to reservations for weaker segments, and also simultaneously give the merit of the students scoring higher marks than wards of army personnel a go by - Hence, there was no power conferred on State Government to grant any exemption in favour of any institution from the operation of any of the provisions of the 2007 Act - Thus, exemption granted by the State Government allowing ACMS to fill 100% of its seats by wards of army personnel violated the basic principles of democratic governance, of the constitutional requirement that executive implement the specific and mandatory policy legislated by the legislature, and violates the provisions of 2007 Act - In fact, the actions of the State Government, were wholly arbitrary, without any basis in law, and ultra vires - S. 14 of the 2007 Act specified that any admission made in contravention of the provisions of the Act or the rules made thereunder, should be void, and further s. 18 provided that those making admissions in contravention of the provisions of the Act may be punished by imprisonment up to three years or a fine up to Rupees one Crore or both - Such provisions clearly demonstrated the intent of the legislature that its policy, as specified in the Act, and the purposes of the Act, not be derogated from in any manner - Hence, provisions of the 2007 Act were mandatory in nature - State Government clearly acted on the basis of a misplaced belief of its powers, under the 2007 Act, a misunderstanding of the statutory language of the Act, and its relevant provisions, and also in complete contravention of constitutional principles - Order accordingly.

(B) Education - Administrative - Constitution - Guru Gobind Singh Indraprastha University Act, 1998 - Delhi Professional Colleges or Institutions (Prohibition of Capitation Fee, Regulation of Admission, Fixation of Non-Exploitative Fee And Other Measures to Ensure Equity And Excellence) Act, 2007 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 15(5), 19(6), 30(1) - Whether ACMS could admit only wards of army personnel to the seats not covered by reservations mandated by 2007 Act, without any regard to the merit of other Delhi or non-Delhi students who may have secured higher marks in common entrance test - Held, concern expressed by the Seven Judge Bench in Inamdar, echoed the concern of SC in TMA Pai was that those seats had to be filled by non-minority institutions on the basis of merit of students, i.e., ranking determined in accordance with marks secured, in the general category, comprising of the entire set of students who had taken the qualifying examination and secured the minimal marks - Neither AWES nor ACMS, were protected by any constitutional provision that allowed it to choose to be an educational institution serving only a small class of students from within the general pool - If army personnel now constituted a 'Socially and Educationally Backward Class', then u/art. 15(5), it was for the State to determine the same, and provide by law, for reservations of wards of army personnel, in consonance with the constitutional jurisprudence extant with regard to how a Socially and Educationally Backward Class was to be delineated, for instance by removal of the creamy layer, and that the extent of reservations to be provided ought not to exceed certain levels etc. - Consequently, all of the permissible restrictions and regulations u/art. 19(6) that non-minority institutions would be subject to would also be applicable with respect to ACMS - Non-minority private unaided professional colleges did not have the right to choose their own 'source' from within the general pool - Equivalence between minority and non-minority unaided institutions, apart from that distinction because of art. 30(1), was to be on the basis that both were subject to reasonable restrictions pursuant to art. 19(6), that neither minority nor non-minority institutions could mal-administer their educational institutions, especially professional institutions, that affected the quality of education, and by choosing students arbitrarily from within the sources that they were entitled to choose from - In the case of non-minority institutions, especially professional institutions, the 'source' could only be the general pool, and selection had to be based on inter-se ranking of students who had qualified and applying or opting to choose to be admitted to such non-minority educational institutions - In the case of minority educational institutions, the 'source' could be delimited to the particular minority the institution belonged to - Protection u/art. 30(1) was granted to minority institutions so long as they maintained their minority status - If the non-minority educational institutions could choose their own sources, minorities which were assured equal protections as non-minorities should certainly had that right too - Added protections to minority educational institutions made sense only in the event that non-minorities were restricted to choosing from the general pool, and minorities from the delimited source of their own minority - Hence, ACMS had only the right to choose students from within the general pool - If any special provisions ought to be made to protect the wards of army personnel, that may possibly be done by the State, by laws protected by art. 15(5) of the Constitution - ACWES, a private society of former and current army personnel by themselves should not unilaterally choose to create special provision to protect the wards of army personnel - Order accordingly.

(C) Constitution - Education - Delhi Professional Colleges or Institutions (Prohibition of Capitation Fee, Regulation of Admission, Fixation of Non-Exploitative Fee And Other Measures to Ensure Equity And Excellence) Act, 2007 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Whether the provisions of art. 15(5) violated the basic structure of the Constitution in so far as they related to enablement of the making of 'special provisions', by law, with respect to admissions of Scheduled Castes, Scheduled Tribes, and Socially and Educationally Backward Classes into private unaided non-minority educational institutions - Held, provisions of new cl. (5) of art. 15 did not purport to take away the power of judicial review, or even access to courts through arts. 32 or 226 - Neither did the provisions of cl. (5) of art. 15 mandated that the field of higher education be taken over by the State itself, either to the partial or total exclusion, of any private non-minority unaided educational institutions, a power that was most certainly granted u/cl. (6) of art. 19, which had been inserted by the 1st Constitutional Amendment in 1951 - State had the power u/art. 19(6), to totally or partially exclude the participation of private sector in the field of higher education - Width of the original powers u/cl. (6) of art. 19, State would at least had the power to make amendments to the Constitution to partially resurrect some of the powers that it had possessed to control access to higher education, and achieve goals of egalitarianism and social justice - What the State had done was to allow private sector to function in the field of higher education, to supplement the role of the State in the field which had been recognized even in TMA Pai - Power of the State to allow such participation of the private sector could only have existed if the State had the power to devise policies based on circumstances to promote general welfare of the country, and the larger public interest - Such power should not be taken to mean that a constitutional amendment had occurred, in a manner that fundamental alteration had occurred in the basic structure itself, whereby the State was denuded of its obligations to pursue social justice and egalitarian ideals, inscribed as an essential part of constitutional identity, in those areas which the State felt that even resources in the private sector would required to be used to achieve such social goals - Hence, insertion of cl. (5) of art. 15, the 93rd Constitutional Amendment had empowered the State to enact legislations that may have very far reaching beneficial consequences for the nation - Cl. (5) of art. 15 strengthened the social fabric in which the Constitutional vision, goals and values could be better achieved and served - Consequently, given the absolute necessity of achieving the egalitarian and social justice goals that were implied by provisions of cl. (5) of art. 15, and the urgency of such a requirement, they were not a violation of the basic structure, but in fact strengthened the basic structure of our constitution - Order accordingly.
