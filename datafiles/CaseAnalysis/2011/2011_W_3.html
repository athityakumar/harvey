Waikhom Yaima Singh v State Of Manipur

Supreme Court of India

18 April 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

V.S. Sirpurkar, T.S. Thakur

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 266; (2011) 13 SCC 125; 2011 ALL MR (Cri) 2048; 2011 CRLJ 2673; JT 2011 (6) SC 355; 2011(4) SCALE 718; [2011] 5 S.C.R. 448

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Conviction - Challenged - FIR was registered against appellant u/s. 302 of IPC - Prosecution relied on the dying declaration made by the deceased in presence of witnesses - Trial Court did not believe the prosecution case and acquitted the appellant - On appeal, HC believed the dying declaration and convicted appellant - Hence, instant appeal - Whether HC erred in holding that that the dying declaration was creditworthy and that the Trial Court had erred in acquitting the appellant? - Held, dying declaration could be the sole basis for conviction, however, such a dying declaration had to be proved to be wholly reliable, voluntary, and truthful and further that the maker thereof must be in a fit medical condition to make it - Oral dying declaration was a weak kind of evidence, where the exact words uttered by the deceased were not available, particularly because of the failure of memory of the witnesses who were said to have heard it - In the instant case, the exact words were not available - They differ from witness to witness - Further, doctor was also not cross-examined by the Public Prosecutor about the medical condition of the deceased and further fact as to whether he was in a fit condition to make any statement - Also, though the witnesses claimed to have reported to doctor about such dying declaration and the name of the assailant, there was no reflection of the name in the FIR - Hence, perfectly probable view was taken by the Trial Court which could not have been set aside for the mere fact that some other view could be taken on the basis of the dying declaration - HC was wrong in holding that that the dying declaration was creditworthy and that the Trial Court had erred in acquitting the appellant - Judgment of the HC set aside and that of the Trial Court was restored confirming the acquittal of the appellant - Appeal allowed.
