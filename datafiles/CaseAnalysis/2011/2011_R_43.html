 Rajiv Sarin & Anr. v State Of Uttarakhand & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

09 August 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Mukundakam Sharma, S.H. Kapadia, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan, Swatanter Kumar, A.R. Dave

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 485; (2011) 8 SCC 708; 2011 (6) AWC 5920; 2011 (4) CLT(SC) 43; JT 2011 (9) SC 524; 2011 (3) RCR(Civil) 946; 2011 (114) RD 130; 2011(8) SCALE 446; [2011] 9 S.C.R. 1012

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution; Land & Property

Keywords: Government of India Act, French Civil Code, Kumaun and Uttarakhand Zamindari Abolition and Land Reforms Act, 1960, 5th Amendment of the Constitution of U.S.A., Italian Constitution, U. P. Private Forests Act, 1948, U.P. Zamindari Abolition (Amendment) Act, 1978, Kumaun and Uttrakhand Zamindari Abolition and Land Reform Rules, 1965

Summary: (A) Land & Property - Administrative - Kumaun and Uttarakhand Zamindari Abolition and Land Reforms Act, 1960, ss. 4A, 8 - Kumaun and Uttrakhand Zamindari Abolition and Land Reform Rules, 1965, r. 41 - Private forest acquisition - Compensation - Entitlement - Appellant's land comprising of large tracts of forest land were vested with the State Government u/s. 4A of the Act by vide notification dated 21-12-1977- District Collector invited objection from appellant - Appellant challenged the vires of 1960 Act and contended that no profit was being made from the property in question - District Collector rejected appellant's objection - Appellant filed writ petition - HC dismissed writ petition - Whether s. 4A of the 1960 Act and r. 41 of the 1965 Rules would be applicable to forest land - Held, it should not be denied that 1960 Act was a statutory enactment, dealing with the agrarian reforms - S. 4A introduced in 1960 Act by the UP Amendment Act 1978 did not require any notification but it specified the date i.e. 1-1-1978 and provided that the right, title and interest of a hissedar (intermediary) in respect of forest land should cease and vest by the application of the statute itself in the State Government - S. 8 of the 1960 Act mandated that such 'hissedar' became by operation of the statute a 'bhumidhar' - 1978 UP Amendment was introduced by way of amendment so as to bring the said Act in parity with the Principal Act, namely UP Zamindari Abolition and Land Reforms Act wherein the rights, title and interest of an intermediary was abolished and vested with the State from the very inception of the said Act as such provision was part of the principal Act itself - Further, r. 41 of the 1965 Rules framed under the 1960 Act declared that the forests belonging to the State should be managed by 'Goan Sabha or any other local authority established' upon a notification issued by the State Government - It was true that s. 4A of 1960 Act, as amended by the UP Amendment Act 1978, provided that Chapter II and Chapter V of the 1960 Act would apply mutatis mutandis and Rule 41 of the 1965 Rules was relatable to Chapter IV of the 1960 Act - However, the necessary consequence of s. 4A of the 1960 Act was that the forest land vested in the State and all that r. 41 of the 1965 Rules did was to provide how the lands vested in the State including forest and non-forest land was to be dealt with - Thus, r. 41 of the 1965 Rules clearly applied to forest lands as it had been specifically so mentioned in the said Rules as well which were vested in the State u/s. 4A of the 1960 Act and therefore had become the land/property of the State, which would be managed by the Goan Sabha - Appeal partly allowed.

(B) Land & Property - Administrative - Constitution - Kumaun and Uttarakhand Zamindari Abolition and Land Reforms Act, 1960 - Kumaun and Uttrakhand Zamindari Abolition and Land Reform Rules, 1965 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 254 - Repugnancy of two Acts - Effect of art. 254 of the Constitution - Whether the provisions of the 1960 Act as amended by the UP Amendment Act 1978 were repugnant to the provisions of the Indian Forests Act 1927, in so far as no compensation was provided for under the UP Amendment Act, 1978 for private forests - Held, 1960 Act dealt with agrarian reforms and in that context dealt with the private forests vested with the State and would therefore be managed by the Goan Sabha - Indian Forest Act, 1927 which was the existing Central law, had nothing to do with agrarian reforms but dealt with forest policy and management, and therefore was in a different field - Further, there was no direct conflict or collision, as the Indian Forest Act, 1927 only gave an enabling power to the government to acquire forests in accordance with the provisions of the Land Acquisition Act 1894, whereas 1960 Act resulted in vesting of forests from the dates specified in s. 4A of the 1960 Act - Hence, it was crystal clear that no case of repugnancy was made out in the instant case as both the Indian Forest Act, 1927 and the 1960 Act operated in two different and distinct fields - Both the Acts were legally valid and constitutional and that being so, there was no requirement of obtaining any Presidential assent - Consequently, art. 254(2) of the Constitution had also no application in the instant case - Appeal partly allowed.

(C) Land & Property - Administrative - Constitution - Kumaun and Uttarakhand Zamindari Abolition and Land Reforms Act, 1960 - Kumaun and Uttrakhand Zamindari Abolition and Land Reform Rules, 1965 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 300A - Acquisition of private forest land - Compensation - Entitlement - Scope of art. 300A - Whether the HC was justified in holding that the appellants were not entitled to any compensation even when their forest land was acquired by the government, merely because the appellants had not derived any income from the said forest - Held, art. 31(2) of the Constitution had since been repealed by the Constitution (44th Amendment) Act 1978 - Art. 300A was inserted by the Constitution (44th Amendment) Act, 1978 by practically reinserting art. 31(1) of the Constitution - Therefore, right to property was no longer a fundamental right but a right envisaged and conferred by the Constitution and that also by retaining only art. 31(1) of the Constitution and specifically deleting art. 31(2), as it stood - Entire concept to right to property had to be viewed with a different mindset than the mindset which was prevalent during the period when the concept of eminent domain was the embodied provision of fundamental rights - However, even now as provided u/art. 300A of the Constitution the State could proceed to acquire land for specified use but by enacting a law through State legislature or by Parliament and in the manner having force of law - When the State exercised the power of acquisition of a private property thereby depriving the private person of the property, provision was generally made in the statute to pay compensation to be fixed or determined according to the criteria laid down in the statute itself - It should be understood in that context that the acquisition of the property by the State in furtherance of the Directive Principles of State Policy was to distribute the material resources of the community including acquisition and taking possession of private property for public purpose - It did not require payment of market value or indemnification to the owner of the property expropriated - Payment of market value in lieu of acquired property was not a condition precedent or sine qua non for acquisition - It should be clearly understood that the acquisition and payment of amount were part of the same scheme and they should not be separated - It was true that the adequacy of compensation should not be questioned in a court of law, but at the same time the compensation should not be illusory - Further, stand taken by the State that the right, title or interests of a hissedar could be acquired without payment of any compensation, as in the instant case, was contrary to the express provisions of 1960 Act itself - S. 12 of the 1960 Act stated that every hissedar whose rights, title or interest were acquired u/s. 4, should be entitled to receive and be paid compensation - Further, s. 4A of the 1960 Act made it clear that the provisions of Chapter II (Acquisition and Modifications of existing rights in Land), including s. 12, should apply mutatis mutandis to a forest land as they apply to a khaikhari land - Further, the intention of the legislature to pay compensation was abundantly clear from the fact that s. 19 itself prescribed that the compensation payable to a hissedar u/s. 12 should, in the case of private forest, be eight times the amount of average annual income from such forest - In the instant case, income also included possible income in case of persons who had not exploited the forest and had rather preserved it - In fact, the persons who were maintaining the forest and preserving it for future and posterity should be penalised by giving nil compensation only because of the reason that they were in fact chose to maintain the forest instead of exploiting it - As mandated by art. 300A, a person could be deprived of his property but in a just, fair and reasonable manner - In an appropriate case the Court may find 'nil compensation' also justified and fair if it was found that the State had undertaken to take over the liability and also had assured to compensate in a just and fair manner - However, 'a law seeking to acquire private property for public purpose could not say that 'no compensation' would be paid - Instant case was a case of payment of 'no compensation' at all - Forest land in question which was vested on the State by operation of law should not be said to be non-productive or unproductive by any stretch of imagination - Property in question was definitely a productive asset and that being so, the criteria to determine possible income on the date of vesting would be to ascertain such compensation paid to similarly situated owners of neighboring forests on the date of vesting - Even otherwise, revenue authority could always make an estimation of possible income on the date of vesting if the property in question had been exploited by the appellants and then calculate compensation on the basis thereof in terms of s. 18(1) (cc) and 19(1) (b) of 1960 Act - Hence, awarding no compensation attracted the vice of illegal deprivation of property even in the light of the provisions of the Act and therefore amenable to writ jurisdiction - That being so, the omission of the s. 39(1) (e) (ii) of the 1960 Act as amended in 1978 was of no consequence since the 1960 Act left no choice to the State other than to pay compensation for the private forests acquired by it in accordance with the mandate of the law - Validity of the 1960 Act and particularly ss. 4A, 18(1) (cc) and 19 (1) (b) of the 1960 Act therefore were upheld, however, direct the respondent Collector to determine and award compensation to the appellants - Appeal partly allowed.
