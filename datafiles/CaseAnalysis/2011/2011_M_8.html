Shankara Co-Op Housing Society Ltd v M. Prabhakar & Ors

Supreme Court of India

05 May 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

H.L. Dattu, D.K. Jain

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 340; (2011) 5 SCC 607; AIR 2011 SC 2161; 2011 (4) ALD(SC) 112; 2011 (2) CLT(SC) 455; JT 2011 (5) SC 494; 2011 (3) RLW 2093; 2011(5) SCALE 423; [2011] 7 S.C.R. 468

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Administration of Evacuee Property Act, 1950, Administration of Evacuee Property (Central) Rules, 1950

Summary: Land & Property - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 226 and 227 - Administration of Evacuee Property (Central) Rules, 1950 - Administration of Evacuee Property Act, 1950 - Displaced Persons (Compensation and Rehabilitation) Act, 1954 - Administration of property left behind in India by evacuees migrated to Pakistan during partition - Compensatory redistribution of same amongst persons who had migrated from Pakistan, leaving behind their property, at the time of partition - Disputed lands originally belonged to one 'M' - 'M' had borrowed paper currency from 'R' and since he failed to discharge the amount due, 'R' had filed a civil suit against 'M' - Court had passed a judgment and decree in favour of 'R' - In the execution proceedings of the decree, it was alleged that 'R' had purchased the disputed lands belonging to 'M' in an auction under the supervision of the Court - After death of 'R' his legal representatives had migrated to Pakistan after partition of India - Deputy Custodian and Collector issued notice to the legal heirs of 'R' under sub-Section (1) of s. 7 of the Evacuee Property Act stating that the 'disputed lands' belonged to 'R' and they have migrated to Pakistan and they were evacuee and, therefore, he would hold an enquiry in the matter and any person having any share or interest in the above 'disputed lands' were directed to participate in the proceedings - Neither respondents nor anybody else had filed any objection to the notice issued u/s. 7(1) of the Evacuee Property Act - Deputy Custodian and Collector issued a Notification, declaring the disputed property in issue as an Evacuee Property u/s. 7 of the Evacuee Property Act - After such declaration, the Central Government acquired the 'disputed lands' by issuing notification u/s. 12 of the Displaced Persons Act for the rehabilitation of the persons who were displaced during the partition - After 15 years, ancestors of the respondents claimed ownership of the 'disputed lands' and made their representation before the authorities under the Evacuee Property Act - Tahsildar issued a letter seeking to auction the 'disputed lands' on yearly lease basis - Aggrieved by the action of the authorities, 'M' claiming to be ancestor of the contesting respondents, preferred a writ petition before the HC seeking a direction restraining the respondents in the petition from auctioning 'disputed lands' - During the pendency of the writ petition, other contesting respondents had filed a Revision Petition u/s. 27 of the Evacuee Property Act before the Deputy Custodian General to revise the notification declaring the disputed lands as evacuee property - Writ petition was dismissed by the HC on the ground that the claim of the respondents was highly belated and they have also not exhausted the alternate remedy provided under the provisions of the Evacuee Property Act - Portion of said land was sold to few persons (allottees) - Meanwhile, Deputy Custodian General remanded the case to Custodian-cum-Collector for re-determination of the evacuee nature of the lands - Pursuant thereto, enquiry was conducted, wherein it was found that there was no evidence to show that 'R' came to be the owner of the land in pursuance of an auction by the Court in execution of any money decree - Allottees had filed a Revision Petition before the Chief Settlement Commissioner of Evacuee Property under the Displaced Persons Act - In view of the pendency of the proceedings, the Tahsildar refused to give possession of the 'disputed lands' to the allottees - Chief Settlement Commissioner of Evacuee Property set aside the aforesaid order of the Collector- cum- Deputy Custodian, and declared that the said property belonged to 'R' and that by virtue of the Notification the disputed lands were evacuee property - Respondents had filed a revision petition before the Secretary to revise/review the order, which was rejected - Contesting respondents filed a writ petition requesting the court to direct the authorities under the Displaced Persons Act to initiate suo-moto proceedings to determine the claim of ownership of the disputed lands - HC dismissed the writ petition, holding that it could not compel any authority to initiate and dispose of the suo moto proceedings u/s. 33 of the Displaced Persons Act - Contesting respondents filed another Writ Petition before the HC, requesting the HC to issue a writ or order directing the Commissioner, Survey Settlement and Land Records/Chief Settlement Commissioner, Evacuee Property to conduct an enquiry into questions of title of 'disputed lands' and correctness of the declaration of the said property as evacuee property in pursuance of proceedings of the Chief Settlement Commissioner - By the impugned judgment, HC allowed the writ petition by setting aside the order passed by the Chief Settlement Commissioner and restored the order passed by the Collector-cum-Deputy Custodian of Evacuee Property - Hence, instant appeals filed by appellant who was the subsequent purchaser of the property in dispute from the allottees under the provisions of the Displaced Persons Act - (A) Whether the contesting respondents have been guilty of delay and laches? - Held, delay and laches was one of the factors that required to be borne in mind by the High Courts when they exercise their discretionary power u/art. 226 of the Constitution of India - In an appropriate case, the HC may refuse to invoke its extraordinary powers if there was such negligence or omission on the part of the applicant to assert his rights taken in conjunction with the lapse of time and other circumstances - In the instant case, there was no explanation offered by the respondents in approaching the writ court after an inordinate delay of nearly 15 years from the date of the notification issued under the Evacuee Property Act - Unexplained delay on the part of the contesting respondents in approaching the HC for redressal of their grievances u/art. 226 of the Constitution was sufficient to justify rejection of the petition - Hence, HC ought not to have entertained and granted relief to the contesting respondents, since there was inordinate and unexplained delay in approaching the court - (B) Whether the judgment and order passed by the HC in the writ petition filed by one of the legal representatives having attained finality in so far as the notification dated issued under the Evacuee Property Act, could have been re-agitated by the other legal heirs of 'M'? - Held, judgment and order of the HC having attained finality was binding on the authorities under the Evacuee Property Act and the HC ought not to have permitted the contesting respondents to re-agitate the correctness or otherwise of the notification in the subsequent writ petition - A subsequent writ petition was not maintainable in respect of an issue concluded between the parties in the earlier writ petition - (C) What was the effect and the consequence of the notification issued u/s. 12(1) of the Displaced Persons Act - It was contended that once the notification u/s. 12 of the Displaced Property Act was issued and the lands were acquired for re-distribution, no proceedings could lie under the Evacuee Property Act - Held, notification dated was valid in law - Since the notification issued u/s. 7 of the Act was valid in law, the evacuee property acquired by the Central Government u/s. 12 of the Displaced Persons Act ceases to be evacuee property and becomes the property of the Central Government - (D) Whether HC committed error while entertaining a writ petition filed u/arts. 226 and 227 of the Constitution, wherein the proceedings u/s. 7 of the Evacuee Property Act was questioned? - Held, HC had not committed an error while entertaining a writ petition filed u/art. 226 and 227 of the Constitution, wherein the proceedings u/s. 7 of the Evacuee Property Act was questioned because under the Evacuee Property Act, there was specific bar for the civil court to adjudicate on the issue whether certain property Was or was not evacuee property - This issue could be decided only by the custodian under the Act - Any person aggrieved by the findings of the custodian could avail the other remedies provided under the Act - Findings and the conclusion reached by the authorities under the Act in an appropriate case could be questioned in a petition filed u/art. 226 of the Constitution even it involves disputed questions of facts - Hence, in view of the specific bar u/s. 46 of the Evacuee Property Act, writ petition filed by the contesting respondents before the HC was maintainable - Appeals allowed.
