Dnyaneshwar Ranganath Bhandare and another v Sadhu Dadu Shettigar (Shetty) and another

Supreme Court of India

30 September 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

R.V. Raveendran, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 631; (2011) 10 SCC 433; 2012 (3) Bom.C.R. 129; JT 2011 (12) SC 487; 2012 (1) RCR(Civil) 499; 2011(11) SCALE 215; [2011] 14 S.C.R. 187

Case Digest

Subject: Rent Control

Keywords: Bombay Rents Hotel, and Lodging House Rates Control Act, 1947

Summary: Rent Control - Bombay Rents Hotel, and Lodging House Rates Control Act, 1947, s. 24(4) - Gratuitous license - Eviction - Failure to prove tenancy - Respondent tenants filed suit for permanent injunction against appellant landlords from evicting them from demised premises - Appellant contended that respondents were gratuitous licensees and no that of tenants and they had encroached upon another room of demised premises - Trial Court dismissed the suit holding that appellants were entitled to the possession of demised premises - Respondents filed first appeal - First Appellate Court held that appellants had failed to prove that the respondents were gratuitous licensees or that they had encroached upon another room of demised premises - Appellants' second appeal before the HC was dismissed thereafter - Whether the respondent had proved that there was a tenancy between appellants and respondents - Held, none of the documents produced or relied upon by respondents evidenced tenancy or payment of rent - Documents on record established that respondents were in possession of a portion of the premises, but that fact was never in dispute - It should be noted that though respondents submitted that they occupied the suit portions in 1982, they did not prove occupation of the suit portions from 1982 - First Appellate Court erroneously held that the appellants had failed to offer satisfactory explanation regarding the documents relied upon by the respondents and held that therefore the suit should be dismissed - First Appellate Court had not recorded any finding that documents produced by respondents established a tenancy - No finding was there in the entire judgment of First Appellate Court that the respondents had proved that they were the tenants - Trial Court found that none of the documents established tenancy - Appellants had explained all documents relied upon by the respondents by demonstrating that they only prove occupation (which was not disputed) but not tenancy - First Appellate Court inferred from documents which disclosed mere occupation of a portion of the house and documents which showed some payments which should not be linked to rent, that appellants failed to prove that the occupation by respondents was as gratuitous licensees - It did not however infer from the documents that there was a tenancy and the entire reasoning was therefore unsound - In spite of that legal lacunae, the HC did not interfere on the ground that no question of law was involved - It failed to notice that the inferences and legal effect from proved facts was a question of law and the inferences drawn by the First Appellate Court were wholly unwarranted - Fact that was proved was possession of suit portions which was not in dispute, but not tenancy in regard to the suit portions, which was in dispute - Hence, judgments of the First Appellate Court and the HC were unsustainable and the finding of the Trial Court that respondents were gratuitous licensees was correct and justified - Appeal allowed.
