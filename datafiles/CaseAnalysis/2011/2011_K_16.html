Milind Shripad Chandurkar v Kalim M. Khan & Anr

Supreme Court of India

03 March 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 163; (2011) 4 SCC 275; AIR 2011 SC 1588; 2011 (2) BC 542; 2011 (2) Bom.C.R. 482; 2011 CRLJ 1912; 2011 (3) CTC 234; 2011 (2) G.L.R. 1670; 2011 (2) JCC (NI) 119; JT 2011 (3) SC 138; 2011 (2) KLT 271; 2011 (3) MLJ(Crl) 774; 2011 (3) MPLJ 67; 2011 (2) RCR(Civil) 687; 2011 (2) RCR(Criminal) 568; 2011(3) SCALE 285; [2011] 3 S.C.R. 698

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Negotiable Instruments Act, SS. 138 and 142(a) - Dishonour of cheque drawn in the name of firm - Complaint filed by the appellant claiming to be the sole proprietor of the firm - The appellant cannot claim to be the payee of the cheque, nor can be the holder in due course, unless he establishes that the cheque had been issued to him or in his favour or that he is the sole proprietor of the concern and being so, he could also be the payee himself and thus, entitled to make the complaint.The firm namely, 'V', has been the payee and the appellant claimed to be its sole proprietor. A complaint under section 142 is maintainable for dishonour of cheque, in the name of the "payee", proprietory concern itself or in the name of the proprietor of the said concern. The appellant cannot claim to be the payee of the cheque, nor can he be the holder in due course, unless he establishes that the cheques had been issued to him or in his favour or that he is the sole proprietor of the concern and being so, he could also be the payee himself and thus, entitled to make the complaint. The appellant miserably failed to prove any nexus or connection by adducing any evidence, whatsoever, worth the name with the said firm. Mere statement in the affidavit in this regard, is not sufficient to meet the requirement of law. The appellant failed to produce any documentary evidence to connect himself with the said firm. The High Court has set aside the judgments of the trial Court as well as the Appellate Court in revision only on the ground that as the appellant did not produce any evidence to show that he was the proprietor of the Firm, he had no locus standi to file the complaint. No cogent reason to interfere with the impugned judgment and order of the High Court.
