National Insurance Company Ltd. v Sinitha & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

23 November 2011

Case Analysis

Bench

J. S. KHEHAR, A.K. Ganguly

Where Reported

2011 Indlaw SC 776; (2012) 2 SCC 356; 2012 (1) ACC 524; 2012 ACJ 1; 2012 (2) ALD(SC) 112; 2012 (1) AWC 556; 2012 (5) Bom.C.R. 820; 2011 (4) KLT 821; 2012 (4) MahLJ 98; 2012 (1) MLJ(SC) 1164; 2012 (165) PLR 552; 2012 (1) RCR(Civil) 205; [2011] 16 S.C.R. 166; 2012 (1) WLN(SC) 19

Case Digest

Subject: Insurance; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Report of inspection

Summary: Insurance - Practice & Procedure - Motor Vehicles Act 1988, ss. 140 and 163A - Accident - Negligence - Compensation - Respondents/claimants filed claim petition before Motor Accidents Claim Tribunal seeking compensation against appellant under Act 1988 for consequent death of her husband in accident - Petitioner contended that claimants were not entitled to raise claim for compensation because accident in question occurred solely and exclusively on account of negligence of deceased - Tribunal held, claimant husband was responsible for accident, even though, issue of 'wrongful act', 'neglect' or 'fault', at hands of deceased were irrelevant for determination of claim made u/s. 163-A, Act 1988 - Tribunal allowed petition and directed appellant to pay compensation with interest from date of claim petition - On appeal, HC upheld one of contentions of appellant by holding, that amount awarded for pain and suffering, was impermissible u/s. 163A, Act 1988 and ordered to be deducted from total compensation awarded by Tribunal - Hence, instant appeal - Whether s. 163A, Act 1988 was governed by 'fault' or 'no-fault' liability principle - Held, where claimant in order to establish his right to claim compensation, to establish that same did not arise out of 'wrongful act' or 'neglect' or 'default' said provision would be deemed to fall under 'fault' liability principle and also, where claim for compensation could be defeated on account of any of aforesaid considerations on basis of a 'fault' ground, same would also fall under 'fault' liability principle - On contrary, if under a provision, a claimant did not establish that his claim did not arise out of 'wrongful act' or 'neglect' or 'default' and conversely, claim could not be defeated on account of any of aforesaid considerations; then provision in question would fall under ' no fault liability' principle - S. 163A had an overriding effect on all other provisions of Motor Vehicles Act, 1988 - On perusal of s. 140 (4), Act 1988 further revealed, that claim of compensation u/s. 140 Act 1988 could not be defeated because of any of 'fault' grounds - Further, on conjoint reading of ss. 140 and 163A, the legislative intent was clear that claim for compensation raised u/s. 163A, Act 1988, need not be based on pleadings or proof at hands of claimants showing absence of 'wrongful act', being 'neglect' or 'default' - Further, to decide whether provision was governed by 'fault' liability principle converse was also to be established i.e. whether a claim raised there under could be defeated by the concerned party (owner or insurance company) by pleading and proving 'wrongful act' or 'neglect' or 'default' - Also it was open to owner or insurance company, as case may be, to defeat claim u/s. 163A, Act 1988 by pleading and establishing through cogent evidence a 'fault' ground - Therefore, s. 163A, Act 1988 was founded under the 'fault' liability principle - Petition dismissed.
