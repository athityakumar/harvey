State Of M.P. v Rakesh Kohli & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

11 May 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Lodha, H.L. Gokhale

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 136; (2012) 6 SCC 312; AIR 2012 SC 2351; 2012 (5) ALD(SC) 80; 2012 (93) ALR 682; 2012 (4) AWC 3598; 2012 (2) CLT(SC) 297; 2012 (3) CTC 770; 2012 (2) JabLJ 170; JT 2012 (5) SC 543; 2012 (5) MLJ(SC) 408; 2012 (3) MPLJ 595; 2012 (3) RCR(Civil) 158; 2012(5) SCALE 467; [2012] 6 S.C.R. 661

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Constitution

Keywords: Constitutionality, Andhra Pradesh Electricity Reform Act, 1998, Indian Stamp (Madhya Pradesh Amendment) Act, 2002, Indian Stamp (Madhya Pradesh Amendment) Act, 1997

Summary: Constitution - Civil Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 14 - Indian Stamp Act, 1899, art. 45(d) - Constitutionality of art. 45(d) of Schedule 1-A - Unreasonable classification - Petitioners sought declaration that art. 45(d) of Schedule 1-A of the 1899 Act which was brought in by the 2002 Act be declared ultra vires - Petitioners submitted that the distinction between an agent who was a blood relation and who was an outsider carved out in art. 45(d) was legally impermissible and the provision violated art. 14 of the Constitution as it sought to create unreasonable classification - HC accepted the constitutional challenge to art. 45(d) of Schedule 1-A of the 1899 Act and held that the said provision was violative of art. 14 of the Constitution - Hence, instant appeal - Whether HC was justified in declaring art. 45(d) of Schedule 1-A of the 1899 Act as unconstitutional being violative of art. 14 of the Constitution - Held, legislative idea behind art. 45(d) of Schedule 1-A of the 1899 Act was to curb tendency of transferring immovable properties through power of attorney and inappropriate documentation - By making a provision like this, the State Govt. sought to collect stamp duty on such indirect and inappropriate mode of transfer by providing that power of attorney given to a person other than kith or kin, without consideration, authorizing such person to sell immovable property situated in Madhya Pradesh would attract stamp duty at two per cent on the market value of the property which was subject matter of power of attorney - In effect, by bringing in this law, it sought to levy stamp duty on such ostensible document, the real intention of which was the transfer of immovable property - Classification, thus, could not be said to be without any rationale - It had a direct nexus to the object of the 1899 Act - Conclusion of the HC, therefore, that the impugned provision was arbitrary, unreasonable and irrational was unsustainable - Appeals allowed.
