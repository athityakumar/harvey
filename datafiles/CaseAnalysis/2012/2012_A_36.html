Amit Kapoor v Ramesh Chander & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

13 September 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 309; (2012) 9 SCC 460; 2012 ALL MR (Cri) 3806; 2012 (4) JCC 2885; JT 2012 (9) SC 312; 2012 (4) RCR(Criminal) 377; 2012(9) SCALE 58; [2012] 7 S.C.R. 988; 2013 (1) WLN(SC) 34

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 107, 306 and 448 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 397 and 482 - Abatement of suicide - Complicity of offence - Quashing of criminal proceedings - Jurisdiction - Challenged - Respondents (accused) allegedly committed offences u/s. 306 and 448 of IPC - Trial Court framed charges against respondents u/ss. 306 and 448 of IPC - Aggrieved respondents filed a revision petition before HC - HC partly allowed petition and quashed charge framed u/s. 306 of IPC - Aggrieved appellant (complainant and son of deceased) filed instant appeal - Appellant contended that HC had exceeded and not appropriately exercised its jurisdiction u/ss. 397 and 482 of CrPC in quashing the charge framed against respondent (accused) u/s. 306 of IPC - Whether HC rightly quashed criminal proceedings, u/s. 397 and 482 of CrPC - Held, revisional jurisdiction of HC was a very limited one and could not be exercised in a routine manner - Inbuilt restriction was that it should not be against an interim or interlocutory order - Court had to keep in mind that exercise of revisional jurisdiction itself should not lead to injustice ex facie - Revisional jurisdiction u/s. 482 of CrPC, particularly while dealing with framing of a charge, had to be more limited - U/s. 227 and 228 of CrPC Court was required to consider the 'record of the case' and documents submitted and, after hearing parties, might either discharge the accused or where it appeared to Court and in its opinion there was ground for presuming that accused had committed an offence, it should frame the charge - Once facts and ingredients of the Section existed, then Court would be right in presuming that there was ground to proceed against accused and frame the charge accordingly - At the stage of framing of charge, Court should form an opinion that accused was certainly guilty of committing an offence, was an approach which was impermissible in terms of s. 228 of CrPC - At the initial stage of framing of a charge, Court was concerned not with proof but with a strong suspicion that accused had committed an offence, which, if put to trial, could prove him guilty - All that Court had to see was that material on record and facts would be compatible with innocence of accused or not - Final test of guilt was not to be applied at that stage - Jurisdiction u/s. 397 of CrPC was a very limited one - Legality, proprietary or correctness of an order passed by a Court was the very foundation of exercise of jurisdiction u/s. 397 of CrPC, but ultimately it required justice to be done - If records disclosed commission of a criminal offence and ingredients of offence were satisfied, then such criminal proceedings could not be quashed merely because a civil wrong was been committed - Power u/s. 397 and 482 of CrPC could not be invoked to stifle or scuttle a legitimate prosecution - Factual foundation and ingredients of an offence being satisfied, Court would not either dismiss a complaint or quash such proceedings in exercise of its inherent or original jurisdiction - Where factual foundation for an offence was laid down, Courts should be reluctant and should not hasten to quash proceedings even on the premise that one or two ingredients have not stated or did not appear to be satisfied if there was substantial compliance to the requirements of offence - In instant case, it was evident from findings recorded by HC, HC itself was of opinion that clear facts and correctness of allegations made could be examined only upon full trial, where was the need for HC to quash the charge u/s. 306 of IPC at that stage - Framing of charge was a kind of tentative view that Trial Court forms in terms of s. 228 of CrPC, which was subject to final culmination of proceedings - Merely because there was civil transaction between parties would not by itself alter the status of allegations constituting criminal offence - Instant case was not a case where allegations were so predominately of a civil nature that it would have eliminated criminal intent and liability - Fact was not even disputed that deceased committed suicide and left a suicide note - Ingredients of s. 306 of IPC appered to exist in instant case in terms of language of s. 228 of CrPC, subject to trial - HC noticed that perusal of suicide note brought to fore the fact that respondent (accused) was not only named but his illegal occupation of house of deceased was stated to be one of primary reasons for deceased in committing suicide - Documents clearly show that respondents had brought in existence circumstances which, as claimed by prosecution, led to extreme step of suicide being took by deceased - HC could pronounce whether the offence falls within ambit and scope of s. 306 of IPC or not - Whether respondents misrepresented and was an attempt to harass deceased and her family which ultimately led to her suicide was a question to be examined by Trial Court - Matter though of some civil nature, but were so intricately connected with criminal nature and have elements of criminality - Grabbing of property, as alleged in suicide note and statement made by son of deceased as well as getting blank papers signed and not giving monies due to them were the circumstances stated to have led to suicide of deceased - HC was not expected to form even a firm opinion at this stage but a tentative view that would evoke the presumption u/s.228 of CrPC - Findings of HC delved into the field of appreciation and evaluation of evidence which was beyond jurisdiction, either revisional or inherent, of HC u/ss. 397 and 482 of CrPC - State of Bihar v. Ramesh Singh, 1977 Indlaw SC 50, referred to - Impugned order of HC was set aside - Appeal allowed.
