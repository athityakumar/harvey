Budhi Singh v State of Himachal Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

13 December 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 555; (2012) 13 SCC 663; 2013 CRLJ 962; JT 2012 (12) SC 460; 2012(12) SCALE 393; [2012] 11 S.C.R. 848

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302 and 304 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant allegedly committed offence of murder - Appellant was convicted by Trial Court u/s. 302 of IPC - Hence instant appeal - Whether conviction of appellant u/s. 302 of IPC was sustainable - Held, there was no prosecution evidence to show that there was animosity between the deceased and the accused or there was any other motive much less a pre-meditation to kill the accused - They had been living in the same house for years - No unpleasant incident or physical fight was stated to have been reported to the Police in the past - If one examined the cumulative effect of the prosecution evidence while keeping the relationship of the parties in mind and the factum of the deceased being in drunken state abusing and assaulting his father, it could reasonably be inferred that there was sudden and grave provocation to the accused - Son would normally not tolerate that his father be insulted, much less assaulted - Of course, the weapon used in crime was used with the knowledge that it could cause grievous hurt endangering the life or even cause death of the deceased but such weapon was most easily available in houses - Premeditation and intention to kill were two vital circumstances amongst others which were to be considered by the Court before holding the accused guilty of offence u/s. 302 or 304 IPC - At the cost of repetition, HC noticed that from the prosecution evidence, it was not established that the accused had the intention to kill the deceased or it was premeditated crime - Respondent/State had contended that the very fact that the accused had come out with tobru completely established the intention to kill and, thus, the offence would fall u/s. 302 IPC - It could not be disputed that the accused came out with tobru but, at the same time, it was also clear that it was the most easily available weapon in that part of the hills and was used regularly by the communities - Beyond that factor, there was no evidence of animosity, premeditation or intention to kill - Accused did give blow by tobru on the head of the deceased which proved fatal - That was result of the grave and sudden provocation where father of both the deceased and the accused was being abused, assaulted and ill-treated by the deceased, who was in drunken state - Thus, in the facts of instant case, sudden and grave provocation took place which would bring the offence within the ambit of exception 1 of s. 300 IPC and hence u/s. 304 Part I IPC as the accused had caused such bodily injury to the deceased which, to his knowledge, was likely to cause death as he had inflicted injuries on the head of the deceased - Rampal Singh v. State of Uttar Pradesh 2012 Indlaw SC 220, relied on - Appeal disposed of.
