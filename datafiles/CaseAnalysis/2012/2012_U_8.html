N.K. Bajpai v Union Of India & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

15 March 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 98; (2012) 4 SCC 653; AIR 2012 SC 1310; 2012 (3) AWC 2184; 2012 (2) CLT(SC) 54; 2012 (2) CTC 449; 2012 (278) E.L.T. 3; JT 2012 (3) SC 354; 2012 (3) MLJ(SC) 1184; [2012] 2 S.C.R. 433; 2012 (3) SLR 823

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution

Keywords: Right To Practise, Practicing, Indian Bar Councils Act, 1926, Constitutionality, Legal Practitioners Act, 1879, Maharashtra Restoration of Lands to Scheduled Tribes Act, 1974, Poor Removal Act, 1846, Bar Council of India Rules, 1975, Karnataka Land Reforms Amendment Act, 1974, West Bengal State Health Service Act, 1990

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 14, 19(1)(g) and 21 - Customs Act, 1962, 129(6) and 146(A) - Advocates Act, 1961, s. 30 - Bar Councils Act, 1926 - Amendment - Restriction to appear - Violation of Fundamental Right - Judicial Review - Constitutional Validity - Appellant joined Indian Customs and Central Excise Service, Class - I and served for several years in different capacities - Appellant demitted his office as Member (Technical) of CEGAT and enrolled as an advocate - S. 129(6) of Act, was introduced in terms of which, the members of the Tribunal were debarred from appearing, acting or pleading before it - Ministry respondent negatively to representation of appellants, aggrieved appellants filed Writ Petition before HC and DB dismissed Writ petitions - Aggrieved instant appeals - Appellant contended that s. 129(6) of Act was ultra vires art. 14, 19(1)(g) and 21 of Constitution - It was further contended that, in any event, s. 129(6) of Act, had no applicability to appellants, in view of fact that amendment was prospective, and when appellants were appointed to Tribunal as well as when they demitted office, said provision was not a part of Act - Whether introduction of s.129 was ultra vires of Constitution - Held, right to practice law was a statutory right and was restricted one and was controlled by provisions of 1961, Act as well as rules framed by the Bar Council - Statutory right cannot be placed at a higher pedestal to a fundamental right - Even fundamental right was subject to restriction and control, it was not possible to imagine a right without restriction and controls in present society - Appellants were enrolled as advocates as well as when they started practicing as advocates, their right was subject to limitations under any applicable Act or under constitutional limitations, as case may be, restriction in present case was a clear example where right to practice before a limited forum was being taken away while leaving all other forums open for practice by appellants - S. 129(6) places a restriction, which was reasonable and valid restriction, provisions of s. 146A of Act would have to be read in conjunction with and harmoniously to s. 129(6) of Act and person who earns a disqualification under this provision cannot derive any extra benefit contrary to s. 129(6) of Act from reading of s. 146A of Act, thus, SC rejected contention - Appeals dismissed.
