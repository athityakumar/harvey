Society For Un-Aided Private Schools Of Rajasthan v U.O.I. & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

12 April 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

S.H. Kapadia, Swatanter Kumar, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 607; (2012) 6 SCC 1; 2012 (4) AWC 4260; 2012 (6) Bom.C.R. 711; JT 2012 (4) SC 137; 2012 (3) MLJ(SC) 993; 2012 (2) RCR(Civil) 775; [2012] 2 S.C.R. 715

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Education; Women & Children

Keywords: Compulsory Education, Central Educational Institutions (Reservation in Admission) Act, 2006, Constitution (Fourth Amendment) Act, 1955, National Rural Employment Guarantee Act, 2005, Commissions for Protection of Child Rights Act, 2005, Constitution (Ninety Third Amendment) Act, 2005, Constitution (Eighty-first Amendment) Act, 2000, Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 1986, Constitution (Eighty-third Amendment) Bill, 1997, Constitution (Seventy seventh Amendment) Act, 1995, Constitution (Ninety-third Amendment) Bill, 2001

Summary: Constitution - Education - Practice & Procedure - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 15, 16, 19, 21, 24, 25, 26, 28, 29, 30, 31C, 340, 41, 45, 46, 47, 51A(k) - Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009, ss. 12, 13, 14, 16, 17, 18(3), 2, 21, 22, 23, 24, 28, 29, 3(1), 30, 34, 35, 35(1), 38 - Free Education - Violation of Articles - Ultra Vires - Validity - Govt. passed an Act by making education a fundamental right of every child between ages of 6 and 14 and specified minimum norms in elementary schools, by passing Act it provided 25% seats in all private schools to children from poor families, also prohibits all unrecognized schools from practice, and makes provisions for no donation or capitation fees and no interview of child or parent for admission - Act further provides that no child shall be held back, expelled, or required to pass a board examination until completion of elementary education, also provided provision for special training of school drop-outs to bring them up to par with students of same age - Several private colleges dissented with Act passed by Govt. and instant petition was filed to declare Act passed by Govt. as ultra vires and in violation of articles of Constitution - Petitioners had wholeheartedly welcomed introduction of art. 21A of Constitution and acknowledged it as a revolutionary step providing universal elementary education for all children, controversy in all these cases was not with regard to validity of art. 21A, but mainly centered around its interpretation and validity of ss. 3, 12(1)(b), 12(1)(c) and some other related provisions of Act, which casted obligation on all elementary educational institutions to admit children of age 6 to 14 years from their neighbourhood, on principle of social inclusiveness - Petitioners also challenged certain other provisions purported to interfere with administration, management and functioning of those institutions - Petitioners, submitted that art. 21A casts an obligation on state and state alone to provide free and compulsory education to children upto age of 6 to 14 years, which would be evident from plain reading of art. 21A r/w art. 45 - Further art. 21A calls for horizontal application of sanction on state actors so as to give effect to fundamental rights guaranteed to people - Whether impugned law infringes a fundamental right within limits justified by directive principles or whether it goes beyond them - Art. 21A had used expression 'State shall provide' not 'provide for' hence Constitutional obligation to provide education was on State and not on non-state actors, expression was clear and unambiguous and to interpret that expression to mean that constitutional obligation or responsibility was on private unaided educational institutions also - Obligation of State to provide free and compulsory education was without any limitation, Parliament in its wisdom had not used expression 'provide for', if preposition 'for' had been used then duty of State would be only to provide education to those who require it but to provide for education or rather to see that it was provided - State had got a constitutional responsibility to see that our children were given quality education - Provisions of statute shall not remain a dead letter - Children in future had to compete with their counter-parts elsewhere in world at each and every level, both in curricular and extra-curricular fields - Quality education and overall development of child was of prime importance upon which entire future of children and country rests - Hence, art. 21A, as such, does not cast any obligation on private unaided educational institutions to provide free and compulsory education to children of age 6 to 14 years, art. 21A casts constitutional obligation on State to provide free and compulsory education to children of age 6 to 14 years - Petition disposed of.
