Ravi Kapur v State Of Rajasthan

Supreme Court of India

16 August 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 447; (2012) 9 SCC 284; 2012 (3) ACC 722; AIR 2012 SC 2986; 2012 CRLJ 4403; 2012 (4) RCR(Criminal) 245; 2012 (4) RLW 3597; [2012] 10 S.C.R. 229

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 279, 337, 338 and 304-A - Rash and negligent driving - Death of passengers - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant allegedly committed offence of Rash driving or riding on public way, Causing hurt by act endangering life or personal safety and Punishment for culpable homicide not amounting to murder - Appellant was charged u/ss. 279, 337, 338 and 304-A IPC - However, Trial Court acquitted appellant - HC on the other hand convicted appellant under the same sections - Hence instant appeal - Whether conviction of appellant by HC was sustainable - Held, there were more than sufficient reasons for the HC to interfere with the judgment of acquittal recorded by the Trial Court - It was not case of non-availability of evidence or presence of material and serious contradictions proving fatal to the case of the prosecution - There was no plausible reason before the Trial Court to disbelieve the eye account given by PW2 and PW4 and the Court could not have ignored the fact that the accused was duly identified at the place of occurrence and even in the Court - Trial Court had certainly fallen in error of law and appreciation of evidence - Once the Trial Court had ignored material piece of evidence and failed to appreciate the prosecution evidence in its correct perspective, particularly when the prosecution had proved its case beyond reasonable doubt, then it would amount to failure of justice - In some cases, such error in appreciation of evidence might even amount to recording of perverse finding - HC might also notice at the cost of repetition that the Trial Court had first delivered its judgment convicting the accused of the offences - However, on appeal, the matter was remanded on two grounds, i.e., considering the effect of non-holding of test identification parade and not examining the doctor. Upon remand, the Trial Court had taken different view than what was taken by it earlier and it had acquitted the accused - That itself became ground for interference by the HC in the judgment of acquittal recorded by the Trial Court - From the judgment of the Trial Court, there did not appear to be any substantial discussion on the effect of non-holding of the test identification parade or the non-examination of the doctor - On the contrary, Trial Court passed its judgment on certain assumptions - None of the witnesses, not even the accused, in his statement, had stated that the jeep was at fast speed but still the Trial Court recorded finding that the jeep was at fast speed and was not being driven properly - Trial Court also recorded that suspicion arose as to whether appellant was actually driving the bus at the time of the accident or not and identification was very important - SC was unable to understand as to how the Trial Court could ignore the statement of the eye-witnesses, particularly when they were reliable, trustworthy and gave the most appropriate eye account of the accident - Appeal dismissed.
