Kotak Mahindra Bank Limited and others v Hindustan National Glass and Industrial Limited and others

Supreme Court of India

11 December 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Patnaik, Swatanter Kumar

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 531; (2013) 7 SCC 369; 2013 (2) ALD(SC) 72; 2013 (1) CompLJ 225; JT 2013 (1) SC 60; 2012(12) SCALE 144; [2012] 13 S.C.R. 697

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; RBI

Keywords: Loans And Advances, Credit Information Companies (Regulation) Act, 2005

Summary: Banking & Finance - RBI - Constitution - Constitution of Indian, 1950, arts. 19,14 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 403,415 - Circular - Willful Defaulter - Penal Sanctions - Sustainability - Appellant-bank sanctioned Derivatives/Forward Contracts facility to respondent no.1 for purpose of hedging foreign currency exposures which on behalf of respondent no.1-company, was accepted by its Joint Managing Director - Appellant and respondent no.1 entered into International Swaps and Derivatives Association (ISDA) Master Agreement and on request respondent no.1, appellant enhanced limit of Derivatives/Forward Contracts facility of the respondent no.1 - Again on request of respondent no.1, appellant increased limit for Derivatives/Forward Contracts facility to and informed respondent no.1 that a sum of Rs.2,43,12,000/- had become due and payable by respondent no.1 which respondent no.1, did not pay - RBI issued Master Circular on Willful Defaulters which contained instructions of RBI to banks and financial institutions regarding reporting of willful defaulters to other banks and financial institutions and measures to be imposed on willful defaulters by such banks and financial institutions - Appellant intimated respondent no.1 that it had classified respondent no.1 as a willful defaulter as it had defaulted to pay amount - Respondent no.1 replied contending that neither appellant was a 'lender' nor respondent no.1 was a 'borrower' within meaning of 'willful default' in Master Circular and, therefore, action under Master Circular could not be taken against respondent no.1 - Respondent No. 1 made several representations but non proved fruitful, aggrieved, respondent no.1 filed Writ Petition before HC, which was held that Master Circular applied only to lending transactions of a bank or financial institution and as in foreign exchange derivative transactions between appellant and respondent no.1, there was no such lending transactions and appellant was not lender and respondent no.1 was not borrower, respondent no.1 could not be declared as a willful defaulter in terms of Master Circular and accordingly no action could be taken against respondent no.1 under Master Circular - Hence instant appeal - Whether HC, was justified in holding that respondent No. 1 did not fall in category of willful defaulter nor appellant was Bank as per Master Circular issued by RBI - Held, Master Circular states that there was scope even under exiting legislations to initiate criminal action against willful defaulters depending upon facts and circumstances of case under provisions of ss. 403, 415 of IPC and banks and financial institutions were strictly advised to seriously and promptly consider initiating criminal action based on facts and circumstances of each case under above provisions of IPC, thus, Master Circular by itself does not have penal consequences, whereas ss. 403, 415 of IPC have penal consequences - Provisions of ss. 403, 415 of IPC obviously had to be strictly construed as these were penal provisions and would get attracted depending on facts and circumstances of each case, but provisions of Master Circular need not be strictly construed, hence Master Circular had to be construed not literally but in its context and words used in definition of 'willful defaulter' in Master Circular had to draw their meaning from context in which Master Circular had been issued - Hence, willful defaulters of parties of dues under a derivative transaction with a bank were covered by Master Circular - Application disposed of.
