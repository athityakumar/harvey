Jitender Kumar and another v State Of Haryana

Supreme Court of India

08 May 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 579; (2012) 6 SCC 204; 2012 CRLJ 3085; JT 2012 (5) SC 397; 2012 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 1929; 2012 (3) MLJ(Crl) 548; 2012 (3) RCR(Criminal) 736

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120-B, 302 r/w s. 34, 323 r/w s. 34, 342 - Murder - Medical evidence - Corroboration - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellants (accused) allegedly committed offences u/ss. 120-B, 302 r/w s.34, 323 r/w s. 34, 342 of IPC - Trial Court convicted appellants u/ss. 302 r/w s. 34, 120-B of IPC - However, acquitted for offences u/ss. 323 r/w s. 34 and 342 of IPC - Aggrieved husband of deceased (PW10) filed a petition before HC, for alleged against action took by Police against him and aggrieved appellants filed an appeal before HC - HC by impugned common judgment upheld the conviction order passed by Trial Court - Hence, instant appeals - Appellants contended that statement of sole witness was not trustworthy and that time of occurrence could not be validly related to expert medical evidence and that FIR was delayed - Whether impugned conviction order passed by Trial Court and upheld by HC was justified - Held, FIR itself was not the proof of a case, but was a piece of evidence which could be used for corroborating the case of prosecution - FIR need not be an encyclopedia of all facts and circumstances on which prosecution relies - It only had to state the basic case - Attending circumstances of each case would further have considerable bearing on application of settled principles to a given situation - In instant case, appellant 'J' was not named in FIR, a definite role was attributed to 'J' by PW10 (who was confined by 'J', and was husband of deceased) - Appellant 'J' was duly identified in Court by witnesses - PW10 and 11 both could not be stated to be planted witnesses - They were natural and reliable witnesses - Trial Court did not cast any doubt on material aspects of occurrence in crime committed by appellants - HC believed PW10, although it observed that PW10 behaved like a husband under fear and exhibited his paramount interest in property - Those observations did not in any way affect case of prosecution because incident, as narrated by prosecution witnesses and particularly by PW10 and 11, was corroborated by other expert evidence on record - Once Court found an accused guilty of s. 120B of IPC, where accused had conspired to commit an offence and actually committed offence with other accused with whom he conspired, they all should individually be punishable for the offence for which such conspiracy was hatched - Thus, by Trial Court was justified in convicting appellants for an offence u/s. 120B r/w s. 302 of IPC - Mere delay in lodging FIR might not prove fatal in all cases, but in given circumstances of a case, delay in lodging FIR could be one of factors which corrode the credibility of prosecution version - Delay in FIR could be a ground by itself for throwing away entire prosecution case - Court has to seek an explanation for delay and check the truthfulness of version put forward - Court was satisfied, then case of prosecution could not fail on that ground alone - Mere fact that appellants were residents of a village at some distance would be inconsequential - As per statement of witnesses, both appellants were seen by them in house of 'R' (accused) where deceased was murdered - It was unable to accept that presence of PW10 and 11 at the place of occurrence was doubtful and their statements were not trustworthy - Trial court and HC believed PW10 and PW11 and have returned a finding of guilt against appellants - Courts adversely commented upon conduct of witnesses but not with regard to material events of prosecution case -It was PW10's own house where murder was took place, thus, his presence in house could not be doubted in normal course - Evidence of PW3 (Doctor) categorically stated that it was possible that deceased was murdered between 1.00 to 1.30 a.m - That was duly corroborated by PW11 - Investigation conducted by PW6, PW12 and 13 indicated that deceased was murdered during that period - Judging time of death from contents of stomach, might not always be determinative test - It would require due corroboration from other evidence - If prosecution was able to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt and cumulatively, evidence of prosecution, including time of death, was proved beyond reasonable doubt and the same points towards guilt of accused, then it might not be appropriate for Court to wholly reject case of prosecution and to determine the time of death with reference to stomach contents of deceased - Determination of time of death with reference to stomach contents was one of relevant considerations - There was no evidence in instant case which established, with exactitude, the time at which appellants, deceased and eye-witness (PW11) had their dinner - Only evidence was that they had dinner late and not in early hours of evening - Evidence of PW3 remained unchallenged that death of deceased took place between said time - Ocular evidence of PW11 was corroborated by medical evidence and partially supported by statement of PW10 - Statements of PW10 and 11 could not be said to be doubtful or which could not be believed by Court - Impugned conviction was upheld - Appeals dismissed.
