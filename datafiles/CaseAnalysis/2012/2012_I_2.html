Union of India v Ibrahim Uddin and another

Supreme Court of India

17 July 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 212; (2012) 8 SCC 148; 2012(5) ALL MR 462; 2012 (94) ALR 895; 2012 (5) AWC 5003; 2012 (3) CLT(SC) 142; 2012 (4) RCR(Civil) 727; 2012 (117) RD 783; 2012(6) SCALE 476; [2012] 8 S.C.R. 35

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Cantonment Land Administration Rules, 1925

Summary: Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 41 r. 27, O. 11 and 12 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 58 and 114(g) - Specific Relief Act, 1963, s. 34 - Suit for declaration of title - Plaintiff filed suit seeking a decree for declaration that he was the owner of the suit property, making averments that the suit land originally had been with the Maratha Government - The ancestors of the plaintiff having close association with the Maratha Government, were made a grant in respect of the suit land in the year 1800 - Further submitted that the suit land was partitioned between the ancestors of the plaintiff in the year 1819 - As plaintiff being the only heir of 'X' and 'Y' became the absolute owner of the land - Defendant No. 1 contended that the suit land belonged to the Union of India a part of which has been leased out to several persons for agriculture work - Trial Court dismissed the suit and held that the plaintiff could not prove any kind of grant by the Maratha Government to his ancestors/great-grandfathers in the year 1800 - Further held that the plaintiff failed to prove the partition between his ancestors in 1819 - Plaintiff preferred the first appeal against said order - During the pendency of the said appeal, plaintiff preferred an application under O. 41 r. 27 of CPC for adducing additional evidence, i.e., Will executed by his maternal grandfather in his favour bequeathing the suit property - Said application was allowed by the First Appellate Court - First Appellate Court also held that defendant No.1 did not produce any document to show its title and failed to produce the original record, thus, adverse inference was drawn against it in view of the provisions of s. 114 (g) of 1872 Act - HC dismissed appeal filed against said order - Hence, the instant appeal - (A) Whether order of the First Appellate Court as affirmed by the HC could be upheld - Held, the First Appellate Court as well as the HC committed grave error in shifting the burden of proof on the defendant No.1, though it could have been exclusively on the plaintiff to prove his case - There was nothing on record to prove the grant/gift by the Maratha Government in favour of ancestors of plaintiff in the year 1800 - The alleged partition in the year 1819 among the ancestors of plaintiff even if had taken place, cannot be a proof of title of the plaintiff over the suit property as the pedigree has not been proved - The First Appellate Court committed a grave error in deciding the application under O. 41 r. 27 of CPC much prior to the hearing of the appeal - The First Appellate Court as well as the HC recorded a finding that the defendant No. 1 failed to prove its title over the suit land - The said courts did not realize that this was not the issue to be determined, rather the issue had been as to whether the plaintiff was the owner of the suit land - Hence, the impugned orders of First Appellate Court as well as the HC to be set aside - (B) Whether not filing a document in rebuttal of a document amounts to an admission - Held, s. 58 of 1872 Act provides that a fact may not need to be proved in any proceeding which the parties thereto agreed to admit at the hearing or which, before the hearing, they agree to admit by any writing under their hands or which they admitted by their pleading, even in that case court may, in its discretion, even if such a admission has been made by the party, require the fact admitted to be proved otherwise than by such admission - Admissions were not conclusive proof but may operate as estoppel against its maker - Documents were necessarily either proved by witness or marked on admission - The finding recorded by First Appellate Court that defendant No.1 failed to produce any document in rebuttal of the Will was not only wrong but preposterous - (C) Application under O. 41 r. 27 of CPC - Held, under O. 41, r. 27 of CPC, application for taking additional evidence on record at a belated stage cannot be filed as a matter of right - The court can consider such an application with circumspection, provided it was covered under either of the prerequisite condition incorporated in the statutory provisions itself - The discretion was to be exercised by the court judicially taking into consideration the relevance of the document in respect of the issues involved in the case and the circumstances under which such an evidence could not be led in the court below - In case the court comes to the conclusion that the application filed comes within the 4 corners of the statutory provisions itself, the evidence may be taken on record, however, the court must record reasons as on what basis such an application has been allowed - However, the application should not be moved at a belated stage - (D) S. 34 of 1963 Act - Held, it was not permissible to claim the relief of declaration without seeking consequential relief - In the instant case, suit for declaration of title of ownership had been filed though, the plaintiff was admittedly not in possession of the suit property - Thus, the suit was barred by the provision of s. 34 of 1963 Act and, therefore, ought to have been dismissed solely on this ground - The HC though framed a substantial question on this point but for unknown reasons did not consider it proper to decide the same - Appeal allowed.
