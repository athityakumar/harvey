Aneeta Hada and others v Godfather Travels and Tours Private Limited and another

Supreme Court of India

27 April 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Dalveer Bhandari, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 508; (2012) 5 SCC 661; AIR 2012 SC 2795; 2012 (3) Bom.C.R. 481; [2012] 172 Comp Cas 75; 2012 (2) CompLJ 433; 2012 CRLJ 2525; 2012 (5) CTC 101; 2012 (2) JCC (NI) 113; JT 2012 (4) SC 489; 2012 (4) MahLJ 527; 2012 (3) MPLJ 314; 2012 (2) RCR(Criminal) 854; [2012] 5 S.C.R. 503

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Negotiable Instruments Laws (Amendment) Act, 1988

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Banking & Finance - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, ss. 138, 141 - Information Technology Act, 2000, ss. 67, 85 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 292 - Cheque bounce - Prosecution of company - Vicarious liability - Challenged - Appellants (accused) were authorized signatories and directors of companies, were prosecuted for offence u/s. 292 of IPC - Appellants challenged initiation of prosecution in a quash petition before HC - HC held that offences were made out against company and its directors u/s. 67 r/w s. 85 of the Act - Aggrieved appellants field appeal before SC - There was difference of opinion between Two-Judge Bench on issue that whether an authorised signatory of a company would be liable for prosecution u/s. 138 of 1881 Act without the company being arraigned as an accused - Hence, instant reference - Appellants contended that Company was not arrayed as an accused, legal fiction created by legislature in s. 141 of 1881 Act would not get attracted - Whether any person who was mentioned in ss. 141(1) and (2) of 1881 Act could be prosecuted without the company being impleaded as an accused - on a reading s. 141 of 1881 Act, it was plain as day that if a person who commits offence u/s. 138 of 1881 was a company, company and every person in charge of and responsible to company for conduct of business of company at the time of commission of offence was deemed to be guilty of offence - First proviso carved out under what circumstances the criminal liability would not be fastened - Ss. 141(2) enlarged criminal liability by incorporating concepts of connivance, negligence and consent that engulfs many categories of officers - It was worth noting that in both provisions, there was a 'deemed' concept of criminal liability - S. 139 of 1881 Act created a presumption in favour of holder - Said provision had to be read in conjunction with s. 118(a) of 1881 Act that dealt with special rules of evidence - S. 140 of 1881 Act stipulated the defence which might not be allowed in a prosecution u/s. 138 of 18881 Act - Thus, there was a deemed fiction in relation to criminal liability, presumption in favour of holder, and denial of a defence in respect of certain aspects - S. 141 of 1881 Act used term 'person' and referred it to a company - There was no trace of doubt that company was a juristic person - Concept of corporate criminal liability was attracted to a corporation and company and it was so luminescent from language employed u/s. 141 of 1881 Act - It was apposite to note that 1881 Act was one where company itself and certain categories of officers in certain circumstances were deemed to be guilty of offence - It was bounden duty of Court to ascertain for what purpose the legal fiction was created - It was duty of Court to imagine the fiction with all real consequences and instances unless prohibited from doing so - Use of term 'deemed' had to be read in its context and further fullest logical purpose and import were to be understood - It was because in modern legislation, term 'deemed' was used for manifold purposes - Word 'deemed' used in s. 141 of 1881 Act applied to company and persons responsible for Acts of company - It crystallized the corporate criminal liability and vicarious liability of a person who was in charge of company - S. 141 of 1881 Act made other persons vicariously liable for commission of an offence on part of company - Vicarious liability gets attracted when condition precedent laid down in s. 141 of 1881 Act stood satisfied - Power of punishment was vested in legislature and that was absolute in s. 141 of 1881 Act which clearly speaks of commission of offence by company - For maintaining prosecution u/s. 141 of 1881 Act, arraigning of a company as an accused was imperative -Anatomy of provisions pertained to s. 141 of 1881 Act would squarely apply to 2000 Act - Thus director could not be held liable for offence u/s.85 of 2000 Act - Impuned proceedings against appellants were quashed - Company was not arraigned as an accused, thus proceeding as initiated in existing incarnation was not maintainable either against company or against director - Sheoratan Agarwal and Another v. State of Madhya Pradesh, 1984 Indlaw SC 350 and Anil Hada v. Indian Acrylic Limited, 1999 Indlaw SC 853, overruled - Appeals allowed.
