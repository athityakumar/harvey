 Sahara India Real Estate Corp. Ltd. & Ors v Securities & Exchange Board Of India & Anr

Supreme Court of India

11 September 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

S.H. Kapadia, D.K. Jain, Surinder Singh Nijjar, Ranjana Prakash Desai, J. S. KHEHAR

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 289; (2012) 10 SCC 603; AIR 2012 SC 3829; 2013 (7) Bom.C.R. 929; 2012 (3) CLT(SC) 343; 2012 (4) CompLJ 197; 2012 CRLJ 4584; JT 2012 (9) SC 123; 2012 (6) MLJ(SC) 772; 2012 (4) RCR(Civil) 801; 2012(8) SCALE 541; [2012] 12 S.C.R. 256

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Media & Entertainment; Securities Exchange Board of India

Keywords: Constitutional Remedy, Prior restraint, Misuse of powers of pre-censorship

Summary: (A) Securities Exchange Board of India - Constitution - Media & Entertainment - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19 (1) (a), 19 (2), 21, 129 and 215 - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971 - Prior restraint - Misuse of powers of pre- censorship - Constitutional remedy - Tribunal passed order dt.18-10-2011, directing appellant to refund amounts invested with them in certain Optionally Fully Convertible Bonds (OFCD) with interest to OFCD holders - Aggrieved by said order, appellant filed instant appeals - On 28-11-2011, SC issued show cause notice to respondent no. 1/SEBI directing appellant to put on affidavit as to how they intend to secure the liabilities incurred to the OFCD holders - Appellant filed affidavit explaining the manner in which it proposed to secure its liability to OFCD holders during the pendency of appeals - SC directed parties that try to reach consensus with respect to an acceptable security in the form of an unencumbered asset - On 7-2-2012, appellant addressed a personal letter to respondent no. 1 enclosing the proposal with details of security to secure repayment of OFCD to investors as pre-condition for stay of the impugned orders dt.23-06-2011 and 18-10-2011 pending hearing of appeals together with the Valuation Certificate indicating fair market value of the assets proposed to be offered as security - There was also an official communication enclosing the said proposal - Subsequently details of said proposal was telecasted on news channel, which was obviously not meant for public circulation - Said channel also named the valuer who had done the valuation of the assets proposed to be offered as security - Appellant contended that disclosure to the media was by respondent no. 1 in breach of confidentiality, which was denied by respondent no. 1 - Hence, appellants filed applications, seeking directions that appropriate guidelines be framed with regard to reporting (in the electronic and print media) of matters which were sub-judice in a court including public disclosure of documents forming part of court proceedings - Respondent no. 1 also filed application denying alleged disclosure - (Whether 'postponement orders' constitute restriction u/art.19 (1) (a) of constitution and whether such restriction was saved u/art. 19 (2) of constitution - Held, orders of postponement should be ordered for a limited duration and without disturbing the content of the publication - Orders of postponement, in the absence of any other alternative measures such as change of venue or postponement of trial, satisfy the requirement of justification u/art. 19 (2) of Constitution and they also help the Courts to balance conflicting societal interests of right to know vis-a-vis another societal interest in fair administration of justice - Excessive prejudicial publicity leading to usurpation of functions of the Court not only interfered with administration of justice which was sought to be protected u/art. 19 (2) of constitution, it also prejudiced or interfered with a particular legal proceedings - In such case, Courts were duty bound under inherent jurisdiction, subject to above parameters, to protect the presumption of innocence which was now recognised by SC as a human right u/art. 21 of constitution, subject to the applicant proving displacement of such a presumption in appropriate proceedings - Further, postponement orders should be integrally connected to the outcome of the proceedings including guilt or innocence of the accused, which would depend on the facts of each case - Hence, postponement orders fall u/art. 19 (2) of constitution and they satisfy the test of reasonableness - Applications disposed of.

(B) Securities Exchange Board of India - Constitution - Media & Entertainment - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19 (1) (a), 19 (2), 21, 129 and 215 - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971 - Whether parties had right to approach HC/SC to seek an order of postponement of the offending publication - Held, anyone, be he an accused or an aggrieved person, who genuinely apprehended on the basis of the content of the publication and its effect, an infringement of his/ her rights u/art. 21 of Constitution to a fair trial and all that it comprehended, would be entitled to approach an appropriate writ court and seek an order of postponement of the offending publication/ broadcast or postponement of reporting of certain phases of the trial (including identity of the victim or the witness or the complainant), and that the court should grant such preventive relief, on a balancing of the right to a fair trial and art. 19 (1) (a) seek an order of postponement of the offending publication, rights, principles of necessity and proportionality that such orders of postponement should be for short duration and should be applied only in cases of real and substantial risk of prejudice to the proper administration of justice or to the fairness of trial - Such neutralizing device (balancing test) would not be an unreasonable restriction and on the contrary would fall within the proper constitutional framework - Further, in instant case, SC was only declaring u/art. 141, the Constitutional limitations on free speech u/art.19 (1) (a) of Constitution, in the context of art.21 of Constitution - Exercise undertaken by SC was an exercise of exposition of constitutional limitations u/art.141 r/w art.129 or art. 215 - What constitutes an offending publication would depend on the decision of the court on case to case basis - Hence, guidelines on reporting could not be framed across the Board - Right to negotiate and settle in confidence was a right of a citizen and was equated to a right of the accused to defend himself in a criminal trial - In instant case, appellant complained to SC on the basis of breach of confidentiality by the Media - In the circumstances, it could not be contended that there was no lis - SC expounded the Constitutional limitations on free speech u/art. 19 (1) (a) in the context of arts.21 and 141 r/w arts.129 and 215 of Constitution which preserved the inherent jurisdiction of the Courts of Record in relation to contempt law - Applications disposed of.
