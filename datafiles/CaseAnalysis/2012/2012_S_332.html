Attar Singh v State of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

14 December 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Gyan Sudha Misra, Swatanter Kumar

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 570; (2013) 11 SCC 719; JT 2012 (12) SC 450; 2013 (2) RCR(Criminal) 294; 2012(12) SCALE 678; [2012] 13 S.C.R. 1213

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Anger

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302, 498-A, 304, Part II -Murder - Conviction - Sustainability -Appellant (accused) allegedly killed his wife (deceased) by hitting her on head with a woodenlog, as appellant suspected loyalty and character of deceased and committed offences u/ss. 302 and 498-A of IPC - Trial Court acquitted appellant for offence u/s. 498-A of IPC - However, convicted appellant for offences u/s. 302 of IPC - Aggrieved appellant filed appeal before HC, which was dismissed - Hence, instant appeal - Appellants contended that offence alleged to have committed by appellant ought to be brought down within ambit of s. 304 Part II of IPC and that no intention to kill deceased as the death had occurred on account of single blow which was not the result of a pre-plan or pre-meditation - Whether impugned conviction of appellants was justified - Held, evidence did further indicate that on the date and time of incident, appellant had not indulged in pre-planning the incident in any manner so as to eliminate his wife by killing her - Evidence of other witnesses indicated that incident of beating had not happened in past and daughter of appellant and deceased deposed that there were heated exchange of words between couple on the date of incident and appellant heaped abuses on his wife and then picked up a wooden log in a fit of anger by which he hit the deceased as a result of which she sustained head injury and bled profusely which lead to her death - Thus, intention to kill his wife and the knowledge that she would be killed due to hard hit blow by log of wood surely could not be ruled out - Appellant although might not be attributed with the intention to kill his wife, sufficient knowledge that his act would result into killing her was definitely there in the appellant's mind and he in fact gave vent to his feeling by finally killing her when he hit her with a wooden log to take revenge for her alleged infidelity without realising that suspicion of her fidelity was not proved and even if it did, that gave no right to him to kill his wife in a brutal manner by hitting her hard enough with a log of wood with such intensity which was sufficient in the ordinary course of nature to kill the victim - But it could not be overlooked or ignored that intensity with which appellant hit his wife after abusing her was indicative of fact that he was not oblivious of consequence which would have resulted from his violent act of beating his wife with a log of wood - Thus, it would inferred that appellant had sufficient knowledge about consequence of his heinous act at least to the extent that it was sufficient in ordinary course of nature to cause death of his wife - Appellant was thus fully aware of consequence that this would result in a serious consequence and in fact it did result in said manner since the wife died as a result of injury inflicted on her - Evidence did not disclose in any manner that appellant had come with a pre-meditated mind to kill his wife, but it was only in course of hot exchange of words and abuses which mindlessly drove him to take the extreme step of beating his wife with a log of wood with such force and intensity that she sustained head injury, profusely bled and finally died on spot - Although conviction and sentence of appellant might not be sustainable u/s. 302 of IPC, it could not be scaled down to s. 304 Part II of IPC - Appellant was fit to be convicted and sentenced u/s. 304 Part I of IPC - Impugned conviction was set aside and appellant was convicted u/s. 304 Part I of IPC for 10 years of imprisonment - Appeal partly allowed.
