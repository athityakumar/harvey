The Deputy Inspector General Of Police & Anr v S. Samuthiram

Supreme Court of India

30 November 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan, Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 491; (2013) 1 SCC 598; AIR 2013 SC 14; 2013 (1) Bom.C.R. 733; 2013 (1) CTC 931; 2013 (136) FLR 99; JT 2012 (11) SC 485; 2013 (1) RCR(Criminal) 329; 2012(11) SCALE 420; [2012] 11 S.C.R. 174; 2013 (3) SLR 554

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure; Women & Children

Keywords: Woman, Obscene, Tamil Nadu Police Subordinate Service (Disciplinary and Appeal) Rules, 1955, Tami Nadu Prohibition of Eve-Teasing Ordinance, 1998, Tamil Nadu Prohibition of Eve-Teasing Act, 1998

Summary: Service - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 509 - Tamil Nadu Police Subordinate Service (Disciplinary and Appeal) Rules, 1955, r. 3(b) - Tamil Nadu Prohibition of Eve-Teasing Act, 1998, s. 4 - Eve Teasing - Domestic Inquiry - Removal from Service - Acquittal - Reinstatement - Sustainability - Eve teasing lead to death of a women in State which led Govt. to bring the Act - Complainant against respondent was that he had approached lady with dubious intention and disclosed that he was police and threatened that if lady does not accompany him he shall register false case against them - Criminal case u/s. 509 and s. 4 of Act was registered and on domestic enquiry DSP found charges against respondent proved and dismissed respondent from service - Respondent appealed before Tribunal which was dismissed, during pendency of appeal respondent was acquittal of criminal case - Respondent filed appeal before HC, which took the view that if a criminal case and departmental proceedings against an official were based on same set of facts and evidence and criminal case ended in an honourable acquittal and not on technical grounds, imposing punishment of removal of delinquent official from service, based on findings of domestic enquiry would not be legally sustainable - Hence instant appeal - Whether HC, was justified in holding that imposing punishment of removal from service even after criminal case ended in acquittal was sustainable - Held, in absence of any provision in service rule for reinstatement, if an employee was honourably acquitted by a Criminal Court, no right was conferred on employee to claim any benefit including reinstatement, reason was that standard of proof required for holding a person guilty by a criminal court and enquiry conducted by way of disciplinary proceeding was entirely different - In a criminal case, onus of establishing guilt of accused was on prosecution and if it failed to establish guilt beyond reasonable doubt, accused was assumed to be innocent - It was settled law that strict burden of proof required to establish guilt in a criminal court was not required in a disciplinary proceedings and preponderance of probabilities was sufficient - There may be cases where a person was acquitted for technical reasons or prosecution giving up other witnesses since few of other witnesses turned hostile etc. - In present case prosecution did not take steps to examine many of crucial witnesses on ground that complainant and his wife turned hostile, Court, therefore, acquitted accused giving benefit of doubt - In cases where service rules provide that on registration of a criminal case, an employee could be kept under suspension and on acquittal, he be reinstated - There may be cases where service rules provide in spite of domestic enquiry, if criminal court acquits an employee honourably, he could be reinstated , issue whether an employee had to be reinstated in service or not depends upon question whether service rules contain any such provision for reinstatement and not as a matter of right, such provisions were absent in Service Rules - HC, was not justified in setting aside punishment imposed in departmental proceedings as against respondent - Appeal allowed.
