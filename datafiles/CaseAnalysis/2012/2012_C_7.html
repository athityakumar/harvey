Ranjan Dwivedi v C.B.I. through the Director General

Supreme Court of India

17 August 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

H.L. Dattu, Chandramauli Kumar Prasad

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 383; (2012) 8 SCC 495; AIR 2012 SC 3217; 2012 CRLJ 4206; 2012 (5) CTC 83; 2012 (4) RCR(Criminal) 880; [2012] 7 S.C.R. 329

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Criminal Trial - Delay - Effect - Petitioners accused were tried for the assassination of then Union Railway Minister - Petitioners filed petition for quashing of charges and proceeding before SC as it was pending for 12 years - Petition was disposed of and directed Trial Court to complete trial on day to day basis - Trial was still pending despite direction of SC - Trial was pending for the 37yrs in Trial Court - Hence instant Petitions - Whether the Trial in question was required to be quashed due to violation of petitioners fundamental right of speedy trial - Held, Constitution did not declare that right to speedy trial as fundamental right - Guarantee of speedy trial was intended to avoid oppression and prevent delay by imposing on Court and prosecution obligation to proceed with trial with reasonable dispatch - Reasons for delay was one of factors which Courts would normally assess in determining as to whether particular accused had been deprived of his or her right to speedy trial including party to whom delay was attributable - Such delay or delays should not be violative of accused's right to speedy trial and needs to be excluded while deciding whether there was unreasonable and unexplained delay - Note produced was not certified with copies of order sheets maintained by Trial Court , since they were not disputed by other party information furnished was as authentic - Note revealed that prosecution apart from seeking 4-5 adjournments right from 1991 till 2012 was not responsible for delay in any manner - Therefore delay in trial of petitioners from 1991 to 2012 was solely attributable to petitioners and other accused persons - There was no basis to hold that right to speedy trial should be quantified into specified number of days, months or years - Mere passage of time was not sufficient to establish denial of right to speedy trial but a lengthy delay which was presumptively prejudicial triggered examination of other factors to determine whether rights have been violated - Delay was occasional by exceptional circumstances - It might not be due to failure of prosecution or by systemic failure but there was good cause for failure to complete trial and such delay was not violative of right of accused for speedy trial - Therefore, direction that required to Trial Court was to take up case on day to day basis and conclude proceedings as early as possible without granting unnecessary and unwarranted adjournments - Petitions dismissed.
