State of Gujarat and another v Gujarat Revenue Tribunal Bar Association and another

Supreme Court of India

16 October 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 632; (2012) 10 SCC 353; 2012 (8) MLJ(SC) 96; [2012] 10 S.C.R. 816; 2012 (6) SLR 568

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Service

Keywords: Bombay Land Revenue Code, 1879, Bombay Revenue Tribunal Act, 1939, Bombay Revenue Tribunal Act, 1957, Gujarat Agricultural Lands Ceiling Act, 1960, Bombay Land Revenue Code, 1874, Bombay Jagirdari and Other Tenure Abolition Act, Gujarat Agriculture and Land Ceiling Act, 1961, Gujarat Private Forest Act, Bombay Public Trusts (Gujarat) Rules, 1961, Gujarat Revenue Tribunal Rules, 1982, Bombay Revenue Tribunal Rules, 1939

Summary: Service - Constitution - Bombay Revenue Tribunal Act, 1957, ss.4, 20 - Gujarat Revenue Tribunal Rules, 1982, r. 3(1)(iii)(a) - Constitution of India, 1950, art.234 - Appointment - Revisionary jurisdiction - Exercise of powers - Govt. in exercise of its power under the Act and the Rules appointed appellant no.2 as President of Gujarat Revenue Tribunal - Appointment of Appellant no. 2 was challenged by respondents, on ground that office of Chairman, being 'judicial office' could not be usurped by person who had been Administrative Officer all his life - Validity of ss. 4, 20 of the Act and r. 3(1)(iii)(a) of the Rules was challenged - Appellants contested writ Petition - During pendency of writ Petition before HC, Govt. made appointment to post of President of Tribunal, but operation of his appointment order was stayed by HC and was finally disposed of - HC held that Tribunal was in strict sense, 'court' and that President, who presided over such Tribunal could, only be 'Judicial Officer', District Judge etc., for which, concurrence of HC was necessary u/art.234 of the Constitution - Hence, instant Appeal - Whether Tribunal could be considered as Court and HC had revisional jurisdiction so far as judgments and orders passed by Tribunal were concerned - Held, supervisory or revisional jurisdiction was considered to be power vesting in any superior Court or Tribunal, enabling it to satisfy itself as regards correctness of orders of inferior Tribunal - However, there was basic difference between appellate and supervisory jurisdiction - Appellate jurisdiction conferred right upon aggrieved person to complain in prescribed manner, to higher forum whereas, supervisory/revisional power had different object and purpose altogether as it conferred right and responsibility upon higher forum to keep subordinate Tribunals within limits of law - Though, expression 'Court' was understood as adjudicating body, which performs judicial functions of rendering definitive judgments having sense of finality and authoritativeness to bind parties litigating before it and it should be in course of exercise of sovereign judicial power transferred to it by State - Court was also Tribunal, however, Courts were only such Tribunals as have been created by concerned statute and belonged to judicial department of State as opposed to executive branch of said State - Therefore any Tribunal or authority that possesses these attributes, may be categorized as court - However, Tribunals have primarily been constituted to deal with cases under special laws and to provide for specialized adjudication alongside courts - Therefore, power of HC of judicial superintendence, even under amended provisions of art. 227 of the Constitution was applicable - Thus, object of consultation/ concurrence was to render consultation meaningful to serve intended purpose and power should be exercised only after consultation, consultation should be conscious, effective, meaningful and purposeful - Hence, it was evident that procedure to be observed u/art.234 of the Constitution goes to extent of true meaning of consultative process and not empty formality - Appeal dismissed.
