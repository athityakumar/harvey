State Tr.P.S.Lodhi Colony New Delhi v Sanjeev Nanda

Supreme Court of India

03 August 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Deepak Verma, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 239; (2012) 8 SCC 450; 2012 (3) ACC 379; AIR 2012 SC 3104; 2012 ALL MR (Cri) 3005; 2012 CRLJ 4174; 2012 (2) OLR 1003; 2012 (3) RCR(Criminal) 897; 2013 (1) RLW 629; 2012(7) SCALE 120; [2012] 12 S.C.R. 881

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Deem, Specific Offence, Road Traffic Act, 1972, Visibility, Road Safety Act, 1967

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34, 201, 304, 304A, 304 Part II - Intoxication - Rash/Negligent driving - Accident - Culpable homicide - Respondent/accused, under intoxication, allegedly rammed his car into group of persons in wee hours killing six persons - Respondent sped away while some of the victims were still underneath car - Trial Court convicted respondent u/s. 304 Part II IPC and sentenced him to undergo five years rigorous imprisonment - On appeal, HC set aside conviction disbelieving a prosecution witness and instead convicted respondent u/s. 304A IPC and sentenced him to undergo two years rigorous imprisonment - Hence instant appeal by State challenging HC order - Appellant State contended that respondent's negligence coupled with intoxication would lead to culpable homicide with knowledge and he knew that persons had been crushed and some of them were underneath his car, yet he continued to drive vehicle till all injured were disentangled from car and later tried to obliterate evidence available - Whether respondent deserved to be held guilty of commission of offence u/s. 304 Part II of IPC - Held, looking to the nature and manner in which accident had taken place, it could safely be held that respondent had no intention to cause death but certainly had the knowledge that his act may result in death - Thus, looking to the matter from all angles, there was no doubt that knowledge could still be attributed to respondent that his act might cause such bodily injuries which may, in ordinary course of nature, be sufficient to cause death but certainly he did not have any intention to cause death - Respondent was not driving vehicle with that intention - There was nothing to prove that he knew that a group of persons was standing on the road he was going to pass through and if that be so, there could not be an intention to cause death or such bodily injury as was likely to cause death - Thus, he had committed an offence u/s. 304 Part II IPC - Judgment and order of conviction passed by HC was partly set aside and order of conviction of Trial Court was restored and upheld - Accused was held guilty u/s. 304 Part II of IPC - In view of facts and circumstances of the case, it would be appropriate to maintain sentence awarded by HC, which respondent had already undergone - However, it was made clear that this had been held so, looking to very peculiar facts and features of this particular case and it might not be treated as precedent of general proposition of law on the point, for other cases - Appeal partly allowed.
