Ravi Yashwant Bhoir v District Collector, Raigad & Ors.

Supreme Court of India

02 March 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, J. S. KHEHAR

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 90; (2012) 4 SCC 407; AIR 2012 SC 1339; 2012(2) ALL MR 962; 2012 (2) Bom.C.R. 859; JT 2012 (3) SC 186; 2012 (4) MLJ(SC) 109; 2012(3) SCALE 303; [2012] 3 S.C.R. 775; 2012 (3) SLR 727

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Municipalities & Local Governments; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Maharashtra Accounts Code, 1971, Maharashtra Municipal Councils (Conduct of Business) Rules, 1966

Summary: (A) Municipalities & Local Governments - Administrative - Maharashtra Municipal Councils, Nagar Panchayats and Industrial Townships Act, 1965, s. 55B - Misconduct - Disqualification of President of Municipal Council - Legality - Appellant President of Municipal Council in question served with show cause notice containing 6 charges on the basis of a complaint - Appellant gave detailed explanation to show cause notice - Chief Minister (CM) passed order declaring appellant was disqualified for his remaining tenure and for a further period of 6 years even as member of Council - Appellant challenged CM order before HC - HC dismissed appellant's writ petition - Hence, instant appeal - (a) Whether CM was justified in passing order disqualifying appellant on the ground that he had failed to convene General Body meeting - Held, CM passed a very cryptic order of punishment and that order did not make any reference to appellant's pleadings either in his reply to show cause or during the course of hearing - Appellant's explanation for not holding was not considered at all - HC also erred in not dealing with any of issues raised by appellant while furnishing his explanation rather relied upon findings recorded by CM - Nothing in HC order indicating that appellant's grievances had been considered or any reasoning had been given to uphold findings recorded by CM imposing a severe punishment - All charges proved against appellant were dealt with exclusively on the basis of documentary evidence, there was nothing on record by which complainant could show that General Body meeting was not called, as statutorily required, by appellant intentionally - No finding of fact was recorded either by CM or by HC that some urgent/important work could not be carried out for want of General Body meeting of Council - Merely not to conduct oneself according to procedure prescribed or omission to conduct a meeting without any corresponding loss to corporate body, would not be an automatic misconduct by inference, unless some positive intentional misconduct was shown - In the absence of any imputation of motive, not calling meeting by appellant could not in itself, be enough to prove charge - Further, in case President failed to call meeting, there were other modes of calling meeting and in such an eventuality where reasonable explanation had been furnished by appellant to show cause notice on that count, competent authority could not have passed a harsh order - (b) Whether CM was justified in passing order disqualifying appellant on the ground that he had accepted tender at higher rate - Held, competent authority failed to appreciate that tenders were accepted by Council itself and not by appellant alone, hence, he could not be held responsible for acceptance of tenders - Instant case was a crystal clear cut case of legal malice - Duly elected member/chairman of Council could not have been removed in such a casual and cavalier manner without giving strict adherence to safeguards provided under statute which had to be scrupulously followed - Hence, impugned HC order and CM order were set aside - Appeal allowed.

(B) Municipalities & Local Governments - Administrative - Misconduct - Meaning of - Held, mere error of judgment resulting in doing of negligent act did not amount to misconduct - However, in exceptional circumstances, not working diligently would be a misconduct - An action that was detrimental to prestige of institution would also amount to misconduct - Acting beyond authority would be a misconduct - Expression 'misconduct' was required to be understood as a transgression of some established and definite rule of action, a forbidden act, unlawful behavior, willful in character - Further, expression 'misconduct' was required to be construed and understood in reference to subject matter and context wherein term occurred taking into consideration scope and object of statute which was being construed - Misconduct was to be measured in terms of nature of misconduct and it should be viewed with consequences of misconduct as to whether it had been detrimental to public interest - Appeal allowed.

(C) Municipalities & Local Governments - Administrative - Disgraceful conduct - Interpretation - Held, disgraceful means giving offence to moral sensibilities and injurious to reputation or conduct or character deserving or bringing disgrace or shame - Disgraceful conduct was to be examined from context in which term had been employed under statute - Disgraceful conduct ought not necessarily be connected with official of office bearer, hence, it would be outside ambit of discharge of his official duty - Appeal allowed.

(D) Municipalities & Local Governments - Administrative - Maharashtra Municipal Councils, Nagar Panchayats and Industrial Townships Act, 1965, s. 55B - Removal of elected office bearer - Scope - Held, an elected member could be removed in exceptional circumstances giving strict adherence to statutory provisions and holding enquiry, meeting requirement of principles of natural justice and giving an incumbent an opportunity to defend himself, for reason that removal of an elected person casted stigma upon him and took away his valuable statutory right - Not only elected office bearer but his constituency/electoral college was also deprived of representation by person of his choice - A duly elected person was entitled to hold office for term for which he had been elected and he could be removed only on a proved misconduct or any other procedure established under law like 'No Confidence Motion' etc - Elected official was accountable to its electorate as he had been elected by a large number of voters and it would have serious repercussions when he was removed from office and further declared disqualified to contest election for a further stipulated period - Appeal allowed.

(E) Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Administrative orders - Recording of reasons - Requirement of - Held, even in administrative matters, reasons should be recorded as it was incumbent upon authorities to pass a speaking and reasoned order - Right to reason was an indispensable part of a sound judicial system, reasons at least sufficient to indicate an application of mind of authority before court - Another rationale was that affected party could know why decision had gone against him - One of salutary requirements of natural justice was spelling out reasons for order made - Appeal allowed.
