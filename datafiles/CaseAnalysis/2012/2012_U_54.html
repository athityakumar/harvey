Monnet Ispat and Energy Limited v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

26 July 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Lodha, H.L. Gokhale

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 230; (2012) 11 SCC 1; 2012(6) SCALE 650; [2012] 7 S.C.R. 644

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Mines & Minerals

Keywords: Granite, Mineral Concession, Tamil Nadu Minor Mineral Concession Rules, 1959, Mining Areas Development Fund, Central, State or Provincial Act, Madhya Pradesh Motor Vehicles Taxation Act, 1947, Haryana Minerals (Vesting of Rights) Act, 1973, U.P. Sugar Undertakings (Acquisition) Act, 1971, Seventh Schedule of Government of India Act, 1935, Bihar Land Reforms (Amendment) Act, 1964

Summary: Mines & Minerals - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950 - Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, 1957, s. 17A - Bihar Land Reforms Act, 1950 - Mineral Concession Rules, 1960, rr. 58 and 59 - Grant of mining lease - Appellant company, was engaged in the business of mining, production of steel, ferro-alloys and power - Appellants have applied for mining leases in a land belonging to Govt. of Jharkhand and it was for iron-ore which was a mineral included in the First Schedule to the 1957 Act in respect of which no mining lease could be granted without the prior approval of the Central Govt. - Appellant was informed that certain portions of Mauza Ghatkuri and the adjoining areas were reserved for public sector exploitation under the two Notifications issued by the Govt. of Bihar on 21-12-1962 (1962 Notification) and 28-2-1969 (1969 Notification) - Later, State Govt. decided to withdraw pending mining lease proposals, including that of appellant - Govt. of India passed an order accepting the request of the State Govt. for withdrawal of the mining proposals made in favour of applicants, including appellant - Challenged - (A) Whether the Notifications dt. 21-12-1962 and 28-2-1969 issued by the State Govt. and the Notification dt. 27-9-2006 (2006 Notification) were legal and valid - Held, reservation made by 1962 and 1969 Notifications was in no manner contrary nor inconsistent with 1957 Act - Impugned Notifications did not impinge upon the legislative power of the Central Govt. - There was no lack of power in the State in making such reservation - (B) Whether the provisions of 1957 Act, as were then existing, enabled the State Govt. to reserve the subject area for its own use through the agency in public sector - Held, since the State Govt.'s paramount right over the iron ore being the owner of the mines did not get affected by 1957 Act, the power existed with the State Govt. to reserve subject areas of mining for exploitation in public sector undertaking - Also, there was nothing in 1957 Act to suggest even remotely and there was no express provision at all that the mines and minerals that vested in the States have been acquired - Scheme and provisions of 1957 Act show that Parliament itself contemplated State legislation for vesting of lands containing mineral deposits in the State Govt. and that Parliament did not intend to trench upon powers of State Legislatures under Entry 18, List II - Hence, what was implied by the provisions originally contained in 1957 Act and 1960 Rules insofar as authority of the State Govt. to reserve any area within its territory for mining in public sector had been made explicit first by amendment in r. 58 in 1980 and later on by introduction of s. 17A in 1957 Act by virtue of amendment effective from 1987 - (C) Impact of omission of r. 58 in 1988 from 1960 Rules and the introduction of s. 17A in 1957 Act - Whether current power of reservation contained in s. 17A of 1957 Act was consistent with erstwhile rr. 58/59 since s. 17A expressly required the approval of the Central Govt. before any State Govt. issues any notification for reservation of mining area in public sector - Held, reservations made prior to insertion of s. 17A continue in force even after the introduction of s. 17A - Reservations made by the State Govt. in 1977 before omission of r. 58 and amendment in r. 59 and insertion of s. 17A in 1957 Act were, thus, held to be unaffected - S. 17A was prospective - There was no indication in s. 17A or in terms of the Amending Act that by insertion of s. 17A the Parliament intended to alter the pre-existing state of affairs - Moreover, lack of knowledge on the part of the State Govt. about the reservation of areas for exploitation in public sector vide 1962 and 1969 Notifications did not affect in any manner the legality and validity of these Notifications once it had been found that these Notifications have been issued by the erstwhile State of Bihar in valid exercise of power which it had - (D) Whether the doctrines of promissory estoppel and the legitimate expectation help the appellants in obtaining the reliefs claimed by them and whether the actions of the State Govt. and the Central Govt. were liable to be set aside by applying these doctrines - Held, what the State Govt. had expressed in MOU was its willingness to extend all possible help and cooperation in setting up the manufacturing/generating facilities by Adhunik - State Govt. agreed to grant mineral concession as per existing Act and Rules - In view of the fact that the subject mining area had been reserved for exploitation in public sector under 1962 and 1969 Notifications, stipulation in the MOU that the State Govt. shall assist in selecting the area for iron ore and other minerals as per requirement of the company and the commitment to grant mineral concession could not be enforced - Stipulation in the MOU was not unconditional - Commitment was dependent on availability and as per existing law - Hence, doctrine of promissory estoppel was not attracted in the present facts, particularly when promise was made - State Govt. could not be compelled to carry out what it could not do in the existing state of affairs in view of 1962 and 1969 Notifications - State Govt. could not be held to be bound by its commitments or assurances or representations made in the MOU because by enforcement of such commitments or assurances or representations, the object sought to be achieved by reservation of the subject area was likely to be defeated and thereby affecting the public interest - None of the appellants was entitled to any relief based on these doctrines - (E) Whether the discretion exercised by the State Govt. suffered from any legal flaw - Held, no person could claim any right in any land belonging to Govt. or in any mines in any land belonging to Govt. except under 1957 Act and 1960 Rules - No person had any fundamental right to claim that he should be granted mining lease or prospecting licence or permitted reconnaissance operation in any land belonging to the Govt. - By the MOU entered into between the State Govt. and appellant certain commitments were made by the State Govt. but firstly, such MOU was not a contract as contemplated u/art. 299(1) of the Constitution and secondly, in grant of mining lease of a property of the State, the State Govt. had a discretion to grant or refuse to grant any mining lease - In view of the fact that area was reserved for exploitation of mineral in public sector, it could not be said that the discretion exercised by the State Govt. suffered from any legal flaw - Appeals dismissed.
