Om Prakash and another v Union of India and another

Supreme Court of India

30 September 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Altamas Kabir, Cyriac Joseph, Surinder Singh Nijjar

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 576

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Indirect Tax; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Customs - Excise - Practice & Procedure - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 9, 9A, 13, 18 to 21 - Customs Act, 1962, ss. 104(3), 135 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 2(l), (c), 155 - Cognizable offences - Billability - Validity - Petitioners were arrested for alleged offences under 1944 Act - Aggrieved petitioners filed instant petitions contending that since expressions 'cognizable' or 'non-cognizable' or even 'bailable offences' had not been defined in either 1944 Act or 1962 Act, one would have to refer to provisions of CrPC to understand the meaning of said expressions in relation to criminal offences and that offences which were punishable under 1944 Act were indicated in s. 9 of 1944 Act and power to arrest a person was u/s. 13 of 1944 Act, such power should be read along with ss. 18 to 21 of 1944 Act along with s. 155 of CrPC - Further contened that offences u/s. 9A of 1944 Act were non-cognizable and Excise Officer, had no power to arrest such a person - Further contended that provisions of 1962 Act were in pari materia with provisions of 1944 Act - Hence, instant petitions - Respondent UOI contended that s. 9A of 1944 Act did not refer to a Central Excise Officer and as such there was no embargo on an Officer under 1944 Act from arresting a person - Whether all offences under 1944 Act and 1962 Act were non-cognizable and, if so, whether such offences are bailable - Held, definition of 'non-cognizable offence' in s. 2(l) of CrPC made clear that a non-cognizable offence was an offence for which a police officer had no authority to arrest without warrant - Expression 'cognizable offence' in s. 2(c) of CrPC means an offence for which a police officer may, in accordance with First Schedule or under any other law for time being in force, arrest without warrant - On a construction of definitions of different expressions used in CrPC and in connected enactments in respect of a non-cognizable offence, a police officer, and, in instant case an excise officer, would have no authority to make an arrest without obtaining a warrant - Same provision was contained in s. 41 of CrPC which specified when a police officer may arrest without order from a Magistrate or without warrant - Offences under 1944 Act could not be equated with offences under IPC which have been made non-cognizable and non-bailable - In fact, in CrPC itself exceptions were carved out in respect of serious offences directed against the security of country, which though non-cognizable were made non-bailable - S. 20 of 1944 Act clearly indicated that offences under 1944 Act, as set out in s. 9 of 1944 Act, were bailable, since Officer in-Charge of a police station was mandated to grant bail to the person arrested and brought before him in terms of s. 19 of 1944 Act - Provisions of ss. 9 and 9A r/w s. 20 of 1944 Act, offences under 1944 Act, besides being non-cognizable, wre also bailable, though not on the logic that all non-cognizable offences were bailable, but in view of provisions of 1944 Act, which indicated that offences under 1944 Act were bailable in nature - Thus, offences under 1944 Act were bailable - Provisions of s. 104(3) of 1962 and s. 13 of 1944 Act, vest Customs Officers and Excise Officers with same powers as that of a Police Officer in charge of a Police Station, which include power to release on bail upon arrest in respect of offences committed under the two enactments which were uniformly non-cognizable - S. 9A of 1944 Act and s. 104(4) of 1962, provided that notwithstanding anything in CrPC, offences under both the Acts would be non-cognizable - Offences under 1962 Act were bailable - Consequently, as in case of offences under 1944 Act, it was held that offences u/s. 135 of 1962 Act, were bailable and if the person arrested offers bail, he should be released on bail in accordance with provisions of s. 104(3) of 1962 Act - Petitions allowed.
