M/S. Real Estate Agencies v Govt. Of Goa & Ors

Supreme Court of India

10 September 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjan Gogoi, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 287; (2012) 12 SCC 170; AIR 2012 SC 3848; 2012 (6) Bom.C.R. 248; 2012 (4) CLT(SC) 136; 2012 (5) CTC 561; JT 2012 (9) SC 277; 2012 (4) RCR(Civil) 496; 2012(8) SCALE 581; [2012] 8 S.C.R. 278

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Municipalities & Local Governments; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Land & Property - Municipalities & Local Governments - Practice & Procedure - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - Petitioner (Real Estate Company) developed a residential colony and sold the same to respondent (purchasers of plot) - Respondents approached HC by way of a civil suit claiming an easementary right in respect of entire vacant/open space, which was residual land and open plots of colony that was meant to be kept open as 'vacant land' (land in question) - Petitioner claimed right and title over land in question - HC decreed the suit - Aggrieved petitioner filed appeal before DB - DB dismissed the same and decree passed by Single Judge was affirmed - However, some part of land in question was acquired by respondent (State) under the Act and paid compensation to petitioner - Thereafter, respondent (State) proposed a project of developing the land in question - Against the activities of respondent (State) petitioner filed a writ petition before HC, which was dismissed as petitioner had an alternative remedy - Hence, instant appeal - Appellant contended that without initiating proceeding to acquiring the land under the Act, respondent awarded work order to develop land in question and that petitioner was deprived of his ownership of property - Whether impugned order of HC was justified - Held, HC by impugned order refused to interdict the developmental works undertook or about to undertake on the ground that petitioner had an efficacious alternative remedy i.e., a suit for injunction - HC u/art. 226 of Constitution, normally would not entertain a dispute which would require it to adjudicate contested questions and conflicting claims of parties to determine correct facts for due application of law - In instant case, petitioner claimed title to land in question on the basis of deed of Indenture dt.16-11-1977; respondent (State) did not claim any title to land in question but contended that petitioner had ceased to hold normal attributes of ownership of immovable property in respect of land in question and its position was more akin to that of a trustee holding the land for benefit of public at large - Respondent (Purchasers of plot) on the other hand, claimed easementary right of enjoyment of open space - It was incumbent on HC to undertake a deeper probe in matter in order to find out whether claim of respondent (State) had any substance or had been so raised merely to relegate petitioner to a more 'lengthy, dilatory and expensive process' that was inherent in a civil suit - HC would have justified to relegate petitioner to Civil Court to seek his remedies by way of a suit - Considering nature of developmental works that were proposed and fact that a part of work might have executed in meantime, respondents should be permitted to complete the remaining work on the land and petitioner should be left with option of raising a claim before appropriate forum for such loss and compensation, if any, to which he might be entitled to in law - Appeal disposed of.
