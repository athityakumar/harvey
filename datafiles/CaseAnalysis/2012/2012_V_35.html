Vodafone International Holdings B.V. v Union Of India & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

20 January 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

S.H. Kapadia, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan, Swatanter Kumar

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 20; (2012) 6 SCC 613; [2012] 170 Comp Cas 369; 2012 (1) CompLJ 225; [2012] 341 ITR 1; JT 2012 (1) SC 410; 2012 (2) MLJ(SC) 913; 2012(1) SCALE 530; [2012] 1 S.C.R. 573; [2012] 204 TAXMAN 408

Case Digest

Subject: Corporate; Income Tax & Direct Taxes; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Preference Shares, Indirect Transfer, Indebtedness, Capital Cost Allowance Act, 1990, Income Tax Assessment Act, 1936, Cayman Island Act, Indian Foreign Investment Rules, RBI Regulation

Summary: S. H. Kapadia, CJ.

(A) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Practice & Procedure - Income Tax Act, 1961, ss. 163, 201 - Overseas acquisition - Offshore transaction - Taxability - Hutchison Telecommunications International Limited (HTIL) issued a press release announcing possible sale of 'its equity interest' in Hutchison Essar Limited (HEL) in Telecom Sector in India - Vodafone Group Plc. made a non-binding offer for HTIL's shareholdings in HEL - Vodafone and HTIL then entered into a Share and Purchase Agreement (SPA) whereunder HTIL had agreed to procure transfer of share capital of wholly owned subsidiary (WOS) CGP Investments (Holdings) Ltd (CGP), incorporated in Cayman Islands by HTIBVI - HTIL had also undertaken that subsidiary companies such as Hutchison Telecommunication (India) Ltd. (HTM), Omega and 3GSPL, would enter into an agreed form 'IDFC Transaction Agreement' as soon as practicable - Vodafone applied to Foreign Investment Promotion Board (FIPB) requesting to grant approval to indirect acquisition by it of 51.96% stake in HEL through an overseas acquisition of entire share capital of CGP from HTIL - FIPB conveyed its approval to transaction subject to compliance of applicable laws and regulations in India - A sum was paid by Vodafone towards consideration for acquisition of share capital of CGP and its name was entered in register of members of CGP kept in Caymen Islands and share certificate of HTIBVI relating to CGP share capital was cancelled - Fresh certificates of incorporation was issued by Registrar of Companies in relation to Indian operating companies whereby word 'Hutchison' was substituted with word 'Vodafone' - Revenue issued a show cause notice to Vodafone u/ss. 201(1) and 201(1A) of the Act as to why Vodafone should not be treated as assessee-in-default for failure to withhold tax - Vodafone filed writ petition for setting aside show-cause notice on the ground that Revenue had no jurisdiction - HC dismissed writ petition, however, SC, in SLP, directed Revenue to determine jurisdictional challenge raised by Vodafone as a preliminary issue - Revenue issued second show-cause notice to Vodafone u/ss. 201 and 201(1A) of the Act - Revenue, subsequently, passed an order upholding jurisdiction of department to tax transaction - Further, a show cause notice was also issued u/s. 163(1) of the Act to Vodafone as to why it should not be treated as an agent/representative assessee of HTIL - Vodafone challenged Revenue's order on various grounds before HC - HC dismissed Vodafone's writ petition - Hence instant appeal - Whether there was any conflict between SC decision in McDowell and Co. Ltd. v. CTO, 1985 INDLAW SC 426 and Union of India v. Azadi Bachao Andolan, 2003 INDLAW SC 823 - Held, majority judgment in McDowell held that tax planning would be legitimate provided it was within framework of law - Thus, it could not be said that all tax planning was illegal/illegitimate/impermissible - Hence, in cases of treaty shopping and/or tax avoidance, there is no conflict between McDowell and Azadi Bachao - Order accordingly.

(B) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961, s. 9(1)(i) - Transfer of capital assets by foreign company - Treatment by look through provision - Whether transfer of shares of foreign company holding shares in an Indian company should be treated as transfer of shares of Indian company on the premise that s. 9(1)(i) covered direct and indirect transfers of capital assets -_ Held, in the case of a resident, it was immaterial whether place of accrual of income was within India or outside India, since, in either event, he was liable to be charged to tax on such income - However, in the case of a non-resident, unless place of accrual of income was within India, he should not be subjected to tax - If any income accrued or arouse to a non-resident, directly or indirectly, outside India was fictionally deemed to accrue or arise in India if such income accrued or arouse as a sequel to transfer of a capital asset situated in India - Further, legislature had not used the words 'indirect transfer' in s. 9(1)(i) and hence, s. 9(1)(i) was not a 'look through' provision covering both direct and indirect transfers - Order accordingly.

(C) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961 - Share Purchase Agreement (SPA) - Scope - Whether HTIL under SPA directly extinguished its rights of control over HEL and its subsidiaries and, consequent upon such extinguishment, there was a transfer of capital asset situated in India - Held, Hutchison structure had been in place and paying income tax since 1994 - SPA indicated 'continuity' of telecom business on exit of its predecessor, namely, HTIL - Thus, it could not be said that structure was created or used as a sham or tax avoidant or HTIL or Vodafone was a 'fly by night' operator/short time investor - If one applied the look at test, without invoking dissecting approach, extinguishment took place because of transfer of CGP share and not by virtue of various clauses of SPA - In the instant case, where structure had existed for a considerable length of time generating taxable revenues right from 1994 and where court was satisfied that transaction satisfied all parameters of 'participation in investment' then in such a case the court need not go into questions such as de facto control vs. legal control - Order accordingly.

(D) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961 - Holding company Control over Subsidiary companies - Nature of - What was the nature of 'control' that a parent company (HTIL) had over its subsidiary (HEL) - Held, even though a subsidiary would normally comply with request of a parent company, it was not just a puppet of parent company - While considering Hutchison structure in its entirety, HTIL as a Group holding company could have only persuaded its downstream companies to vote in a given manner as HTIL had no power nor authority under the said structure to direct any of its downstream companies to vote in a manner as directed by it (HTIL) - Facts showed that both parent and subsidiary companies worked as a group since 1994 - Further, as a practice, subsidiaries did comply with arrangement suggested by Group holding company in the matter of voting, failing which smooth working of HEL generating huge revenues was not possible - Further, parent company exercised shareholder's influence on its subsidiaries would not obliterate decision-making power or authority of its (subsidiary's) directors - Order accordingly.

(E) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961 - Creation of investment vehicle - Motive - Whether CGP stood inserted at a later stage in transaction in order to bring in a tax-free entity (or to create a transaction to avoid tax) and thereby avoid capital gains - Held, CGP was incorporated in 1998 in Cayman Islands and was in Hutchison structure from 1998 - Impugned transaction was of divestment and, therefore, transaction of sale was structured at an appropriate tier, so that buyer really acquired the same degree of control as was hitherto exercised by HTIL - Vodafone agreed to acquire companies and companies it acquired controlled 67% interest in HEL - CGP was an investment vehicle and through its acquisition, Vodafone had advantage of indirect acquisition of rights and obligations of GSPL in various Framework Agreements - Order accordingly.

(F) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961 - Situs of CGP - Whether the situs of CGP was situated in India - Held, under Indian Companies Act, 1956, situs of shares would be where company was incorporated and where its shares should be transferred - In the instant case, Vodafone asserted that transfer of CGP share was recorded in Cayman Islands, where register of members of CGP was maintained - Vodafone's assertion was neither rebutted in impugned order of department nor traversed in pleadings filed by Revenue - Hence, Revenue's contention that situs of CGP share was situated in place (India) where underlying assets stood situated could not be accepted - Order accordingly.

(G) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961 - Overseas acquisition - Sale of controlling interest - Extent - Whether Vodafone had acquired 67% controlling interest in HEL as claimed by Revenue - Held, on the basis of shareholding test, HTIL could be said to have a 52% control over HEL - Balance 15% stakes in HEL remained with Vodafone's Indian partners who had through their respective group companies invested 15% in HEL through its subsidiary companies and, consequently, HTIL had no control over 15% stakes in HEL - HTIL/Vodafone could not be said to have a control over 15% stakes in HEL - However, HTIL had invested through its non-100% wholly owned subsidiaries in 9.62% of HEL (through pro rata route) - Thus, in the sense of shareholding, one could say that HTIL had an effective shareholding (direct and indirect interest) of 51.96% (approx. 52%) in HEL - It was for that reason that even FIPB gave its approval to transaction by saying that Vodafone was acquiring or had acquired effective shareholding of 51.96% in HEL - Order accordingly.

(H) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961, s. 9 - Overseas acquisition - Basis of consideration - Whether payment of consideration by Vodafone to HTIL on the basis of an enterprise value of 67% of share capital of HEL would attract s. 9 of the Act - Held, valuation should not be the basis of taxation - Enterprise Value was made up of two parts, namely, value of HEL, value of CGP and companies between CGP and HEL- Revenue could not invoke s. 9 of the Act on value of underlying asset or consequence of acquiring a share of CGP - Price paid as a percentage of enterprise value had to be 67% not because the figure of 67% was available in praesenti to Vodafone, but on account of fact that competing Indian bidders would have had de facto access to entire 67%, as they were not subject to limitation of sectoral cap - Order accordingly.

(I) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961, s. 9 - Overseas acquisition - Taxability - Whether HC was justified in holding that impugned transaction was subjected to capital gain tax - Held, HC was not justified in dissecting controlling interest from ownership of shares - Right of a shareholder would assume character of a controlling interest where extent of shareholding enabled shareholder to control management - Shares, and rights which emanated from controlling interest, flow together and should not be dissected - Further, call and put option was not encashed by GSPL (subsidiary of HTIL) under Framework Agreement till date, hence s. 9(1)(i) of the Act was not applicable - HC failed to examine nature of non-compete agreement, control premium, call and put options, consultancy support, customer base, brand licences etc. - HC ought to have examined entire transaction holistically - Order accordingly.

(J) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961, s. 195 - Overseas acquisition - Tax deduct at source (TAS) - Liability - Whether s. 195 had casted an obligation on Vodafone to deduct TAS from payment of consideration - Held, shareholding in companies incorporated outside India (CGP) was property located outside India - Shareholding in question became subject matter of offshore transfer between two non-residents, there was no liability for capital gains tax and hence, question of deduction of TAS would not arise - Further, transaction was entered into on principal to principal basis, therefore, no liability to deduct TAS arose - Order accordingly.

(K) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961, ss. 161, 163 - Overseas acquisition - Representative Assessee - Whether Vodafone could be proceeded against as 'representative assessee' u/s. 163 or s. 161 of the Act - Held, merely because a person was an agent or was to be treated as an agent, would not lead to an automatic conclusion that he became liable to pay taxes on behalf of non-resident - Further, s. 161 of the Act made a representative assessee liable only if eventualities stipulated in s. 161 were satisfied - Hence, Vodafone could not be proceeded against u/s. 161 or 163 of the Act as a representative assesse - Order accordingly.

(L) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961, ss. 161, 163 - Overseas acquisition - Offshore transaction - Jurisdiction of tax authority - Whether Indian Tax Authority had jurisdiction to tax impugned transaction - Held, impugned offshore transaction was a bonafide structured FDI investment into India which fell outside India's territorial tax jurisdiction, hence not taxable - Impugned transaction evidenced participative investment and not a sham or tax avoidant preordained transaction - Transaction was between HTIL (a Cayman Islands company) and Vodafone (a company incorporated in Netherlands) and subject matter of transaction was transfer of CGP (a company incorporated in Cayman Islands) - Hence, Indian Tax Authority had no territorial tax jurisdiction to tax impugned offshore transaction -Order accordingly.

K. S. Radhakrishnan, J

(A) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Overseas acquisition - FDI through Mauritian company - Taxability - Scope - (a) Whether setting up of wholly owned subsidiary (WOS) in Mauritius for routing FDI in India could be considered as a set up for tax evasion especially in the absence of Limitation of Benefit (LoB) Clause and presence of Circular No. 789 of 2000 dated 13-4-2000 - Held, on a sale/transfer/disinvestment of shares by Mauritian company, after a reasonable time, sale proceeds would be received by Mauritius Company as registered holder/owner of such shares, such benefits could be sent back to Foreign Principal/100% shareholder of Mauritius company either by way of a declaration of special dividend by Mauritius company and/or by way of repayment of loans received by Mauritius company from Foreign Principal/shareholder for purpose of making investment - Apart from Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement (DTAA), which provided for tax exemption in case of capital gains received by a Mauritius company/shareholder at time of disinvestment/exit and fact that Mauritius did not levy tax on dividends declared and paid by a Mauritius company/subsidiary to its Foreign Shareholders/Principal, there was no other reason for this quantum of funds to be invested from/through Mauritius - Hence, setting up of a WOS Mauritius subsidiary/Special Purpose Vehicle by Principals/genuine substantial long term FDI in India from/through Mauritius, pursuant to DTAA and Circular No. 789 should never be considered to be set up for tax evasion - (b) Whether Mauritius Tax Residency Certificate (TRC) was so conclusive that Tax Department could not pierce veil and look at substance of transaction - Held, DTAA and Circular No. 789 would not preclude Income Tax department from denying tax treaty benefits, if it was established, on facts, that Mauritius company had been interposed as owner of shares in India, at time of disposal of shares to a third party, solely with a view to avoid tax without any commercial substance - Tax department, in such a situation, notwithstanding the fact that Mauritian company was required to be treated as beneficial owner of shares, under Circular No. 789 and DTTA, was entitled to look at entire transaction of sale as a whole and if it was established that Mauritian company had been interposed as a device, it was open to Tax department to discard device and take into consideration real transaction between parties, and transaction should be subjected to tax - Order accordingly.

(B) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Tax planning - Avoidance of tax burden - Legitimacy - Whether SC decision of McDowell and Co. Ltd. v. CTO, 1985 INDLAW SC 426, was required to be reconsidered in the context of instant case - Held, McDowell laid down the principles that Revenue could not tax a subject without a statute to support and every tax payer was entitled to arrange his affairs so that his taxes should be as low as possible and that he was not bound to choose that pattern which would replenish treasury - Revenue's stand that ratio laid down in McDowell was contrary to what was laid down in later SC decision in Union of India v. Azadi Bachao Andolan, 2003 INDLAW SC 823, was unsustainable and, therefore, called for no reconsideration by a larger branch - Order accordingly.

(C) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Overseas acquisition - Wholly owned subsidiary (WOS) - Interposition in Corporate structure - Motive - Determination - Whether CGP's interposition in HTIL Corporate structure and its disposition, by way of transfer, for exit, was for a commercial or business purpose or with an ulterior motive for evading tax - Held, parties were free to choose whatever lawful arrangement which would suit their business and commercial purpose, but true nature of transaction could be ascertained only by looking into legal arrangement actually entered into and carried out - In the instant case, HTIL structure was created over a period of time and this was consolidated in 2004 to provide a working model by which HTIL could make best use of its investments and exercise control over and strategically influence affairs of HEL - HTIL in its commercial wisdom noticed disadvantage of preferring Array Holdings Limited (Array), a subsidiary company of CGP, which would have created problems for HTIL - Hutchison Teleservices (India) Mauritius had a subsidiary, namely 3GSPL which carried on call centre business in India and transfer of CGP share would give control over 3GSPL, an indirect subsidiary which was incorporated in year 1999 - It would also obviate problems arising on account of call and put options arrangements and voting rights enjoyed by 3GSPL - If Array was transferred, disadvantage was that HTIL had to deal with call and put options of 3GSPL - Hence, HTIL in their commercial wisdom thought of transferring CGP share rather than going for any other alternatives - Further, 3GSPL was also a party to various agreements entered into between itself and other holding companies such as AS, AG and IDFC Group - If Array had been transferred disadvantage would be that the same would result in hiving off call centre business from 3GSPL - Consolidation operations of HEL were evidently done in year 2005 not for tax purposes but for commercial reasons - CGP was already part of HTIL's Corporate Structure and decision taken to sell CGP share so as to exit from Indian Telecom Sector was not fall out of a tax exploitation scheme, but a genuine commercial decision taking into consideration best interest of investors and corporate entity - Order accordingly.

(D) Corporate - Practice & Procedure - Overseas acquisition - Situs of share of WOS - Determination - Whether situs of share of CGP had been shifted to India after acquisition in question - Held, situs of shares situated at place where company was incorporated and/ or place where share could be dealt with by way of transfer - CGP share was registered in Cayman Island and materials indicated that Cayman Island law, unlike other laws, did not recognize multiplicity of registers - Facts in the instant case as well as provisions of Caymen Island Act would clearly indicated that CGP share situated in Caymen Island and on transfer, situs would not be shifted to India - Order accordingly.

(E) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Overseas acquisition - Transfer of shares of Holding Company - Controlling interests of subsidiaries - Effect - (a) Whether the impugned acquisition had resulted in transferring of controlling interests of subsidiaries of CGP to Vodafone - Held, Vodafone on acquisition of CGP share got controlling interest of 42% over HEL through voting rights through eight Mauritian subsidiaries, the same was the position of HTIL as well - On acquiring CGP share, CGP had become a direct subsidiary of Vodafone, but both were legally independent entities - Vodafone did not own any assets of CGP - Management and business of CGP vested on Board of Directors of CGP but Vodafone could appoint or remove members of Board of Directors of CGP - HTIL had never sold nor Vodafone purchased any shares of Mauritian subsidiaries, but only CGP, share of which situated in Cayman Islands - CGP would appoint or remove any of its directors in subsidiaries, if it wished by a resolution in general body of subsidiary - Further, Vodafone on purchase of CGP share got controlling interest in Mauritian Companies and incident of transfer of CGP share should not be considered to be two distinct and separate transactions, one shifting of share and another shifting of controlling interest - Transfer of CGP share automatically resulted in host of consequences including transfer of controlling interest and that controlling interest as such could not be dissected from CGP share without legislative intervention - Controlling interest might have percolated down the line to operating companies but that controlling interest was inherently contractual and not a property right unless otherwise provided for in statue - Acquisition of shares, would carry acquisition of controlling interest which was purely a commercial concept and tax could be levied only on transaction and not on its effect - On transfer of CGP share situated in Cayman Islands, entire rights, which accompany stood transferred not in India, but offshore and facts reveal that offshore holdings and arrangements made by HTIL and Vodafone were for sound commercial and legitimate tax planning, not with motive of evading tax - (b) Whether HC was right in holding that rights and interests accrued to Vodafone from various agreements entered into by parties were subjected to tax - Held, HC failed to note on transfer of CGP share, there was only transfer of certain off-shore loan transactions which was unconnected with underlying controlling interest in Indian Operating Companies - HC had ignored vital fact that as far as put options were concerned there were pre-existing agreements between beneficiaries and counter parties and fresh agreements were also on similar lines - Further, HC committed an error in holding that there were some rights vested in HTIL under Share holders agreement (SHA) which was also an agreement, conferring no right to any party and accordingly none could have been transferred - HC had also committed an error in holding that some rights vested with HTIL under agreement dated 1-8-2006, in fact, that agreement conferred right on Hutichison Telecommunication (India) Ltd., which was a Mauritian Company and not HTIL, vendor of SPA - HC had also ignored vital fact that FIPB had elaborately examined nature of call and put option agreement rights and found no right in presenti had been transferred to Vodafone - Order accordingly.

(F) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Overseas acquisition - Debts/Loans through Intermediaries - Taxability - Whether assignment of debts/loans would amount to capital assets attracting capital gains tax on transfer of CGP share - Held, Share Purchase Agreement (SPA) contained provisions for assignment of loans either at Mauritius or Cayman Islands and all loans were assigned at face value - Agreements including provisions for assignments in SPA, indicated that all loan agreements and assignments of loans took place outside India at face value and, hence, there was no question of transfer of any capital assets out of those transactions in India, attracting capital gains tax - Order accordingly.

(G) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961, s. 2 (47) - Overseas acquisition - Right of preference shares - Taxability - Whether right to preference shares would amount to capital assets attracting capital gains tax - Held, Vodafone while determining bid price had taken into consideration, inter alia, its ownership of redeemable preference shares in Indian companies which restructured shareholding of HEL - Right to preference shares or rights thereto should not be termed as transfer in terms of s. 2(47) of the Act - Any agreement with Indian partners contemplated fresh investment, by subscribing to preference shares were redeemable only by accumulated profit or by issue of fresh capital and hence any issue of fresh capital should not be equated to continuation of old preference shares or transfer thereof attracting capital gains tax - Order accordingly.

(H) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961 - Overseas acquisition - Non compete agreement - Taxability - Whether provisions of non-compete agreement in SPA would amount to capital assets attracting capital gains tax - Held, SPA contained a Non-Compete Agreement which was a pure Contractual Agreement, a negative covenant, purpose of which was only to see that transferee did not immediately start a compete business - A non-compete agreement as an adjunct to a share transfer, which was not for any consideration, should not give rise to a taxable income - A non-compete agreement entered into outside India would not give rise to a taxable event in India - An agreement for a non-compete clause was executed offshore and, by no principle of law, could be termed as 'property' so as to come within the meaning of capital gains taxable in India in absence of any legislation - Order accordingly.

(I) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961 - Overseas acquisition - Brand name - Taxability - Whether provisions of intellectual property right in using brand name in SPA would amount to capital assets attracting capital gains tax - Held, bare license to use a brand free of charge, was not itself a 'property' and if right to property was created for first time and that too free of charge, it should not give rise to a chargeable income - Under SPA, a limited window of license was given and it was expressly made free of charge and, therefore, assurance given by HTIL to Vodafone that brand name would not cease overnight, should not be described as 'property' rights so as to consider it as a capital asset chargeable to tax in India - Order accordingly.

(J) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Corporate - Income Tax Act, 1961 - Overseas acquisition - Oracle license - Taxability - Whether transfer of Oracle license to Vodafone would amount to capital assets attracting capital gains tax - Held, Oracle license was an accounting license, benefit of which was extended till such time acquired company replaced it with its own accounting package - However, there was nothing to show that accounting package software was transferred to Vodafone - In any view, said license should not be termed as a capital asset since it had never been transferred to Vodafone - Order accordingly.

(K) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Income Tax Act, 1961, s. 9 - Application of s. 9 - Scope - Whether s. 9 of the Act was applicable in the instant case - Held, s. 9 referred to a property that yielded an income and that property should have situs in India and it was income that aroused through or from that property which was taxable - S. 9, therefore, covered only income arising from a transfer of a capital asset situated in India and it did not purport to cover income arising from indirect transfer of capital asset in India - Hence, s. 9 did not have any application in the instant case - Order accordingly.

(L) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Income Tax Act, 1961, s. 9 - Look through provision - Source Test and Resident Test - Whether impugned transaction had a deep connection with India i.e. ultimately to transfer control over HEL and hence source of gain to HTIL was in India - Held, source in relation to an income was construed to be where transaction of sale takes place and not where item of value, which was subject of transaction, was acquired or derived from - HTIL and Vodafone were off-shore companies and since sale took place outside India, applying source test, source was also outside India - Substantial territorial nexus between income and territory which sought to tax that income, was of prime importance to levy tax - Expression used in s. 9(1)(i) was 'source of income in India' which implied that income aroused from that source and there was no question of income arising indirectly from a source in India - Expression used was 'source of income in India' and not 'from a source in India' - Further, s. 9 had no 'look through provision' and such a provision should not be brought through construction or interpretation of a word 'through' in s. 9 - In any view, 'look through provision' would not shift situs of an asset from one country to another and hence s. 9 had no inbuilt 'look through mechanism' - S. 9(1)(i), therefore, would not apply to transaction in question or on rights and entitlements, stated to have transferred, as a fall out of sale of CGP share, since Revenue had failed to establish both tests, Resident Test as well Source Test - Order accordingly.

(M) Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Income Tax Act, 1961, ss. 163, 195(1) - Representative Assessee - Failure to deduct tax at source - Whether Vodafone could be proceeded against u/s. 195(1) for not deducting tax at source and, alternatively, u/s. 163 of the Act as a representative assessee - Held, CGP share was transferred offshore and companies in question were incorporated not in India but offshore - Vodafone and CGP had no income or fiscal assets in India, left aside the question of transferring those fiscal assets in India - Tax presence had to be viewed in context of transaction in question and not with reference to an entirely unrelated transaction - S. 195 would apply only if payments made from a resident to another non-resident and not between two non-residents situated outside India - In the instant case, transaction was between two non-resident entities through a contract executed outside India where consideration was also passed outside India and said transaction had no nexus with underlying assets in India - In order to establish a nexus, legal nature of transaction was required to be examined and not indirect transfer of rights and entitlements in India - Hence, Vodafone was not legally obliged to respond to s. 163 notice relating to treatment of a purchaser of an asset as a representative assessee - Order accordingly.
