C. N. Ramappa Gowda v C. C. Chandregowda (Dead) by Lrs. And another

Supreme Court of India

23 April 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Gyan Sudha Misra, T.S. Thakur

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 174; (2012) 5 SCC 265; AIR 2012 SC 2528; 2012 (5) ALD(SC) 1; 2012 (92) ALR 679; 2012 (3) AWC 3040; 2012 (3) Bom.C.R. 640; 2013 (4) CTC 865; JT 2012 (4) SC 280; 2012 (6) KarLJ 1; 2012 (5) MahLJ 521; 2012 (5) MLJ(SC) 611; 2012 (4) MPLJ 23; 2012 (3) RCR(Civil) 331; 2012 (116) RD 536; 2012(4) SCALE 541; [2012] 5 S.C.R. 453

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 8 r. 10 - Non-filing of written statement - Ex-parte decree - Opportunity of trial - Challenged - Entitled to - Appellant (plaintiff) filed a suit for partition and separate possession of joint family property and claimed half share - Respondents (defendants) appeared before Trial Court but written statement (WS) was not filed inspite of numerous opportunities - Trial Court decreed suit basing upon ex-parte evidence lead by appellant and directed that appellant should be entitled to half share in property - Aggrieved respondents filed appeal before HC, which was allowed and matter was remanded to Trial Court for its retrial - Hence, instant appeal - Appellant contended that inspite of service of summons and represented by counsel, defendants failed to file WS before Trial Court - Further contended that respondents did not dispute factum of joint family property and no reason was assigned by respondents for non-filing of WS and documents when their right to file the same was stood forfeited - (B)Whether Trial Court before whom respondents failed to file WS, could straightway pass a decree in favour of appellant without entering into merits of case - Held, it was noticed from decree of Trial Court that appellant was sought to prove his case that suit property was a joint family property only on strength of affidavit, which he had filed and has failed to lead any oral or documentary evidence to establish that property was joint in nature - Even case of appellant was correct, it was of vital importance for Trial Court to scrutinize appellant's case by directing him to lead some documentary evidence worthy of credence that property sought to be partitioned was joint in nature - But Trial Court relied upon case of appellant merely placing reliance on affidavit filed by appellant which was fit to be tested on at least a shred of some documentary evidence even if it were by way of an ex-parte assertion - Trial Court placed reliance on affidavit in a blindfold manner merely on ground that respondent had failed to file written statement would amount to punitive treatment of suit and the resultant decree would amount to decree which would be nothing short of a decree which was penal in nature - Where WS was not filed, Court should be a little more cautious in proceeding under O. 8 r. 10 of CPC and before passing a judgement, it should ensure that even if facts set out in plaint are treated to have been admitted, a judgement and decree could not possibly be passed without requiring appellant to prove the fact pleaded in plaint - It was only when Court for recorded reasons was fully satisfied that there was no fact which needs to be proved at the instance of appellant in view of deemed admission by defendant, Court could conveniently pass a judgement and decree against respondent who had not filed written statement - But, if plaint indicated that there are disputed questions of fact involved in case, gave rise to two versions, it would not be safe for Court to record an ex-parte judgement without directing appellant to prove facts so as to settle factual controversy - In that event, ex-parte judgement although might appear to have decided the suit expeditiously, it ultimately gives rise to several layers of appeal after appeal which ultimately compounds delay in finally disposing of suit giving rise to multiplicity of proceeding which hardly promotes the cause of speedy trial - However, if Court was clearly of the view that the appellant's case even without any evidence was prima facie unimpeachable and respondent's approach was clearly a dilatory tactic to delay passing of a decree, it would be justified in appropriate cases to pass even an uncontested decree - What would be nature of such a case ultimately would have to be left to the wisdom and just exercise of discretion by Trial Court who was seized of trial of suit - (B) Whether respondents could be granted fresh opportunity by HC to file WS and order for retrial - Held, Trial Court clearly adopted an erroneous approach by inferring that merely because there was no evidence of denial or rebuttal, appellant's case could be held to be proved - Trial Court, therefore, while accepting plea of appellant ought to have recorded reasons even if it were based on ex-parte evidence that appellant had succeeded in proving jointness of property on the basis of which a decree of partition could be passed in appellant's favour - HC was legally justified in setting aside judgement and decree of Trial Court and allowing appeal to limited extent of remanding matter to Trial Court for a de-novo trial after permitting respondent to file written statement - Impugned order was upheld - Appeal dismissed.
