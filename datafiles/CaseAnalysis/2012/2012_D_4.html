State Of Punjab v Dalbir Singh

Supreme Court of India

01 February 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Ganguly, Jagdish Singh Khehar

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 80; (2012) 3 SCC 346; AIR 2012 SC 1040; 2012 CRLJ 1579; 2012 (2) JCC 1053; JT 2012 (2) SC 300; 2012 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 711; 2012 (2) MLJ(Crl) 118; 2012 (1) RCR(Criminal) 699; 2012(2) SCALE 126; [2012] 4 S.C.R. 608

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Constitutionality, U.S. Constitution, Statements of Objects and Reasons of Act, 1988, Offences against the Person Act, 1873

Summary: Criminal - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302 and 307 - Arms Act, 1959, ss. 27 and 27(3) - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 21, 139 and 359 - Murder - Ultra Vires - Acquittal - Sustainability - Appellant constable of CRPF was convicted for offence u/ss. 302 and 307 of IPC and u/s. 27 of Act -Trial Court convicted appellant u/ss. 302 and 307 of IPC, but HC reversed conviction holding that there was some irreconcilable inconsistency in prosecution case - Aggrieved instant appeal - (A) Whether reversal of conviction by HC was sustainable - Held, there was no reason to interfere with order of acquittal given by HC - Order of HC was neither perverse nor not based on proper appreciation of evidence, therefore, on merits of order of acquittal granted by HC there was no reason to interfere - (B) Whether s. 27(3) of Act was ultra vires of Constitution - Held, in s. 27(3) of the Act provision of mandatory death penalty was more unreasonable inasmuch it provided whoever used any prohibited arms or prohibited ammunition or acted in contravention of s. 7 and if such use or act resulted in death of any other person then that person guilty of such use or acting in contravention of s. 7 should be punishable with death - Word 'use' was not defined in the Act, hence, word 'use' had to be viewed in its common meaning - In view of such very wide meaning of word 'use' even an unintentional or an accidental use resulting in death of any other person should subject person so using to a death penalty - Both words 'use' and 'result' were very wide and such a law was neither just, reasonable nor was it fair and fell out of 'due process' test as laid down in Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India, 1978 INDLAW SC 212 - Further, in the case of s.27(3) law was totally devoid of any guidelines and no exceptions were carved out - In view of mandate of art.13 of the Constitution which was an Article within Part-III of Constitution, s. 27(3) having been enacted in clear contravention of Part-III rights, s. 27(3) of the Act was repugnant to arts. 14 and 21 and was void - Further, s. 27(3) of the Act also deprived judiciary from discharging its Constitutional duties of judicial review whereby it had power of using discretion in sentencing procedure - S. 27(3) of the Act while purporting to impose mandatory death penalty sought to nullify salutary provisions of ss. 235(2) and 354(3) of CrPC, which was contrary to law laid down in Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab - 1980 INDLAW SC 586 - Hence, s. 27(3) of the Act was ultra vires concept of judicial review which was one of basic features of Constitution - Appeal dismissed.
