Status: Negative Judicial Treatment

Namit Sharma v Union Of India

Supreme Court of India

13 September 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 311; (2013) 1 SCC 745; AIR 2012 SC (Supp) 867; 2012 (6) ALD(SC) 145; 2012 (3) CLT(SC) 360; JT 2012 (9) SC 166; 2012 (4) RCR(Civil) 903; 2012(8) SCALE 593

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: Competence Of The Legislature, Freedom of Information Act, 2002, Constitutionality, Privacy and Government Information Legislation Amendment Bill, 2010, Government Information (Public Access) Act, 2009, Tribunals, Courts and Enforcement Act, 2007, Personal and Privacy Information Act, 1998, Mahatma Gandhi Rural Guarantee Act, 2005, Freedom of Information Act, 1966

Summary: (A)Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, Part III, art. 14 - Right to Information Act, 2005, ss. 12(5)(6) and 15(5) - Criteria of appointment of Commissioners - Constitutionality of provisions - Challenged - Instant writ petition was filed by petitioner and alleged that criterion for appointment of persons who were to adjudicate the disputes under 2005 Act were too vague, general, ultra vires the Constitution and contrary to established principles of law laid down by a plethora of judgments of SC - Persons who were appointed to discharge judicial or quasi-judicial functions or powers under 2005 Act ought to have a judicial approach, experience, knowledge and expertise - Petitioner questioned the Constitutional validity of s. 12(5), (6) and s. 15 (5), (6) of 2005 Act - Those provisions primarily dealt with eligibility criteria for appointment to the posts of Chief Information Commissioners and Information Commissioners, both at Central and State levels - Hence, instant petition - Whether s. 12(5), (6) and s. 15 (5), (6) of 2005 Act were Constitutionally valid - Held, to examine Constitutionality or otherwise of a statute or any of its provisions, most relevant considerations was the object and reasons as well as the legislative history of statute - It would be necessary for Court to examine the reasons of enactment of a particular provision so as to find out its ultimate impact vis-a-vis the Constitutional provisions - S. 12(5) of the Act did not suffered from infirmity of providing no criteria resulting in the introduction of the element of arbitrariness or discrimination - Provision required the persons to be of eminence and with knowledge and experience in law, science and technology, social service, management, journalism, mass medial or administration and governance, in social public life - Knowledge and experience in those fields normally should be preceded by a minimum requisite qualification prescribed in that field - Vagueness or doubt did not introduce the element of discrimination in provision - Persons from those various walks of life were considered eligible for appointment to post of Chief Information Commissioner (CIC) and Information Commissioners (IC) in respective ICs - That gave a wide zone of consideration and that alleged vagueness could always be clarified by the appropriate Govt. in exercise of its powers u/s. 27 and 28 of the Act, respectively - S. 12(6) of the Act, was silent as to what class of persons would be eligible - Element of arbitrariness and discrimination was evidenced by language of s. 12(6) of the Act - However, SC would normally adopt an approach which was tilted in favour of constitutionality and would prefer reading down provision, if necessary, by adding some words rather than declaring it unconstitutional - Disqualifications would only come into play once a person was appointed as CIC/IC at level and he would cease to hold any office of profit or carry any business or pursue any profession that he did prior to such appointment - It was thus implicit in this provision that a person could not hold any of the posts specified in s. 12(6) of the Act simultaneous to his appointment as CIC or IC - In fact, cessation of his previous appointment, business or profession was a condition precedent to commencement of his appointment as CIC or IC - Petition partly allowed.

(B) Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, Part III, art. 14 - Right to Information Act, 2005, ss. 12(5)(6) and 15(5) - Scheme of the Act - Appointment of Commissioners - Amendment - Whether prescribed criteria and classification resulting therefrom was discriminatory, arbitrary and had no nexus to object of the Act - Held, it was not for Courts to spell out what ought to be qualifications or experience for appointment to a particular post - If legislature itself provided 'knowledge and experience' as basic criteria of eligibility for appointment, that per se, would not attract the rigors of art. 14 of Constitution - It would be appropriate to interpret and read into s. 12(5) of the Act that 'knowledge and experience' in a particular subject would be deemed to include the basic qualification in that subject - S. 12(5) of the Act had inbuilt guidelines to effect that knowledge and experience, being two distinct concepts, should be construed in their correct perspective, that would include basic qualification and experience in respective field, both being pre-requisites for that section - Ambiguity, if any, resulting from language of provision was insignificant, being merely linguistic in nature and same was capable of being clarified by framing appropriate rules in exercise of powers of Central Govt. u/s. 27 of the Act - Certain requirements of law and procedure would have to be read into this provision to sustain its constitutionality - With the aid of principles 'reading into' and/or 'reading down', relevant provisions, Court could bridge the gap that was left by legislature inadvertently - Both said principles have to be applied while interpreting s. 12(5) of the Act - Application of said principles would render the provision constitutional and not opposed to doctrine of equality - There existed a definite requirement for appointing person to those posts with legal background and acumen so as to ensure faith and confidence of public in independent functioning of IC and for fair and expeditious performance of its functions - Person to be appointed as CIC or IC, should have wide knowledge and experience in law and other specified fields - Expression 'experience in law' was an expression of composite content and would take within its ambit both requisite qualification in law and experience in the field of law - Experience in absence of basic qualification would certainly be insufficient in its content and would not satisfy requirements of said provision - Wide knowledge, refer to knowledge relatable to education in such field whereas experience would necessarily related to experience attained by doing work in such field - Both should be read together in order to satisfy the requirements of ss. 12(5) and 15(5) of the Act - Experience in respective field referred to in s. 12(5) of the Act would be an experience gained by the person upon possessing basic qualification in that field - Field itself did not prescribe any degree or appropriate course, but it would be applicable for the fields like law, engineering, science and technology, management, social service and journalism - CIC and members of Commission were required to possess wide knowledge and experience in the respective fields - They were expected to be well versed with procedure that they were to adopt while performing adjudicatory and quasi-judicial functions in accordance with statutory provisions and scheme of the Act - Legislative scheme of the Act clearly postulated passing of a reasoned order in light of provision of statute and Constitution - A reasoned order would help parties to question the correctness of order effectively and within legal requirements of writ jurisdiction of SC and HCs - Provisions of ss. 12(5) and 15(5) of the Act were constitutionally valid - Expression 'knowledge and experience' appearing in those provisions would mean and include a basic degree in respective field and experience gained thereafter - It was preferred to read those provisions as having effect 'post-appointment' - Cessation/termination of holding of office of profit, pursuing any profession or carrying any business was a condition precedent to appointment of a person as CIC or IC at Centre or State levels - There was absolute necessity for legislature to reword or amend the provisions of ss. 12(5), 12(6) and 15(5), 15(6) of the Act - Under scheme of the Act, it was clear that orders of Commissions were subject to judicial review before HCs and then before SC - Information Commission was bound by the law of precedence, i.e., judgments of Hcs and SC, in order to maintain judicial discipline and consistency in functioning of Commission - Petition partly allowed.
