A. Shanmugam v Ariya Kshatriya Rajakula Vamsathu Madalaya Nandhavana Paripalanai Sangam Represented By Its President Etc.

Supreme Court of India

27 April 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Dalveer Bhandari, Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 159; (2012) 6 SCC 430; AIR 2012 SC 2010; 2012 (5) ALD(SC) 41; 2012(3) ALL MR 946; 2012 (92) ALR 730; 2012 (4) ALT 5; 2012 (2) CLT(SC) 203; JT 2012 (4) SC 383; 2012 (5) MLJ(SC) 618; 2012 (3) RCR(Civil) 1; 2012 (116) RD 567; 2012(4) SCALE 666; [2012] 4 S.C.R. 74

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - Suit for title and recovery of possession - Suit property belonged to 'X', who dedicated said property for construction of a Dharamshala for the benefit of the Ariya Kshatriya community - Appellant's father was engaged as a Watchman on a monthly salary by the respondent-society to look after the Dharamshala and in that capacity lived in the premises with his family including the appellant - In the year 1994, the respondent-society claiming to be the owner of the suit property tried to dispossess the appellant by force necessitating the appellant to file a suit - Trial court dismissed said suit and held that appellant was in possession of the suit property in the capacity of a Watchman - First Appellate Court allowed appeal filed against said order - Respondent-Society preferred a second appeal before the HC who set aside the judgment of the First Appellate Court - HC also restored the judgment of the trial court - Hence, the instant appeal - Whether order of the HC could be upheld and respondent-society was entitled to the possession of the suit property - Held, once the court discovers falsehood, concealment, distortion, obstruction or confusion in pleadings and documents, the court should in addition to full restitution impose appropriate costs - The court must ensure that there was no incentive for wrong doer in the temple of justice - Truth was the foundation of justice and it has to be the common endeavour of all to uphold the truth and no one should be permitted to pollute the stream of justice - Watchman, caretaker or a servant employed to look after the property can never acquire interest in the property irrespective of his long possession - The Watchman, caretaker or a servant was under an obligation to hand over the possession forthwith on demand - According to the principles of justice, equity and good conscience, Courts were not justified in protecting the possession of a Watchman, caretaker or servant who was only allowed to live into the premises to look after the same - The Watchman, caretaker or agent holds the property of the principal only on behalf the principal - He acquires no right or interest whatsoever in such property irrespective of his long stay or possession - The protection of the Court can be granted or extended to the person who has valid subsisting rent agreement, lease agreement or licence agreement in his favour - In the instant case, a well-reasoned judgment and a decree passed by the trial court ought not to have been reversed by the First Appellate Court - It was reiterated that the appellant's father was engaged as a watchman on a monthly salary and in that capacity he was allowed to stay in the impugned property and after his death his son (the appellant) continued to serve the respondent-society as a Watchman and was allowed to live in the premises - The suit property was admittedly owned by the respondent-society - Appellant has also failed to prove the adverse possession of the impugned property - Only by obtaining the ration card and the house tax receipts, the appellant cannot strengthen his claim of adverse possession - Hence, HC was fully justified in reversing the judgment of the First Appellate Court and restoring the judgment of the trial court - No interference was called for - Looking to the fact that the appellant was a Watchman and may not be able to bear the financial burden, Court directed to appellant to pay very nominal costs of Rs. 25,000/- within a period of 2 months - Appeals dismissed.
