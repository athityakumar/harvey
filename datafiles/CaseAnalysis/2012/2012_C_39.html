Columbia Sportswear Company v Director Of Income Tax, Bangalore

Supreme Court of India

30 July 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Patnaik, Swatanter Kumar, S.H. Kapadia

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 438; (2012) 11 SCC 224; AIR 2012 SC 3038; 2012 (3) CompLJ 406; 2012 (283) E.L.T. 321; [2012] 346 ITR 161; 2012 (6) MLJ(SC) 737; [2012] 7 S.C.R. 187; [2012] 210 TAXMAN 42

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 136, 226 and 227 - Income Tax Act, 1961 - Special leave petition - Exercise of power - Maintainability - Instant petition u/art. 136 of Constitution seeking special leave to appeal against the order of the Authority for Advance Rulings (Income Tax) constituted Chap. XIX-B of the Income Tax Act, 1961 - It was contended that instant Court might, however, decline to interfere with the order passed by the Authority in exercise of its powers u/art. 136 of Constitution where it feels that it would be more appropriate that the order of the Authority must first be examined by the HC u/art. 226/227 of Constitution - Whether an advance ruling pronounced by the Authority could be challenged by the applicant or by the Commissioner or any income-tax authority subordinate to him u/art. 226/227 of the Constitution before the HC or u/art. 136 of the Constitution before instant Court - Held, meaning of the expression 'tribunal' in art. 136 and the expression 'tribunals' in art. 227 of the Constitution had been explained in Harinagar Sugar Mills v. Shyam Sunder 1961 Indlaw SC 372 - Thus, the test for determining whether a body was a tribunal or not was to find out whether it was vested with the judicial power of the State by any law to pronounce upon rights or liabilities arising out of some special law and this test has been reiterated by instant Court in Jaswant Sugar Mills Ltd. v. Lakshmi Chand & Ors. 1962 Indlaw SC 79 - SC considered the observations of the Authority but thought that it could hold that an advance ruling of the Authority could only be challenged u/art. 136 of the Constitution before this Court and not u/arts. 226 and/or 227 of the Constitution before the HC - SC in L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India and Others 1997 Indlaw SC 2816, a Constitution Bench of instant Court has held that the power vested in the HC's to exercise judicial superintendence over the decisions of all courts and tribunals within their respective jurisdictions was part of the basic structure of the Constitution - Therefore, to hold that an advance ruling of the authority should not be permitted to be challenged before the High Court u/arts. 226 and/or 227 of Constitution would be to negate a part of the basic structure of Constitution - SC thus, of the opinion that when an advance ruling of the Authority was challenged before the HC u/arts. 226 and/or 227 of Constitution, the same should be heard directly by a DB of the HC and decided as expeditiously as possible - (B) Whether SC should entertain instant petition u/art. 136 of the Constitution or ask the petitioner to approach the HC u/arts. 226 and/or 227 of Constitution - Held, art. 136 of the Constitution itself state that SC may, 'in its discretion', grant special leave to appeal from any order passed or made by any court or tribunal in the territory of India - Words 'in its discretion' in art. 136 of Constitution made the exercise of the power of instant Court in art. 136 discretionary - Hence, even if good grounds were made out in a Special Leave Petition u/art. 136 for challenge to an advance ruling given by the Authority, SC might still, in its discretion, refuse to grant special leave on the ground that the challenge to the advance ruling of the authority could also be made to the HC u/arts. 226 and/or 227 of the Constitution on the selfsame grounds - In Sirpur Paper Mills Ltd. v. Commissioner of Wealth Tax, Hyderabad, 1970 Indlaw SC 276, it has been observed that SC did not encourage an aggrieved party to appeal directly to SC against the order of a Tribunal exercising judicial functions unless it appeared to the Court that a question of principle of great importance arises - Unless, therefore, a Special Leave Petition raises substantial questions of general importance or a similar question is already pending before instant Court for decision, SC did not entertain a Special Leave Petition directly against an order of the tribunal - In instant Special Leave Petition, SC did not find that a substantial question of general importance arises nor was it shown that a similar question was already pending before instant Court for which the petitioner should be permitted to approach instant Court directly against the advance ruling of the Authority - Liberty granted to petitioner to move the appropriate HC u/art. 226 and/or 227 of the Constitution - Appeal disposed of.
