Prakash Chandra v Narayan

Supreme Court of India

23 April 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya, G.S. Singhvi

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 176; (2012) 5 SCC 403; AIR 2012 SC 2826; 2012 (4) ALD(SC) 161; 2012 (92) ALR 908; 2012 (3) AWC 3007; 2012 (3) Bom.C.R. 681; 2012 (2) CLT(SC) 239; JT 2012 (4) SC 349; 2012 (6) MLJ(SC) 108; 2012 (3) RCR(Civil) 335; 2012 (116) RD 616; 2012(4) SCALE 583; [2012] 5 S.C.R. 444

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Summary: Civil Procedure - Specific Relief Act, 1963, s. 20 (2) - Suit for specific performance - Failure to raise the defence of hardship - Appellant filed suit against respondent for specific performance of agreement for sale in respect of agricultural land - Trial Court decreed suit for specific performance and directed respondent to deliver possession of the suit land to appellant with clear condition - Aggrieved by said order, respondent filed appeal, First Appellate Court allowed appeal but reversed the judgment and decree thereby allowing discretion in favour of respondent by directing him to pay earnest money with interest - On appeal, HC dismissed second appeal on the ground that the first Appellate Court factually found that respondent would be landless as against appellant who was having various businesses as well - Hence instant appeal - Appellant contended that in the absence of any defence taken by respondent that he would become landless if the relief for specific performance was granted and in absence of any material on record, the finding of the First Appellate Court could not be sustained - Whether the grant of relief for specific performance would cause hardship to respondent - Held, from evidence on record, it was clear that no issue relating to hardship of respondent was framed by below courts - In a case of Specific performance, hardship was a good defence provided such defence was taken by defendant and evidence in support of such defence was brought on record, while in this case no such defence was taken by respondent and no evidence was brought on record in its support - Appellant proved that he was always ready and willing to perform his part of the contract - Further, HC failed to notice that respondent had not taken any defence of hardship and no such issue was framed and in absence of any such evidence on record, First Appellate Court held that he would be landless should the decree for specific performance be granted - Impugned orders were set aside - Appeal allowed.
