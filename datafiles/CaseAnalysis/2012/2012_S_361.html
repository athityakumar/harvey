Anju Chaudhary v State of Uttar Pradesh and another

Supreme Court of India

13 December 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 558; (2013) 6 SCC 384; 2014 ALL MR (Cri) 5226; 2013 CRLJ 776; 2013 (1) CTC 714; JT 2012 (12) SC 582; 2013 (1) KLT 549; 2013 (1) MLJ(Crl) 176; 2013 (1) RCR(Criminal) 686; 2012(12) SCALE 619; [2012] 13 S.C.R. 901

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: U.P. Gangsters and Activists Prevention Act

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 156(3), 154 and 220 - Question as to - Number of FIRs - In one incident - Question of public importance and one that was likely to arise more often than not in relation to the lodging of the First Information Report (FIR) with the aid of s. 156(3) of CrPC or otherwise independently within the ambit of s. 154 of CrPC was as to whether there could be more than one FIR in relation to the same incident or different incidents arising from the same occurrence - Held, it was true that law recognized common trial or common FIR being registered for one series of acts so connected together as to form the same transaction as contemplated u/s. 220 of CrPC - There could not be any straight jacket formula, but instant question had to be answered on the facts of each case - SC in one case had held that the expression 'same transaction' from its very nature was incapable of exact definition - It was not intended to be interpreted in any artificial or technical sense - Common sense in the ordinary use of language should decide whether or not in the very facts of case, it could be held to be one transaction - Mohan Baitha v. State of Bihar 2001 Indlaw SC 1989, relied on - It was not possible to enunciate any formula of universal application for the purpose of determining whether two or more acts constituted the same transaction - Such things were to be gathered from the circumstances of given case indicating proximity of time, unity or proximity of place, continuity of action, commonality of purpose or design - Where two incidents were of different times with involvement of different persons, there was no commonality and the purpose thereof different and they emerged from different circumstances, it would not be possible for the Court to take view that they form part of the same transaction and therefore, there could be common FIR or subsequent FIR could not be permitted to be registered or there could be common trial - Similarly, for several offences to be part of the same transaction, test which had to be applied was whether they were so related to one another in point of purpose or of cause and effect, or as principal and subsidiary, so as to result in one continuous action - Thus, where there was commonality of purpose or design, where there was continuity of action, then all those persons involved could be accused of the same or different offences "committed in the course of the same transaction" - Appeal dismissed.
