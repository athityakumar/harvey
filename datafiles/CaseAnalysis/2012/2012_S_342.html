Gurmail Singh v State of Punjab and another

Supreme Court of India

21 November 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, Swatanter Kumar

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 580; (2013) 4 SCC 228; [2012] 13 S.C.R. 517

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Lalkara

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 304 Part I, 326 r/w 149, 324 r/w 34, 148 - Arms Act, 1959 Act, s. 25 - Unlawful assembly - Common intention - Murder - Conviction - Challenged - Appellants (accused) family and deceased's families were in a dispute related to land - Appellants allegedly committed offences u/ss. 302, 304 Part I, 326 r/w 149, 324 r/w 34 and 148 of IPC - Trial Court convicted appellants for offences u/ss. 304 Part I, 324 of IPC and u/s. 25 of the Act - However, acquitted for offences u/s. 302, 149 - Aggrieved appellants filed appeals before HC - HC by impugned judgment dismissed appeals by reversing judgment passed by Trial Court and convicted the appellants u/ss. 302 r/w s. 149, 324 r/w s. 34, 326 r/w s. 149 and 148 of IPC - Hence, instant appeals - Appellants contended that ingredients of s. 149 of IPC were not made out - Whether impugned judgment passed by HC reversing findings of Trial Court in acquitting appellants was justified - Held, in instant case, 8 persons had assembled with guns and sharp-edged 'gandasas' - It was clear that assembly was not without a purpose - Their getting together and firing a few shots in air before the incident actually took place suggested that they gathered to either display a show of strength or commit an offence - Fact that assembly was armed, it would not be off the mark to expect it to be for a somewhat disreputable purpose and not merely by way of a show of strength - That view was fortified by what actually transpired at the scene of occurrence, (lalkara, challenge) given members of assembly that no one from deceased's party would be spared - It was necessary to keep in mind the antecedent circumstances, viz, land dispute between parties and murder of 'M' (son of deceased) by 'N' (whose nephews were appellants/accused) - Land dispute was not a trivial matter was clear from the fact that it even led to murder of 'M' by 'N' - Father of 'M' was an eyewitness to murder - Thus, not only was there a motive for committing crime but motive had already led to a murder on an earlier occasion - Overall pictured emerged was that appellants had assembled with a common object of committing an offence and not merely as a show of strength and thus, constituted an unlawful assembly - At least one injury caused to deceased with a firearm was on a vital part of his body and deceased had as many as 116 lacerated wounds and 15 pallets were found in his body - It was not as if only 1 gunshot was fired or 1 gandasa blow given to deceased - 2 shots were fired at deceased and gandasa blows were given - Evidence was clear that offence committed was murder of deceased - Given the number and nature of injuries, it was difficult to come to any conclusion other than that injuries were sufficient in ordinary course of nature to cause death - In fact deceased did succumb to injuries - No doubt that offence committed by unlawful assembly was murder of deceased and injuries to other members of his party - It was clear that assembly of 8 had come from 2 different villages with an aggressive intent was clear from the fact that 2 of them were armed with guns and others with sharp-edged gandasas - 2 members of assembly gave a challenge to effectively 'finish off' deceased and his party - On totality of facts and circumstances of case, was compelling that attack on deceased was with object of killing him and injuring those with him - Requirement of s. 149 of IPC was met in instant case - All ingredients of s. 149 of IPC have met - Impugned conviction orders passed by HC was upheld - Appeals dismissed.
