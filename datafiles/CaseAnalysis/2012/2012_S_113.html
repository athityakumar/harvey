Narender Kumar v State (Nct Of Delhi)

Supreme Court of India

25 May 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 144; (2012) 7 SCC 171; AIR 2012 SC 2281; 2012 ALL MR (Cri) 2400; 2012 CRLJ 3033; 2012 (190) DLT 1; 2012 (3) JCC 1888; JT 2012 (5) SC 442; 2012 (3) MLJ(Crl) 707; 2012 (2) OLR 383; 2012 (3) RCR(Criminal) 66; 2012(5) SCALE 657; [2012] 6 S.C.R. 148

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 376 - Evidence of prosecutrix - Reliability - Appellant was convicted for offences punishable u/s. 376 of IPC - Appeal filed against conviction before HC was dismissed - Hence, instant appeal - Appellant submitted that prosecutrix (PW.1) could not be relied upon because there were material contradictions in her deposition and that the prosecutrix was an unchaste woman - Whether Courts below erred in convicting appellant merely relying on the deposition of the prosecutrix and considering the deposition of doctor - Held, prosecution had to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt and could not take support from the weakness of the case of defence - There must be proper legal evidence and material on record to record the conviction of the accused - Conviction could be based on sole testimony of the prosecutrix provided it lends assurance of her testimony - However, in case the court had reason not to accept the version of prosecutrix on its face value, it may look for corroboration - In case the evidence was read in its totality and the story projected by the prosecutrix was found to be improbable, the prosecutrix case becomes liable to be rejected - Hence, court must act with sensitivity and appreciate the evidence in totality of the background of the entire case and not in the isolation - Further, even if the prosecutrix was of easy virtue/unchaste woman that itself could not be a determinative factor and the court was required to adjudicate whether the accused committed rape on the victim on the occasion complained of - In the instant case, if the evidence of the prosecutrix was read and considered in totality of the circumstances alongwith the other evidence on record, in which the offence was alleged to had been committed, her deposition did not inspire confidence - Prosecution did not disclosed the true genesis of the crime - Hence, appellant was entitled to the benefit of doubt - Judgment and order passed by HC and Trial Court set aside - Appeal allowed.
