Gajoo v State Of Uttarakhand

Supreme Court of India

13 September 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 310; (2012) 9 SCC 532; 2013 CRLJ 88; 2012 (4) JCC 2658; JT 2012 (9) SC 10; 2012 (4) RCR(Criminal) 921; 2012(9) SCALE 32; [2012] 7 S.C.R. 1033

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Defect in investigation - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant (accused) allegedly committed murder of deceased - Trial Court convicted appellant u/s. 302 of IPC - Aggrieved appellant filed an appeal before HC, which was dismissed confirming the conviction and sentence passed by Trial Court - Hence, instant appeal - Appellant contended PW2 and PW3, so-called eye-witnesses, were not genuine and were related to PW1 (informant) and that there were material contradictions between medical and oral evidence (Ext.Ka10) and statements of PW2 and PW3 - Further contended that 'Daranti' (Ext.2) and blood stained 'pyjama' (Ext.Ka5) which were recovered, were not sent for FSL examination and no serological report was obtained - Whether impugned order of HC confirming conviction of appellant was justified - Held, related witness did not necessarily mean or was equivalent to an interested witness - A witness might be called 'interested' only when he or she derives some benefit from the result of litigation; in the decree in a civil case, or in seeing an accused person punished - In instant case, once presence of PW2 and PW3 was shown to be natural, then to doubt their statement would not be a correct approach in law - It had unequivocally come on record through various witnesses, including PW4 (son of deceased), that there was a 'Satyanarayan Katha' at house of one villager , which was attended by various villagers - It was on their way back at midnight when PW2 and PW3 had saw the occurrence in dark with the help of torches that they were carrying - Mere fact that PW2 happens to be related to PW1 and to deceased, would not result in doubting the statement of those witnesses which otherwise have credence, were reliable and were duly corroborated by other evidence - Once it was established that their depositions did not suffer from material contradictions, were trustworthy and in consonance with said principles, Courts would not be justified in overlooking such valuable piece of evidence - Appellant contended that according to medical evidence, death resulted from asphyxia - As per post mortem report (Ex.Ka10) 2 injuries were noticed and Doctor specifically recorded that both lungs of deceased were congested, the larynx and trachea were found congested and expert judgment of Doctor based on those factors was that death occurred due to asphyxia because of strangulation - Injuries were evidently inflicted by appellant holding 'Daranti 'in one hand and holding the neck of deceased with other hand - It was pressing of her neck and body to earth by appellant and another accused of much greater strength than deceased, that resulted in her death - Once, medical evidence and ocular evidence, were co-jointly read, it did not falsify either statement of witnesses, PW2 and 3 or post-mortem report and in fact, both of them should be read as complimentary to each other - Even if there was some variation, still, it would be so immaterial and inconsequential that it would not give any benefit to appellant - Ext. 2 and Ext. 5 were not sent to laboratory for obtaining serologist report, but absence thereof per se would not give any advantage to appellant - That was merely a defect in investigation - A defective investigation, unless affects the very root of prosecution case and was prejudicial to appellant, should not be an aspect of material consideration by Court - Ext. 2 and Ext. 5 were recovered by IO (PW6) and recoveries were duly established before Court - Recoveries were proved and case of prosecution was duly supported by witnesses, who proved the case of prosecution beyond any reasonable doubt - It was clear that defect in investigation or omission on the part of IO, could not prove to be of any advantage to appellant - No doubt IO ought to have obtained serologist's report both in respect of Ext.2 and Ext.5 and matched it with blood group of deceased - That was a definite lapse on the part of IO which could not be overlooked by Court, it was directed to take disciplinary action against IO (PW6) - Madan Gopal Kakad v. Naval Dubey & Anr, 1992 Indlaw SC 446, referred to - Impugned order of HC was justified and upheld - Appeal dismissed.
