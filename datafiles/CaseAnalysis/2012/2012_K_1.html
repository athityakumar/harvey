K. B. Nagur M. D. (Ayu.) v Union of India

Supreme Court of India

24 February 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, S.H. Kapadia, A.K. Patnaik

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 435; (2012) 4 SCC 483; AIR 2012 SC 1774; 2012 (3) AWC 2396; JT 2012 (2) SC 626; 2012 (2) RCR(Civil) 481; [2012] 1 S.C.R. 1023

Case Digest

Subject: Election; Health & Drug; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Siddha, Central Council

Summary: Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Indian Medicine Central Council Act, 1970, ss. 3,4,7 - Statutory Body - Mandatory Election - Amendment - Ultra Vires - Sustainability - Central Council of Indian Medicine was a statutory body, constituted in terms of s. 3 of Act and s. 4 of Act mandates that election u/cl. (a) or cl. (b) of sub-S. (1) of s. 3 of Act shall be conducted by Central Government in accordance with rules as may be made in this behalf - Central Council discharges very significant and important functions which would affect not only education in these three systems but even their practice and treatment of thousands of patients under these systems - Statute places an obligation upon Central Government to hold elections and ensure that Central Council works smoothly and in accordance with provisions of Act, s. 7 of Act refers to tenure that an elected member was entitled to enjoy, upon his election to Central Council - Petition was an Ayurvedic doctor holding degree of Ayurvedic Medicine, claimed that he held and still holds various offices in different organizations and contended that they had not been properly constituted and for which elections had not been held for last 20-25 years - Petitioner, having failed to achieve results at hands of Central Government or Central Council, filed petition u/art. 32 of Constitution, for direction to UOI to discharge its statutory duty in terms of s. 3 of Act, to fill up membership of Governing Body of Central Council with regard to members who had completed term of five years within stipulated period and for striking down of provision of s. 7 of Act as unconstitutional, undemocratic and violative of arts. 14,16 of Constitution - (A) Whether provision of s. 7 of Act were unconstitutional, undemocratic and violative of arts. 14,16 of Constitution and ultra vires - Held, words of s. 7 of Act were intended to operate in an extra-ordinary situation, as normal course should be that Central Government hold elections within a period of five years from date of notification of elected candidates for previous tenure, even where recourse to this exceptional situation becomes necessary, even there, concept of reasonable time would come into play, in a situation where no definite period had been prescribed by Legislature itself - Courts could always supply such lacuna in interpretation of provisions of a law so as to achieve object of Act particularly when such interpretation would be in consonance with legislative object of statute - Thus, a period of 3 months would be more than sufficient time for completing election process, in event of exceptional circumstances and if elections had not been commenced and completed within period of previous tenure of five years, as was requirement of law, and Govt. could not abjure its obligation to do so within a maximum period of 3 months, hence s. 7 of Act, or any part thereof was neither ultra vires nor violative of arts. 14 and/or 16 of Constitution - (B) Whether Act mandates Central Govt. to hold election every 5 years - Held, it was mandatory that Central Govt. shall discharge all its duties and functions as contemplated u/ss. 3, 4, 7 of Act, without default, delay and within required intervals, further it was obligation of Central Govt. to hold election to Central Council within period of five years i.e., before expiry of , term of office of President/Vice-President and Member of Central Council, as provided u/s. 7 of Act - Petitions dismissed.
