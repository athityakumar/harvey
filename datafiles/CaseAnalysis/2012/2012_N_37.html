Raheja Universal Limited v Nrc Limited & Ors

Supreme Court of India

07 February 2012

Case Analysis

Bench

Swatanter Kumar, S.H. Kapadia, K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan

Where Reported

2012 Indlaw SC 52; (2012) 4 SCC 148; AIR 2012 SC 1440; 2012 (4) AWC 3783; 2012 (1) BC 686; 2012 (1) CLT(SC) 250; [2012] 170 Comp Cas 256; 2012 (2) CompLJ 1; 2012 (2) CTC 103; 2012 (1) DRTC 761; JT 2012 (1) SC 631; 2012 (3) MLJ(SC) 280; 2012 (2) RCR(Civil) 506; 2012(2) SCALE 468; [2012] 3 S.C.R. 388

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Corporate; Land & Property

Keywords: Rehabilitation Scheme, State Sales Tax Act

Summary: (A) Corporate - Banking & Finance - Sick Industrial Companies (Special Provisions) Act, 1985, ss. 17(3), 22(3) - Scope and ambit of s. 22 - Respondent company was declared as sick unit by Board for Industrial and Financial Restructuring (BIFR) - Respondent sought to dispose of surplus land for bringing additional funds required for financial restructuring - Respondent entered into a Memorandum of Understanding (MoU) and several agreements for sale with appellant company for selling its land - Since appellant failed to disburse third installment of sale consideration, respondent could not even clear dues of employees, who had taken benefit of early retirement scheme - Respondent applied to BIFR - BIFR fixed cut-off date for financial revival by an order u/s. 17(3) of the Act, however, directed to obtain prior approval before sale of land - Parties appealed to Appellate Authority for Industrial and Financial Reconstruction (AAIFR) - AAIFR held that scheme could be approved but subject to pre-payment of entire remaining consideration, as per its directions, for setting off labour dues - AAIFR, therefore, permitted land, though an asset of the company, to be sold - Parties including labour union challenged AAIFR order before HC - HC held that BIFR order was within the scope of s. 22(3) of the Act and AAIFR order permitting sale of land in furtherance to agreement between parties was not sustainable as it was part of revival scheme and sale had been permitted subject to final orders of BIFR - Hence, instant appeal - What is the scope and ambit of s. 22 of the Act - Held, intent of introducing s. 22 was to empower BIFR to issue any direction to sick industrial company, its creditors and shareholders, in interest of company or even in public interest, directing company not to dispose of any assets, except with consent of BIFR - S. 22 of the Act was reservoir of statutory powers empowering BIFR to determine a scheme, right from its presentation till its complete implementation in accordance with law, free of interjections and interference from other judicial processes - Further, s. 22(3) of the Act contemplated that any remedy for enforcement of a right which would be available to a third party and any such proceedings before any court or tribunal should remain stayed or be continued subject to such declaration - S. 22A of the Act dealt with restrictions and prohibitory orders which BIFR could pass, all for purposes of preparation of scheme and proper implementation and effective management of revival of sick industrial company - Ss. 22 and 22A were required to be read along with provisions of s. 26 of the Act that ousted jurisdiction of civil courts and vested exclusive jurisdiction for specified purposes with BIFR - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Corporate - Banking & Finance - Land & Property - Sick Industrial Companies (Special Provisions) Act, 1985, ss. 22(3) - Transfer of Property Act, 1882 - Overriding effect of 1985 Act - Whether the provisions of 1985 Act would prevail over the provisions of 1882 Act - Held, 1882 Act was a general law, controlling and operating in a very wide field - 1882 Act did not have application to a particular situation or class of persons, however, 1985 Act was a special legislation providing for imperative functioning of specialized bodies like BIFR and AAIFR and was intended to apply to a very specific situation, i.e., where a company was a sick industrial company - 1985 Act had no application even to other different kinds of companies within purview of Companies Act, 1956, except sick industrial companies - Legislature had undoubtedly given an overriding effect to provisions of 1985 Act and even restricted jurisdiction of civil courts, as was demonstrated from the language of ss. 26 and 32 of the 1985 Act - Thus, provisions of 1985 Act would prevail over provisions of the 1882 Act - Appeal dismissed.

(C) Corporate - Banking & Finance - Land & Property - Sick Industrial Companies (Special Provisions) Act, 1985, ss. 17(3), 22(3) - Transfer of Property Act, 1882, s. 53A - Sale of assets of sick industry - Right of purchaser - Determination - Whether agreement to sell by respondent in relation to an immovable property transferred or created any right or title in immovable property itself in favor of appellant purchaser - Held, MoU and agreements for sale of land belonging to company between appellant and respondent were signed prior to presentation of scheme before BIFR - However, second supplementary agreement for sale was executed not only subsequent to presentation of scheme before BIFR but even after BIFR had passed an order u/s. 17(3) of the 1985 Act - Further, sale proceeds received under agreements were utilized for revival of respondent company to a large extent - Once asset of the company and/or its sale proceeds had been integral part of formation and finalization of revival scheme, such transaction should not be stated to be beyond ambit and scope of s. 22(3) of the 1985 Act - Further, provisions of s. 53A of 1882 Act recognized a right of a transferee, however, even that provision did not create title of transferee in property in question but gave him a very limited right, that too, subject to satisfaction of conditions stated in s. 53A of the 1882 Act - Hence, agreements in the instant cases did not transfer or create any right or title in property in question in favor of appellant purchaser - Appeal dismissed.

(D) Corporate - Banking & Finance - Sick Industrial Companies (Special Provisions) Act, 1985, ss. 17, 18, 22(3) - Direction by BIFR - Jurisdiction - Whether in facts and circumstances of case, BIFR had jurisdiction to issue a direction or make a declaration in relation to agreement in question in exercise of powers vested in it u/s. 22(3) of the Act - Held, order of BIFR was passed clearly at the stage of consideration of revival scheme which was approved by consortium of banks for debt restructuring (CDR Group) as well as secured creditors - Scheme for revival of company on long term basis was primarily dependent upon sale proceeds of land in question on the one hand and utility of remaining land for revival of company on the other - Land was paramount asset of company for its revival and successful implementation of scheme in accordance with law - Asset was duly taken into consideration in formulation of the scheme as contemplated u/ss. 17 and 18 of the Act and appropriate directions, prohibitory orders of BIFR were issued within the ambit and scope of ss. 22(1), 22(3) and 22A of the Act - Appeal dismissed.

(E) Corporate - Banking & Finance - Sick Industrial Companies (Special Provisions) Act, 1985, ss. 22(3) - Sale of assets of sick industry - Direction by BIFR - Jurisdiction - Whether HC or BIFR had exceeded its jurisdiction while passing impugned orders - Held, AAIFR had disturbed BIFR order and held that contract between parties could not be suspended u/s. 22(3) and it was not in interest of respondent - However, AAIFR order was set aside and BIFR order of was restored by HC - No jurisdictional or other error was there in HC order in restoring BIFR order - Land being primary asset of respondent, could not be permitted to be dissolved by sale or otherwise without consent and approval of BIFR - BIFR was authority proprio vigore and required to oversee entire affairs of a sick industrial company and to ensure that the same were within framework of scheme formulated and approved by Board for revival of company in accordance with provisions of the 1985 Act - On facts as well, neither BIFR nor HC had exceeded its jurisdiction in passing impugned orders - It was not that respondent had been divested of its right by BIFR but only suspended final transfer of property in its favour in accordance with provisions of Act and limitations imposed therein - However, once scheme was implemented or period specified under provisions of ss. 22(3) and 22(4) expired, declaration would cease to exist and appellant would be entitled to enforce its rights in accordance with law as if no such declaration or restriction ever existed - Hence, impugned HC was maintained and directed parties to proceed the matter before BIFR in accordance with law - Appeal dismissed.
