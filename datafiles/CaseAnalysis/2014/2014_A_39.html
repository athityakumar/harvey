Amarendra Kumar Mohapatra and others v State of Orissa and others

Supreme Court of India

19 February 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

T.S. Thakur, Vikramajit Sen

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 105; (2014) 4 SCC 583; AIR 2014 SC 1716; JT 2014 (3) SC 244; 2014(2) SCALE 589; [2014] 2 S.C.R. 1031; 2014 (3) SLR 496

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Keywords: Orissa Service of Engineers (Validation of Appointment) Act, 2002, Orissa Engineering Service (Recruitment & Condition of Service) Rules, 1994, Orissa Service of Engineers' Rules, 1941

Summary: Service - Orissa Service of Engineers (Validation of Appointment) Act, 2002 - Orissa Service of Engineers' Rules, 1941 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts.14 and 16 - Irregular appointment - Legality - Primary issue that fell for determination touched the Constitutional validity of what was described as Act by which appointment of 881 ad hoc Assistant Engineers belonging to Civil, Mechanical and Electrical Engineering Wings of the State Engineering Service have been validated, no matter all such appointments were in breach of Rules - HC had in a batch of writ petitions filed before it struck down the impugned Legislation on the ground that the same violated the fundamental rights guaranteed to the writ petitioners u/arts. 14 and 16 of the Constitution - (A) What was the true nature and purport of the impugned legislation - More particularly was the impugned legislation a validation enactment or was it an enactment that granted regularisation to those appointed on ad hoc basis -

Held, pre-requisite of a piece of legislation that purported to validate any act, rule, action or proceedings were considered by HC - Shri Prithvi Cotton Mills Ltd. and Ann v. Broach Borough Municipality and Ors. 1969 Indlaw SC 296, relied on - Two essentials were identified by HC for any such legislation to be valid - These were: (a) Legislature enacting the Validation Act should be competent to enact the law and - (b) Cause for ineffectiveness or invalidity of the Act or the proceedings needed to be removed- Validation Act might have become necessary if the Govt. had appointed the ad hoc Assistant Engineers on a substantive basis in violation of the relevant recruitment Rules - For in that case, the Govt. would have done an act which was legally invalid requiring validation by a legislative measure - But a legislation that did not validate any such non-existent act, but simply appointed the ad hoc Assistant Engineers as substantive employees of the State by resort to a fiction, could not be described as a validating law - Legislation under challenge was in that view not a Validation Act as it purported to be but an enactment that regularised the appointment of graduate Stipendiary Engineers working as ad hoc Assistant Engineers as Assistant Engineers - While affirming the view taken by the HC that the validation law was not Constitutionally sound, SC proceeded on the assumption that the legislation with which it was dealing with was a Validation Act in the true sense - It was on that assumption that SC looked into the invalidity and held that the Validation Act did nothing except validating the appointments without removing the basis on which such appointments could be invalidated - SC had not proceeded on any such assumption in the instant case especially because counsel for some of the parties had argued that the legislation under challenge was not a Validation Enactment - Enactment in the case at hand dealt with the law relating to regularisation of incumbents holding public office on ad hoc or temporary basis, much in the same way as regularisation of such temporary appointments was ordered in terms of a scheme for that purpose - Only difference was that while a regularisation scheme could be framed by the Govt. in exercise of its executive power, the regularisation ordered in the case at hand was by way of a legislation - It was trite that what could be achieved by the Govt. by exercise of its executive power could certainly be achieved by legislation, as indeed it was achieved in the case at hand - (B) If the impugned enactment simply granted regularisation, did it suffer from any Constitutional infirmity - Held, decision of SC in one case had examined that question and explained the principle regarding regularisation - State of Karnataka v. M.L. Kesari and Ors. (2010) 9 SCC 247 2010 Indlaw SC 569, relied on - Decision in that case summed up the three essentials for regularisation (1) employees worked for ten years or more, (2) that they had so worked in a duly sanctioned post without the benefit or protection of the interim order of any court or tribunal and (3) they should have possessed the minimum qualification stipulated for the appointment - Subject to those three requirements being satisfied, even if the appointment process did not involve open competitive selection, the appointment would be treated irregular and not illegal and thereby qualify for regularisation - (C) Did s. 3(2) of Act/impugned legislation sufferred from any unconstitutionality, insofar as the same purports to grant Stipendiary Assistant Engineers seniority with effect from the date they were appointed on ad hoc basis - Held, SC was dealing with a somewhat similar fact situation - Narender Chadha & Ors. v. Union of India & Ors. 1986 Indlaw SC 661, relied on - Petitioners in that case were not promoted by following the actual procedure prescribed by the relevant Service Rules even though the appointments were made in the name of the President by the competent authority - Petitioners had based on such appointments, continuously held the post to which they were appointed and received salary and allowances payable to incumbent of such post - Incumbents were entered in the direct line of their promotion - Question, however, was whether it would be just and proper to hold that such promotees had no right to the post held by them for 15-20 years and could be reverted unceremoniously or treated as persons not belonging to the service at all - Thus, SC did not find any illegality or Constitutional infirmity in the provisions of ss. 3(2) or 3(3) of Act - Appeals allowed.
