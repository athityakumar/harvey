Ashok Rai v State of Uttar Pradesh and others

Supreme Court of India

15 April 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjana Prakash Desai, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 248; (2014) 5 SCC 713; 2014 CRLJ 3085; JT 2014 (5) SC 257; 2014 (2) RCR(Criminal) 536; 2014(5) SCALE 73

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Interested Witnesses, Cries

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 147, 148, 149, 302, 34 and 449 - Murder - Conviction - Challenged - Trial court convicted accused No. 6 u/s. 302 r/w ss. 34 and 449 of IPC and sentenced him to undergo imprisonment for life on both the counts - Accused Nos. 1 to 5, 7 and 8 were acquitted - Accused No. 6 carried an appeal against his conviction to the HC - During the pendency of the appeal accused No. 6 died and therefore his appeal abated - State carried an appeal against the acquittal of the other accused to the HC - HC held accused Nos. 1 and 3 guilty and convicted them for the offences punishable u/ss. 302 and 449 of IPC and u/s. 302 r/w. ss. 34 and 449 of IPC respectively - Each of them was sentenced to life imprisonment for each of the offences - Acquittal of the other accused was upheld - Instant appeal was preferred by accused No. 3 - Whether HC rightly convicted accused No. 3 for the offences punishable u/ss. 302 and 449 of IPC and u/s. 302 r/w. ss. 34 and 449 of IPC -

Held, perversity of the trial court's judgment becomes apparent when one finds the undue importance given by it to the diary entries made by the investigating officer PW7 - PW7 stated that it was mentioned by him in the case diary that it was the opinion of general public that involvement of the accused No. 6 was false - Trial court made a perverse observation that the investigating officer never tried to find out whether this rumour was true and submitted charge-sheet - Such reliance on diary entries was not permissible - Besides, the general feeling of the society had no relevance to a criminal case - A court deciding a criminal case must go by the legal evidence adduced before it - Trial court's order thus suffered from a gross error of law warranting the HC's interference - Evidence of PW4 was forthright and convincing - According to her, she woke-up when accused No. 3 pressed her mouth - She saw accused no. 6 pressing his husband's head hard - She saw accused no. 1 cut her husband's neck with a dao - Further stated that accused No. 6 had a gandasa in his hand and accuse no. 3 had a sword in his hand - HC rightly overturned the Trial court's order so far as it acquitted the accused No. 3 - Trial court's view was totally perverse and unreasonable - Undoubtedly, there were compelling and substantial reasons for the HC to interfere with it - Impugned order was upheld - Appeal dismissed.
