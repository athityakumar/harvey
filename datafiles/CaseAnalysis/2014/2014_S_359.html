Dashrath Rupsingh Rathod v State of Maharashtra and another

Supreme Court of India

01 August 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Vikramajit Sen, C. Nagappan, T.S. Thakur

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 494; (2014) 9 SCC 129; 2014 (7) ADJ 115; AIR 2014 SC 3519; 2014 (5) ALD(SC) 1; 2014 ALL MR (Cri) 3333; 2014 (5) ALT(SC) 33; 2014 (3) BC 513; 2014 (5) Bom.C.R. 243; 2014 (3) CompLJ 414; 2014 CRLJ 4350; 2014 (4) CTC 666; 2014 (212) DLT 737; 2014 (3) G.L.R. 2700; 2014 (3) JCC (NI) 147; JT 2014 (9) SC 81; 2014 (5) KarLJ 499; 2014 (3) KLT 605; 2014 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 2697; 2014 (6) MahLJ 404; 2014 (3) MLJ(Crl) 475; 2014(4) M.P.H.T. 257; 2014 (4) MPLJ 407; 2014 (2) OLR 584; 2014 (176) PLR 77; 2014 (3) RCR(Criminal) 904; 2014 (3) RLW 2494; 2014(9) SCALE 97; [2014] 11 S.C.R. 921; 2014 (4) WLN(SC) 19

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Venue, Environment (Protection) Act, 1986, Civil Action, Resides, Uttar Pradesh (Temporary) Control of Rent and Eviction Act, 1947, Kerala Land Acquisition Act, 1961, Andhra Pradesh (Agricultural Produce & Livestock) Markets Rules, 1969, Haryana Service of Engineers Rules, 1960

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, ss. 138, 138(c), 142, 145(2) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 177, 182(1), 184, 220 - Dishonour of cheque - Drawee bank - Cause of action - Jurisdiction - Whether return of the cheque by the drawee bank alone constitutes the commission of the offence and indicates the place where the offence was committed -

Held, s. 142 correctly employ the term 'cause of action' as compliance with the three factors contained in the proviso were essential for the cognizance of the offence, even though they were not part of the action constituting the crime - SC, however, was of the view that so far as the offence itself the proviso had no role to play - S. 138 of the Act in conjunction with s. 177 CrPC leave no manner of doubt that the return of the cheque by the drawee bank alone constitutes the commission of the offence and indicates the place where the offence was committed - In that analysis SC hold that the place, situs or venue of judicial inquiry and trial of the offence should logically be restricted to where the drawee bank, was located - Law should not be warped for commercial exigencies - As it was s. 138 of the Act has introduced a deeming fiction of culpability, even though, s. 420 was available in case the payee finds it advantageous or convenient to proceed under that provision - An interpretation should not be imparted to s. 138 of the Act which would render it as a device of harassment i.e. by sending notices from a place which had no casual connection with the transaction itself, and/or by presenting the cheque(s) at any of the banks where the payee may have an account - Interpretation of s. 138 of the Act which commend itself was that the offence contemplated therein stands committed on the dishonour of the cheque, and accordingly the JMFC at the place where it occur was ordinarily where the complaint should be filed, entertained and tried - Cognizance of the crime by the JMFC at that place however, could be taken only when the concomitants or constituents contemplated by the Section concatenate with each other - SC clarified that the place of the issuance or delivery of the statutory notice or where the Complainant chooses to present the cheque for encashment by his bank were not relevant for purposes of territorial jurisdiction of the complaints even though non-compliance thereof will inexorably lead to the dismissal of the complaint - On considerable consideration SC thought it expedient to direct that only those cases where, post the summoning and appearance of the alleged accused, the recording of evidence commenced as envisaged in s. 145(2) of the Act, would proceeding continue at that place - To clarify, regardless of whether evidence led before the Magistrate at the pre-summoning stage, either by affidavit or by oral statement, the complaint would be maintainable only at the place where the cheque stands dishonoured - To obviate and eradicate any legal complications, the category of complaint cases where proceedings have gone to the stage of s. 145(2) or beyond should be deemed to have been transferred from the Court ordinarily possessing territorial jurisdiction, as now clarified, to the Court where it was presently pending - All other Complaints should be returned to complainant for filing in the proper Court, in consonance with our exposition of the law - If such complaints were filed/refiled within thirty days of their return, they should be deemed to have been filed within the time prescribed by law, unless the initial or prior filing was itself time barred - Appeals dismissed.

T.S. Thakur, J.

Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, ss. 138, 138(c), 142, 145(2) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 177, 182(1), 184, 220 - Dishonour of cheque - Drawee bank - Cause of action - Jurisdiction - Whether return of the cheque by the drawee bank alone constitutes the commission of the offence and indicates the place where the offence was committed - Held, entirely agree with the conclusions which Vikramajit Sen, J. drawn based on a remarkably articulate process of reasoning that illumines the draft judgment authored by him - Fact that the view being taken by SC strikes a discordant note on certain aspects which have for long considered settled by earlier decisions of SC being only an additional reason for the modest addition that propose to make - An offence u/s. 138 of the Act was committed no sooner a cheque drawn by the accused on an account being maintained by him in a bank for discharge of debt/liability was returned unpaid for insufficiency of funds or for the reason that the amount exceeds the arrangement made with the bank - Cognizance of any such offence was however forbidden u/s. 142 of the Act except upon a complaint in writing made by the payee or holder of the cheque in due course within a period of one month from the date the cause of action accrues to such payee or holder s. 138(c) of the Act - Cause of action to file a complaint accrues to a complainant/payee/holder of a cheque in due course if (a) the dishonoured cheque was presented to the drawee bank within a period of six months from the date of its issue - (b) If complainant has demanded payment of cheque amount within thirty days of receipt of information by him from the bank regarding the dishonour of the cheque and - (c) If the drawer has failed to pay the cheque amount within fifteen days of receipt of such notice - Facts constituting cause of action do not constitute the ingredients of the offence u/s. 138 of the Act - Proviso to s. 138 simply postpones/defers institution of criminal proceedings and taking of cognizance by the Court till such time cause of action in terms of cl. (c) of proviso accrues to the complainant - Once the cause of action accrues to the complainant, the jurisdiction of the Court to try the case would be determined by reference to the place where the cheque was dishonoured - General rule stipulated u/s. 177 of CrPC applied to cases u/s. 138 of the Act - Prosecution in such cases could, therefore, be launched against the drawer of the cheque only before the Court within whose jurisdiction the dishonour takes place except in situations where the offence of dishonour of the cheque punishable u/s. 138 of the Act was committed along with other offences in a single transaction within the meaning of s. 220(1) r/w s. 184 CrPC or was covered by the provisions of s. 182(1) r/w ss. 184 and 220 CrPC thereof - Appeals dismissed.
