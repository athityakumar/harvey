Ram Niranjan Roy v State of Bihar and others

Supreme Court of India

31 March 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjana Prakash Desai, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 208; (2014) 12 SCC 11; 2014 ALL MR (Cri) 1937; 2014 CRLJ 2283; JT 2014 (4) SC 477; 2014 (2) RCR(Criminal) 705; 2014(4) SCALE 428; [2014] 4 S.C.R. 583

Case Digest

Subject: Practice & Procedure

Summary: Practice & Procedure - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971, s.14 - Contempt of court - Punishment - Sustainability - Appellant, who was holding the post of Deputy Superintendent of Police, Crime Investigation Department (CID), Bihar, filed an intervention application - Appellant claimed in the application that he was the President of Bihar Police Seva Sangh, a service association of members of Bihar Police Service - Appellant stated in the application that the transfers and postings of the officers of Bihar Police Service were done arbitrarily in violation of guiding principles framed by the Home Department of Government of Bihar - Appellant appeared in-person before the HC - Appellant shouted and told the court that he was intervener and that the HC did not focus its attention on the wrong policies of transfers within the police dept. - Appellant raised his voice with impertinence and declared that the HC was not taking up his case wherein he has challenged his transfer and posting made in the police dept. - HC, then, asked appellant whether he had been granted leave by the Director General of Police to present his case - Appellant again shouted at the court and stated that he had applied for leave but whether leave was granted to him or not is not the concern of the court - HC had observed that appellant could not show to the court that leave had been granted to him by the Police Headquarters to argue his case in-person and challenge transfer policy of the police dept. - HC had further observed that the appellant baited the court - Appellant wanted his writ application to be considered out-of-turn on the ground that it was concerning transfers and postings of police officers - HC, therefore, called for the record, perused the appellant's application and found out that it mainly related to his own transfer - Appellant, then, claimed to be an office bearer of Bihar Police Seva Sangh and stated that the Police Manual had declared him a member of the protected staff and he had immunity from transfers and he could not be touched - Appellant produced a letter addressed by a Cabinet Minister to the Chief Minister of Bihar questioning why he was transferred from one establishment to another, though, within the city - Said letter was quoted in the impugned order - Appellant did not show the slightest remorse nor regret and instead continued to bait the court and repeat that even the Minister had given him protection and had granted stay of his transfer - In view of the contumacious behaviour, the HC directed that the appellant should be taken into custody by the Court Officer and the Sergeant and sent to jail as punishment for a day i.e. for twenty four hours - Appellant's intervention application came to be rejected - Hence instant appeal - Whether appellant committed contempt of court -

Held, SC had extensively referred to the contents of the impugned order of the HC with a purpose - It reflected the appellant's rude behavior - Intemperate language used by the appellant while addressing Judges of the HC was most objectionable and contumacious - Appellant was Deputy Superintendent of Police - Appellant claimed to be the President of Bihar Police Seva Sangh - Responsible police officer was not expected to behave in such undignified and unruly manner in the Court - Appellant shouted at the Judges - When Judges asked him whether the police headquarters had granted him any permission to argue his case in-person and challenge transfer policy of the police department, he rudely stated that that was not the concern of the court - Appellant was, however, unable to produce any permission - Thereafter, appellant told the court that his application should be heard along with Public Interest Litigation as it related to postings and transfers of police officers - On scrutiny, it was found that it mainly related to his transfer - Thus, appellant made a wrong statement before the Court - Appellant, then, stated that he was a protected staff member and had immunity from transfer and he could not be touched - Appellant tried to overawe the court by producing a Cabinet Minister's letter addressed to the Chief Minister recommending his case - Appellant did not show any remorse - Appellant did not tender any apology, but, continued his rude behaviour of shouting at the court and baiting the court - By such behaviour appellant lowered the dignity and authority of the HC - Appellant challenged the majesty of the HC by showing utter disrespect to it - Undoubtedly he committed contempt of the HC in its presence and hearing - Appellant was, therefore, guilty of having committed contempt in the face of the HC - Appellant's case is squarely covered by s. 14 of Act - Appellant's contention that no opportunity was given to him to make his defence should be rejected - In one such case before SC while dealing with the nature and scope of power conferred upon SC and the HC, being courts of record u/arts. 129 and 215 of the Constitution of India respectively, SC observed that the said power was an inherent power under which the SC and the HC could deal with contempt of itself - Jurisdiction vested was a special one not derived from any other statute but derived only from arts. 129 and 215 of Constitution - In Pritam Pal v. High Court of Madhya Pradesh, Jabalpur, through Registrar, 1992 Indlaw SC 221, relied on - SC further clarified that the constitutionally vested right could not be either abridged, abrogated or cut down by legislation including the Act - Appeal disposed of.
