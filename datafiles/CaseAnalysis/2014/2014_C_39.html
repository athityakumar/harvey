Keshar Bai v Chhunulal

Supreme Court of India

07 January 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjana Prakash Desai, J. Chelameswar

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 10; (2014) 11 SCC 438; AIR 2014 SC 1394; 2014 (4) ALD(SC) 26; 2014(2) ALL MR 937; 2014 (1) JabLJ 220; JT 2014 (1) SC 379; 2014 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 261; 2014 (1) RCR(Rent) 183; 2014(1) SCALE 170; [2014] 1 S.C.R. 166

Case Digest

Subject: Rent Control

Keywords: A.P. Tenancy Act

Summary: Civil Procedure - Land & Property - M.P. Accommodation Control Act, 1961, s. 12(1)(c) - Suit for eviction - Denial of title - Default in payment of rent - Maintainability - Appellant/landlady purchased a building by a registered sale deed - At the time of purchase of the said building, respondent/tenant was occupying one room (suit premises) - Respondent agreed to pay the rent to appellant but failed to pay it and denied the title of appellant - Appellant filed suit for eviction against respondent under the 1961 Act - Trial Court decreed the suit u/s. 12(1)(c) of the Act - On appeal First Appellate Court confirmed the judgment passed by Trial Court - On further appeal HC by impugned order set aside the eviction decree passed by the Courts below - Hence instant appeal - How denial of title falls within the ambit of s. 12(1)(c) of the Act - Held, there was specific reference to registered document under which appellant purchased suit building from earlier landlord in plaint - In written statement respondent denied title of appellant - SC noticed that there were several documents on record relating to ownership of appellant, apart from registered sale deed, such as municipal tax receipts, ration card etc - Respondent refused to acknowledge appellant's title and he denied it in his evidence - Instant case was not simple case of denial of derivative title by person, who did not know about purchase of building by landlord - HC accepted that in his cross-examination respondent stated that he was not accepting appellant as his landlady - HC however, said that piece of evidence no decree of eviction could be passed against respondent u/s. 12(1)(c) of the Act because respondent would have no occasion to establish in what circumstances he denied title of appellant - HC further held that respondent was within permissible limit in asking appellant to produce documentary evidence about his title as landlord - According to SC, HC erred in drawing such conclusion - Even denial of landlord's title in written statement could provide ground for eviction of tenant - It was not necessary that denial of title by landlord should be anterior to institution of eviction proceedings - In eviction proceedings question of title to properties in question might be incidentally gone into, but could not be decided finally - HC was wrong in setting aside concurrent finding of fact recorded by Courts below that respondent had denied title of appellant - Hence, impugned judgment of HC was set aside and eviction decree passed by Trial Court and confirmed by First Appellate Court u/s. 12(1)(c) of the Act was restored - Appeal allowed.
