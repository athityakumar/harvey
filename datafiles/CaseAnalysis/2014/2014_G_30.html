Guttikonda Venkataramaiah v Godavarthy Venkateswarlu and another

Supreme Court of India

13 October 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Anil R. Dave, Uday U. Lalit

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 699; (2015) 2 SCC 46; 2015 (1) ALD(SC) 1; 2014 (107) ALR 738; 2015 (2) AWC 2044; 2014 (4) CLT(SC) 458; JT 2014 (13) SC 54; 2014 (5) Law Herald (P&H) 4093; 2015 (4) MahLJ 96; 2014 (4) RCR(Civil) 953; 2014(12) SCALE 24; 2014 (4) WLN(SC) 156

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Civil Procedure - Practice & Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 21 r. 64, O. 21 r. 66, O. 21 r. 90 - Execution of auction sale - In the instant appeal, appellant (an auction purchaser) had challenged the validity of judgment of the HC - Respondent No. 2 had filed petition against respondent No. 1 for recovery of Rs.1,78,000/- - An ex-parte decree was passed and the decretal amount was Rs.3,55,732/- - Thereafter, the execution proceedings had been initiated by respondent no.2 for sale of immovable property-agricultural land belonging to respondent no.1 (the principal debtor) - Said property had been sold for Rs.13,05,000/- in an auction and the said amount had been paid by the appellant - Executing Court had also permitted the appellant to take possession of the property in question - Subsequently, respondent no.1 had filed Civil Revision Petition challenging the order passed by the executing Court submitting irregularities in execution proceedings - HC allowed said petition and set aside the sale and directed the Executing Court to take appropriate action for sale of the property in question in accordance with the provisions of rr. 64 and 66 of O. 21 of the CPC - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, respondent no.1 ought to have raised his objection at the stage when the property in question was to be sold by an auction. Respondent No. 1 did not do so. Subsequently, after the property was sold at the auction, he approached the HC, though a proper remedy for him was to file an application u/r. 90 of O. 21 of the CPC. When the High Court had directed him to file appropriate proceedings before an appropriate forum, respondent No. 1 did so, but there also he was so careless that the proceedings had been concluded against him on account of defaults committed by him. It shows that the intention of the respondent no. 1 is to avoid making payment to the decree holder. If the judgment delivered by the HC is upheld, the entire proceedings with regard to execution will commence de novo and one does not know as to when the proceedings would be concluded and the decree holder would get the decretal amount. In the interest of justice, the impugned judgment delivered by the HC deserves to be quashed and set aside. Appeal allowed.
