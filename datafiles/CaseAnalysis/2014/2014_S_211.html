Dipak Babaria and another v State of Gujarat and others

Supreme Court of India

23 January 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

H.L. Gokhale, J. Chelameswar

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 43; (2014) 3 SCC 502; AIR 2014 SC 1792; 2014(3) ALL MR 446; JT 2014 (2) SC 234; 2014(1) SCALE 550; [2014] 2 S.C.R. 71

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Land & Property

Keywords: Bombay Land Revenue Code, 1879, Earth, Dictation, Gujarat Tenancy and Agricultural Lands (Vidarbha Region and Kutch Areas) Act, 1958, Bombay Tenancy and Agricultural Lands Rules, 1959

Summary: Administrative - Land & Property - Gujarat (earlier 'Bombay' prior to the amendment in its application in the State of Gujarat) Tenancy and Agricultural Lands (Vidarbha Region and Kutch Areas) Act, 1958, ss. 89A, 89A(5) - PIL - Land purchased for industrial purpose - Default in setting up industry - Transfer of land to third party - Permission granted by collector - Breach of law - Legality - Petition filed by appellants sought to challenge the permission granted by the Collector, to sell certain parcels of agricultural land in question - Which were purchased earlier by respondent No. 4 (IG) for industrial purpose in favour of respondent No. 5 (A), as being impermissible under the provisions of the 1958 Act - Writ petition criticised the role of the Collector and the Revenue Minister of the State Govt., and sought an inquiry against them and also a direction to the state authorities to resume the concerned land - Impugned judgment and order rejected the said writ petition on two grounds, firstly that there was delay in initiating the said Public Interest Litigation (PIL), and petitioner had suppressed the material facts before the Court concerning the investment claimed to have been made by respondent No. 5 - Hence instant appeal - Appellants contended that there was no provision for any further transfer of agricultural land from one industrial purchaser to any third party, once again, for industrial purpose when the first purchaser of agricultural land had defaulted in setting up the industry - Further contended that apart from being in breach of the law, the transaction was stated to be against public interest, and a mala-fide one resulting into a serious loss to the public exchequer - Whether the decision taken by the Govt. to permit the transfer of the agricultural land from respondent No. 4 to respondent No. 5, was legal and justified and whether there was any breach thereof -

Held, there was no need of any such direction to hold an enquiry, in view of the letter of respondent No. 4 itself, dt. 6-12-2008, wherein, it clearly stated that they were no more interested in putting up any industrial project in the said land - Consequently, there would be an order that the land shall vest in the State Govt. free from all encumbrances - Vesting order, however, had to be on payment of appropriate compensation to the purchaser as the Collector may determine - In instant case, there was no need of having determination, for the reason that respondent No. 4 had received from respondent No. 5 Rs. 1.20 crores as against the amount of 70 lakhs, which it had paid to the agriculturists when it bought those lands in 2003 - Neither respondent No. 4 nor 5 was making any grievance towards this figure or the payment thereof - It was the case of both of them that the direct sale by respondent No. 4 to respondent No. 5 for the amount as permitted by the State Govt. be held valid - That being so, amount of Rs. 1.20 crores would be set-off towards the compensation which would be payable by the State Govt. to the purchaser respondent No. 4, since the land was originally purchased by respondent No. 4, and was now to vest in the State Govt. - Land was to be disposed of by the State Govt., having regard to the use of the land - Land was supposed to be used for the industrial activity on the basis of the utilization of bauxite found and respondent No. 5 had proposed a plant based on use of bauxite - Disposal of the land would, however, have to be at least as per the minimum price that would be receivable at the Govt. rate - Having noted that respondent No. 5 claimed to have made some good investment, and that respondent No. 5 had also offered to pay, without prejudice, the difference between Rs.4.35 crores and Rs.1.20 crores i.e. Rs.3.15 cores to the State, the land would be permitted to be disposed of by the State Govt. to respondent No. 5, provided respondent No. 5 pay amount of Rs. 3.15 crores to the State Govt. - Although HC did not approve the action of the State Govt., and hold it to be clearly arbitrary and untenable, HC was of the view that the aforesaid order would be appropriate to do complete justice in the matter - Impugned judgment and order passed by the HC set aside - Direction of the State Govt. dt. 18-12-2009 and the consequent order issued by the Collector on 15-1-2010 was arbitrary, and bad in law for being in violation of the scheme and the provisions of ss. 89 and 89A of the 1958 Act - Direct sale of land by respondent No. 4 to respondent No. 5 was also held to be bad in law, and inoperative - Order would have been to direct the Collector to proceed in accordance with s. 89A(5) of the 1958 Act viz., to hold an enquiry to decide whether the purchaser viz. IG had failed to commence the industrial activity and the production of goods and services within the period specified - Petition filed by appellants was allowed by holding that the order passed by the State Govt. and were held to be arbitrary and bad in law - Appeal partly allowed.
