Indian Bank Association and others v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

21 April 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan, Vikramajit Sen

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 529; (2014) 5 SCC 590; AIR 2014 SC 2528; 2014 (5) ALD(SC) 100; 2014 ALL MR (Cri) 4178; 2014 (2) BC 654; [2014] 184 Comp Cas 377; 2014 (3) CompLJ 277; 2014 CRLJ 3119; 2015 (1) DRTC 348; 2014 (2) KLT 788; 2014 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1685; 2014 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 1897; 2014 (6) MahLJ 10; 2015(1) M.P.H.T. 116; 2014 (4) MPLJ 278; 2014 (174) PLR 550; 2014 (2) RCR(Civil) 826; 2014 (4) RLW 3598; [2014] 5 S.C.R. 386

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Constitution; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Dishonour of cheques, Banking, Public Financial Institutions and Negotiable Instruments Laws (amendment) Act, 1988, Association, Requirement of, Cheque bounce cases, Summary trial procedure, Directions to Lower Courts

Summary: Constitution - Banking & Finance - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 32 - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, ss. 138, 143, 145, 145(2) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 261 to 265, 263(h), 251, 263(g), 263(h) - Negotiable Instruments (Amendment and Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 2002 - Banking, Public Financial Institutions and Negotiable Instruments Laws (Amendment) Act, 1988, s. 4 - Cheque bounce cases - Summary trial procedure - Directions to Lower Courts - Requirement of - Petitioner (association) and others filed instant petition u/art. 32 of Constitution seeking to laying down appropriate guidelines/directions to be followed by all Courts within the territory of India competent to try a complaint u/s. 138 of the Act to follow and comply with the mandate of s. 143 of the Act r/w ss. 261 to 265 of CrPC for summary trial of such complaints filed or pending before said Courts; issue a writ of mandamus for compliance with the guidelines of SC indicating various steps to be followed for summary trial of complaints u/s. 138 of the Act and report to SC; issue a writ of mandamus, directing respondents, to adopt necessary policy and legislative changes to deal with cases related to dishonor of cheqeus so that the same were expeditiously disposed of in accordance with the intent of the Act and guidelines to be laid down by SC -

Held, u/s. 145 of the Act, complainant could give his evidence by way of an affidavit and such affidavit shall be read in evidence in any inquiry, trial or other proceedings in the Court, which makes it clear that a complainant was not required to examine himself twice i.e. one after filing the complaint and one after summoning of accused - Affidavit and documents filed by complainant along with complaint for taking cognizance of the offence were good enough to be read in evidence at both the stages i.e. pre-summoning stage and the post summoning stage - In other words, there was no necessity to recall and re-examine the complaint after summoning of accused, unless the Magistrate passes a specific order as to why the complainant was to be recalled - Such an order was to be passed on an application made by accused or u/s. 145(2) of the Act suo moto by the Court - In summary trial, after accused was summoned, his plea was to be recorded u/s. 263(g) of CrPC and his examination, if any, could can be done by a Magistrate and a finding could be given by the Court u/s. 263(h) of CrPC and the same procedure could be followed by a Magistrate for offence of dishonour of cheque since offence u/s. 138 of the Act was a document based offence - It was cleared that if proviso (a), (b) and (c) to s. 138 of the Act were shown to have been complied with, technically the commission of the offence stand completed and it was for the accused to show that no offence could have been committed by him for specific reasons and defences - Considering all aspects, few HCs of country have laid down certain procedures for speedy disposal of cases u/s. 138 of the Act - Many of the directions given by various HCs, were worthy of emulation by Criminal Courts all over the country dealing with cases u/s. 138 of the Act - Thus, it was directed that Metropolitan Magistrate/Judicial Magistrate, on the day when the complaint u/s. 138 of the Act was presented, should scrutinize the complaint and, if complaint was accompanied by affidavit, and the affidavit and documents, if any, were found to be in order, take cognizance and direct issuance of summons - Metropolitan Magistrate/Judicial Magistrate should adopt a pragmatic and realistic approach while issuing summons - Summons should be properly addressed and sent by post as well as by e-mail address got from the complainant - Court, in appropriate cases, might take the assistance of Police or the nearby Court to serve notice to the accused - For notice of appearance, a short date be fixed - If the summons was received back un-served, immediate follow up action be taken - Court might indicate in the summon that if the accused makes an application for compounding of offences at the first hearing of the case and, if such an application was made, Court might pass appropriate orders at the earliest - Court should direct the accused, when he appear to furnish a bail bond, to ensure his appearance during trial and ask him to take notice u/s. 251 of CrPC to enable him to enter his plea of defence and fix the case for defence evidence, unless an application was made by accused u/s. 145(2) of the Act for re-calling a witness for cross-examination - Court concerned should ensure that examination-in-chief, cross-examination and re-examination of the complainant should be conducted within 3 months of assigning the case - Court has option of accepting affidavits of witnesses, instead of examining them in Court - Witnesses to the complaint and accused should be available for cross-examination as and when there was direction to that effect by the Court - All the Criminal Courts in the country dealing with s. 138 of the Act cases were directed to follow said procedures for speedy and expeditious disposal of cases falling u/s. 138 of the Act - Petition disposed of.
