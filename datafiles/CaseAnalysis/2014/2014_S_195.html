Joshinder Yadav v State of Bihar

Supreme Court of India

20 January 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjana Prakash Desai, J. Chelameswar

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 35; (2014) 4 SCC 42; AIR 2014 SC 1043; 2014 CRLJ 1175; 2014 (2) JCC 894; JT 2014 (2) SC 316; 2014 (1) MLJ(Crl) 607; 2014 (1) OLR 738; 2014 (1) RCR(Criminal) 789; 2014(1) SCALE 426; [2014] 1 S.C.R. 567

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Murder, Poisoning, Dowry harassment

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 498a, 302 r/w s, 149 and 201 -Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 101, 106 - Dowry harassment - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant (accused no 2) along with other accused were tired for alleged offence u/ss. 498A and 302 r/w ss. 149 and 201 of IPC - Trial Court convicted appellants u/s. 302 r/w s. 149 of IPC and sentenced them to suffer life imprisonment and 3 and 7 years of RI for offences u/ss. 498A and 201 of IPC, respectively - Aggrieved appellant and other accused filed an appeal before HC, which was dismissed by impugned judgment - Aggrieved appellant filed instant appeal - Whether impugned judgment passed by HC was justified - Held, evidence of (complainant) father and brother of deceased (PW9 and 10) and other attendant circumstances such as strong motive; the fact that accused did not lodge any complaint about missing of deceased; accused 6 went to the house of PW9 to enquire about deceased and then suddenly deserted PW9 and 10 when they were going to the house of accused, that all the accused absconded from their house with their belongings and that the house was completely empty, lead to an irresistible conclusion that accused were responsible for the death of deceased - Prosecution having established that accused treated the deceased with cruelty and that they subjected her to harassment for dowry, the accused ought to have disclosed the facts which were in their personal and special knowledge to disprove the prosecution case that they murdered deceased - S. 106 of 1872 Act covers such a situation - Burden which had shifted to the accused was not discharged by them - Accuse failed to discharge the burden which had shifted to them u/s. 106 of the Act - Prosecution was not expected to give the exact manner in which the deceased was killed - Adverse inference needs to be drawn against the accused as they failed to explain how the deceased was found dead in the river in one foot deep water - In cases where poisoning was suspected, it would be advisable and in the interest of justice to ensure that viscera was sent to the FSL and FSL report was obtained - That was because not in all cases there was adequate strong other evidence on record to prove that the deceased was administered poison by accused - It was direct that in cases where poisoning was suspected, immediately after post- mortem, viscera should be sent to FSL - Prosecuting agencies should ensure that viscera was, in fact, sent to FSL for examination and FSL should ensure that viscera was examined immediately and report was sent to the investigating agencies/Courts post haste - If viscera report was not received, concerned Court should for explanation and should summon the concerned officer of the FSL to give an explanation as to why the viscera report was not forwarded to investigating agency/Court - Criminal Court should ensure that it was brought on record - Impugned judgment was upheld - Appeal dismissed.
