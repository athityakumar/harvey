Somnath Chakraborty and another v Appollo Gleneagles Hospitals Limited and others

Supreme Court of India

23 July 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla, A.K. Sikri

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 479; (2015) 1 SCC 586; 2014 (5) AWC 4926; 2014 (4) CalLT(SC) 1; 2014 (3) CLT(SC) 119; JT 2014 (9) SC 369; 2014(8) SCALE 694; [2014] 8 S.C.R. 949

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Salami

Summary: (A)Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Utilization charges - Sustainability - Appeals were directed against a common judgment of the DB of HC - What would be claim of the appellants for utilization charges, interest and costs -

Held, insofar as the utilization charges were concerned, according to the appellants they were deprived of the use of their land by the Appollo Hospitals at least from the year 1991 when the hospital was put in possession pursuant to the registered lease deed dt. 21-6-1991 - It was, therefore, contended that when the Appollo Hospitals agreed to pay 10% of the value of the salami of land, namely, Rs.98,41,300 i.e. sum of Rs.9,84,130 per annum by way of lease rent for the entire extent of lands at least insofar as the appellants' lands were concerned, they were entitled for normal return on the average value of land from June, 1991 - Referring to the land value which was prevailing in June, 1991 at sum of Rs.3,15,997 per cottah and the land value in June, 2013 at Rs.24,04,188 per cottah, the average land value was claimed to be Rs.13,60,092.50 per cottah and on that basis 10% of the said value was claimed as utilization charges i.e. the sum of Rs.1,36,009 per annum per cottah - As against the claim, on behalf of the Appollo Hospitals, it was contended that what was agreed to be paid by way of annual lease amount at the rate of 10% on the total salami of land was taken into account the larger extent of 34,147 square metres within which the Appellant's land which was a small portion of 743.21 square metres - It was further stated that the lands were marshy lands in 1991 totally undeveloped till the Appollo Hospitals made improvements by constructing the hospitals and, therefore, if at all, any utilization charges was to be considered, it should be the normal economic rent of 6% on the investment/capital value which would be on a sum of Rs.10,001 per annum - Having considered the respective submissions and having noted the salient factors in the case on hand, it will have to be borne in mind that after strenuous fight in the Court proceedings the rights of the appellants came to be crystallized as regards their ownership in the Second Appeal which was decreed on 25-7-1986 - That was the first round of litigation where the appellants could ultimately succeed and establish their right of ownership over the lands in question - Thereafter, when they were confronted with the subsequent proceedings under the provisions of the Act, they had to launch the proceedings again by approaching HC and by way of writ petition, in which the instant impugned order ultimately came to be passed by DB of HC on 8-12-2009 - Therefore, for establishing their right to their property, enormous time, energy and money was spent by the appellants - Right to property was protected u/art.300A of the Constitution - In view of the nature of the location of the land which was in a land-locked position, DB of SC had rightly found that the grievance of the appellants could be redressed by giving directions as contained in paragraph 13 of the impugned judgment by which the respondents were directed to compensate the Appellants for use of the lands - Keeping the factors in mind, SC was of the view that to formulate the basis for fixing the utilization charges the method adopted in the lease deed dt. 21-6-1991 as between the Appollo Hospitals and the State of West Bengal could be followed - As per the said lease deed the Appollo Hospitals agreed to pay 10% of the value of the salami land value, namely, Rs.98,41,300 which worked out in a sum of Rs.9,84,130 per annum as lease rent during the period of lease, namely, 30 years - Total land for which the lease rent was fixed was 34,147 square metres - Applying the very same formula for arriving the rental value of the lands of the Appellants which was 743.21 square metres, we find that the same will work out to Rs.21,355.62 per annum [(i.e.) 743.21 ÷ 34147 x 100 = 2.17 : 9,84,130 x 2.17 ÷ 100 = 21,355.62] - On that basis, Sc was convinced that the utilization charges could be fixed by rounding it off to Rs.21,500 - Accordingly, SC held that the utilization charges should be fixed in a sum of Rs.21,500 per annum (rounding off Rs.21,355.62 as Rs.21,500) and for a period of 23 years, namely, between 1991 and 2014, the utilization charges can be arrived at - Same came to Rs.4,94,500 - Claim of the appellants that utilization charges should be at the rate of Rs.5 lacs or on the basis of the average value of the land which was claimed at a sum of Rs.1,36,009 could not be accepted, inasmuch as, it would have to be noted that the appellants would be getting the value of the entire 11.66 cottah of lands as per the instant day market value which was fixed at Rs.24,04,188 per cottah - It could not be lost sight that the value of the land in 1991 was far lower than what was arrived at based on the Valuer's report which was for the year 2013 - If the Appollo Hospitals was to pay the lease rent per annum based on the salami, namely, the royalty value of Rs.98,41,300 it would be more appropriate to fix the lease rent on the very same basis on which it was fixed under the lease dt. 21-6-1991 - In that way, Appollo Hospitals could not also have any grievance inasmuch as apart from salami of Rs.98,41,300 for the land, they agreed to pay 10% of its value by way of lease rent for the first 30 year - SC, therefore, held that utilization charges so arrived at should be in the sum of Rs.21,500 and for the period between 1991 and 2014, namely, for 23 years - Respondent No.1 was liable to pay a sum of Rs.4,94,500 - Appeals disposed of.

(B)Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Urban Land (Ceiling and Regulation) Act, 1976 - Interest payable - On value of land - What would be interest payable on the value of the land now determined by the Valuer -

Held, since the market value as of the years 2013 was fixed as payable for the 11.66 (11.659) cottah of the lands, SC found that the deprivement of the use of the land of the appellants and the State was certainly a right which was a Constitutional Right and the same was deprived for no fault of theirs - It was true that the land value was fixed based on the value which was prevailing in the year 2013 while the respondent No.1 was put in possession of the lands in the year 1991 and the stand of the Hospital that the land value should have been fixed in 1991 though was not acceptable, the said stand could certainly be taken into account while considering the claim of the appellants for payment of interest - SC wished to reiterate that in the whole process, appellants had lost their property rights once and for all and on the other hand respondents would get absolute ownership right in respect of 11.66 (11.659) cottah of land which it could always cherish and enjoy without any hindrance from any quarters - Therefore, taking an overall view of the grievances of the appellants, SC was convinced that appellants were entitled for payment of interest though not to the extent they claimed - According to respondent No.1 even if interest was to be granted that could only be on the value that was prevailing in 1991 which according to respondent No.1 was Rs.10,001 per cottah and on that value interest at the rate of 6% per annum could be made - Instead, SC held that the appellants were entitled for a nominal interest at the rate of 2% per annum on the total value of the land as accepted by both the parties, namely, Rs.2,80,32,832 - On that basis, the interest payable by the respondent No.1 would work out to a sum of Rs.1,28,95,102 for 23 years - With that the only other claim to be considered was costs - Appellants were fighting the litigation by stepping into the shoes of their predecessor in interest from the year 1960 onwards in Title Suit No.79 of 1960 which was ultimately decreed in Second Appeal by the decree dt. 25-7-1986 - Even thereafter the appellants had to initiate the second round of litigation for ascertaining their rights as against the proceedings issued under the provisions of Act which had taken another two decades - Thereby the appellants were knocking at the doors of the Court for the past 54 years - Therefore, appellants were surely entitled for cost of the litigation which SC wanted to quantify in a lump sum of Rs.5 lacs - Appeals disposed of.
