Dr. Subramanian Swamy and others v State of Tamil Nadu and others

Supreme Court of India

06 January 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Balbir Singh Chauhan, Sharad Arvind Bobde

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 615; (2014) 5 SCC 75; AIR 2015 SC 460; 2015 (2) ALD(SC) 33; 2015(2) ALL MR 408; 2014 (1) MLJ 622; [2014] 1 S.C.R. 308

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Trusts & Associations

Keywords: Res Judicata, Principle Of Res Judicata, Life Insurance Corporation (Modification of Settlements) Act, 1976, Madras Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Act, 1927

Summary: Constitution - Trusts & Associations - Tamil Nadu Hindu Religious and Charitable Endowments Act, 1959, ss. 45 and 107 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Petitioner filed appeals against order of the HC who rejected the claim of the petitioner to administer the temple - Petitioner has raised the issue of violation of the Constitutional rights protected u/art. 26 of Constitution in relation to the claim by Dikshitars Smarthi Brahmins to administer the properties of the Temple in question dedicated to Lord Natraja - Petitioner submitted that art. 26 of Constitution confers certain fundamental rights upon the citizens and particularly, on a 'religious denomination' which can neither be taken away nor abridged - However, in the instant case, the Dikshitars had been declared by this Court, in a lis between Dikshitars and the State and the Religious Endowments Commissioner, that they were an acknowledged 'religious denomination' and in that capacity they had a right to administer the properties of the Temple - Though in view of the provisions of s. 45 r/w s. 107 of the Act, the State may have a power to regulate the activities of the Temple, but lacks competence to divest the Dikshitars from their right to manage and administer the Temple and its properties - Whether Dikshitars constitute a 'religious denomination' and whether they have a right to participate in the administration of the Temple.

Held, even if the management of a temple is taken over to remedy the evil, the management must be handed over to the person concerned immediately after the evil stands remedied. Continuation thereafter would tantamount to usurpation of their proprietary rights or violation of the fundamental rights guaranteed by the Constitution in favour of the persons deprived. Therefore, taking over of the management in such circumstances must be for a limited period. Thus, such expropriatory order requires to be considered strictly as it infringes fundamental rights of the citizens and would amount to divesting them of their legitimate rights to manage and administer the temple for an indefinite period. Impugned order is liable to be set aside for failure to prescribe the duration for which it will be in force. Super-session of rights of administration cannot be of a permanent enduring nature. Power under the Act for appointment of an Executive Officer could not have been exercised in the absence of any prescription of circumstances/ conditions in which such an appointment may be made. More so, the order of appointment of the Executive Officer does not disclose as for what reasons and under what circumstances his appointment was necessitated. Even otherwise, the order in which no period of its operation is prescribed, is not sustainable being ex facie arbitrary, illegal and unjust. Appeals allowed.
