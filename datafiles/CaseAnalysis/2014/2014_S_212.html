J. V. Baharuni and others v State of Gujarat and others

Supreme Court of India

16 October 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

N.V. Ramana, Ranjana Prakash Desai

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 717; (2014) 10 SCC 494; 2015 ALL MR (Cri) 357; 2015 (1) CTC 284; 2015 (1) DRTC 169; JT 2014 (12) SC 496; 2014 (5) Law Herald (P&H) 4579; 2015 (4) MahLJ 192; 2015 (2) MPLJ 490; 2014 (4) RCR(Civil) 763; 2014 (4) RCR(Criminal) 696; 2014(12) SCALE 200; [2014] 10 S.C.R. 1061

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Legality, Cheque in question

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, s. 138 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 326(3), 262-265, 313, 263 (h), 263 (g) - Cheque in question - Legality - Appellants issued cheques to discharge liability to respondent complainant - When cheques were presented for realization, they bounced with the endorsement 'not arranged for' - Respondents filed complaint against appellant u/s. 138 of the Act - Trial Court acquitted appellant -Aggrieved respondent filed appeal before HC - Appeal was allowed by HC and remanded the matter to the Trial Court for a de novo trial - Hence instant appeal.

Held, without strong, cogent, unimpeachable evidence on record that cases were tried 'summarily' but not as regular trial, the Court below gravely erred in remanding to the Trial Court for a de novo trial. Courts while dealing with matters under the Act should keep in mind that difference between summary and summons trial for purpose of the Act is very subtle but has grave repercussions in case of mistaken identification of trial which is de novo trial in the light of s. 326 (3) CrPC. Exercise of remitting the matter to Trial Court for de novo trial should be done only when the Appellate Court is satisfied after thorough scrutiny of records and then recording reason for the same that the trial is not summons trial but summary trial. The non-exhaustive list which may indicate the difference between both modes of trial is framing of charges, recording of statement u/s. 313 CrPC, whether trial has been done in the manner prescribed u/ss. 262-265 of CrPC, how elaborately evidence has been adduced and taken on record, the length of trial etc. In summary trial, the accused is summoned, his plea is recorded u/s. 263 (g) of CrPC and finding thereof is given by the Magistrate u/s. 263 (h) of CrPC of his examination. Ratio in Nitinbhai Saevatilal Shah and another v Manubhai Manjibhai Panchal and another 2011 Indlaw SC 559 must not be followed mechanically to remand matters to Trial Courts for de novo trial. There should be proper application of judicial mind and evidence on record must be thoroughly perused before arriving at any conclusion with regard to mode of trial. Thus, opinion of HC failed to appreciate the evidence on record in its true perspective. The HC erred in arriving at a conclusion that the mode of trial in all these matters was summary trial whereas the record of the trial Court adequately shows that regular trial was undertaken in these matters. Hence, the matters are required to be remanded back to HC for consideration on merits. The HC should, by conducting an independent inquiry and by reasoned order, dispose of the cases on their own merits as expeditiously as possible. Thus, impugned judgments passed by the HC was set aside and matters were remanded to the HC for consideration on merits. Appeals allowed.
