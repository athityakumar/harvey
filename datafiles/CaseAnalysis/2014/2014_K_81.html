Krishnanand (Dead) Through Lrs. v Deputy Director of Consolidation and others

Supreme Court of India

28 October 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Sharad Arvind Bobde, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 887; (2015) 1 SCC 553; 2015 (1) AWC 820; 2014 (125) RD 621; 2014(12) SCALE 430; [2014] 11 S.C.R. 1001

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Land & Property

Keywords: Consolidation proceedings, Objections

Summary: Land & Property - Administrative - Uttar Pradesh Consolidation of Holdings Act, 1953, s. 9(2) - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Consolidation proceedings - Objections - Legality - Land in dispute was initially was recorded in the name of appellant (since deceased) and others - Objections were filed before the Asst. Consolidation Officer, claiming co-tenancy over the said property - Thereafter, appellant filed his objection u/s. 9(2) of the Act for cancellation of a compromise, which was entered into by his brother with other, which was rejected - Aggrieved, appellant filed an appeal before Appellate Authority, which was allowed and quashed Order passed by Consolidation Officer and remitted the matter to him, with directions to decide the matter afresh on merits - Accordingly, Consolidation Officer reconsidered the matter, and dismissed claim of respondent, petitioner's paternal sister, over the suit property and declared her a trespasser - on appeal, Settlement Officer, dismissed her claim - Thereafter respondent filed a revision before the Dy. Director of Consolidation, which was also dismissed - Aggrieved respondent a petition before HC, which was allowed by impugned order - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, impugned order shows that HC had committed an error in re-appreciating the evidence by setting aside the findings of fact, which is normally impermissible in the exercise of its jurisdiction u/art. 226 of Constitution. Three Authorities had come to the conclusion that the respondent is a trespasser. However, the HC in the petition filed by respondent re-appreciated the entire evidence on record as if it was hearing an appeal. HC has committed an error in reversing the findings of fact arrived at by the Authorities below in coming to the conclusion that there was a partition. No doubt, the HC did so in exercise of its jurisdiction u/art. 226 of Constitution. It is a settled law that such a jurisdiction cannot be exercised for re-appreciating the evidence and arrival of findings of facts unless the authority which passed the impugned order does not have jurisdiction to render the finding or has acted in excess of its jurisdiction or the finding is patently perverse. In instant case, though the HC reversed the concurrent findings of the Authorities below and came to the opposite conclusion on matter of facts, the HC did not do so on the ground that the authorities below acted in excess of their jurisdiction or without jurisdiction or that the finding is vitiated by perversity. HC ought not to have entered into re-appreciation of evidence and reversed the findings of fact arrived at by the 3 Authorities below, especially since, the Authorities had neither exceeded their jurisdiction nor acted perversely. The HC has nowhere stated that it was of the view that there is any perversity, much less the HC failed to demonstrate any such circumstances. Impugned order of HC was set aside. Appeal allowed.
