Chennai Metropolitan Water Supply and Sewerage Board and others v T. T. Murali Babu

Supreme Court of India

10 February 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, H.L. Gokhale

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 78; (2014) 4 SCC 108; AIR 2014 SC 1141; 2014(2) ALL MR 900; 2014 (141) FLR 772; JT 2014 (2) SC 574; 2014 (2) MLJ 369; 2014(2) SCALE 163; [2014] 1 S.C.R. 987; 2014 (3) SLR 398

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Keywords: Absence From Duty, Chennai Metropolitan Water Supply and Sewerage Board Employees (Discipline and Appeal) Regulations, 1978

Summary: Service - Continuous absent - No intimation - Charge sheet - Disciplinary action - Dismissal from service - Legality - Respondent remained continuously absent from duty without any intimation to the employer - Respondent did not respond to the repeated memoranda/reminders requiring him to explain his unauthorized absence from duty and to rejoin duty - Charge memo was issued which contained two charges - Though the charge memo was duly acknowledged by respondent yet he chosen not to submit his explanation, much after the charge-sheet was issued - Enquiry was conducted, charges were proved and respondent was dismissed from service - On appeal preferred by respondent Board rejected the appeal - Further respondent preferred writ petition, Single Judge allowed the petition - On appeal filed by appellants DB accepted the conclusion of Single Judge - Hence instant appeal - Whether HC was justified in applying the doctrine of proportionality -

Held, doctrine of proportionality in the context of imposition of punishment in service law gets attracted when the court on the analysis of material brought on record comes to the conclusion that the punishment imposed by the Disciplinary Authority or the appellate authority shocks the conscience of the court - As was seen from the analysis made by the HC, it had given emphasis on past misconduct of absence and first time desertion and thereafter proceeded to apply the doctrine of proportionality - Said approach was obviously incorrect - It was telltale that respondent had remained absent for a considerable length of time - Respondent had exhibited adamantine attitude in not responding to the communications from the employer while he was unauthorisedly absent - As it appeared, he has chosen his way, possibly nurturing the idea that he can remain absent for any length of time, apply for grant of leave at any time and also knock at the doors of the court at his own will - Respondent by remaining unauthorisedly absent for such a long period with inadequate reason had not only shown indiscipline but also made an attempt to get away with it - Such a conduct was not permissible and SC was inclined to think that the HC had erroneously placed reliance on the authorities where SC had interfered with the punishment - Doctrine of proportionality does not get remotely attracted to such a case - Punishment was definitely not shockingly disproportionate - Irresistible conclusion was that the interference by the HC with the punishment was totally unwarranted and unsustainable, and further the HC was wholly unjustified in entertaining the writ petition after a lapse of 4 years - Impugned judgment's and orders passed by HC were set aside - Appeal allowed.
