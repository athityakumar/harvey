Mangat Ram v State of Haryana

Supreme Court of India

27 March 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

K.S. Panicker Radhakrishnan, Vikramajit Sen

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 192; (2014) 12 SCC 595; AIR 2014 SC 1782; 2014 CRLJ 2425; 2014 (3) JCC 1730; JT 2014 (6) SC 562; 2014 (2) MLJ(Crl) 207; 2014 (1) OLR 980; 2014 (2) RCR(Criminal) 389; 2014 (2) RLW 1343; 2014(4) SCALE 153; [2014] 4 S.C.R. 988

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss.498A and 304B - Evidence Act, 1872, s.113A - Dowry death - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant allegedly committed offence of dowry harassment and death - Appellant was convicted by Trial Court u/ss.498A and 304B IPC - HC concurred with decision of Trial Court - Hence instant appeal - Whether Trial Court's order of conviction was sustainable -

Held, circumstances of the case pointed out by the prosecution were totally insufficient to hold that the accused had abetted his wife to commit suicide and the circumstances enumerated u/s.113A of Act have also not been satisfied - SC in one case had examined the scope of s.113A of Act, wherein SC had reiterated the legal position that the legislative mandate of s.113A of Act was that if a woman commits suicide within seven years of her marriage and it was shown that her husband or any relative of her husband had subjected her to cruelty, as per the presumption defined in s. 498-A IPC, the Court might presume, having regard to all other circumstances of the case, that such suicide had been abetted by the husband or such person - Pinakin Mahipatray Rawal v. State of Gujarat, 2013 Indlaw SC 569, relied on - SC held that, though a presumption could be drawn, the burden of proof of showing that such an offence was committed by the accused u/s.498-A IPC was on the prosecution - SC held that the burden was on the prosecution to establish the fact that the deceased committed suicide and the accused abetted the suicide - In the instant case, there was no evidence to show whether it was an accidental death or whether the deceased had committed suicide - SC had every reason to believe that, in the instant case, the death was accidental, for the following reasons - Though not proved in her dying declaration, it had come out in evidence that the deceased was suffering from Epilepsy for the last three years i.e. before 15-3-1993, the date of incident - Such fact was fortified by the evidence of doctor, who was examined as DW1 - DW1 deposed that the deceased was suffering from Epilepsy and was under his treatment from 23-12-1992 to 2-4-1993 at hospital - DW1 evidence was brushed aside by the Trial Court on the ground that DW1 was not a Psychiatrist - It might be noted that Epilepsy was not a Psychiatrist problem - It was a disease of nerves system and a MD (Medicine) could treat the patient of Epilepsy - Reasoning given by Trial Court for brushing aside the evidence of DW1 could not be sustained - Therefore, the possibility of an accidental death, since she was suffering from Epilepsy, could not be ruled out - Evidently, deceased was in the kitchen and, might be, during cooking she might have suffered Epileptic symptoms and fell down on the gas stove and might have caught fire, resulting her ultimate death - DW2/ IO of the case, deposed that he had recorded the statements of the deceased wherein she had stated that she was suffering from Epilepsy for the last three years before the incident and that on 15-9-1993 while she was preparing meals on stove, she had an attack of fits and fell on the stove and caught fire - Deceased had also deposed at that time that her husband was away at duty - Evidence of DW2 had to be appreciated in the light of overall facts and circumstances of the case - Taking into consideration all aspects of the matter, SC was of the view that the prosecution did not succeed in establishing the offence u/ss.498-A and 306 IPC against the appellant - Appeal allowed.
