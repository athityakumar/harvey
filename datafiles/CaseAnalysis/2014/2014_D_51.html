 Dr. Subramanian Swamy and another v Director, Central Bureau of Investigation and another

Supreme Court of India

06 May 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Lodha, A.K. Patnaik, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya, Dipak Misra, Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 332; (2014) 8 SCC 682; AIR 2014 SC 2140; JT 2014 (7) SC 474; 2014 (3) KarLJ 340; 2014 (4) MLJ 603; 2014 (2) RCR(Criminal) 822; 2014(6) SCALE 146; [2014] 6 S.C.R. 873; 2014 (4) SLR 739

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Investigation of public servants by CBI, Requirement of, Approval of Central Government

Summary: Constitution - Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32, 14 - Central Vigilance Commission Act, 2003, (Act 45 of 2003), s. 26( c) - Delhi Special Police Establishment Act, 1946 , s. 6-A - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 - Investigation of public servants by CBI - Approval of Central Government - Requirement of - Petitioners filed instant petition in regard to constitutional validity of s. 6-A of the 1946 Act filed u/art. 32 of the Constitution - Since s. 6-A of the 1946 Act came to be inserted by s. 26(c) of the 2003 Act, Constitutional validity of s. 26(c) of the 1946 Act was raised - Whether s. 6-A of the 1946 Act which required prior approval from Central Government for prosecuting any public servant , was valid -

Held, s. 6-A of the 1946 Act replicated single Directive 4.7(3)(i), which was struck down by the Court, only change was that executive instruction was replaced by legislation - Essence of police investigation was skilful inquiry and collection of material and evidence in manner by which potential culpable individuals were not forewarned - Previous approval from Government necessarily required u/s. 6-A of the 1946 Act would result in indirectly putting to notice officers to be investigated before commencement of investigation - Further , if CBI was not even allowed to verify complaints by preliminary enquiry - Preliminary enquiry was intended to ascertain whether prima facie case for investigation was made out or not - If CBI was prevented from holding preliminary enquiry, at the very threshold, fetter was put to enable CBI to gather relevant material - As matter of fact, CBI was not able to collect material even to move Government for purpose of obtaining previous approval from Central Government - It was important to bear that as per CBI Manual, preliminary enquiry related to allegations of bribery and corruption should be limited to scrutiny of records and interrogation of bare minimum persons which being necessary to judge whether there was any substance in allegations which were being enquired into and whether case was worth pursuing further or not - Even this exercise of scrutiny of records and gathering relevant information to find out whether case was worth pursuing further or not was not possible - In criminal justice system, inquiry and investigation into offence was domain of police, very power of CBI to enquire and investigate into allegations of bribery and corruption against certain class of public servants and officials in public undertakings was subverted and impinged by s. 6-A of the 1946 Act - There was no rational basis to classify two sets of public servants differently on ground that one set of officers was decision making officers and not other set of officers - If there was accusation of bribery, graft, illegal gratification or criminal misconduct against public servant, then Court failed to understand as to how status of offender was of any relevance - Where there were allegations against public servant which amount to offence under the1988 Act, no factor pertaining to expertise of decision making was involved - Yet, s. 6-A of the Act made distinction - It was this vice which renders s. 6-A of the 1946 Act violative of art. 14 of the Constitutuion - Further, result of impugned legislation was that very group of persons, namely, high ranking bureaucrats whose misdeeds and illegalities might have to be inquired into, would decide whether CBI should even start inquiry or investigation against them or not - There would be no confidentiality and insulation of investigating agency from political and bureaucratic control and influence because approval was to be taken from Central Government which would involve leaked and disclosures at every stage - Provision in s. 6-A of the 1946 Act thus, impeded tracking down corrupt senior bureaucrats as without previous approval of Central Government, CBI should not even hold preliminary inquiry much less investigation into allegations - Protection in s. 6-A of the 1946 Act had propensity of shielding corrupt - Object of s. 6-A of the 1946 Act was that senior public servants of level of Joint Secretary and above who took policy decision should not be put to any harassment, side-tracks fundamental objective of the 1988 Act dealt with corruption and act against senior public servants - CBI was not able to proceed even to collect material to unearth prima facie substance into merits of allegations - Thus, object of s. 6-A of the 1946 Act itself was discriminatory - That being position, discrimination should not be justified on ground that there was reasonable classification because it had rational relation to the object sought to be achieved - Hence, Court held that s. 6- A(1)of the Act, which required approval of Central Government to conduct any inquiry or investigation into any offence alleged to have been committed under the 1988 Act where such allegation related to the employees of Central Government of level of Joint Secretary and above and such officers as were appointed by Central Government in corporations established by or under any Central Act, government companies, societies and local authorities owned or controlled by Government, was invalid and violative of art. 14 of the Constitution, as necessary corollary, provision contained in s. 26(c) of the Act 45 of 2003 to that extent was also declared invalid - Petitions allowed.
