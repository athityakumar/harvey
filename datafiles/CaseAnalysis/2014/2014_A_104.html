 State of Karnataka and others v Associated Management of (Government Recognised - Unaided - English Medium) Primary and Secondary Schools and others

Supreme Court of India

06 May 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Patnaik, R.M. Lodha, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya, Dipak Misra, Fakkir Mohamed Ibrahim Kalifulla

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 330; (2014) 9 SCC 485; AIR 2014 SC 2094; 2014 (5) ALD(SC) 114; 2014 (105) ALR 723; 2014 (5) AWC 5243; 2014 (3) Bom.C.R. 521; JT 2014 (7) SC 326; 2014 (3) KarLJ 161; 2014 (2) KLT 578; 2015 (2) MahLJ 10; 2014 (4) MLJ 362; 2014 (3) RLW 1958; 2014(7) SCALE 53; [2014] 5 S.C.R. 1104; 2015 (2) SLR 526; 2014 (3) UPLBEC 1752

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Education

Keywords: Freedom Of Speech And Expression, Government Policy, Decency, Speech And Expression, Morality, Punjabi University Act, 1961

Summary: (A)Education - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19(1)(a), 19(1)(g), 26, 29, 30(1) and 350A - Mother Tongue to be medium of instruction in schools - Validity - Aggrieved by the clauses of the Govt. Order dt 29-4-1994 which prescribed that the medium of instruction in classes I to IV in all Govt. recognized schools would be mother tongue or Kannada only, the Associated Management of Primary and Secondary Schools in Karnataka filed Writ Petition and contended inter alia that the right to choose the medium of instruction in classes I to IV of a school was a fundamental right u/arts. 19(1)(a), 19(1)(g), 26, 29 and 30(1) of the Constitution and that the impugned cls. of the order dt. 29-4-1994 of the Govt. of Karnataka were ultra vires the Constitution - HC accordingly allowed the writ petitions and quashed cls. 2, 3, 6 and 8 of the Govt. order dt. 29-4-1994 in their application to schools other than schools run or aided by the Govt. but upheld rest of the Govt. order dt. 29-4-1994 - Hence instant petition - What did Mother tongue mean, if it referred to as the language in which the child was comfortable with, then who would decide the same -

Held, mere reading of art. 350A of the Constitution would show that it casted a duty on every State and every local authority within the State to provide adequate facilities for instruction in the mother tongue at the primary stage of education to children belonging to linguistic minority groups - Hence, the expression 'mother tongue' in art.350A of Constitution means the mother tongue of the linguistic minority group in a particular State and that would obviously mean the language of that particular linguistic minority group - Mother tongue in the context of the Constitution would, therefore, mean the language of the linguistic minority in a State and it was the parent or the guardian of the child who would decide what the mother tongue of child was - Constitution nowhere provided that mother tongue was the language which the child was comfortable with, and while the meaning of 'mother tongue' might be a possible meaning of the 'expression', that was not the meaning of mother tongue in art.350A of the Constitution or in any other provision of the Constitution and hence SC could not either expand the power of the State or restrict a fundamental right by saying that mother tongue was the language which the child was comfortable with - Order accordingly.

(B) Education - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 21 and 21A - Mother Tongue to be medium of instruction in schools - Validity - Whether a student or a parent or a citizen had a right to choose a medium of instruction at primary stage -

Held, art. 21 of the Constitution provided that no person should be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law - In one case before SC a Constitution Bench of SC had held that u/art. 21 of the Constitution every child/citizen of the country had a right to free education until he completed the age of 14 years - Unni Krishnan, J.P. & Ors. v. State of Andhra Pradesh & Ors. 1993 Indlaw SC 1056, relied on - Art.21A of the Constitution provided that the State should provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of six to fourteen years in such manner as the State might, by law, determine - U/arts. 21 and 21A of the Constitution, therefore, a child had a fundamental right to claim from the State free education up to the age of 14 years - Language of art.21A of the Constitution further made it clear that such free education which a child could claim from the State would be in a manner as the State may, by law, determine - If, therefore, the State determined by law that in schools where free education was provided u/art. 21A of the Constitution, the medium of instruction would be in the mother tongue or in any language, the child could not claim as of right u/arts. 21 or 21A of the Constitution that he had a right to choose the medium of instruction in which the education should be imparted to him by the State - HC was not right in coming to the conclusion that the right to choose a medium of instruction was implicit in the right to education u/arts. 21 and 21A of the Constitution - SC's answer to question therefore, was that a child, and on his behalf his parent or guardian, had the right to choose the medium of instruction at the primary school stage u/art. 19(1)(a) of Constitution and not u/arts. 21 or 21A of the Constitution - Order accordingly.

(C) Education - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 19, 29 and 30 - Mother Tongue to be medium of instruction in schools - Validity - Would the imposition of mother tongue in any way affect the fundamental rights u/arts.14, 19, 29 and 30 of Constitution -

Held, though the experts might be uniform in their opinion that children studying in classes I to IV in the primary school could learn better if they were taught in their mother tongue, the State could not stipulate as a condition for recognition that the medium of instruction for children studying in classes I to IV in minority schools protected u/arts. 29(1) and 30(1) of the Constitution and in private unaided schools enjoying the right to carry on any occupation u/art.19(1)(g) of the Constitution would be the mother tongue of the children as such stipulation - SC accordingly answered question and held that the imposition of mother tongue would affect the fundamental rights u/arts. 19, 29 and 30 of the Constitution - Order accordingly.

(D) Education - Constitution - Mother Tongue to be medium of instruction in schools - Validity - Whether the Govt. recognized schools were inclusive of both government-aided schools and private and unaided schools -

Held, in one case SC had held that the right to establish an educational institution did not carry with it the right to recognition or the right to affiliation and that recognition and affiliation were essential for meaningful exercise of the right to establish and administer educational institutions - In that judgment, the two Judges of SC had also held that recognition might be granted either by the Govt. or by any other authority or body empowered to accord recognition and affiliation might be granted by the academic body empowered to grant affiliation - In that judgment, the two Judges of SC had further held that it was open to a person to establish an educational institution, admit students, impart education, conduct examination and award certificates but the educational institution had no right to insist that the certificates or degrees awarded by such institution should be recognized by the State and therefore the institution had to seek such recognition or affiliation from the appropriate agency - Unni Krishnan, J.P. & Ors. v. State of Andhra Pradesh & Ors. 1993 Indlaw SC 1056 - Same views were expressed by SC in another case - T.M.A. Pai Foundation & Ors. v. State of Karnataka & Ors. 2002 Indlaw SC 1375, relied on - Thus, it was clear that all schools, whether they were established by the Govt. or whether they were aided by the Govt. or whether they were not aided by the Govt., require recognition to be granted in accordance of the provisions of the appropriate Act or Government order - Accordingly, Govt. recognized schools would not only include government aided schools but also unaided schools which were granted recognition - Order accordingly.

(E) Education - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 30(1) and 350A - Mother Tongue to be medium of instruction in schools - Validity -Whether the State could by virtue of art.350-A of the Constitution compelled the linguistic minorities to choose their mother tongue only as medium of instruction in primary schools -

Held, SC had extracted art.350A of the Constitution noticed that in that article it was provided that it should be the endeavour of every State and of every local authority within the State to provide adequate facilities for instruction in the mother tongue at the primary stage of education to children belonging to linguistic minority groups - SC had already held that a linguistic minority u/art. 30(1) of the Constitution had the right to choose the medium of instruction in which education would be imparted in the primary stages of the school which it had established - Art.350A of Constitution therefore could not be interpreted to empower the State to compel a linguistic minority to choose its mother tongue only as a medium of instruction in a primary school established by it in violation of the fundamental right u/art. 30(1) of Constitution - SC accordingly held that State had no power u/art.350A of the Constitution to compel the linguistic minorities to choose their mother tongue only as a medium of instruction in primary schools - Order accordingly.
