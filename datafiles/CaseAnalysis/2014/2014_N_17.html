Nallabothu Ramulu and another v State of Andhra Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

22 April 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjana Prakash Desai, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 275; (2014) 12 SCC 261; 2014 CRLJ 2487; 2014 (3) JCC 1818; JT 2014 (5) SC 404; 2014 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 2523; 2014(5) SCALE 436

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 147, 148, 149, 302, 307, 323, 324 and 364 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 161 - Murder - Conviction - Challenged - Appellants were charged and tried by the IInd Additional Sessions Judge for offences u/ss. 147, 148, 324, 307, 302 r/w s. 149 of the IPC - Trial Court acquitted all the appellants - HC set aside the order of acquittal and convicted the appellants u/s. 302 of IPC and sentenced each one of them to undergo rigorous imprisonment for life - Appellant Nos.3 and 4 were convicted u/s. 324 of IPC and sentenced to undergo rigorous imprisonment for 3 years each - Appellant No.25 was convicted u/s. 324 of IPC and also u/s. 324 r/w s. 149 of IPC and sentenced to undergo rigorous imprisonment for 1 year on each count - Hence, instant appeal - Whether order of the HC could be upheld -

Held, while PW13, the Head Constable stated that the injured were first taken to the village and then to the hospital, PW28 Sub Inspector stated that the injured were directly taken to the hospital - If, as stated by PW13 the injured witnesses were first taken to the village and then to the hospital, then it was possible that after consultation with villagers they implicated the appellants - This makes a dent in the prosecution story - Therefore, it could not be said with certainty which appellant attacked whom -Moreover, there were so many omissions and contradictions in the evidence of prosecution witnesses, that the entire fabric of prosecution case appears to be ridden with gaping holes - These discrepancies have been meticulously noted by the Trial Court - Moreover, HC had not stated why it felt that the Trial court's view was perverse - It had not stated what were the compelling reasons, which persuaded it to disturb the order of acquittal - Opinion the trial court's view was not perverse - It was taken after thorough marshalling of evidence - Impugned judgment and order was set aside - Appeals allowed.
