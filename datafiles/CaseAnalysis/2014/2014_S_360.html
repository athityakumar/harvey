Pooja Ravinder Devidasani v State of Maharashtra and another

Supreme Court of India

17 December 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

N.V. Ramana, Sudhansu Jyoti Mukhopadhaya

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 866; AIR 2015 SC 675; 2015 ALL MR (Cri) 419; 2015 (1) BC 208; 2015 (1) Bom.C.R. 248; [2015] 190 Comp Cas 106; 2015 (2) CompLJ 11; 2015 CRLJ 1165; 2015 (2) JCC (NI) 105; JT 2014 (14) SC 1; 2015 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 234; 2015 (1) MLJ(Crl) 32; 2015 (1) OLR 435; 2015 (1) RCR(Civil) 287; 2015 (1) RCR(Criminal) 271; 2014(14) SCALE 170

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881, ss. 138, 141 - Dishonour of cheque - Liability of Director of Company - Respondent No. 2 complainant extended trade finance to company in question - Company issued cheques - Cheques were dishonoured - Respondent No. 2 filed criminal complaint against appellant, who was in charge of the company - Trial Court took cognizance and issued process to appellant - Appellant filed writ petition seeking to quash criminal proceedings - HC dismissed writ petitions - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, HC did not deal the issue in a proper perspective and committed error in dismissing the writ petitions by holding that in the complaints filed by the respondent No. 2, specific averments were made against the appellant. The appellant was neither a Director of the accused Company nor in charge of or involved in the day to day affairs of the Company at the time of commission of the alleged offence. There is not even a whisper or shred of evidence on record to show that there is any act committed by the appellant from which a reasonable inference can be drawn that the appellant could be vicariously held liable for the offence with which she is charged. In the entire complaint, neither the role of the appellant in the affairs of the Company was explained nor in what manner the appellant is responsible for the conduct of business of the Company, was explained. The appellant resigned from the Board of Directors much before the issuance of cheques. Hence, continuation of the criminal proceedings against the appellant u/s. 138 r/w s. 141 of the Act is a pure abuse of process of law and it has to be interdicted at the threshold. Thus, impughned HC order was set aside and criminal proceedings pending against the appellant before the Trial Court was quashed. Appeals allowed.

Ratio - A Director of a Company is liable to be convicted for an offence committed by the Company if he/she was in charge of and was responsible to the Company for the conduct of its business or if it is proved that the offence was committed with the consent or connivance of, or was attributable to any negligence on the part of the Director concerned.
