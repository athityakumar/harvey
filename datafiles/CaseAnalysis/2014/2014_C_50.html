Balaji Steel Re-Rolling Mills v Commissioner of Central Excise and Customs

Supreme Court of India

14 November 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

R.K. Agrawal, Kurian Joseph, A.R. Dave

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 868; 2014 (310) E.L.T. 209; 2015 (1) RCR(Civil) 436; 2014(13) SCALE 48; [2014] 11 S.C.R. 1168; 2014 (36) S.T.R. 1201

Case Digest

Subject: Excise; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Appellate Tribunal Rules, 1946, Appeal against determination of capacity of production, Dismiss for default

Summary: Excise - Practice & Procedure - Central Excise Act, 1944, s. 35C - Customs, Excise and Service Tax Appellate Tribunal (Procedure) Rules, 1982, r. 20 - Appeal against determination of capacity of production - Dismiss for default - Justifiability - Appellant was a partnership firm engaged in manufacture and sale of Hot Re-rolled products - Commissioner by its order re-fixed the annual capacity of production and duty liability of the appellant - Aggrieved appellant approached Tribunal, which remanded the matter back to Commissioner with a direction to determine the capacity of production - Thereafter, Commissioner once again affirmed its earlier order - Appellant filed an appeal before Tribunal, which was dismissed appeal for want of prosecution, against which an appeal was filed before HC - HC by impugned order dismissed the same - Hence, instant appeal - Whether Appellate Tribunal has the power to dismiss the appeal for want of prosecution or not.

Held, the Act enjoins upon the Tribunal to pass order on the appeal confirming, modifying or annulling the decision or order appealed against or may remand the matter. It does not give any power to the Tribunal to dismiss the appeal for default or for want of prosecution in case the appellant is not present when the appeal is taken up for hearing. Tribunal could not have dismissed the appeal filed by the appellant for want of prosecution and it ought to have decided the appeal on merits even if the appellant or its counsel was not present when the appeal was taken up for hearing. The HC also erred in law in upholding the order of the Tribunal. Impugned order of HC is set aside and the Tribunal is directed to decide the appeal on merits. Appeal allowed.
