India Yamaha Motor Private Limited v Dharam Singh and another

Supreme Court of India

20 August 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Jagdish Singh Khehar, Arun Mishra

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 697; (2015) 2 SCC 108; 2015(4) ALL MR 958; 2014 (143) FLR 488; JT 2014 (11) SC 363; 2014(11) SCALE 402; [2014] 10 S.C.R. 587; 2015 (4) SLR 438

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Keywords: Other benefits, Equal salary

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Uttar Pradesh Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, ss. 5C, 6I, 6I(1), 6I(2), 6I(3) - Uttar Pradesh Industrial Disputes Rules, 1957, rr. 40, 40(1)(i)(a), 40(1)(i)(b), 40(1)(i)(c) - Equal salary - Other benefits - Legality - Instant appeals were filed by appellant management to assail the competence of respondents workmen to be represented before Tribunal, through five of the respondents/workmen out of 113 workmen who were agitating industrial dispute before Tribunal -

Held, respondents were inducted into employment of appellant before 1989. Conciliation proceedings were initiated on their behalf by employees Union in 1989. The respondents were seeking regularization from date of their employment, and wages and other allied benefits connected to the wages being paid to permanent employees. Process of conciliation continued for about a decade, whereupon, State Govt. made reference of industrial dispute raised by respondents. The appellant was also dissatisfied with determination of State in transferring the adjudication of dispute from Labour Court, to Tribunal, Meerut vide its order. Challenge to the same was raised before HC repeatedly. Eventually, by an order appellant by consent accepted adjudication of dispute by Tribunal, Meerut. This was where matter was ordered to be determined by State, at asking of workmen. What is important is that large number of years came to be wasted in something which was eventually acceded to voluntarily by appellant. It is not understandable why appellant was dissatisfied with representation of 5 of the workmen before Tribunal. It is not possible for SC to understand what prejudice could have been caused to appellant if 5 workmen had represented respondents before Tribunal. Sequence of facts noted reveals that claim which commenced in 1989 and was referred for adjudication by State in 1998, has still not been taken up for consideration. While the services of one of the respondents, were terminated in the year 2000, the services of all the remaining workmen were terminated in the year 2005. SC viewed that appellant has abused the judicial process and tired out the workmen, in the legitimate pursuit of their alleged rights. This is not the purpose for which these adjudicatory processes have been awarded for. It is for expeditious relief to workmen employed in industries, that these beneficial legislations have been enacted. Compensation should be awarded to respondents for having remained involved in this assiduously long process of litigation. Appeals dismissed.
