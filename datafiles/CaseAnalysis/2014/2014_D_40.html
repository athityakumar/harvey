 Hindustan Petroleum Corporation Limited v Dilbahar Singh

Supreme Court of India

27 August 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Lodha, Dipak Misra, Madan B. Lokur, Sharad Arvind Bobde, Kurian Joseph

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 565; (2014) 9 SCC 78; AIR 2014 SC 3708; 2014(5) ALL MR 941; 2014 (107) ALR 697; 2014 (5) CTC 217; JT 2014 (9) SC 543; 2014 (4) KLT 182; 2014 (4) Law Herald (P&H) 3245; 2014 (6) MLJ 597; 2014 (4) RCR(Civil) 162B; 2014 (2) RCR(Rent) 210; 2014 (3) RLW 2753; 2014(9) SCALE 657

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Rent Control

Keywords: Power Of Revision, Kerala Buildings (Lease and Rent Control) Act, 1965, Mysore Rent Control Act, 1961

Summary: Constitution - Rent Control - Haryana Urban (Control of Rent & Eviction) Act, 1973 - Kerala Buildings (Lease and Rent Control) Act, 1965 - Tamil Nadu Buildings (Lease and Rent Control) Act, 1960 - Scope of revisional power of HC under Rent Control Acts - Whether the HC (as revisional authority) could interfere with the findings of fact of the first appellate Court/first appellate authority -

Held, none of the Rent Control Acts entitles the HC to interfere with the findings of fact recorded by the First Appellate Court/First Appellate Authority because on re- appreciation of the evidence, its view is different from the Court/Authority below. The consideration or examination of the evidence by the HC in revisional jurisdiction under these Acts is confined to find out that finding of facts recorded by the Court/Authority below is according to law and does not suffer from any error of law. In that event, the HC in exercise of its revisional jurisdiction under the above Rent Control Acts shall be entitled to set aside the impugned order as being not legal or proper. The HC is entitled to satisfy itself the correctness or legality or propriety of any decision or order impugned before it. Order accordingly.

Ratio - To satisfy itself to the regularity, correctness, legality or propriety of the impugned decision or the order, the High Court shall not exercise its power as an appellate power to re-appreciate or re-assess the evidence for coming to a different finding on facts.
