 State of Tamil Nadu v State of Kerala and another

Supreme Court of India

07 May 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Lodha, H.L. Dattu, Chandramauli Kumar Prasad, Madan B. Lokur, M.Y. Eqbal

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 336; (2014) 12 SCC 696; JT 2014 (6) SC 260; 2014 (2) KLT 603; 2014 (4) MLJ 177; 2014(6) SCALE 380

Case Digest

Subject: Parliament & Legislature; Water

Keywords: Separation Of Powers, Suit For Injunction, Constitutionality, Indian Independence Act, 1947, Safely, Inter-State Water Disputes Act, 1956, States Reorganisation Act, 1956, Government of India Act, 1919, Crown, Life Insurance Corporation (Modification of Settlements) Act, 1976, Government of India Act, 1858, Securities Exchange Act, 1934, West Bengal Criminal Law Amendment (Special Courts) Act, 1949, India (Provisional Constitution) Order, 1947, Indian Extradition Act, 1903, Kerala Irrigation and Water Conservation Act, 2003, Doctrine Of Separation Of Powers, Karnataka Cauvery Basin Irrigation Protection Ordinance, 1991, Municipalities (Validation) Act, 1963, Kerala Irrigation and Water Conservation (Amendment) Act, 2006, Entitlement of, Haryana Civil Services (Executive) Branch and Allied Services and other Services, Common/Combined Examination Act, 2002, T.N. Land Reforms (Fixation of Ceiling on Land) Amendment Act, 1978, Representation of People Amendment Ordinance, 2002, U.S. Constitution (Fourteenth Amendment), Criminal Law (Special Provisions) Act, 1962, Pitts Act, 1784, Constitution of Ceylon, Inter-sate water dispute, Obstruction to water level, Increase of water level, Validity of legislation, Riparian States, Inter-state river

Summary: (A)Water - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Kerala Irrigation and Water Conservation Act, 2003 -Kerala Irrigation and Water Conservation (Amendment) Act, 2006 - States Reorganisation Act, 1956, s. 108 - Indian Independence Act, 1947, s. 7(1)(b) - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 3, 4 131, 363 - Inter-sate water dispute - Obstruction to water level - Suit for injunction - Legality - In 1979 with regard to safety of 'M' dam in question, respondent State Govt. wrote to plaintiff State Govt. to take immediate steps to strengthen the dam - A team from Central Water Commission (CWC) inspected the dam and suggest strengthening measures - It was decided by plaintiff and defendant that 3 level measures, emergency, medium and long term to strengthen the dam and that after the completion of emergency and medium-term strengthening measures, water level in reservoir could be restored up to 145 ft. - However, after completion of emergency, medium and long term strengthening measurers, water level in the reservoir was not raised and led to the filing of number of writ petitions in Kerala and Madras HCs - Thereafter, transfer petitions were filed before SC - SC permitted the water level in dam to be raised up to 142 ft. - Defendant State passed the 2006 Amendment Act, amending the 2003 Act - Plaintiff State filed instant suit u/art. 131 of Constitution contending that 1956 Act amounts to usurpation of judicial power inasmuch as defendant State Legislature has arrogated to itself the role of a judicial body and had itself determined the questions regarding the dam safety and raising the water level when such questions fall exclusively within the province of the judiciary and have already been determined by SC in its judgment dt.27.2.2006 and that Amendment Act was beyond legislative competence of defendant State insofar as it affects the dam in view of s. 108 of 1956 Act which was a law made by Parliament u/arts. 3 and 4 of Constitution and that impugned legislation, in its application to the dam, violates the rule of law and federal structure and separation of power under Constitution - Further defendant State having participated in adjudicatory process before SC could not become a Judge in its own cause and seek to reverse the decision of SC because it had gone against it and that impugned legislation not only fixes and limits the Full Reserve Level (FRL) to 136 ft. in direct contravention of the judgment of SC but also proceeds to authorise the Dam Safety Authority of defendant State to disobey and disregard the decision of SC - Whether suit was maintainable and whether original lease deed of 1886 was valid, binding on defendant and enforceable by plaintiff against defendant -

Held, nature of 1886 Lease Agreement being not political was already concluded by SC in 2006 Indlaw SC 69, wherein it was held that 1886 Lease Agreement was an ordinary agreement being a lease agreement and it was wholly non-political in nature - Thus, there was no merit in contention of defendant that 1886 Lease Agreement lapsed under the main provision of s. 7(1)(b) of 1947 Act - Defendant had not produced any material or document to show that there was express denouncement of that nature by the Ruler of Travancore insofar as 1886 Lease Agreement was concerned - Fact that on 24.5.1949 the States of Travancore and Cochin merged together also establishes that Indian States which acceded to the Dominion continued as entities - It was pertinent to observe that defendant entered into the supplemental agreements with plaintiff in 1970 - In those supplemental agreements, continuance of 1886 lease was stated in clear and unambiguous words - Thus, embargo of art. 363 of Constitution and proviso to art. 131 of Constitution have no application - 1886 Lease Agreement would not come within the purview of art. 363 and jurisdiction of SC was not barred - Dispute arising out of 1886 Lease Agreement was not barred u/art. 131 of Constitution proviso as well - Instant suit filed by plaintiff State was maintainable u/art. 131 of Constitution - Suit based on a legal right claimed under the lease deed executed between the Govt. of the Maharaja of Travancore and Secretary of State for India in Council on 29.10.1886 was not barred by the proviso to art. 131 of Constitution - Defendant was estopped from raising the plea that lease deed dated 29.10.1886 has lapsed, in view of the supplemental agreements dated 28.05.1970 - Lease deed executed between the Govt. of the Maharaja of Travancore and Secretary of State for India in Council on 29.10.1886 was valid and binding on the defendant and it was enforceable by plaintiff against the defendant - Suit allowed.

(B)Water - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Kerala Irrigation and Water Conservation Act, 2003 -Kerala Irrigation and Water Conservation (Amendment) Act, 2006 - States Reorganisation Act, 1956, s. 108 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 3, 4 131, 363 - Inter-sate water dispute - Increase of water level - Validity of legislation - Constitutionality - Whether 2006 Amendment Act was unconstitutional and ultra vires, in its application to and effect on the dam and does impugned legislation amount to usurpation of judicial power and it was violative of rule of law -

Held, it was abundantly clear that on the one hand there was a finding of fact determined by SC on hearing the parties on basis of evidence/materials placed on record in judgment in 2006 Indlaw SC 69 and on the other in 2006 Amendment Act, defendant legislature has declared the dam being an endangered one and fixed the water level in the dam at 136 ft. - If judgment of SC Court in 2006 Indlaw SC 69 and the 2006 Amendment Act were placed side by side insofar as safety of dam for raising the water level from 136 ft. to 142 ft. was concerned, it was obvious that judgment of SC and the law enacted by defendant State legislature could not stand together and they were irreconcilable and inconsistent - Impugned law was a classic case of nullification of a judgment simpliciter, as in the judgment of SC the question of safety of dam was determined on the basis of materials placed before it and not on the interpretation of any existing law and there was no occasion for the legislature to amend the law by altering the basis on which the judgment was founded - When impugned law was not a validation law, there was no question of the legislature removing the defect, as the Court had not found any vice in the existing law and declared such law to be bad - Any unilateral law enacted by one of the parties that results in overturning the final judgment was bad not because it was affected by the principles of res judicata but because it infringes the doctrine of separation of powers and rule of law, as by such law, legislature had clearly usurped the judicial power - Principles of res judicata were clearly attracted in instant present case - Claim of defendant State in the earlier proceeding that water level could not be raised from its present level of 136 ft. was expressly not accepted and the obstruction by defendant to the water level in the dam being raised to 142 ft. on the ground of safety was found untenable - Judgment dt.27.2.2006 of SC, thus, operates as res judicata in respect of the issue of safety of the dam by increasing its water level from 136 ft. to 142 ft. - 2006 Amendment Act, was unconstitutional and ultra vires in its application to and effect on the dam - Rights of plaintiff, crystallized in judgment dt.27.2.2006 passed by SC in 2006 Indlaw SC 69 could not be nullified by a legislation made by defendant State legislature - Earlier judgment of SC given on 27.2.2006 operates as res judicata on the issue of safety of dam for raising water level to 142 ft. and ultimately to 152 ft. after completion of further strengthening measures on the dam - Plea raised by Kerala relating to lease deed dt.29.10.1886 and structural safety of dam were finally decided by judgment of SC dt.27.2.2006 and defendant was estopped from raising or re-agitating these issues in instant suit - Defendant could not obstruct plaintiff from increasing the water level of dam to 142 ft. and from carrying out repair works as per judgment dt.27.2.2006 - Suit allowed.

C) Water - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Kerala Irrigation and Water Conservation Act, 2003 -Kerala Irrigation and Water Conservation (Amendment) Act, 2006 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 3, 4 131, 363 - Inter-sate water dispute - Riparian States - Inter-state river - Legality - Whether defendant was estopped from contending that Periyar River was not an inter-State river -

Held, it was true that averment of plaintiff in plaint that the 2 States were riparian States was not right in its entirety because plaintiff was not a riparian State but the status of Periyar river as inter-State river, on the basis of what Court observed could not be overlooked - It was not open to defendant to take a totally inconsistent plea and begin fresh controversy about the status of Periyar river on the ground that earlier plea was founded on some erroneous premise - Defendant could not be permitted to contend that Periyar river was not an inter-State river - Suit allowed.

(D) Water - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Kerala Irrigation and Water Conservation Act, 2003 -Kerala Irrigation and Water Conservation (Amendment) Act, 2006 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 3, 4 131, 363 - Inter-sate water dispute - Suit for injunction - Entitlement of - Whether plaintiff was entitled to a permanent injunction -

Held, in facts, it could safely be said that plaintiff was able to establish that invasion on its rights was substantial - Plaintiff was able to make out a case for grant of injunction on the principles laid down by SC in 2013 Indlaw SC 116 - Moreover, instant suit was not a suit for injunction simpliciter as the main prayer was that 20065 Amendment Act, be declared unconstitutional and ultra vires in its application to and effect on the dam - 2006 Amendment Act, passed by defendant State legislature was unconstitutional in its application to and effect on the dam - Defendant State was restrained by a decree of permanent injunction from applying and enforcing the impugned legislation or in any manner interfering with or obstructing the State of Tamil Nadu from increasing the water level to 142 ft. and from carrying out the repair works as per the judgment of SC dt.27.2.2006 - A 3-member Supervisory Committee was constituted - Committee should have one representative from CWC and one representative each from the 2 States - Representative of CWC should be the Chairman of Committee and Committee would select the place for its office, which should be provided by Kerala -Plaintiff should bear the entire expenditure of Committee - Suit allowed.
