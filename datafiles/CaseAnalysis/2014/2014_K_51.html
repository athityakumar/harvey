B. A. Linga Reddy Etc. v Karnataka State Transport Authority and others

Supreme Court of India

18 December 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Arun Mishra, Jagdish Singh Khehar

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 883; (2015) 4 SCC 515; 2015 (1) ACC 148; AIR 2015 SC 767; 2015 (2) ALD(SC) 207; 2015 (2) AWC 1426; 2015 (1) MLJ 361; 2015 (1) RCR(Civil) 336; 2014(14) SCALE 314

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Carriers & Transportation

Keywords: Disclosure

Summary: Carriers & Transportation - Administrative - Motor Vehicles Act, 1988, s. 102 - Motor Vehicles Act, 1939, s. 39 - Stage carriage service schemes - Modification - Violation of principles of natural justice - Respondent State notified various schemes u/s. 39 of the 1939 Act to operate stage carriages on notified routes only for State Transport Corporation (STC) - However, schemes were modified by providing operation of services by inter-state permit-holders - STC filed objections to various modifications made in schemes - Respondent State published modified scheme in Gazette - STC challenged modification order before HC - HC quashed modification orders - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, it is apparent from the provisions that the scheme is framed for providing efficient, adequate, economical and properly coordinated road transport service in public interest. S. 102 of the 1988 Act does not lay down the requirement of recording any express finding on any particular aspect; whereas the duty is to hear and consider the objections. It requires the State Govt. to act in public interest to cancel or modify a scheme after giving the STC or any other affected person by the proposed modification an opportunity of hearing. The State is supposed to be acting in public interest while exercising the power under the provision. However, that does not dispense with the requirement to record reasons while dealing with objections. In the instant case, there is no consideration of the objections except mentioning the arguments of the rival parties. Objections both factual and legal have not been considered much less reasons assigned to overrule them. Even in brief, reasons have not been assigned indicating how objections are disposed of. Thus, modification of the Schemes could not be said to be in accordance with the principles of natural justice in the absence of reasons so as to reach the conclusion that private operators are meeting the genuine demands of the public in excess of the service provided by the State transport operators, hence, it cannot be said to be sustainable. Hence, State Govt. is directed to hear the objections, consider and decide the same in accordance with law by a reasoned order. Appeals dismissed.

Ratio - Modification of the transport scheme is a quasi-judicial function where the State Govt. is duty-bound to consider the objections and to give reasons either to accept or reject them.
