Shabnam Hashmi v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

19 February 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjan Gogoi, P. Sathasivam, Shiva Kirti Singh

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 99; (2014) 4 SCC 1; 2014 (2) ADJ 619; AIR 2014 SC 1281; 2014 (3) ALD(SC) 89; 2014(2) ALL MR 944; 2014 (4) AWC 3691; 2014 (1) CLT(SC) 288; 2014 (2) KLT 444; 2014 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1229; 2014 (4) MahLJ 933; 2014 (2) MLJ 379; 2014(5) M.P.H.T. 244; 2014 (3) MPLJ 343; 2014 (1) RCR(Civil) 1052; 2014 (3) RLW 2304; 2014(2) SCALE 529; [2014] 2 S.C.R. 1101; 2015 (1) WLN(SC) 7

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Family & Personal; Women & Children

Keywords: Right to adopt and to be adopted

Summary: Constitution - Family & Personal - Women & Children - Constitution of India, 1950, Part III, arts. 21, 32, 44 - Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2000, ss. 41 to 44 - Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Rules, 2007, rr. 33(2) to 33(4) - Special Marriage Act, 1954 - Adoption - Right to adopt and to be adopted - Legality - Petitioner filed instant petition u/art. 32 of Constitution and prayed for a declaration that the right of a child to be adopted and that of the prospective parents to adopt be declared a fundamental right u/art. 21 of Constitution and sought to lay down guidelines enabling adoption of children by persons irrespective of religion, caste, creed and further for a direction to respondent (UOI) to enact an optional law the prime focus of which was the child with considerable like religion - Petitioner contended that the 2000 Act enabled any person, irrespective of religion he profess, to take a child in adoption and it was akin to the 1954 Act - Personal Law Board contended that Islamic Law did not recognize an adopted child to be at par with a biological child and that 'Kafala' system which was recognized by the UN's Convention of the Rights of Child u/art. 20(3) was one of the alternate system of child care contemplated by 2000 Act -

Held, u/r. 33(2) of the Rules, guidelines issued by Central Adoption Resource Agency (CARA), as notified by Central Govt. u/s. 41(3) of the 2000 Act, were made applicable to all matters relating to adoption - The 2000 Act did not mandate any compulsive action by any prospective parent leaving such person with the liberty of accessing the provisions of 2000 Act, if he so desires - Such a person was always free to adopt or choose not to do so and, instead, follow what he comprehends to be the dictates of the personal law applicable to him - The 2000 Act was a small step in reaching the goal enshrined by art. 44 of Constitution - Personal beliefs and faiths, though should be honoured, could not dictate the operation of the provisions of an enabling statute - An optional legislation that did not contain an unavoidable imperative could not be stultified by principles of personal law which, however, would always continue to govern any person who chooses to so submit himself until such time that the vision of a uniform Civil Code was achieved - The same could only happen by the collective decision of the generation(s) to come to sink conflicting faiths and beliefs that were still active as on date - Enactment of 2000 Act should receive due respect - Conflicting viewpoints prevailed between different communities, as on date, on the subject makes the vision contemplated by art. 44 of Constitution i.e., a Uniform Civil Code, a goal yet to be fully reached and Court was reminded of the anxiety expressed by it earlier with regard to necessity to maintain restraint - It was not an appropriate time and stage where the right to adopt and right to be adopted could be raised to the status of a fundamental right and/or to understand such a right to be encompassed by art. 21 of Constitution - Petition disposed of.
