Shatrughan Chauhan and another v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

21 January 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Sathasivam, Shiva Kirti Singh, Ranjan Gogoi

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 34; (2014) 3 SCC 1; 2014 CRLJ 1327; 2014 (1) CTC 484; JT 2014 (2) SC 106; 2014 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 471; 2014 (1) MLJ(Crl) 326; 2014 (1) RCR(Criminal) 741; 2014(1) SCALE 437; [2014] 1 S.C.R. 609

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Murder, Maintainability, Rape, Death sentence, Rejection of Mercy petition, Commutation of death sentence, Delay in Execution, Suffering from insanity/mental illness

Summary: (A) Criminal - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 376, 34 - Murder - Rape - Death sentence - Rejection of Mercy petition -Commutation of death sentence - Entitlement - Petitioners accused all of them where charged on various offences punishable u/ss. 302, 376, 34 IPC - Accused where convicted and sentenced to death - Hence instant petitions by accused or by their family members or by public-spirited bodies based on rejection of mercy petitions by Governor and President of India - Whether petitioners were entitled for commutation of their death sentence into life imprisonment - Held, series of Constitution Benches of Court upheld Constitutional validity of death sentence in India over span of decades but these judgments in no way took away duty to follow due procedure established by law in execution of sentence - Like death sentence was passed lawfully, execution of sentence should also be in consonance with Constitutional mandate and not in violation of Constitutional principles - Exercising of power u/arts. 72, 161 of the Constitution by President or Governor was Constitutional obligation and not mere prerogative - Considering high status of office, Constitutional framers did not stipulate any outer time limit for disposing mercy petitions under said articles, which means it should be decided within reasonable time - However, when delay caused in disposing mercy petitions was seen to be unreasonable, unexplained and exorbitant, it was duty of Court to step in and consider this aspect - Right to seek for mercy u/art. 72, 161 of the Constitution was Constitutional right and not at discretion or whims of executive - Every Constitutional duty should be fulfilled with due care and diligence otherwise judicial interference was command of Constitution for upholding its values - Hence, petitioners death sentence was commuted to life imprisonment - Petitions allowed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of Indian, 1950, art. 32 r/w s. 21 - Death sentence - Delay in Execution - Maintainability - Whether aspect of delay was one of supervening circumstances warranted commutation of death sentence into life imprisonment - Held, undue long delay in execution of sentence of death would entitle condemned prisoner to approach Court u/art. 32 of the Constitution - However, Court would only examine circumstances surrounding delay that occurred and those that have ensued after sentence was finally confirmed by judicial process - Court should not reopen conclusion already reached but might consider question of inordinate delay to decide whether execution of sentence should be carried out or should be altered into imprisonment for life - Keeping convicts in suspense while consideration of their mercy petition by President for many years was certainly agony for him/her - It created adverse physical conditions and psychological stresses on convicts under sentence of death - Indisputably, Court, while considering rejection of clemency petition by President, u/art. 32 r/w s. 21 of the Constitution should not excuse agonizing delay caused to convicts only on basis of gravity of crime - Accordingly, if there was undue unexplained and inordinate delay in execution due to pendency of mercy petitions or executive as well as Constitutional authorities failed to take note of/consider relevant aspects, Court was well within its powers u/art. 32 of the Constitution to hear grievance of convicts and commute death sentence into life imprisonment on this ground alone - However, only after satisfying that delay was not caused at instance of accused himself - Delay caused by circumstances beyond prisoners' control mandates commutation of death sentence - Accordingly, delay in disposal of mercy petition was one of relevant circumstances for commutation of death sentence - Petitioners have made out a case for commutation of death sentence into life imprisonment - Petitions allowed.

(C) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of Indian, 1950, art. 21 - Death sentence - Delay in Execution - Suffering from insanity/mental illness - Maintainability - Whether aspect of insanity and mental illness was one of supervening circumstances warranted commutation of death sentence into life imprisonment - Held, convicts prayed for commutation of death sentence into sentence of life imprisonment on ground that unconscionably long delay in deciding mercy petition caused onset of chronic psychotic illness, and in view of this execution of death sentence would be inhuman and against well-established canons of human rights - Directions of United Nations International Conventions, of which India was party clearly showed that insanity/mental illness/schizophrenia was crucial supervening circumstance, which should be considered by Court in deciding whether in facts and circumstances of case death sentence could be commuted to life imprisonment - After it was established that death convict was insane and it was duly certified by competent doctor, undoubtedly art.21 of the Constitution protected him and such person should not be executed without further clarification from competent authority about his mental problems - It was highlighted by relying on commentaries from various countries that civilized countries have not executed death penalty on insane person - United Nations Resolution against execution of death sentence, debate of General Assembly, decisions of International Court of Justice, Treaties, European Conventions, 8th amendment in United States which prohibited execution of death sentence on insane person - In view of well established laws both at national as well as international sphere, Court inclined to consider insanity as one of supervening circumstances that warrants for commutation of death sentence to life imprisonment - Hence, mental illness, caused to accused should not be executed -On this ground, death sentence was commuted to life imprisonment - Petitions allowed.
