Rajat Prasad v C.B.I.

Supreme Court of India

24 April 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjan Gogoi, N.V. Ramana, P. Sathasivam

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 296; (2014) 6 SCC 495; 2014 CRLJ 2941; 2014 (2) JCC 1376; JT 2014 (7) SC 206; 2014 (3) MLJ(Crl) 81; 2014 (2) RCR(Criminal) 690; 2014(5) SCALE 574; [2014] 8 S.C.R. 636

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120B, 120A - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 12, 107 - Sting operation - Criminal charges - Quashing of - Refusal - Sustainability - Trial court framed charges against appellants/accused u/s. 120B IPC and s. 12 of the Act - Appellant assailed the said order before HC whereby HC refused to interfere with the said order of Trial Court - Hence instant appeals - Should such an individual i.e. the sting operator be held to be criminally liable for commission of the offence that was inherent and inseparable from the process by which commission of another offence was sought to be established and should the commission of the first offence be understood to be obliterated and extinguished in the face of claims of larger public interest that the sting operator seeks to make, namely, to expose the main offender of a serious crime injurious to public interest, could the commission of the initial offence by the sting operator be understood to be without any criminal intent and only to facilitate the commission of the other offence by the 'main culprit' and its exposure before the public -

Held, crime does not stand obliterated or extinguished merely because its commission was claimed to be in public interest - Any such principle would be abhorrent to our criminal jurisprudence - At the same time the criminal intent behind the commission of the Act which was alleged to have occasioned the crime would have to be established before the liability of the person charged with the commission of crime could be adjudged - Doctrine of mens rea, though a salient feature of the Indian criminal justice system, found expression in different statutory provisions requiring proof of either intention or knowledge on the part of accused - Such proof was to be gathered from the surrounding facts established by the evidence and materials before the Court and not by a process of probe of the mental state of accused which the law does not contemplate - Offence of abetment defined by s. 107 of IPC or the offence of criminal conspiracy u/s. 120A of IPC would, thus, require criminal intent on the part of the offender like any other offence - Both the offences would require existence of a culpable mental state which was a matter of proof from the surrounding facts established by the materials on record - Therefore, whether the commission of offence u/s. 12 of the Act r/w s. 120B IPC was occasioned by the Acts attributed to appellants or not, ideally, was a matter that could be determined only after the evidence in the case was recorded - What accused appellants assert was that in view of the fact that the sting operation was a journalistic exercise, no criminal intent could be imputed to the participants therein - Whether the operation was really such an exercise and the giving of bribe to A-1 was a mere sham or pretence or whether the giving of the bribe was with expectation of favours in connection with mining projects, are questions that can only be answered by the evidence of the parties which yet to come - Journalist or any other citizen who had no connection, even remotely, with the favour that was allegedly sought in exchange for the bribe offered, could not be imputed with the necessary intent to commit the offence of abetment u/s. 12 of the Act or that of conspiracy u/s. 120B IPC - Non applicability of said provisions of law in such situations, therefore, may be ex-facie apparent - Cause of journalism and its role and responsibility in spreading information and awareness would stand subserved - It was only in cases where the question reasonably arose whether the sting operator had a stake in the favours that were allegedly sought in return for the bribe that the issue would require determination in the course of a full- fledged trial - above was certainly not exhaustive of the situations where such further questions may arose requiring a deeper probe - As such situations were myriad, if not infinite, any attempt at illustration must be avoided - HC refusing to interfere with the charges framed against appellants was fully justified - Appeals dismissed.
