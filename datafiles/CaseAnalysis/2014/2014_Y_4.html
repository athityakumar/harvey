Yogendra Yadav and others v State of Jharkhand and another

Supreme Court of India

21 July 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjana Prakash Desai, N.V. Ramana

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 465; (2014) 9 SCC 653; AIR 2014 SC 3055; 2014 CRLJ 3935; JT 2014 (8) SC 357; 2014 (3) RCR(Criminal) 869; 2014(8) SCALE 634

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 341, 323, 324, 504 and 307 - Attempt to murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellants allegedly committed offence of attempt to murder - Appellants were convicted by Trial Court u/ss. 341, 323, 324, 504 and 307 IPC - Hence instant appeal - Whether SC could compound the offences u/ss. 326 and 307 of the IPC which were non-compoundable -

Held, needless to say that offences which were non-compoundable could not be compounded by the court - Courts drew the power of compounding offences from s.320 of CrPC - Said provision had to be strictly followed in one case - Gian Singh v. State of Punjab, 2012 Indlaw SC 314, relied on - However, in a given case, HC could quash a criminal proceeding in exercise of its power u/s. 482 of CrPC having regard to the fact that the parties have amicably settled their disputes and the victim had no objection, even though the offences were non-compoundable - In which cases the HC could exercise its discretion to quash the proceedings would depend on facts and circumstances of each case - Offences which involve moral turpitude, grave offences like rape, murder etc. could not be effaced by quashing the proceedings because that would have harmful effect on the society - Such offences could not be said to be restricted to two individuals or two groups - If such offences were quashed, it might send wrong signal to the society - However, when HC was convinced that the offences were entirely personal in nature and, therefore, did not affect public peace or tranquility and where it feels that quashing of such proceedings on account of compromise would bring about peace and would secure ends of justice, it should not hesitate to quash them - In such cases, the prosecution becomes a lame prosecution - Pursuing such a lame prosecution would be waste of time and energy - That would also unsettle the compromise and obstruct restoration of peace - In same case SC had observed that where HC quashed a criminal proceeding having regard to the fact that the dispute between the offender and the victim was settled although the offences were not compoundable, it did so as in its opinion, continuation of criminal proceedings would be an exercise in futility and justice in the case demands that the dispute between the parties was put to an end and peace was restored; securing the ends of justice being the ultimate guiding factor - Needless to say that the above observations were applicable to SC also - Appeal disposed of.
