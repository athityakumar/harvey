U. C. Raman v P. T. A. Rahim and others

Supreme Court of India

01 August 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

Shiva Kirti Singh, R.M. Lodha

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 547; (2014) 8 SCC 934; AIR 2014 SC 3477; 2014 (6) ALD(SC) 138; 2014(6) ALL MR 946; 2014 (106) ALR 208; 2014 (4) CLT(SC) 183; JT 2014 (9) SC 117; 2014(9) SCALE 139; [2014] 11 S.C.R. 909

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Election

Keywords: Disqualified, Parliament (Prevention of Disqualification) Act, 1959, Haj Committee Act, 2002, Parliament (Prevention of Disqualification) Act 2006, Haj Committee Rules, 2002, Holding office of profit

Summary: Election - Constitution - Representation of People Act, 1951, ss. 116A, 116B - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 191, 191(1)(a) - Parliament (Prevention of Disqualification) Act, 1959, s. 3 - Haj Committee Act, 2002 - Haj Committee Rules, 2002, r. 11 - Holding office of profit - Disqualification - Legality - Respondent was nominated as one of the member of a committee and was elected as Chairperson of said committee - Respondent and appellant filed nominations for State Legislative Assembly election and respondent - Appellant raised objections against acceptance of nomination of respondent by reason of respondent holding an 'office of profit' under State Govt., which was rejected - However, respondent was declared elected - Aggrieved appellant filed election petition before HC - Single Judge by impugned judgment held that office of Chairperson of Committee was an office under the State Govt. and that appellant miserably failed to prove that respondent was holding an 'office of profit' as contemplated u/art. 191 of Constitution and thus acceptance of his nomination did not suffer from any impropriety or illegality and dismissed the election petition - Hence, instant appeal - U/s. 116A read with Section 116B of 1951 Act was whether for the purpose of art. 191(1)(a) of Constitution, respondent no. 1 held an office of profit under GOI and for that reason his nomination ought to have been rejected -

Held, r. 11 of the Rules did not entitle the Chairperson and members anything besides travelling allowance (TA) and daily allowance for attending meetings - It was also an admitted fact that although State Govt. was vested with power to prescribe allowances to the members of Committee u/s. 20 of the Act but such power was not exercised by State Govt. so far - Keeping in view the nature of TA and daily allowance in mind, HC had come to the conclusion that not only the pecuniary benefits received by respondent were only compensatory in nature but as a matter of fact the post did not carry any other benefits which might be categorized as pecuniary benefits 'receivable' by respondent, so as to classify the office in question as an 'office of profit' - In instant case, as per evidence on record respondent was neither in receipt of any pay, salary, emoluments, remuneration or commission, nor anything of such nature was payable to him - Respondent was in receipt of only TA and daily allowance which were compensatory allowance and those alone were 'receivable' also - Inclusion of Haj Committee of India constituted u/s. 3 of the Act within the purview of s. 3 of 1959 Act could not help the case of appellant because respondent happened to be Chairperson of State Haj Committee and allowances admissible to Haj Committee of India have not been shown to be same as that for State Haj Committee - Further the reply on behalf of respondent that amendment might have been introduced by way of abundant caution was also plausible and could not be brushed aside - Plea raised by appellant that word 'profit' should include even status and influence etc., besides the pecuniary profits, was not found acceptable in view of long line of judgments of SC - Court had given categorical clarification on more than one occasion that an 'office of profit' was an office which was capable of yielding a profit or pecuniary gain - Word 'profit' had always been treated equivalent to or a substitute for the term 'pecuniary gain' - The very context, in which word 'profit' was used after the words 'office of', shows that not all offices were disqualified but only those which yield pecuniary gains as profit other than mere compensatory allowances, to the holder of office - There was no requirement to make a departure from the long line of established precedents on that issue - If the submissions of appellant were to be accepted, it would add a great amount of uncertainty in deciding whether an office was an 'office of profit' or not - Appeal dismissed.
