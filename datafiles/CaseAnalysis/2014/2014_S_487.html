 Uttar Pradesh Hindi Sahitya Sammelan v State of Uttar Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

04 September 2014

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Lodha, Dipak Misra, Sharad Arvind Bobde, Kurian Joseph, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2014 Indlaw SC 579; (2014) 9 SCC 716; AIR 2015 SC 1154; 2015(1) ALL MR 401; 2014 (106) ALR 879; 2014 (6) AWC 5830; JT 2014 (10) SC 40; 2014 (3) KLT 1112; 2014 (6) MLJ 624; 2014 (4) RCR(Civil) 350; 2014(10) SCALE 119; 2014 (3) UPLBEC 2360

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: Official Languages Act, 1963, Language Of The Union, Uttar Pradesh Official Language Act, 1951, U.P. Official Language Act, 1951, Uttar Pradesh Official Language (Amendment) Act, 1989, Uttar Pradesh Official Language (Amendment) Ordinance, 1982, U.P. Official Language (Amendment) (3rd) Ordinance, 1983, U.P. Official Language (Amendment ) Act, 1989, Uttar Pradesh Official Language (Amendment) Bill, 1989, Second language Urdu, Constitutional permissibility

Summary: Constitution - Administrative - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 345, 347 - Uttar Pradesh Official Language Act, 1951, ss. 2, 3 - Uttar Pradesh Official Language (Amendment) Ordinance, 1982 - Uttar Pradesh Official Language (Amendment) (3rd) Ordinance, 1983 - Uttar Pradesh Official Language (Amendment) Act, 1989, s. 3 - Second language Urdu - Constitutional permissibility - 1982 Ordinance was replaced by Ordinance 44 of 1983 - Constitutionality of Ordinance No. 44 of 1983 was put in issue before High Court by appellant (Sahitya Sammelan), which was dismissed by DB, though by separate judgments - 1989 Amendment Act was enacted by the State to amend 1951 Act - By that Amendment Act, s. 3 was inserted after s. 2 in 1951 Act providing for Urdu language as second official language for such purposes as might be notified by State Govt. from time to time - State Govt. issued a notification on 7-10-1989 notifying use of Urdu language as second official language for 7 purposes - Appellant, which had filed writ petition no. 285 of 1984 earlier before the HC challenging the Constitutionality of Ordinance No. 44 of 1983, filed another writ petition before HC challenging the 1989 Amendment Act and Notification dt.7-10-1989 - In view of difference of opinion between the DB, Bench directed the papers to be laid before the CJ of HC for referring questions to a third Judge for his opinion - The third Judge opined that impugned enactment was, however, valid piece of legislation in view of judgment of DB in writ Petition no. 285 of 1984 and impugned enactment did not suffer from the vice of excessive delegation and impugned enactment and notification were valid and Constitutional - However, DB by impugned judgment dismissed the writ petitions - Hence, instant appeals - Whether it was Constitutional for respondent State Legislative Assembly to declare Urdu as second official language through the 1989 Amendment Act once it had declared Hindi as the official language in 1951 u/art. 345 of Constitution -

Held, argument of appellant that text of art. 345 of Constitution gives 2 options to the State Legislature, one, adoption of any one or more of the languages in use in the State (Option 1) and the other, Hindi (Option 2) and once Option 2 is exercised, the power of the State Legislature gets exhausted. If the argument of appellant is accepted, it would mean that the use of the word 'or' signifies that Option 1 would be available to the Legislature of State only if it does not go in for Option 2. Once the State Legislature has exercised Option 2, and adopted Hindi as the language to be used for all or any of the official purposes of the State, it cannot go down the route of Option 1. Court find it difficult to accept the submission of appellant. Merely because Hindi is mentioned explicitly or separately and it is adopted as official language by the State, Court do not think that the Constitution forecloses the State Legislature's option to adopt any other language in use in the State as official language. The plain language of art. 345 of Constitution which empowers the State Legislature to make law for adoption of one or more of the languages in use in the State leaves no manner of doubt that such power may be exercised by the State Legislature from time to time. A different intention does not appear from the plain language of art. 345 of Constitution. Court did do not find any indication that the power can be exercised by the State Legislature only once and that power gets exhausted if the State Legislature adopts Hindi as the official language of the State. State Legislature is at liberty to exercise its discretion u/art. 345 from time to time for specified purpose. It does not appear to Court that Hindi once adopted as official language of the State in exercise of its power by State Legislature u/art. 345 of Constitution, the State Legislature ceases to have any law making power u/art. 345 of Constitution. If Hindi is in use in a particular State then it does not foreclose the State's power or discretion to adopt any language other than Hindi as the official language provided such language is 'in use' in that State. The use of the word 'may' in art. 345 of Constitution is not without significance. It indicates that State has discretion in adopting the language or languages in use in the State and so also Hindi. Such discretion can be exercised any number of times by the State Legislature as it deems proper. The only restriction to such legislative power is in art. 347 of Constitution in a given situation which we shall explain after some more discussion. In the absence of direction issued by President u/art. 347 of Constitution, there is no restriction, restraint or impediment for the State Legislature in adopting one of the languages in use in the State as an official language u/art. 345 of Constitution. It will be unreasonable to construe art. 345 of Constitution in the manner suggested by appellant. It is said that law and language are both organic in their mode of development. In India, these are evolving through the process of accepting legitimate aspirations of the speakers of different languages. Indian language laws are not rigid but accommodative - the object being to secure linguistic secularism. Neither insertion of s. 3 in 1989 Amendment Act nor impugned notification notifying Urdu as the second language for seven purposes is unconstitutional. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - Purpose of using Hindi separately in art. 345 of Constitution is to facilitate adoption of Hindi across the States and any other construction to art. 345 would compromise its language adopted by the Constitution.
