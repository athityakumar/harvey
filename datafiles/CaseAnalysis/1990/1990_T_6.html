Era Sezhiyan v T. R. Ballu and Others

Supreme Court of India

01 March 1990

Case Analysis

Bench

M.H. Kania, Kuldip Singh

Where Reported

1990 Indlaw SC 651; (1990) Supp SCC 322; AIR 1990 SC 838; JT 1990 (1) SC 392; 1990(1) SCALE 377; [1990] 1 S.C.R. 767

Case Digest

Subject: Election

Keywords: Ballot Box, Polling Station

Summary: Election - Representation of the People Act, 1951 - Election - Sustainability - Appellant/petitioner filed election petition before HC challenging election of 6 member to Rajya Sabha by elected member of Tamil Nadu Legislative Assembly - Election was under preferential system of voting - It was case of appellant that out of 33 first preference votes cast in favour of appellant, one ballot paper was rejected by respondent no. 8/Returning Officer, on ground that said ballot paper was marked by voter otherwise than with article supplied for that purpose - Working result sheets of counting were prepared and respondent no. 8 announced that respondents Nos. 1 to 6 were declared as duly elected and appellant was declared as having lost election - HC dismissed election petition - Hence instant appeal - Appellant contended that express on 'article supplied for the purpose' used in r. 39A(2)(b) and r. 73(2)(e) of Election Rules was misconstrued by HC - Further contended that in context of election law and instructions contained in hand-books to which reference would be made that expression should be interpreted as meaning 'actually given' or 'handed over' - Held, in case of election to Rajya Sabha or a Legislative Council, situation was entirely different - Number of voters was limited - One could assume that they were reasonably familiar with procedure of voting; and article supplied for marking preference was a fountain pen or ball-point pen - In these circumstances, there was hardly any warrant for requiring that procedure of handing over personally to each voter article for marking his preference should be followed and it was quite' adequate if article for marking preference, namely, fountain pen or ball-point pen was made available in voting booth with clear instructions that same should be used in marking preference - It should also be borne in mind that there was no express rule or instruction in connection with elections to Rajya Sabha by Members of State Assemblies or elections to Legislative Councils of States which specifically required that article for marking preference should be handed over to each voter personally - In these circumstances, HC was right in interpreting expression 'article supplied for the purpose' in r. 39A(2)(b) and r. 73(2)(e) of Election Rules as meaning 'made available for the purpose' or 'provided for the purpose' - With regard to rejection of ballot paper, ballot paper was rejected on ground that it was marked otherwise than with an article supplied for the purpose - Figure 1 indicating first preference in said ballot paper was marked in green ink whereas in ball-point pen kept in voting booth with ballot box, ink used was blue - Returning officer took view that said marking of preference in green ink clearly established that it was done with a bail-point pen other than the one which was supplied for marking preference and hence the vote was invalid - In instant case r. 39(2)(b) which was applicable to the election petition clearly prescribed that vote should be cast by voter in accordance with said r. 39 (2) of Election Rules, with article supplied for purpose - Hence, unless ball-point pen kept with ballot box was not to be regarded as article supplied for marking preference, intention of elector in instant case could not be given effect to as it was expressed in a manner inconsistent with provisions in rules - R. 56 (b) (2) of Election Rules provided inter alia that if ballot paper contained mark made on it otherwise than with instrument supplied for the purpose, returning officer should reject said ballot paper - Further, in respect of 3 votes, figure 1 was marked, not in right-hand column opposite name of respondent No. 1, but in left-hand column containing name of candidate and opposite name of respondent No. 1 - Appellant unsuccessfully objected to the validity of these 3 ballot papers on ground that first preference was not marked in space provided for that purpose opposite name of candidate concerned, namely, respondent No. 1, as required by r. 37A(2) - SC was in full agreement with reasoning and conclusions given by HC in its impugned judgment in coming to conclusion that returning officer was justified in rejecting objections preferred by appellant to the said 3 votes and holding that the same were valid -Appeal dismissed.
