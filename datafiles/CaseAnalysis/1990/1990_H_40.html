Hiralal and Another v Gajjan and Others

Supreme Court of India

30 January 1990

Case Analysis

Bench

M. Fathima Beevi, K.N. Saikia

Where Reported

1990 Indlaw SC 678; (1990) 3 SCC 285; AIR 1990 SC 723; 1990 (1) AWC 286; JT 1990 (1) SC 95; 1990 RD 75; 1990(1) SCALE 82; [1990] 1 S.C.R. 164; 1990 (1) UJ 561

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Code of Civil Procedure 1908, Uttar Pradesh Zamindar Abolition and Land reforms Act 1950 (Uttar Pradesh 1 of 1951)

Summary: Civil Procedure - Land & Property - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, ss. 100 and 100(1)(c) - Uttar Pradesh Zamindar Abolition and Land reforms Act, 1950, ss. 20(b)(i) and 240-A - Uttar Pradesh Land Reforms (supplementary) Act, 1952, s. 3 - U. P. Land Revenue Act, 1901, ss. 28 and 33 - (A) Whether High Court, while exercising its power u/s. 100 CPC has no jurisdiction to interfere with finding of fact recorded by first appellate court? - S. 100(1)(c) referred to a substantial error or defect in procedure; if in dealing with a question of fact lower appellate court has placed onus on wrong party and its finding of fact is result substantially of this wrong approach that may be regarded as a defect in procedure - Held, when first appellate court discarded evidence as inadmissible and High Court is satisfied that evidence was admissible that may introduce an error or defect in procedure; so also in a case where court below ignored weight of evidence and allowed judgment to be influenced by inconsequential matters, HC would be justified in re appreciating evidence and coming to its own independent decision - (B) Whether plaintiff-respondent's father was a sub-tenant in respect of land in question? - Held, Khasra entry showed that respondent's father was a sub-tenant, therefore as rightly stated by HC, it is not for plaintiff to prove that this entry is correct; it was for defendants to show that entry had been introduced surreptitiously out of ill-will or hostility and in absence of such proof, genuineness has to be presumed and entry accepted as evidence of sub-tenancy in favour of respondent's name as person in possession; HC was well within its powers in appreciating evidence and arriving at its own conclusion - (C) Whether second appeal abated on account of non-filing of substitution application after death of defendants 6, 10 and 11? - Held, these defendants were only proforma parties and HC was right in holding that appeal did not abate - (D) Whether revenue court had exclusive jurisdiction and civil court had no jurisdiction in present case? - Held, objection to jurisdiction of civil court to try suit on ground that revenue court had exclusive jurisdiction is not sustainable, suit being one for permanent injunction and question of title arises only incidentally - Appeal dismissed.
