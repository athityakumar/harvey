 Delhi Transport Corporation v D.T.C. Mazdoor Congress

Supreme Court of India

04 September 1990

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Mukharji, B.C. Ray, K. Ramaswamy, L.M. Sharma, P.B. Sawant

Where Reported

1990 Indlaw SC 224; (1991) Supp1 SCC 600; (1991) SCC (L&S) 1213; AIR 1991 SC 101; 1991 (1) CompLJ 1; JT 1990 (3) SC 725; 1991 LabIC 91; 1991 (1) LLJ 395; 1991 (1) LLN 613; 1990 SCALE 1; [1990] 1 S.C.R. 142; 1991 (1) SLJ 56; 1990 (5) SLR 311; [1990] Supp1 S.C.R. 142

Case Digest

Subject: Service; Contract & Commercial

Keywords: Power To Terminate, Solicitor, Delhi Road Transport Authority Act, 1950, Constitution (First Amendment) Act, 1951Constitution (Second Amendment) Act, 1952, Delhi Road Transport Authority (Conditions of Appointment and service) Regulation 1952

Summary: Sabyasachi Mukharji, J.

Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950 - Delhi Road Transport Authority (Conditions of Appointment & Service) Regulations, 1952, regn. 9 - Termination of service - Constitutional validity of right of employer to terminate services of permanent employees without holding any inquiry in certain circumstances by reasonable notice or pay in lieu of notice - Appellant-Delhi Transport Corporation, a statutory body, terminated the services of respondents Nos. 2 to 4 on ground that they were inefficient in their work and were inciting other staff members not to perform their duties - They were served with termination notices u/regn. 9(b) of the Regulations - Writ petitions were filed challenging the constitutional validity of u/regn. 9(b) of the Regulations - HC allowed the said writ petition and struck down regn. 9(b) of the Regulations, and directed the appellant to pay back respondents' wages and benefits - Instant appeals - Whether the clauses permitting the employers or authorities concerned to terminate the employment of the employees by giving reasonable notice or pay in lieu of notice but without holding any inquiry, were constitutionally valid and, if not, what would be the consequences of termination by virtue of such clauses or powers, and further whether such powers and clauses could be so read with such conditions which would make such powers constitutionally and legally valid? - Held, constitutionality of the conferment of power sustained by reading that the power must be exercised on reasons relevant for the efficient running of the services or performing of the job by the societies or the bodies - Power must, therefore, be with authorities to take decision quickly, objectively and independently - Power must be assumed with certain conditions of duty - Manner in which such exercise of power should be made should ensure fairness, avoid arbitrariness and mala fide and create credibility in the decisions arrived at or by exercise of the power - Reasons, good and sound, must control the exercise of power - These power must, therefore, be so read that the powers could be exercised on reasons, reasons should be recorded, reasons need not always be communicated, must be by authorities who were competent and are expected to act fairly, objectively and independently - Termination simpliciter u/regn. 9(b) or similar powers could be exercised only in circumstances other than those in regn. 9(a) - Exercise of such powers could only be for purposes germane and relevant to the statute - Terminating the services of permanent employees without holding any enquiry in the stated contingencies and this would be by either virtue of the silence of the provision indicating the contingencies of termination or by virtue of constitutional inhibitions - Appeals disposed of.

B.C. Ray, J.

Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 12, 14 and 19 - Constitutional validity of right of employer to terminate services of permanent employees without holding any inquiry in certain circumstances by reasonable notice or pay in lieu of notice - Whether regn. 9(b) of the Regulations which provides for termination of services of permanent employees on giving simply one month's notice or pay in lieu thereof without recording any reason therefor in the order of termination was arbitrary, illegal, discriminatory and violative of Audi Alterant Partem Rule and so constitutionally invalid and void? - Held, regn. 9(b) was wholly arbitrary, uncanalised and unrestricted violating principles of natural justice as well as art. 14 of the Constitution - Regn. 9(b) conferred unbridled, uncanalised and arbitrary power on the authority to terminate the services of a permanent employee without recording any reasons and without conforming to the principles of natural justice - There was no guideline in the Regulations or in the Act, as to when or in which cases and circumstances this power of termination by giving notice or pay in lieu of notice can be exercised - Regn. 9(b) did not expressly exclude the application of the 'audi alteram partem' rule and as such the order of termination of service of a permanent employee could not be passed by simply issuing a month's notice u/regn. 9(b) or pay in lieu thereof without recording any reason in the order and without giving any hearing to the Further, rights of the parties in the instant cases could not be governed by the general principle of master and servant, and the management could not have unrestricted and unqualified power of terminating the services of the employees - Regn. 9(b) could not be upheld for lack of adequate and appropriate guidelines - Appeals disposed of.

K. Ramaswamy, J

Service - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 12, 14 and 19 - Constitutional validity of right of employer to terminate services of permanent employees without holding any inquiry in certain circumstances by reasonable notice or pay in lieu of notice - Whether the statutory corporation or the instrumentality or the authority u/art. 12 of the Constitution was validly empowered to terminate the services of a permanent employee in terms of the contract of employment or rules without conducting an enquiry or an opportunity of show cause of proposed order of termination of the service? - Held, art. 14 read with 16(1) accords right to equality or an equal treatment consistent with the principles of natural justice - Any law made or action taken by the employer, corporate statutory or instrumentality u/art. 12 must be fair, just and reasonably - Right to fair treatment is an essential inbuilt of natural justice - Exercise of unbridled and uncanalised discretionary power impinges upon the right of the citizen; vesting of discretion was no wrong provided it was exercised purposively judiciously and without prejudice - Provisions of a statute, regulations or rules that empower an employer or the management to dismiss, remove or reduce rank of an employee, must be consistent with just, reasonable and fair procedure - Further, right to public employment which includes right to continued public employment till the employee was superannuated as per rules or compulsorily retired or duly terminated in accordance with the procedure established by law was an integral part of right to livelihood which in turn was an integral facet of right to life assured by art. 21 of the Constitution - Any procedure prescribed to deprive such a right to livelihood or continued employment must be just, fair and reasonable procedure - Hence, employee in a public employment also must not be arbitrarily unjustly and unreasonably be deprived of his/her livelihood which was ensured in continued employment till she/he was terminated in accordance with just, fair and reasonable procedure - Regn. 9(b), deliberately conferred wide power of termination of services of the employee without following the principle of audi alteram partem or even modicum of procedure of representation before terminating the services of permanent employee - Conferment of power with wide discretion without any guidelines, without any just, fair or reasonable procedure was constitutionally anathema to arts. 14, 16(1), 19(1)(g) and 21 of the Constitution - Appeals disposed of.

Constitution - Constitution of India, arts. 14 and 21 - Doctrine of reading down - Applicability of - Held, Courts though, have no power to amend the law by process of interpretation, but do have power to mend it so as to be in conformity with the intendment of the legislature - Doctrine of reading down was one of the principles of interpretation of statute in that process - But when the offending language used by the legislature was clear, precise and unambiguous, violating the relevant provisions in the constitution, resort could not be had to the doctrine of reading down to blow life into the void law to save from unconstitutionally or to confer jurisdiction on the legislature - It could not be taken aid of to emasculate the precise, explicit, clear and unambiguous language to confer arbitrary, unbridled and uncanalised power on an employer which was a negation to just, fair and reasonable procedure envisaged u/arts. 14 and 21 of the Constitution and to direct the authorities to record reasons, unknown or unintended procedure, in the manner argued by the learned counsel for the appellants - Doctrine of reading down could not be extended to such a situation - Appeals disposed of.
