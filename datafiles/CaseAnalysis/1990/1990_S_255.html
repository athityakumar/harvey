Kerala Hotel and Restaurant Association and Others v State of Kerala and Others

Supreme Court of India

21 February 1990

Case Analysis

Bench

J.S. Verma, N.D. Ojha, S. Ranganathan

Where Reported

1990 Indlaw SC 719; (1990) SCC (Tax) 309; (1990) 2 SCC 502; AIR 1990 SC 913; 1990 (2) CompLJ 119; JT 1990 (1) SC 324; 1990 (1) KLT 825; 1990(1) SCALE 252; [1990] 1 S.C.R. 516; 1990 (77) STC 253; 1990 (1) UJ 754

Case Digest

Subject: Indirect Tax; Sales Tax

Keywords: Constitutionality, Allegation of discrimination, Sales tax of cooked food sold in luxury hotels

Summary: Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Kerala General Sales Tax Act, 1963 - Tamil Nadu General Sales Tax Act, 1959 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Sales tax of cooked food sold in luxury hotels - Allegation of discrimination - Constitutionality - Eligibility to sales tax of cooked food sold only in luxury hotels was challenged on ground that it amounted to hostile discrimination - Kerala HC dismissed challenge and Madras HC had upheld challenge - Hence instant appeal - Whether imposition of sales tax on sale of cooked food in the more costly eating places alone violates guarantee of equality enshrined in Constitution that cooked food sold in modest eating places catering to need of common man was not similarly taxed - Held, Classification was made in instant case to bring within tax net hotels or eating houses of higher status excluding therefrom more modest ones - Rational nexus exists of classification with object for which it was made and classification was rounded on intelligible differentia - Legislature have chosen existing classification instead of resorting to fresh method of classification, it should not be ground of invalidity even assuming there were other better modes of permissible classification - That was clearly within domain of legislative wisdom intrusion into which of judicial review was unwarranted - There was no material placed to indicate that with reference to purpose for which classification had been made in instant case there was grouping together of dissimilar eating houses or that similar eating houses have been excluded from class subject to tax burden - Attack to Constitutional validity of impugned provisions in both States had no merit since classification made was neither discriminatory nor arbitrary - Order accordingly.
