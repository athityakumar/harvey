Murli Manohar And Co. And Anr. v State Of Haryana And Anr. Etc. Etc.

Supreme Court of India

25 October 1990

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Ranganathan, K. Jayachandra Reddy, K.N. Saikia

Where Reported

1990 Indlaw SC 504; (1991) 1 SCC 377; JT 1990 (4) SC 189; 1990(2) SCALE 821; [1990] 2 S.C.R. 343; 1991 (80) STC 79; [1990] Supp2 S.C.R. 343

Case Digest

Subject: Sales Tax

Keywords: Gross Turnover, Registered Dealer, Purchase Tax, Raw Materials, Sale In The State, Sale In The Course Of Export

Summary: Sales Tax - Haryana Sales Tax Act, 1973, ss. 9(1) - Punjab General Sales Tax Act, 1948 - Tax levied - Liability - Each of the appellants/ petitioners was a registered dealer in respondent/state - Appellants purchased certain raw materials in respondent without paying tax thereon, in view of the provision contained in s. 24 of 1973 Act - Appellants then manufactured certain goods in State with the aid of said raw materials - Appellants then sold the manufactured goods to dealers who, in turn, exported those goods out of India - On these facts, it was claimed, the assessee was not liable to pay the purchase tax on the raw materials imposed under s. 9(1) of 1973 Act - Claim was rejected by the taxing authorities and HC - Petitions were filed directly in SC in view of single Judge of the HC having decided the issue against the assessees which was also affirmed by a DB later - Some of the provisions of 1973 Act were amended with retrospective effect when actually a predecessor Act i.e., 1948 Act was in force - Whether applying sales tax on goods manufactured by appellants was sustainable - Held, contention of State about s. 6 of 1973 Act which was the charging section, both originally and after its retrospective amendment in 1990, imposed a tax on all sales and purchases effected by a dealer - In the original section, there was an Explanation which restricted the meaning of purchases for the purposes of the section - It provided that only purchases of declared goods, goods specified in Schedule C and goods falling u/s. 9 of 1973 Act would be part of the 'turnover' - In other words purchased which did not fall u/s. 9 of 1973 Act i.e. which could not be taxed u/s.9 of 1973 Act could not be taxed then u/s. 6 of 1973 Act either - But the 1990 amendment had omitted the Explanation retrospectively - Result was that all purchases were taxable in the State - Contention was an interesting one but it overlooked the effect of s. 2(p) r/w. s. 15 of 1973 Act - Though s. 6 of 1973 Act, as amended, purported to make dealers liable to pay tax on their sales as well as purchases, the Actual charge of tax, u/s. 15 of 1973 Act, was only imposed on the sales and purchases that form part of his taxable turnover - To ascertain what that one had to turn to s. 2(p) of 1973 Act - Definition included, within the definition of 'turnover' the purchase value of goods liable to tax u/s. 9 of 1973 Act but, the goods in question were not laible to tax u/s. 9 of 1973 , not only as contended for the assessees and held by SC but also on the hypothesis as to the invalidity of s. 9(1)(b) of 1973 Act on which the argument on behalf of the Revenue proceeded - Definition also excluded from its purview "the sale proceeds of goods on which purchase tax was leviable under that 1973 Act" and "the purchase value of any goods on the sales of which tax was leviable under 1973 Act" - There could be no dispute that a tax was leviable under 1973 Act on the goods in question when they were sold by a dealer and, indeed, the assessees would have had to pay tax on the sales made to the purchases effected by them but for a claim for exemption u/s. 24 of 1973 Act - Definition of 'turnover' clearly postulated that goods were either to be taxed at the point of purchase or sale and the same transaction could not be taxed as a sale in the hands of the dealer who sells to the assessees and as a purchase in the hands of the assesses - Only exception was the limited class of goods covered by s. 9 of 1973 Act but even that exception was left out with complete retrospective effect - SC did not, therefore, think that State was right in arguing that the purchase tax on the raw materials could be upheld u/s. 6 of 1973 Act itself even if the charge u/s. 9 of 1973 Act failed - Petitions allowed.
