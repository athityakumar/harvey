Automobile Products of India Employees' Union v Association of Engineering Workers, Bombay and Others

Supreme Court of India

27 March 1990

Case Analysis

Bench

P.B. Sawant, Kuldip Singh

Where Reported

1990 Indlaw SC 774; (1990) SCC (L&S) 293; (1990) 2 SCC 444; AIR 1990 SC 1159; 1990 (2) Bom.C.R. 574; 1990 (61) FLR 369; JT 1990 (3) SC 351; 1990 LabIC 935; 1990 (2) LLJ 395; 1990 (2) LLN 269; 1990(1) SCALE 693; [1990] 2 S.C.R. 177; 1990 (4) SLR 764; 1990 (2) UJ 188

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Keywords: Bargaining Agent, Trade Union Act, 1926, Maharashtra Recognition of Trade Unions and Prevention of Unfair Practices Act, 1971

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 227 - Trade Union Act, 1926 - Maharashtra Recognition of Trade Unions and Prevention of Unfair Practices Act, 1971, ss. 12, 13(1)(i) and 14 - Whether the procedure adopted by the Industrial Court for granting recognition to the appellant-union was illegal? - When the applicant-union seeks recognition for itself by displacing the existing recognised union, the applicant-union has to satisfy that not only it had 30% of the membership during the 6 calendar months immediately precediately the calendar months in which it made its application but had also a larger membership during the said period than the membership of the recognised union; what should further be not lost sight of is the paramount fact that it is the membership of the workmen of the union over a period vouched by the relevant documents and not their vote on a particular day which under the Act gives the union its representative character; however overwhelming therefore the vote may be in its favour in a ballot, it will not entitle a union to recognition under the Act and the recognition by ballot or by any method other than that laid down in the Act is, therefore, alien to the Act - Held, the court in present case allowed the parties to circumvent the provisions of the Act and by adopting a simplistic method directed that whoever commanded a majority of votes of the employees voting on a particular day, would be entitled to the status of the recognised union the court also failed to find out whether any of those workers who voted were members of any of the two unions at any time including on the day of the ballot; this is apart from the fact that what has to be found is the exclusive membership of the contesting unions continuously over the specified period, the overlapping membership being ignored and the consent of the parties to follow a procedure which is against the mandatory provisions of the Act, cannot cure the illegality, hence, the Industrial Court's order granting recognition under the Act to the appellant-union by following the method of ballot is prima facie illegal being in breach of the provisions of the Act and the High Court had rightly interfered with the said order - Appeals dismissed.
