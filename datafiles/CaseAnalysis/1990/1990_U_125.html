Major G. S. Sodhi and another v Union of India and another

Supreme Court of India

30 November 1990

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Jayachandra Reddy, S. Ratnavel Pandian

Where Reported

1990 Indlaw SC 598; (1991) SCC (Cr) 357; (1991) 2 SCC 382; AIR 1991 SC 1617; 1991 (1) Crimes 207; 1991 CRLJ 1947; 1991 CRLR 25; JT 1991 (5) SC 55; 1991 (1) LLN 365; 1990(2) SCALE 1173; 1990 (7) SLR 39

Case Digest

Subject: Defence & Security Forces

Keywords: Army Rules 1954

Summary: (A) Service - Army Act, 1950 - Army Rules, 1954m rr. 22 to 25, 96, 97 - Court martial - violation of rules - Legality - Challenged - Petitioner was a Major in the Indian Army, he was charge-sheeted and tried by the court-martial for certain offences and was found guilty and punishment of removal from service was ordered - Hence instant petition - Court-martial proceedings and the order of punishment were questioned on various grounds - Petitioner contended that inquiring officer was prejudiced and biased against petitioner and mala fides were also alleged against him and the initiation of summary of evidence was irregular and illegal as the same was not in conformity with the rr. 22 to 25 of the Rules - Further contended that the general court-martial proceedings were not conducted as per law and that no grounds were made out for court-martial - Whether the enquiry conducted was biased and mala fide and there were irregularities and infirmities in the procedure of enquiry and whether there was gross violation of Rules - Held, aspects of bias and mala fides were concerned SC discussed and held that the plea could not be accepted - SC reiterate that the allegations made against Lt. Col. SKM were not really substantial - SC could not but hold that it was not reasonable on petitioners part to apprehend that the said officer would act in a biased and partisan manner - Enquiry conducted by him was only a preliminary one and he was not the concerned authority for determining the guilt of petitioner - U/r. 96 in certain general and district courts-martial the counsel was allowed if the convening officer declares that it was expedient to allow appearance of counsel - R. 97 prescribed the requirements for appearance of counsel - It appeared that generally it was the defending officer selected by the convening officer who defended accused and the accused was allowed in special cases if the convening officer declared that it was expedient to allow the appearance of the counsel which was exceptional - In instant case SC did not make a roving investigation on said aspect because SC did not find any illegality or irregularity that vitiate the trial nor we find any prejudice having been caused to accused - Under the rules the defending officer so selected was authorised to represent accused and examine and cross-examine the witnesses - All that was done duly in instant case - Therefore SC was unable to agree that prejudice has been caused to petitioner's defence - SC was of the firm view that there was no flagrant violation of any of the provisions of the Act and the Rules dealing with the procedure which has caused prejudice to petitioner - Petition dismissed.

(B) Service - Court martial - violation of rules - Legality - Challenged - Whether there was any violation of rr. 22 to 25 of the Rules dealing with the court of inquiry and recording of summary of evidence - Held, there was no violation of the said rules and at any rate no prejudice has been caused to petitioner in his defence - However, as already noted petitioners who were officers did not exercise their options u/r. 25 of Rules - Therefore the question of violation of rr. 22 to 24 of the Rules in the manner alleged by petitioner did not arise - SC find that in the court-martial which was important, petitioners have duly participated - It should also be borne in mind that the army authorities were entrusted with certain powers and duties under the Act which also enjoined on them certain important responsibilities particularly in the matter of holding the enquiries and trials - Parliament in its wisdom in exercise of its powers u/art. 33 has enacted this law and the officers are to be guided factors like exigencies of service, maintenance of discipline in the army, speedier trial, the nature of the offence and the person against whom the offence and the person against whom the offence was committed - Normally having regard to the high office they hold there should not be any scope to apprehend deliberate lapse or intentional omission on their part - SC did not find any illegalities or material irregularities in the conduct of the trial - Petition dismissed.
