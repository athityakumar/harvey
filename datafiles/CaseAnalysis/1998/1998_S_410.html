Suresh Budharmal Kalani Alias Pappu Kalani v State of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

15 September 1998

Case Analysis

Bench

M.K. Mukherjee, S.S.M. Quadri

Where Reported

1998 Indlaw SC 1193; (1998) 7 SCC 337; (1998) SCC (Cr) 1625; AIR 1998 SC 3258; AIR 1998 SCW 3182; 1998 (5) Bom.C.R. 590; 1998 (4) CCR 7; 1998 (4) Crimes 1; 1998 CRLJ 4592; 1999 (1) JCC 30; JT 1998 (6) SC 371; 1998 (4) RCR(Criminal) 433; 1998(5) SCALE 282; 1998 (7) SLT 545; [1998] Supp1 S.C.R. 608; 1998 (7) Supreme 378

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120B, 212 - Terrorist and Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, ss. 3(3), 3(4), 20A(2) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 161, 173 (2) - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 30 - Armed gang - Murder - Framing of charges - Sustainability - A group of persons armed with pistols and AK 47 assault rifles stormed into ward of hospital and opened fire upon accused admitted there due to injuries sustained earlier resulting in death of accused, two policemen on guard duty besides injuring five others - Incident, according to prosecution was, conspiracy by gangster Dawood Ibrahim to avenge murder of his brother-in-law by members of rival of gang of which deceased accused was member - Charge sheet was filed after investigation and designated court took cognizance of offence and passed impugned Orders of framing charges u/s. 3(3) of the 1987 Act and s. 120B IPC against Kalaniand and u/s. 3(4) of 1987 Act and 212 IPC against Dr. Desai - Hence instant appeals - Whether Order of Trial Court could be sustained - Held, U/s. 30 of 1872 Act confession of accused would be relevant and admissible against co-accused if both were jointly facing trial for same offence - Since, admittedly Dr. Bansal had been discharged from case and would not be facing trial with Kalani his confession should not be used against Kalani - Impugned order showed that Designated Court was fully aware of the legal position but, surprisingly enough, it still decided to rely upon confession on specious ground that prosecution was not in any way precluded from examining Dr. Bansal as witness in trial for establishing facts disclosed in his confession - This again, was perverse approach of Designated Court while dealing with question of farming charges - At that stage the court was required to confine its attention to only those materials collected during investigation which should be legally translated into evidence and not upon further evidence that prosecution might, adduce in trial, which would commence only after charges were framed and accused denied charges - Designated Court was, therefore not at all justified in taking into consideration confessional statement of Dr. Bansal for framing charges against Kalani - Prosecution alleged that knowing fully well that it was medicolegal case Dr. Desai entertained accused Shrikant and arranged for his operation by another doctor at his private hospital and thereby helped Shrikant to abscond after he recuperated - Bare perusal of statement of Desai would make it abundantly clear that it was self exculpatory and hence inadmissible in evidence as 'confession' - Hence, charges framed against appellants were quashed - Appeals allowed.
