Whirlpool Corporation v Registrar Of Trade Marks, Mumbai & Ors

Supreme Court of India

26 October 1998

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Saghir Ahmad, K.T. Thomas

Where Reported

1998 Indlaw SC 13; (1998) 8 SCC 1; AIR 1999 SC 22; AIR 1998 SCW 3345; 1998 ARBLR 553; 1999 (2) Bom.C.R. 70; 1999 (1) CLT 15; JT 1998 (7) SC 243; 1998 (18) PTC 717; 1998 (3) RAJ 483; 1998(5) SCALE 655; [1998] 2 S.C.R. 359; 1998 (8) SLT 380; [1998] Supp2 S.C.R. 359; 1998 (8) Supreme 176

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Intellectual Property

Keywords: Indian Merchandise Marks Act, 1889, Trade Merchandise Marks Act, 1958

Summary: (A) Intellectual Property - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Trade Merchandise Marks Act, 1958, s. 58(4) - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - Availability of alternative remedy - Dismissal of writ petition - Justifiability - Appellant/Company, being a registered Company in USA, applied for registration of its trademark 'Whirlpool' in India - Registration was granted and subsequently, renewed twice for a period of 7 years each up to 1977, however, the registration was not renewed after 1977 - Since further renewal was not obtained after 1977, appellant's trademark was removed from the Register of Trademarks but the appellants continued to publicize their trademark 'Whirlpool' through different modes - A Trust, subsequently, applied for the registration of trademark 'Whirlpool' - Appellant filed objection, however, respondent/Registrar of Trademarks rejected appellant's objection and awarded registration to the Trust - Appellant filed a suit for passing off and temporary injunction against the Trust before the HC - Single Judge granted temporary injunction and which was upheld by the DB in appeal - Appellant, in the meantime, applied for renewal of registration of its trademark - Respondent/Registrar renewed appellant's registration for three successive periods - Trust wrote to respondent/Registrar to cancel the Certificate of Renewal granted to appellant by taking a suo moto action u/s. 58(4) of the Act - Respondent/Registrar issued show cause notice to the appellant as to why the Certificate of Registration be not cancelled - Appellant challenged the show cause notice before the HC - HC dismissed appellant's writ petition - Hence the present appeal - Whether HC was justified in dismissing appellant's writ petition u/art. 226 of the Constitution - Held, power to issue prerogative writs u/art. 226 of the Constitution is plenary in nature and is not limited by other provisions of the Constitution - HC, u/art. 226 of the Constitution, having regard to the facts of the case, has a discretion to entertain or not to entertain a writ petition - HC has imposed upon itself certain restriction one of which is that if an effective and efficacious remedy is available, it would not normally exercise its jurisdiction - However, availability of alterative remedy was not operate as a bar in entertaining writ petition for at least three contingencies, namely, for the enforcement of Fundamental Rights or against the violation of principles of natural justice or the impugned order of proceedings are wholly without jurisdiction or challenge to vires of an Act - Jurisdiction of HC in entertaining a writ petition u/art. 226 of the Constitution, in spite of the alternative statutory remedies, is not affected especially where the authority against whom the writ is filed is shown to have had no jurisdiction or had purported to usurp jurisdiction without any legal foundation - Therefore, HC was not justified in dismissing the writ petition of appellant at the initial stage without examining the question of jurisdiction of the respondent/Registrar - Appeal allowed.

(B) Intellectual Property - Practice & Procedure - Trade and Merchandise Marks Act, 1958, ss. 2(1)(x), 56(4), 109 - Matter pending before the HC - Interference by Registrar of Trademarks - Legality - Whether the Registrar of Trademarks has the jurisdiction issue suo motu show cause notice to appellant for canceling the Certificate of Renewal especially when the matter is pending before the HC - Held, definition of Tribunal u/s. 2(1)(x) in the Act indicates that HC and the Registrar, on their own, are not 'Tribunal', however, they become 'Tribunal' if the proceedings concerned comes to be pending before them - If the proceeding is pending before the Registrar, it would become the 'Tribunal' and if the proceeding is pending before the HC, it would be treated as 'Tribunal' - Jurisdiction of the Registrar and the HC, though apparently concurrent in certain matters, is mutually exclusive - If a particular proceedings is pending before the Registrar, any other proceeding, which may, in any way, related to the pending proceeding, would have to be initiated before and taken up by the Registrar and the HC would act as the Appellate Authority of the Registrar u/s. 109 of the Act - If the proceedings are pending before the HC, the Registrar would keep his hands off and not touch those or any other proceeding which may, in any way, relate to those proceedings as the HC, which has to be the HC having jurisdiction as set out in s. 3 of the Act - Besides being the Appellate Authority of the Registrar, HC has primacy over the Registrar in all matter under the Act - Therefore, Registrar did not have any jurisdiction to issue any suo motu notice to the appellant u/s. 56(4) of the Act for canceling the Certificate of Renewal already granted, in view of pendency of proceedings in the HC - Impugned show cause notice is quashed - Appeal allowed.
