Madan Gopal Bagla v Commissioner of Income Tax, West Bengal

Supreme Court of India

08 May 1956

Case Analysis

Bench

Natwarlal Harilal Bhagwati, Sudhi Ranjan Das, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar

Where Reported

1956 Indlaw SC 26; AIR 1956 SC 571; [1956] 30 ITR 174; 1956 (2) MLJ(SC) 60; 1956 SCJ 668; [1956] 1 S.C.R. 551

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Keywords: Capital Loss, Bad Debt, Borrowing, Trading Loss, Money Lending

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Indian Income Tax Act, 1922 - Bad Debt - Borrowing - Trading Loss - Money Lending - No part of monies borrowed on joint security of appellant and 'M' from Imperial Bank of India - But they were all utilised by 'M' for his own business - Monies were not required to finance timber business of appellant, nor was debt due by 'M' and respect of which account was opened by appellant in his ledger name of 'M' to timber business of appellant - Loss incurred by appellant in transaction of his joining 'M' as surety in loan which 'M' procured from Imperial Bank of India and urged to be treated as a trading loss or bad debt of appellant's business - Business in Bombay used to borrow monies on joint security from Banks in order to facilitate getting financial assistance from Banks and too lower rates of interest - Joined 'M' as surety in loan procured by 'M' from Imperial Bank of India, a transaction in course of carrying on his own timber business - To make loss in transaction a trading loss or a bad debt of timber business of appellant - Old pronotes jointly executed by appellant and others, which were submitted before Appellate Assistant Commissioner did not carry case of appellant far enough and stopped short of proving custom alleged by appellant - Whether loss suffered by appellant in transaction was a capital loss or was a trading loss or a bad debt - Held, debt must be one which can properly be called a trading debt and a debt of trade, profits were computed - Assessee were not a person carrying on a business of standing surety for other persons - Appellant was simply a timber-merchant - There seems to have been some evidence before Appellate Assistant Commissioner that he had from time to time obtained finances for his business by procuring loans on joint security of himself and some other person - Any loss suffered by reason of having to pay a debt borrowed for benefit of another would had been a capital loss to him and not a business loss at all - Any monies had been borrowed by appellant in timber business would certainly had been his capital and whatever loss he incurred therein would have been his capital loss - Appeal dismissed.
