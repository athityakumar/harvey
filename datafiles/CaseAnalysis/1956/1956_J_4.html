Jumman and Others v State of Punjab

Supreme Court of India

15 November 1956

Case Analysis

Bench

P. Govinda Menon, Syed Jaffer Imam

Where Reported

1956 Indlaw SC 130; AIR 1957 SC 469; 1957 CRLJ 586

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Criminal Procedure Code 1898, ss. 107, 151, 374, 375, 376 and 377 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 149, 148, 307, 34, 109, 423 and 300, Exception 4 - Code of Civil Procedure, O.41 - Accused charged with murder and rioting - Sentences confirmed by High Court - (A) Appeal heard on evidence - Reference u/s. 374, CrPC by Sessions Judge, submitting to High Court proceedings before him for confirmation of sentences of death passed by him - In such circumstances case before High Court is a continuation of trial of accused on same evidence and any additional evidence and High Court given power to take fresh evidence - High Court has to satisfy itself as to whether a case beyond reasonable doubt has been made out against accused persons for infliction of penalty of death - If judgment under appeal analyzed, only finding of a meagre narration and a summary of evidence let in before Sessions Court, and judgment of High Court does not seem to have exercised its independent judgment on material facts placed before Court - Held, since High Court has not dealt with appeal as it should have, we proceed to hear appeal on evidence - (B) Whether Appellant 2 and Appellant 3 have a common object along with four other accused? - Opposite factions headed in same direction - For this purpose armed themselves in order to protect themselves - Judgment of Sessions Judge and High Court does not give any detail from which it is possible to find a pre-concerted action on part of accused - Parties were not animated by any pre-concerted intention to commit an offence or bring about a riot - Appellant 1 inflicted injuries and Appellant 2 and Appellant 3 were members of party - No premeditated intention to inflict injuries on prosecution party, thus Appellant 2 and Appellant 3 did not have a common object along with four other accused - Held, that on basis of evidence there was no unlawful assembly in which Appellant 2 and Appellant 3 were members and that being case, they should be acquitted of all offences - (C) Whether in a case where a mutual conflict develops and there is no reliable and acceptable evidence as to how it started and as to who was aggressor, would it be correct to assume private defence for both sides? - Such a situation does not permit plea of private defence on either side and would be a case of sudden fight and conflict and has to be dealt with u/s. 300, IPC, exception 4 - No premeditation and therefore, when contending factions met accidentally and attacked each other, conflict resulted in a sudden fight, in heat of passions, upon a sudden quarrel and without accused having taken undue advantage or acted in a cruel or unusual manner - On finding that both parties had arms, there was no undue advantage taken by either - Held, that hence, exception 4 to s.300, applies, with result that offence is u/s. 304 (Part 1), IPC - Sentence u/s. 302 set aside but convicted u/s. 304 Part 1.
