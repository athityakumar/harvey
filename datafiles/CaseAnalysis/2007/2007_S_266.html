M/S A.P. Products v State Of Andhra Pradesh & Ors

Supreme Court of India

09 July 2007

Case Analysis

Bench

Dalveer Bhandari, Ashok Bhan

Where Reported

2007 Indlaw SC 710; (2007) 6 SCC 365; 2007 (5) ALD(SC) 21; 2007 (214) E.L.T. 485; JT 2007 (9) SC 58; 2007(9) SCALE 66; 2007 (5) SCJ 563; [2007] 8 S.C.R. 126; [2007] 8 VST 373

Case Digest

Subject: Sales Tax

Keywords: Appeals dismissed., Sales Tax Law, Raw Materials, Spices Board Act, 1996

Summary: Sales Tax - Andhra Pradesh General Sales Tax Act, 1957 - Section 2(n), Spices Board Act, 1996 - Exigibility to tax - Masala powder - Appellant engaged in the business of purchasing various spices like Cumin Seed, Fenugreek Seeds, Cinnamon, Caraway Seeds etc. from the registered dealers in the State of Andhra Pradesh - The items were subjected to sales tax at the point of first sale under Entry No.182 of the First Schedule to the Andhra Pradesh General Sales Tax Act, 1957 - Appellant by mixing and grinding all these spices together produced 'masala powder' - Filed income tax returns claiming exemption on the ground that as the ingredients used for the preparation of 'masala powder' were chargeable only at the first sale point, the 'masala powder' is not further exigible to sales tax - Contention rejected by Commercial Taxes Officer - Appeal rejected by Appellate Deputy Commissioner - On further appeal, the Sales Tax Appellate Tribunal rejected the appeal, which was further upheld by the High Court - Appellant submitted that there was no chemical or mechanical process, except simple grinding and mixing involved in producing the 'masala powder', and the activity did not amount to manufacture of a commercially new and distinct commodity liable for payment of tax - Respondents submitted that mixing and grinding of different spices along with other ingredients brings about a commercially different products liable to tax - Ratio in State of Tamil Nadu v. Pyare Lal Malhotra & Others (1976) 1 SCC 834 - Held, ingredients which are used in preparation of masala after grinding and mixing lose their own identity and character and a new product separately known to the commercial world comes into existence - The process of obtaining 'Garam Masala' is not confined to merely grinding of condiments, but involves something more than grinding to bring into existence a product different from those which are mixed - 'Garam Masala' is not excluded by the Rules from the definition of manufacture contained in s. 2(j) - 'Masala powder' prepared after grinding and mixing of various spices and condiments in certain proportion is commercially a different commodity liable to be taxed.
