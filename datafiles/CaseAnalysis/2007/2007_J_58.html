Dilip N. Shroff v Joint Commissioner Of Income Tax, Mumbai & Anr

Supreme Court of India

18 May 2007

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, P.K. Balasubramanyan

Where Reported

2007 Indlaw SC 896; (2007) 6 SCC 329; AIR 2007 SC (Supp) 1280; 2007 (219) E.L.T. 15; [2007] 291 ITR 519; 2007(8) SCALE 304; 2007 (5) SCJ 856; [2007] 7 S.C.R. 499

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Keywords: Tenancy Rights, Taxation Laws (Amendment) Act, 1975

Summary: Income Tax - Sections 28, 271(1)(c), Income Tax Act, 1961 - Penalty - Concealment of income - Appellant is a Hindu undivided family - For the assessment year, an income was declared by it showing a long-term capital loss which arose on account of sale of property being land and building - Appellant contended that the first respondent in the order of assessment, did not record his satisfaction that the assessee had concealed the particulars of his income or furnished inaccurate particulars which were conditions precedent for initiating penalty proceeding under s. 271(1)(c) and as the explanation offered by the assessee was bona fide, no penalty proceeding could have been initiated - Respondent contended that the factors governing concealment of income and furnishing of inaccurate particulars overlap with each other and as such it may not be possible for the authority while issuing notice to specify whether it is a concealment of income or furnishing of inaccurate particulars and existence of mens rea is no longer an essential element for initiating the penalty proceeding - Held, the primary burden of proof is on the Revenue - Once the primary burden of proof is discharged, the secondary burden of proof would shift on the assessee - Before a penalty can be imposed, the entirety of the circumstances must reasonably point to the conclusion that the disputed amount represented income and that the assessee had consciously concealed the particulars of his income or had furnished inaccurate particulars thereof - As the authorities did not arrive at a finding that the consideration amount fixed for the sale of property was wholly inadequate; did not show that what are the inaccurate particulars furnished by the appellant and also did not state that what should have been the accepted principles of valuation, the submissions of the respondent that concealment or furnishing of inaccurate particulars would overlap with each other would not mean that they do not represent different concepts - Impugned order suffers from non-application of mind and is bound to comply with the principles of natural justice - Impugned order set aside.
