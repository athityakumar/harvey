Samar Ghosh v Jaya Ghosh

Supreme Court of India

26 March 2007

Case Analysis

Bench

Dalveer Bhandari, B.N. Agrawal, P.P. Naolekar

Where Reported

2007 Indlaw SC 1000; (2007) 4 SCC 511; 2007 (4) ALD(SC) 11; 2007 (3) ALT 62; 2007 (5) AWC 4820; 2007 (6) Bom.C.R. 834; 2007 (2) G.L.R. 1520; 2007 (2) JCC 1028; JT 2007 (5) SC 569; 2007 (2) MLJ(SC) 1185; 2007 (146) PLR 618; 2007 (2) RCR(Civil) 595; 2007 (2) RCR(Criminal) 515; 2007(5) SCALE 1; [2007] 4 S.C.R. 428

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal

Keywords: Grounds For Divorce, Matrimonial Offence, Local Custom, Divoice Reform Act, 1969, Matrimonial Causes Act, 1973, Divorce and Matrimonial Causes Amendment Act, 1920,

Summary: Family & Personal - Special Marriage Act, 1954 - Hindu Marriage Act, 1955 - Divorce Reform Act, 1969 - Matrimonial Causes Act, 1973 - Divorce and Matrimonial Causes Amendment Act, 1920, - Divorce - Mental cruelty - What constitute mental cruelty? - Held, as a ground for divorce, one spouse's course of conduct (not involving actual violence) that creates such anguish that it endangers life, physical health, or mental health of other spouse - No uniform standard can ever be laid down for guidance, yet we deem it appropriate to enumerate some instances of human behaviour which may be relevant in dealing with cases of 'mental cruelty' - Instances indicated in succeeding paragraphs are only illustrative and not exhaustive - (i) On consideration of complete matrimonial life of parties, acute mental pain, agony and suffering as would not make possible for parties to live with each other could come within broad parameters of mental cruelty - (ii) On comprehensive appraisal of entire matrimonial life of parties it becomes abundantly clear that situation is such that wronged party cannot reasonably be asked to put up with such conduct and continue to live with other party - (iii) Mere coldness or lack of affection cannot amount to cruelty, frequent rudeness of language, petulance of manner, indifference and neglect may reach such a degree that it makes married life for other spouse absolutely intolerable - (iv) Mental cruelty is a state of mind - Feeling of deep anguish, disappointment, frustration in one spouse caused by conduct of other for a long time may lead to mental cruelty - (v) A sustained course of abusive and humiliating treatment calculated to torture, discommode or render miserable life of spouse - (vi) Sustained unjustifiable conduct and behaviour of one spouse actually affecting physical and mental health of other spouse - Treatment complained of and resultant danger or apprehension must be very grave, substantial and weighty - (vii) Sustained reprehensible conduct, studied neglect, indifference or total departure from normal standard of conjugal kindness causing injury to mental health or deriving sadistic pleasure can also amount to mental cruelty - (viii) Conduct must be much more than jealousy, selfishness possessiveness which causes unhappiness and dissatisfaction and emotional upset may not be a ground for grant of divorce on ground of mental cruelty - (ix) Mere trivial irritations, quarrels, normal wear and tear of married life which happens in day to day life would not be adequate for grant of divorce on ground of mental cruelty - (x) Married life should be reviewed as a whole and a few isolated instances over a period of years will not amount to cruelty - Ill-conduct must be persistent for a fairly lengthy period, where relationship has deteriorated to an extent that because of acts and behaviour of a spouse, wronged party finds it extremely difficult to live with other party any longer, may amount to mental cruelty - (xi) If a husband submits himself for an operation of sterilization without medical reasons and without consent or knowledge of his wife and similarly if wife undergoes vasectomy or abortion without medical reason or without consent or knowledge of her husband, such an act of spouse may lead to mental cruelty - (xii) Unilateral decision of refusal to have intercourse for considerable period without there being any physical incapacity or valid reason may amount to mental cruelty - (xiii) Unilateral decision of either husband or wife after marriage not to have child from marriage may amount to cruelty - (xiv) Where there has been a long period of continuous separation, it may fairly be concluded that matrimonial bond is beyond repair - Marriage becomes a fiction though supported by a legal tie - By refusing to sever that tie, law in such cases, does not serve sanctity of marriage, on contrary, it shows scant regard for feelings and emotions of parties - In such like situations, it may lead to mental cruelty - Taking into consideration aforementioned factors along with an important circumstance that parties are admittedly living separately for more than sixteen and half years (since 27-8-1990) irresistible conclusion would be that matrimonial bond has been ruptured beyond repair because of mental cruelty caused by respondent - High Court seriously erred in reversing judgment of learned Addl. District Judge which is based on carefully watching demeanor of parties and their respective witnesses and ratio and spirit of judgments of this Court and other Courts - Consequently, impugned judgment of HC is set aside - Appeal disposed of.
