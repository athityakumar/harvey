Rajesh Kumar Singh v High Court Of Judicature Of Madhya Pradesh, Bench Gwalior

Supreme Court of India

31 May 2007

Case Analysis

Bench

R.V. Raveendran, Lokeshwar Singh Panta

Where Reported

2007 Indlaw SC 1650; AIR 2007 SC 2725; 2007 CRLJ 3753; JT 2007 (9) SC 92; 2007 (3) RCR(Civil) 502; 2007 (3) RCR(Criminal) 694; 2007(9) SCALE 44; [2007] 7 S.C.R. 869

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Practice & Procedure - Advocates & Judges - Contempt of Courts Act, 1971 - Contempt of court - Magistrate lodged a complaint against 'A' with the IG of Police alleging that 'A' threatened a Judicial Officer in court and interrupted the court proceedings - IG of Police forwarded the complaint to the Superintendent of Police for inquiry and necessary disciplinary action who, in turn, sent it to appellant with a direction to hold an inquiry and submit a detailed report - Appellant recorded the statements of several witnesses in the course of such inquiry and recorded the statements made by the witnesses that 'A' had not misbehaved with the Magistrate - Appellant found 'A' guilty of misbehaving in Court and recommended his punishment - Subsequently, HC directed notices to be issued to the IG of Police and the appellant, to show cause why they should not be punished for contempt of court, for having enquired into the conduct of a Judge, without the permission of the HC and recording the statements made by the witnesses that 'A' had not misbehaved with the Magistrate, thereby contradicting the record made by the Magistrate - HC dropped the proceedings against the IG of Police but chose to proceed against the appellant - HC held that holding an inquiry in respect of the conduct of 'A' amounted to holding an inquiry into the conduct of the Magistrate and that was not permissible without the permission of the HC - HC imposed the punishment of seven days' simple imprisonment and fine of Rs. 2,000 - Hence, instant appeal - (A) Whether it can be said, that recording the statements of 'A' and several other persons in the course of the preliminary inquiry, amounts to holding an inquiry in regard to the conduct of a Judge? - Held, it was only in pursuance of directive from his superiors, the appellant held a preliminary inquiry in respect of the conduct of 'A' - As the inquiry was against 'A' the appellant had to give an opportunity to him, to make his statement - He also had to record the statements of persons, whom 'A' stated were present at the time of the incident - The inquiry by the appellant was a prelude to the disciplinary action against 'A' - Appellant submitted a report holding 'A' guilty of having used unwarranted language in court and recommending punishment - It cannot therefore, be said, that recording the statements of 'A' and several other persons in the course of the preliminary inquiry, amounts to holding an inquiry in regard to the conduct of a Judge - (B) Whether the appellant was guilty of contempt? - Held, relied on SC judgment in Security and Finance (P) Ltd, v. Dattatraya Raghav Agge 1970 CriLJ 756 wherein it was held that an authority holding an inquiry in good faith in exercise of the powers vested in it by a statute was not guilty of contempt of Court, merely because a parallel enquiry was imminent or pending before a Court - Hence, act of appellant holding the preliminary inquiry, could not be considered to be contempt of court - (C) Whether obtaining the permission of HC was necessary? - Held, when appellant held the preliminary inquiry, no contempt proceedings had been initiated by the HC and there was also no other proceedings pending before the Magistrate or any other court in regard to the said incident - Therefore, the question of seeking or obtaining the permission of HC or other court, for holding such inquiry, did not arise - (D) Whether recording the statements of some persons, amounted to scandalizing the court, if those statements were contrary to the report of the incident contained in the order-sheet? - Held, attributing improper motive to a Judge or scurrilous abuse of a Judge would amount to scandalizing the court - Appellant neither attributed any improper motive to the Judge, nor abused the Judge - Appellant submitted a report holding 'A' had used unwarranted language in court and that he should be punished - It could not, therefore, be said that appellant recorded the statements of witnesses with an ulterior motive of helping 'A' to create a false defence - There was no material to show that the appellant acted with any ulterior motive - Appellant was not guilty of contempt of court - Appeal allowed.
