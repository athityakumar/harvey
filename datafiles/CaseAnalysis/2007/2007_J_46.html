State of Uttar Pradesh and Others v Jeet S. Bisht and Another

Supreme Court of India

18 May 2007

Case Analysis

Bench

Markandey Katju

Where Reported

2007 Indlaw SC 949; (2007) 6 SCC 586; 2007 (4) ALJ 548; 2007 (4) AWC 3241; 2007 (1) CCC 234; 2007 (2) CPR 506; JT 2008 (8) SC 59; 2008 (4) MLJ(SC) 551; 2007(8) SCALE 35; [2007] 7 S.C.R. 705

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Consumer Protection

Keywords: Affirmative Action, Comprehensive, Right To Be Informed

Summary: Consumer Protection - Constitution - Administrative Tribunals Act, 1985 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 21, 229, 254 and 50 - Consumer Protection Act, 1986, art. 142, ss. 10(3), 16, 16(2) and 9 - Grievance of Writ Petitioner was of charging excessive electricity bills, by the State Electricity Board - Hence, the present appeal - Whether the court could make binding laws to effectuate the functioning of the Consumer Forum - Held, that the Central and State Govts., were requested to consider fixing adequate salaries and allowances for members of the Consumers Fora at all three levels, so that they could function effectively and with a free mind, were also requested to fill up vacancies expeditiously so that the Fora could function effectively - When Parliament in its wisdom had nominated a particular authority, in the present case the State Govt., to fix the salaries and allowances of the members of the State and District Fora, the Court could not override the clear language of the statute and substitute the words 'the SC' for the words 'the State Govt.' u/s. 10(3) and s. 16(2), of the 1986 Act - It was a well settled principle of interpretation that the Court could not add or substitute words in a statute - No doubt the Court could make a recommendation to the State Govts., that the salaries and allowances of the members of the State and District Fora were inadequate and should be increased, but that was about as far as the Court could go - By a judicial verdict the court could not amend the law made by Parliament or the State Legislature - Judiciary should therefore exercise self-restraint and eschew the temptation to encroach into the domain of the legislature or the administrative or statutory authorities - Appeal allowed.
