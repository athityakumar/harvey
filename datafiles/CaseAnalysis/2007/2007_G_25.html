Geejaganda Somaiah v State of Karnataka

Supreme Court of India

12 March 2007

Case Analysis

Bench

Arijit Pasayat, Lokeshwar Singh Panta

Where Reported

2007 Indlaw SC 1554; (2007) 9 SCC 315; AIR 2007 SC 1355; 2007 (2) CalLT(SC) 86; 2007 CRLJ 1792; JT 2007 (4) SC 380; 2007 (3) RCR(Criminal) 255; 2007(4) SCALE 314; [2007] 3 S.C.R. 899

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 14 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 106, 114, 114(a), 114(s), 25, 26, 27 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 34 - Presumption - Circumstantial Evidence - Sustainability - Judgment rendered by DB of HC was challenged dismissing appeal filed by appellant questioning correctness of conviction recorded by Fast Track Court - Court found appellant guilty of offence punishable u/s. 302 IPC and sentenced to undergo imprisonment for life - Trial Court on consideration of evidence on record found appellant guilty - Co-accused was given benefit of doubt and order of acquittal was recorded - Evidence was circumstantial in nature, as there were no eye witnesses to actual assault - HC found that circumstances were conclusive to prove guilt of accused and, therefore, confirmed conviction and sentence and dismissed appeal - Hence, instant appeal - Whether order passed by HC was justified - Held, Court where case rests squarely on circumstantial evidence inference of guilt could be justified only when all incriminating facts and circumstances were found to be incompatible with innocence of accused or guilt of any other person - Circumstances from which inference as to guilt of accused was drawn had to be proved beyond reasonable doubt and have to be shown to be closely connected with principal fact sought to be inferred from those circumstances - In instant case disclosure statements were made by accused persons on next day of commission of offence and property of deceased was recovered at their instance from places where they had kept such properties, on same day - Appeal dismissed.
