Kanwarjit Singh Dhillon v Hardyal Singh Dhillon and Others

Supreme Court of India

12 October 2007

Case Analysis

Bench

Tarun Chatterjee, Dalveer Bhandari

Where Reported

2007 Indlaw SC 1419; (2007) 11 SCC 357; AIR 2008 SC 306; 2008 (1) CTC 80; JT 2007 (13) SC 50; 2008 (1) MLJ(SC) 527; 2007 (4) RCR(Civil) 733; 2007(12) SCALE 282; 2007 (6) SCJ 43; 2007 (8) SCJ 49; [2007] 11 S.C.R. 368

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Summary: Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 14 r. 2 - Hindu Succession Act, 1956 - Eight years after the death of the father of the appellant, respondent no. 1 claimed his ownership over the moveable and immoveable property executed in his favor through a will, left by the deceased - Appellant contended that the said will was forged and fabricated - Civil court held that since the appellant had not challenged the probate proceedings, the present court would have no jurisdiction to entertain the suit and so the suit was dismissed - Hence, the present appeal - Whether the Civil Court had no jurisdiction to entertain the suit - Held, that the HC as well as the Civil Court had acted illegally and with material irregularity in the exercise of their jurisdiction in dismissing the suit on the aforesaid preliminary issue by holding that after the probate having been granted by the competent probate court and affirmed by this Court, the Civil Court had no jurisdiction to proceed with the suit - Plaint clearly stated that the civil suit was for a declaration to the effect that the suit properties were joint Hindu family properties of the HUF, that by utilizing the income from the ancestral agricultural land, various properties including the suit properties were acquired, such being the allegations made in the plaint which could only be decided on trial after parties were permitted to adduce evidence in respect of their respective claims, it was difficult to hold that only because probate of the will of the deceased had been granted, the suit for title and injunction should be held to be not maintainable in law - Appeal allowed. .
