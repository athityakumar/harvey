Bharat Sewa Sansthan v Uttar Pradesh Electronics Corporation Limited

Supreme Court of India

29 August 2007

Case Analysis

Bench

Lokeshwar Singh Panta, H.K. Sema

Where Reported

2007 Indlaw SC 1580; (2007) 7 SCC 737; AIR 2007 SC 2961; 2007 (69) ALR 517; 2007 (4) AWC 3565; JT 2007 (1) SC 463; 2007 (4) RCR(Civil) 98; 2007 (2) RCR(Rent) 315; 2007(10) SCALE 446; [2007] 9 S.C.R. 533

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR; Constitution; Practice & Procedure

Summary: (A) Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, s. 8 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 11 r. 14 - Arbitration - Reference to - Appellant leased suit premises to respondent/Corporation - Since respondent was not ready to pay the increased rent, appellant filed a suit for eviction and for recovery of arrears of rent - Respondent filed an application u/s. 8(1) of the Act for referring the matter to arbitration and another application u/O. 11 r. 14 of CPC for summoning the original lease deed from the custody of appellant - Appellant contended that the original lease deed was not in their possession - Trial Court dismissed both the applications of respondent - However, HC directed Trial Court to refer the matter to arbitration - Held, respondent-Corporation placed on record of the Trial Court photocopies of the agreements along with an application u/s. 8(1) of the Act - HC has rightly held that the photocopies of the lease agreements could be taken on record u/s. 8 of the Act for ascertaining the existence of arbitration clause and thus, the dispute raised by the appellant against the respondent in terms of the arbitration clause contained in the lease agreement is arbitral - No any perversity or infirmity in the order of the HC to warrant any interference - Appeal dismissed.

(B) Practice & Procedure - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 142 - Enforcement of decree or order of SC - Jurisdiction - Held, nature and ambit of the power of SC u/art. 142 of the Constitution is meant to do complete justice between the litigating parties but at the same time SC has to bear in mind that the power is conceived to meet the situations which cannot be effectively and appropriately tackled by the existing provisions of law - Human and equitable approach should be balanced to do complete justice to both the parties and not be tilted in favour of either party without ignoring the statutory provisions - SC in exercise of its jurisdiction can grant appropriate relief where there is some manifest illegality, or where there is manifest want of jurisdiction, or where some palpable injustice is shown to have resulted to the parties - However, SC would not be obliged to bypass the provisions of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 in exercise of its power and jurisdiction u/art. 142 of the Constitution - Appeal dismissed.
