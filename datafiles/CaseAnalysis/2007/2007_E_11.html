Southern Petrochemical Industries Company Limited v Electricity Inspector and E.T.I.O. and Others

Supreme Court of India

15 May 2007

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, Markandey Katju

Where Reported

2007 Indlaw SC 1002; (2007) 5 SCC 447; AIR 2007 SC 1984; 2007 (3) CTC 273; JT 2007 (7) SC 613; 2007 (4) MLJ(SC) 723; 2007(7) SCALE 392; [2007] 6 S.C.R. 955

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Gold (Control) Act, 1968, Tamil Nadu Electricity (Taxation on Consumption) Act, 1962, Constitutionality, Tamil Nadu Tax on Consumption or Sale of Electricity Act, 2003, Tamil Nadu Electricity Duty Act, 1939, Parliament enacted the Electricity (Supply) Act, 1948, Interpretation Act, 1978, Environmental Protection Act 1990, Life Insurance Corporation (Modification of Settlements) Act, 1976, Punjab Electricity (Duty) Rules, 1958

Summary: Constitution - Electricity & Energy - Tamil Nadu Tax on Consumption or Sale of Electricity Act, 2003, ss. 3, 4, 20, 21, 12 and 14 - Government of India Act, 1935, item. 48(B), list. II, sch. VII, s. 154(A) - Constitution of India, 1950, list. III, sch. VII, arts. 288, 14, 265, 246, 245 and 254(2) - Tamil Nadu Electricity Duty Act, 1939, s. 3 - Indian Electricity Act, 1910, ss. 3, 5, 6 and 10(b) - Parliament enacted the Electricity (Supply) Act, 1948, s. 12 - Tamil Nadu Electricity (Taxation on Consumption) Act, 1962, ss. 3, 13, 14 and 12 - Electricity Regulatory Commissions Act, 1998, s. 29 - Validity and/or application of Tamil Nadu Tax on Consumption or Sale of Electricity Act, 2003 is in question in the appeal - Whether the provisions of s. 14 of the 2003 Act should be read in such a manner so as to make it in consonance with Article 14 of the Constitution? - S. 14 is loosely worded - The premise on which the said submission was made is that electricity cannot be stored - The 2003 Act was enacted not only to consolidate but also to rationalize the Act - There is no constitutional or statutory embargo that a consolidating Act must also be an amending Act - When different terms are used in the new Act, it would not be proper for the Court to refer to the provisions of a repealed statute - Appeal allowed
