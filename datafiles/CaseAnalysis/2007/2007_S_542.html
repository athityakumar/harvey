Shajahan and Others v State of Kerala and Another

Supreme Court of India

26 February 2007

Case Analysis

Bench

Arijit Pasayat, R.V. Raveendran

Where Reported

2007 Indlaw SC 656; (2007) 12 SCC 96; AIR 2007 SC (Supp) 186; 2007 CRLJ 2291; 2007 (2) RCR(Criminal) 503; 2007(3) SCALE 618; [2007] 3 S.C.R. 212

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Self-Defence, Civil Case, Right Of Self-Defence, Inimical Witness, Non-Explanation, Injuries On Accused

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34, 96, 97, 98, 99, 100, 101, 102, 105, 302, 304 II, 323 and 324 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 105 - (A) Whether non explanation of injuries, results in automatic rejection of prosecutions case? - Held, non-explanation of injuries sustained by accused may assume greater importance where defence gives a version which competes in probability with that of prosecution - Where evidence is clear, cogent and creditworthy and Court can distinguish truth from falsehood mere fact that injuries are not explained by prosecution cannot by itself be a sole basis to reject such evidence - If witnesses examined on behalf of prosecution are believed by Court in proof of guilt of accused beyond reasonable doubt, question of obligation of prosecution to explain injuries sustained by accused will not arise - (B) Whether High Court was right in accepting, that accused was acting in exercise of right to private defence? - Right of private defence is essentially a defence right circumscribed by governing statute available only when circumstances clearly justify it - Further held, that in guise of self-preservation, what really has been done is to assault original aggressor, even after cause of reasonable apprehension has disappeared, plea of right of private-defence can legitimately be negatived - Commensurate with danger apprehended to him where assault is imminent by use of force, it would be lawful to repel force in self-defence and right of private-defence commences, as soon as threat becomes so imminent - Batu Singh v. State of Punjab (13161991 Indlaw SC 47) - Burden of proof is on accused, who sets up plea of self-defence, and, in absence of proof, it is not possible for Court to presume truth of plea of self-defence - It is a right of defence, not of retribution, expected to repel unlawful aggression and not as retaliatory measures - Appeal dismissed.
