Gurdit Singh and Others v Munsha Singh and Others

Supreme Court of India

29 November 1976

Case Analysis

Bench

Jaswant Singh, A.N. Ray, M. Hameedullah Beg

Where Reported

1976 Indlaw SC 411; (1977) 1 SCC 791; AIR 1977 SC 640; 1977 HLR 122; [1977] 2 S.C.R. 250

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Punjab Limitation (Customs) Act, 1920, s. 5, sch., art. 2 - Limitation Act, 1908, s. 14(1) - Punjab Customs (Power to Contest) Act, 1920, ss. 4 and 7 - Declaratory decree obtained by predecessors-in-interest of plaintiffs in 1902 - Sale by a person on death of his adoptive son ( who left a son to succeed him) - Revisionary interest holder got decree to the effect that his interest will not be affected by sale - That adoptive son's line extict on his death - And Adoptive son's son not traceable - (A) Declaratory decree in 1902 - Suits for possession filed in 1945 - Dismissed in 1951 - As premature with finding that factum of death of adoptive son's son had not been established - Subsequent suits 1952 and 1953 alleging his non traceability - (1) Applicability of s. 108 of Evidence Act - (2) Entitlement to benefits u/s.14 (1) of Limitation Act - (B) Amendment of s. 7 of Punjab Customs (Power to Contest) Act in 1973 - In view of saving clause in s. 4, amendment to s. 7 is not retrospective - Effect on Declaratory decree - Held, in absence of proof of death, it could not be said that the present suits had been brought within three years of the accrual of the right to sue - Even assuming death within seven years of the institution of the present suits, benefit of s.14 of Limitation Act could not be allowed - Amendment, held did not have the effect of abrogating the declaratory decree and alienation in 1885 in any case, not at all affected by Punjab Customs (Power to Contest) Act - Appeals dismissed .
