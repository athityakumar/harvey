S. Abdul Karim and Others v M. K. Prakash And Ors

Supreme Court of India

30 January 1976

Case Analysis

Bench

R.S. Sarkaria, M. Hameedullah Beg, P.N. Bhagwati

Where Reported

1976 Indlaw SC 92; (1976) SCC (Cr) 217; (1976) 1 SCC 975; AIR 1976 SC 859; 1976 CRLJ 643; 1976 CrLR(SC) 102; [1976] 3 S.C.R. 276

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: (A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 143, 147 and 506 - Contempt of Courts Act, 1952, s. 3 - Criminal contempt - R1 presented a petition through R2, to police, alleging that the accused persons (P and his men) were likely to trespass to remove her timber - There was an apprehension that P and his men would demolish the sheds - Case u/ss. 143, 147 and 506 IPC was registered against 'P' and others - R1 made an application, before Magistrate/R3 praying that the seized logs be handed over to him - Thereafter, 'P' also made an application to R3 claiming the timber to be his property and prayed for delivery of its possession to him - R3 passed an order, directing the Forest Range officer to keep the logs in his custody pending further investigation by the Police - Against the said order 'P' filed revision petition in HC - While the revision was pending before HC - Police officer/R4, after completing the investigation, obtained the opinion of the Assistant Public Prosecutor and submitted a Final Report to R3 - Upon that report R3 passed order to return timber logs to R1 - 'P' made a petition in HC complaining that R1 to R5 had committed contempt of HC within the meaning of s. 3 of the 1952 Act - HC convicted appellants/R1 to R5 - Hence instant appeals - Whether such an order was made by the HC and had been shown to R3 before he made the order for urgent delivery of the timber - Held, reply given by R3 was at least, sufficient to dispel the suspicion that in making the order in regard to the delivery of the timber to R1 he was actuated by a motive to impede or obstruct or defeat the course of justice - Notoriety of the case looming large in their minds, the HC without due consideration rather hastily rejected the explanation of R3 that he had directed, urgent delivery of the timber to R1 because on seeing the copy of the HC's order, which was shown to him, he was of the opinion that such a course was indicated therein - Appeals disposed of.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 143, 147 and 506 - Delivery of timber - Opportunity to be heard - Whether order made by the HC and had been shown to the Magistrate before he made the order for urgent delivery of the timber - Held, rather, the order whereby P's request for ad-interim stay or injunction with regard to these logs was declined by the HC, could have induced R3 to go ahead with the making of the ex-parte final order in regard to the delivery of the logs to R1 - It was true that the Magistrate was aware that P's criminal revision petition against his interim order, was then pending in the HC - In such a situation, prudent course for him was to postpone the making of any final order in regard to' the delivery of timber till the final disposal of the revision petition by the HC - It would also have been proper for him to issue notice to 'P' and give an opportunity of being heard before making any order - Appeals disposed of.

(C) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 143, 147 and 506 - Improper motive - Pending revision case - Whether R3 deliberately did not follow the prudent course or whether he misdirected himself owing to an error of judgment - Held, stark circumstances viz. that HC had declined to issue any interim injunction or stay order in favour of 'P' in the criminal revision pending before it - That there was an observation in the HC's order, stressing the need for speedy removal of the cut timber and the possibility of its being damaged by in-coming rainy season, that he was labouring under the impression, though wrongly, that the order, was merely an interim order which had exhausted itself on completion of police investigation and presentation of the Final Report by the police in which there was a positive finding that the timber belonged to R1 and R2 and they were entitled to its restoration-taken in their totality, go to show that in making the wrong order regarding delivery of the timber - R3 was not actuated by any improper motive or deliberate design to thwart, impede, obstruct or interfere with the course of justice or to circumvent or defeat the proceedings in revision pending before the HC - In the absence of any mens rea, R3 had at the most committed only a technical contempt of the HC - R3's appeal was allowed and his conviction and sentence was set aside - No conviction for contempt of court was recorded against appellants in the companion appeals by the HC - In R1 and R2 case was that the High Court had made sweeping observations with regard to the civil rights, which might prejudice them in establishing their claims by a regular suit - Appeals disposed of.
