Lakshmi Singh and Others v State of Bihar

Supreme Court of India

10 September 1976

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Murtaza Fazal Ali, P.N. Bhagwati

Where Reported

1976 Indlaw SC 477; (1976) 4 SCC 394; (1976) SCC (Cr) 671; AIR 1976 SC 2263; 1976 (13) AllCriC 372; 1976 CRLJ 1736; 1977 CRLR 76

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 148, 149, 302, 324 and 326 - Murder - Conviction - Challenged - Appellant and his brother were cousins of deceased persons - Two families were living together in the same house in different portions - There was a dispute over property - As per prosecution appellant assaulted deceased and thereafter all the accused persons fled away - Chargesheet was filed against the seven accused -Appellant convicted u/s. 302 of IPC and all the other accused were convicted u/s. 302/149 of IPC - Appeals filed thereagainst dismissed by HC - Hence, present appeals - (A) Non-explanation of the injuries by the prosecution - Effect thereof - Held, in a murder case, the non-explanation of the injuries sustained by the accused at about the time of the occurrence or in the course of altercation is a very important circumstances from which the court can draw the following inferences:(1) that the prosecution has suppressed the genesis and the origin of the occurrence and has thus not presented true version; (2) that the witnesses who have denied the presence of the injuries on the person of the accused are lying on a most material point and therefore their evidence is unreliable; (3) that in case there is a defence version which explains the injuries on the person of the accused it is rendered probable so as to throw doubt on the prosecution case - Further, omission on the part of the prosecution to explain the injuries on the person of the accused assumes much greater importance where the evidence consists of interested or inimical witnesses or where the defence gives a version which competes in probability with that of the prosecution one - In the present case, when it is held, as it must be, that the appellant received serious injuries which have not been explained by the prosecution, then it will be difficult for the court to rely on the evidence of PWs 1 to 4 and 6, more particularly, when some of these witnesses have lied by stating that they did not see any injuries on the person of the accused - As the appellant had a grievous injury, it cannot be said that the accused had no right of private defence - Thus HC was, therefore, in error in brushing aside this serious infirmity in the prosecution case on unconvincing premises - (B) Omission on the part of the prosecution to send the bloodstained earth found at the place of occurrence for chemical examination - Held, in almost all criminal cases, the bloodstained earth found from the place of occurrence is invariably sent to the Chemical Examiner and his report along with the earth is produced in the court, and yet this is one exceptional case where this procedure was departed from for reasons best known to the prosecution - This also, therefore, shows that the defence version may be true - (C) Conviction - Held, in view of the inherent improbabilities, the serious omissions and infirmities, the interested or inimical nature of the evidence, prosecution failed to prove the case against the appellants beyond reasonable doubt - Convictions and sentences passed on the appellants set aside - Appeals allowed.
