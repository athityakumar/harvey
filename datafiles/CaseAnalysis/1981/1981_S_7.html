 S.P. Gupta v Union Of India & Anr.

Supreme Court of India

30 December 1981

Case Analysis

Bench

P.N. Bhagwati, A.C. Gupta, D.A. Desai, E.S. Venkataramiah, R.S. Pathak, S. Murtaza Fazal Ali, V.D. Tulzapurkar

Where Reported

1981 Indlaw SC 599; (1981) Supp SCC 87; AIR 1982 SC 149; 1981(4) SCALE 1974A; [1982] 2 S.C.R. 365

Case Digest

Subject: Environment, Wildlife & Animal

Keywords: Public Nuisance, Constituent Assembly, Right To Know

Summary: Constitution of India, 1950 - Questions raised - (A) Validity of the transfer of Hon'ble Chief Justice of Patna High Court to the Madras HC - (B) Validity of non-extension of the term of Addl. Judge of the Delhi HC - (C) Validity of the circular of the Union Law Minister to all States D/- 18-03-1981 - Major issues - (1) Locus standi of lawyers to file writ petitions by way of public interest litigation - (2) Independence of Judiciary - Circular by Law minister, transferring Hon'ble Chief Justice of Patna HC and the non-extension of the term of Addl. Judge were alleged to be encroachments on judiciary - (3) Validity of Union Law Minister's circular D/- 18-03-1981 - (4) Nature of power to appoint/transfer HC Judges and procedure to be followed - Consultation with constitutional functionaries - Consent of concerned Judges etc. - (5) Validity of transfer of Judges from one HC to another - Matters to be taken into consideration, viz. public interest, personal inconvenience, regional language, consent of concerned Judge etc. - (6) Other incidental legal aspects like privileged documents etc. - For (A), (B) and (C) : A seven Judges Constitutional bench upheld the three questions by majority through a momentous verdict - (Note - A reasonably long and nicely considered judgment) - On the major issues Supreme Court held, that - (1) Held, lawyers had interest and locus standi to file the petitions and they could not be told off at the gates - (2) The power to appoint Judges is executive in nature and the President is bound by the advice of the Cabinet by virtue of art. 74 - Concept of independence of judiciary, its scope and limitations are discussed in the context of the procedure followed in India in the matter of appointment of Judges - (3) Validity of the circular is upheld - (4) Consultation with constitutional functionaries must be meaningful and result oriented and that the proposal can emanate from any of the constitutional functionaries - It is also unanimously agreed that none of the constitutional functionaries can exercise veto in this matter - (5) There is unanimity that transfer of a Judge from one HC to another must be in public interest and not by way of punishment and prior consent of the Judge concerned was held not necessary - (6) Privilege in matter of production of certain documents by Govt. held that no privilege could be claimed in respect of documents which constituted material for forming the opinions in the matter of appointment/transfer of Judges - Other legal points considered while discussing the main issues - Petition dismissed
