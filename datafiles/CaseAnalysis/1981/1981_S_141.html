Ram Ashrit and Others v State of Bihar

Supreme Court of India

13 January 1981

Case Analysis

Bench

R.S. Pathak, R.S. Sarkaria

Where Reported

1981 Indlaw SC 116; (1981) 2 SCC 60; (1981) SCC (Cr) 324; AIR 1981 SC 942; 1981 BBCJ 57; 1981 BLJ 322; 1983 (1) Crimes 130; 1981 CRLJ 484; 1981 CRLR 177

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Conviction, Cross-Case, Cross-Complaint, Sustainability, Dacoity with murder

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 396 - Dacoity with murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellants (accused) allegedly committed offence u/s. 396 of IPC - Trial Court convicted appellants u/s. 396 of IPC and sentenced them to undergo imprisonment for life and in another case initiated on complaint of DW1 for offence u/ss. 307, 342 of IPC - Trial Court acquitted the appellants - Aggrieved State and appellants filed appeals before HC - Appeal filed against conviction of appellants was dismissed - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, all material prosecution witness who testified with regard to the occurrence, are interrelated or otherwise interested in the prosecution. The story that the appellants came armed and committed dacoity and caused the injuries to persons of the complainant party whilst committing dacoity is not true. The surrounding circumstances, negative as well as positive suggest that there was a fight between both the parties in the course of which members of both the parties received injuries and that the cause of the fight was not dacoity, but probably the dispute over the mango grove which had been decided by the Court concerned in favour of those appellants, who are descendants of original owner of a plot. There are many other infirmities and contradictions in the evidence of the prosecution witnesses. In the absence of corroboration to a material extent in all material particulars, it was extremely hazardous to convict the appellants on the basis of the testimony of these highly interested, inimical and partisan witnesses, particularly when it bristles with improbable versions and material infirmities. Impugned conviction of appellant is set aside. Appeal allowed.
