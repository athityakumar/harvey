State of Assam and Others v Banshidhar Shewbhagwan and Company

Supreme Court of India

01 September 1981

Case Analysis

Bench

A. Varadarajan, V.D. Tulzapurkar

Where Reported

1981 Indlaw SC 193; (1981) 4 SCC 283; AIR 1981 SC 1957; 1981(3) SCALE 1803; [1982] 1 S.C.R. 554; 1981 UJ 772

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Defence & Security Forces

Keywords: Defence Regulations

Summary: Defence & Security Forces - Administrative - Constitution of India, 1950, art.226 - Defence of India Act, 1962, s.29(i) - Requisition of land - For carrying out defence activities - Sustainability - Appeal by special leave was by the respondents against the Judgment and order passed by the DB of the HC allowing the Writ petition - That writ petition was filed u/art. 226 of the Constitution for quashing the order of requisition issued by the Deputy Commissioner, the second appellant in this appeal and the respondent No.1 in the writ petition-under Memo dt. 25-10-1966 - Memo was issued in exercise of the powers conferred by s. 29 (i) of Act r/w. the Notification of the Govt. of India, Ministry of Home Affairs dt. 10-6-1965 in respect of the properties described in the schedule attached thereto viz. X Tea Estate and Y Beta Tea Estates, situate in Z District on the ground that the lands were necessary for securing the defence of the country and efficient conduct of military operations - Whether order of requisition of land for defence purpose was sustainable - Held, it was not disputed before SC that the war with Pakistan started in June 1965 - SC could even take judicial notice of that fact - Impugned requisition order was passed on 25-10-1966 - Therefore, it could not be stated that there was no need of the land for defence purposes in October 1966 from the mere fact that Deputy Minister for Defence had stated in his letter dt. 20-12-1962 that the current lease could not be extended because the land was required for defence purposes and the Minister for Defence had stated in his letter dt. 1-4-1963 that as several tea planters had evinced interest in the land it would be in the public interest to auction the leasehold right in the land on a yearly basis alone subject to the condition that it could be resumed at a short notice for defence purposes - That letter of the Minister for Defence did not altogether rule out the possibility of the land being required for defence purposes at any time and being made available for those purposes at short notice - Defence requirements might change from time to time depending upon various factors including intelligence reports about the enemy's movements and preparations for war - HC had held in favour of the respondent on the question of want of bonafides on the part of the appellants on the basis that in the aforesaid title suits filed by the respondent it was not pleaded by the appellants that the land was required for defence purposes - HC appeared to have accepted the submission made before them on behalf of the respondent in appeal that no such plea had been raised in the pleadings in the title suits filed by the respondent - That submission was incorrect, and it was unfortunate that the attention of Judges had not been invited to the material on record to show that such a contention was in fact put forward by appellant No.4 in his pleading in the title suits - In the auction notice dt. 20-3-1963 itself it was stated that the lease would be subject to the condition that whenever the Govt. needed the land for defence purposes it would be determined by issue of notice giving 30 days time without payment of any compensation - In the written statement filed by appellant No.4 in the Title Suit, it was stated in respect of the allegation made in plaint in that suit that since the land was required for defence purposes the defendant was not bound to renew the lease and that even in Title Suit the defendants had filed written statement contesting the claim of the respondent/plaintiff - It was also stated in that written statement in regard to the allegations made in plaint that the land was required for defence purposes - In regard to the allegations in paragraph 26 of the plaint it was contended in the written statement that the land was required for defence purposes and that any lease under the present emergency would be detrimental to the interests of the defence of the country - Even in the counter affidavit filed in the application for interim injunction moved in the Title Suit the appellant No.4 had stated that the land was required for defence purposes, and there was no question of holding any auction for lease of the land, that if the order of interim injunction was not vacated the defence preparation of the country would be hampered as the land was urgently needed for defence purposes and the interest of the nation would suffer, that no irreparable loss or damage which could not be compensated in money would result from vacating the injunction and that on the other hand denying the use of the land for defence purposes at this critical juncture would cause irreparable loss to the Govt. and the nation as a whole - In the written statement filed in Title Suit appellant No.4 had stated with regard to allegations made in plaint that it is asserted that the land was bonafide required for defence purposes - Thus it was seen from the materials on record that at least in Title Suits, the plea that the land was required urgently for defence purposes was taken by appellant No.4 who appeared to have put forward the defence of the appellants in the appeal as a whole - HCs were, therefore, not right in observing in their judgment that the intention of the Govt. was to lease the land to the highest bidder in the hope of getting a large amount of money because the land had been developed into a working tea garden, that the purpose could not be said to be bonafide and that it should be held that the land was being requisitioned only for collateral purposes - Only basis for this inference of HC was the supposed failure of the defendants in the title suits filed by the respondent to take the plea that the land was required for defence purposes - That basis being found to be wrong and unavailable it was not possible to agree with HC that the requirement of the land for defence purposes was not bonafide - Govt. of India whose case appellant No.4 had put forward in the respondent's title suits was the most competent authority to know when the need for defence purposes would arise or has arisen, and there was no material on record to hold in this case that the land was not required on the date of impugned requisition bonafide for defence purposes and that the appellants were putting forward such a case in the impugned order only as a ruse to auction the land for larger amount of rent - Appeal allowed.
