K. P. Varghese v Income Tax Officer, Ernakulam and Another

Supreme Court of India

04 September 1981

Case Analysis

Bench

P.N. Bhagwati, E.S. Venkataramiah

Where Reported

1981 Indlaw SC 334; (1981) 4 SCC 173; (1981) SCC (Tax) 293; AIR 1981 SC 1922; 1981 (24) CTR 358; [1981] 131 ITR 597; 1981(3) SCALE 1315; [1982] 1 S.C.R. 629; 1981 TAXLR 1448; [1981] 7 TAXMAN 13

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Income Tax Act, 1961, ss. 52(2) and 148 - Capital gains - Assessee sold his house to his relatives for the sum of Rs. 16,500/- at which he purchased the same in year 1958 - Assessee had not included said amount in his return as capital gain on the ground that the impugned house was sold by the assessee at the same price at which it was purchased and no capital gains accrued or arose to him as a result of the transfer - However, Income Tax Officer (ITO) claimed that there was understatement of consideration to avoid tax and proposed to fix the fair market value of the house sold by the assessee at Rs. 65,000/- as against the consideration of Rs. 16,500/- for which the house was sold and assess the difference of Rs. 48,500/- as capital gains in the hands of assessee - The objections raised by the assessee were overruled and an order of reassessment was passed by the ITO including the sum of Rs. 48,500/- as capital gains and bringing it to tax under sub-section (2) of s. 52 of Act - Assessee filed writ petition challenging the validity of the order of reassessment contending that the understatement of consideration in transfer of property was a necessary condition for attracting the applicability of s. 52 Sub-section (2) of the Act and as assessee had not concealed anything, s. 52 Sub-section (2) of the Act could not be attracted - Said writ petition was dismissed by the HC - Hence, the instant appeal - Whether understatement of consideration in a transfer of property was a necessary condition for attracting the applicability of s. 52 Sub-section (2) of the Act or it was enough for the Revenue to show that the fair market value of the property as on the date of the transfer exceeds the full value of the consideration declared by the assessee in respect of the transfer by an amount of not less than 15% of the value so declared? - Held, sub-section (2) of s. 52 of Act can be invoked only where the consideration for the transfer had been understated by the assessee - Sub-section (2) of s. 52 of the Act had no application in case of an honest and bonafide transaction where the consideration received by the assessee had been correctly declared or disclosed by him and there was no concealment or suppression of the consideration - The burden to prove an understatement or concealment was on the revenue - If the Revenue seeks to bring a case within sub-section (2) of s. 52 of the Act, it must show not only that the fair market value of the capital asset as on the date of the transfer exceeds the full value of the consideration declared by the assessee by not less than 15% of the value so declared, but also that the consideration has been understated and the assessee had actually received more than what was declared by him - In the instant case sub-section (2) of s. 52 of the Act had no application and the ITO could have no reason to believe that any part of the income of the assessee had escaped assessment so as to justify the issue of a notice u/s. 148 of the Act as there was no concealment by the assessee - The order of reassessment made by the ITO pursuant to the notice issued u/s. 148 of the Act was accordingly without jurisdiction - Impugned order of the HC dismissed - Appeal allowed.
