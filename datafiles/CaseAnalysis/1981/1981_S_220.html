Mohammad Usman Mohammad Hussain Maniyar and Another v State of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

03 March 1981

Case Analysis

Bench

Baharul Islam, O. Chinnappa Reddy

Where Reported

1981 Indlaw SC 368; (1981) 2 SCC 443; (1981) SCC (Cr) 477; AIR 1981 SC 1062; 1981 CRLJ 588; 1981 CRLR 238; 1981 CrLR(SC) 238; 1981 MLJ 438; 1981(1) SCALE 445; [1981] 3 S.C.R. 68

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Defence & Security Forces; Municipalities & Local Governments

Keywords: Explosive, Explosive Substances Act 1908

Summary: Criminal - Explosive Substances Act, 1908, ss. 2 and 5 - Essential Supplies (Temporary Powers) Act, 1946 - Indian Explosives Act, 1884 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 120 B - A number of murders were perpetrated by a gang of murderers - On investigation potassium cyanide was found to have been used for poisoning victims - Appellants convicted u/ss. 3, 5, 5 (3) (b) and 25(1)(a) - Poisons Act, s. 6(1)(a) r/w r. 2 of Poison Rules - (A) Whether substances seized from appellant were 'explosives'? - Held, on a consideration of evidence of explosives inspector and other evidence - Substances in question were explosive substances within definition of expression 'explosive' u/s. 4 of Explosive Substances Act, 1908 - (B) Whether an offence u/s. 5 of Explosive Substances Act was committed by appellants? - Appellants had been dealing with substances in question for a long time and they certainly knew or atleast they shall be presumed to have known what these substances were and for what purpose they were used - Evidence clearly establishes that appellants did know nature and character of substance - They knew that substances in question were explosive substances - Further held, Courts were right in holding that an offence u/s. 5 of Explosive Substances Act was committed and appellants were not in 'conscious possession' of substance seized - (C) Whether appellant is guilty of an offence u/s. 120 B of IPC, 1860 ? - For an offence u/s. 120 of IPC, 1860, the prosecution need not necessarily prove that perpetrators expressly agreed to do or cause to be done illegal act and agreement may be proved by necessary implication - Further held, fact that appellants were possessing and selling explosive substances without a valid licence for a pretty long time leads to inference that they agreed to do and/or cause to be done said illegal act, for, without such an agreement act could not have been done for such a long time, hence, appellants are liable u/s. 120 B of IPC, 1860 - Appeal dismissed.
