Shah Babulal Khimji v Jayaben D. Kania and Another

Supreme Court of India

10 August 1981

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Murtaza Fazal Ali, A. Varadarajan, Amarendra Nath Sen

Where Reported

1981 Indlaw SC 379; (1981) 4 SCC 8; AIR 1981 SC 1786; 1981(3) SCALE 1169; [1982] 1 S.C.R. 187

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Keywords: Government of India Act

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Practice & Procedure - Civil Procedure Code, 1908, ss. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 104, 105 and O. 43, r. 1 - Civil Procedure Code, 1877, ss. 588, 589, 590 and 591 - Arbitration Act, 1940, ss. 39, 10, 16, 17, 20, 43 and 92 - Companies Act, 1956, s. 202 - (A) Whether s. 588 of CPC, 1877 affects provisions of Letters Patent? - Held, it did not - Those orders of Trial Judge which fell beyond s. 588 could be appealable to a larger Bench under Letters Patent if those orders amounted to judgment within meaning of Letters Patent - S. 104 of CPC, 1908, made it clear that appeals against orders mentioned in O. 43, r. 1 were not in any way inconsistent with Letters Patent and merely provided an additional remedy by allowing appeals against miscellaneous orders passed by Trial Judge to a larger Bench - (B) Applicability of O. 43, r. 1 - Combined reading of various provisions of CPC, 1908, leads to conclusion that s. 104 r/w O. 43, r. 1 clearly applies to proceedings before Trial Judge of High Court - It merely gives an additional remedy by way of an appeal from orders of Trial Judge to a larger Bench - Provisions of CPC does not apply only to revenue courts - (C) Scope of s. 104 of CPC, 1908 - Object of legislature in enacting s. 104(2) was to make it clear that there was no second appeal under CPC, 1908, from orders specified in sub-s. (1) of s. 104, and that (2) was not intended to override express provisions of Letters Patent - Once s. 104 applies, there is nothing in Letters Patent to restrict its application - There is nothing to show that Letters Patent ever contemplated that even after one appeal lay from subordinate court to Single Judge, a Second Appeal would again lie to a Division Bench of Court - Further held, that where a Trial Judge had passed an order in an appeal against an order passed by District Judge under O. 43, r. 1, a further appeal under Letters Patent was not maintainable - (D) Whether orders u/O. 43, r. 1 is judgment within cl. 15 of letter patent? - Held, that word 'judgment' has not been defined in Letters Patent but whatever tests may be applied, order passed by Trial Judge appealed against must have traits and trappings of finality and there can be no doubt that appealable orders indicated in various clauses of O. 43, r. 1 are matters of moment deciding valuable rights of parties and in nature of final orders so as to fall within definition of judgment - (E) Meaning of word 'judgment' in cl. 15 - Held, a judgment means decision which affects merits of question between parties by determining some right or liability - It may be either final, or preliminary, or interlocutory, difference between them being that a final judgment determines whole cause or suit, and a preliminary or interlocutory judgment determines only a part of it, leaving other matters to be determined - (G) Test for determining whether an order passed by Trial Court is a judgment - Held, that an order is a judgment when: (1) a decision which affects merits of question between parties; (2) by determining some right or liability; (3) order determining right or liability may be final, preliminary or interlocutory, but determination must be final or one which decides even a part of controversy finally leaving other matters to be decided later - An exhaustive or a comprehensive definition of 'judgment' as contemplated in cl. 15 of Letters Patent cannot be properly given and it will be wise to remember that in Letters Patent itself, there is no definition of word 'judgment' - Order accordingly.
