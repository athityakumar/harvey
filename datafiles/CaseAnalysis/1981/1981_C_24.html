Chinnamarkathian Alias Muthu Gounder & Another v Ayyavoo Alias Periana Gounder & Others

Supreme Court of India

10 December 1981

Case Analysis

Bench

D.A. Desai, A.D. Koshal, R.B. Misra

Where Reported

1981 Indlaw SC 315; (1982) 1 SCC 159; AIR 1982 SC 137; 1982 (1) MLJ(SC) 17; 1981(3) SCALE 1891; [1982] 2 S.C.R. 146; 1982 UJ 100

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Land & Property; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Default in rent, Power of Revenue Divisional Officer

Summary: D. A. Desai, J.

Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Madras Cultivating Tenants Protection Act, 1955, s. 3 (4)(a), (b) - Default in rent - Eviction - Power of Revenue Divisional Officer - Legality - Respondents (landlords) filed petitions before Revenue Divisional Officer (RDO) seeking eviction of tenants on the allegation of arrears of rent - RDO held that appellants (tenants) were in arrears of rent and respondents were entitled to commence action for recovery of recovery and eviction of appellants for failure to pay rent in arrears - Aggrieved appellants filed revision petitions before HC, which were dismissed holding that order of RDO was not one for eviction, but at a later stage in impugned judgment of HC, HC took a somersault when it observed that nothing wrong in order which RDO had passed - Aggrieved appellants filed instant appeal - Whether in view of language u/s. 3(4)(a) and (b) of the Act, RDO erred in passing a composite order for payment of rent found to be in arrears within time prescribed by him and on default, without any further proceeding, directing eviction of appellants - Held, scheme of the Act was that merely on determination of rent in arrears the RDO was not to conclude that there was such default which become irreparable and that he was under an obligation to evict the tenant - In fact, statute granted locus poeniteniae to the tenant by making it obligatory upon RDO to grant some time to tenant to repair the default - If after the time so granted expires and tenant failed to comply with order calling upon him to deposit arrears there would be a default which might become irreparable and eviction might follow - Till then there was no jurisdiction in RDO to direct eviction - HC opined that composite order was not contemplated by s. 3(4)(b) of the Act - Scheme of s. 3(4)(b) of the Act required the RDO to determine, arrears, ascertain the exact amount payable by the tenant, fix the time for payment after taking into consideration the relevant circumstances of landlord and the cultivating tenant and then stop there - There was no power in RDO at that stage to pass an order for eviction - However, fact that initial proceedings before RDO started in 1961, 2 decades have rolled by - Ground of eviction was a technical ground of default repaired by orders of HC when the rent found in arrears was deposited - Respondents have been paid, might be not specifically within the time prescribed by RDO but within the time prescribed by HC - It was not necessary to decide in instant case whether the time prescribed by RDO, if challenged in superior court i.e. HC, HC would have jurisdiction to prescribe its own time calling upon the tenant to deposit the amount to repair the default - That question be kept open but in facts of instant case amount having been deposited way back in 1961-62, it would be merely adding to agony of parties for a very technical consideration to remit case to RDO - It was not just and proper to remit the case to RDO - Appellants have qualified for protection of the Act and they were not liable to be evicted - Appeals allowed.

A.D. KOSHAL, J.

Held, if legislature intended to make it obligatory on the part of RDO to fix a time for deposit of the arrears in all cases covered by s. 3(2)(a), (aa) of the Act there was no reason why it should have used the word 'may' in relation to grant of time - While the opportunity of depositing the arrears of rent could not be denied to a cultivating tenant during the course of proceedings u/s. 3(3) of the Act, same was not available as of right under cl. (b) of sub-s. 4. The difference in the language used by the legislature is significant and not without purpose - Intention of legislature appeared to be that normally a defaulting tenant should seek the help of Court all by himself and that if he did so he should be protected; but that a defaulting tenant who waits for payment of rent till he was sought to be evicted by landlord was not necessarily entitled to same protection - Why the word 'may' occurring in s. 3(4)(b) of the Act be not given its ordinary meaning as denoting the conferment of a discretion on RDO and be equated with 'shall' so as to make it obligatory on him to grant time to cultivating tenant - Appeals allowed.
