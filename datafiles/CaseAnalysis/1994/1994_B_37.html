State of Punjab v Balbir Singh

Supreme Court of India

01 March 1994

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Jayachandra Reddy, S. Ratnavel Pandian

Where Reported

1994 Indlaw SC 79; (1994) 3 SCC 299; (1994) SCC (Cr) 634; AIR 1994 SC 1872; AIR 1994 SCW 1802; 1994 (3) AllCriC 351; 1994 (1) AWC 388; 1994 (1) Crimes 753; 1994 CRLJ 3702; 1997 (69) ECR 260; 1994 (70) E.L.T. 481; JT 1994 (2) SC 108; [1994] 2 S.C.R. 208

Case Digest

Subject: Customs; Excise; Indirect Tax

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985, ss. 41, 42, 43, 49, 50, 57 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 100, 165 - Offences under NDPS Act - Non-compliance of provisions in respect of arrest, search and seizure - Validity of trial - Respondent accused allegedly committed the offence under the provisions of the Act - Trial Court acquitted the accused on the ground that the arrest, search and seizure were in violation of some of the relevant and mandatory provisions of the NDPS Act - Appellant State filed appeal, however, HC declined to grant leave to appeal against the said order of acquittal - Hence the instant appeal by State - Whether any arrest and search of a person or search of a place without conforming to the provisions of the NDPS Act would became illegal and consequently vitiate the conviction - Held, NDPS Act was not a complete code incorporating all the provisions relating to search, seizure or arrest etc - NDPS Act after incorporating the broad principles regarding search, seizure or arrest etc, in ss. 41,42,43 and 49 had laid down in s. 51 that the provisions of CrPC should apply in so far as they were not inconsistent with the provisions of the NDPS Act to all warrants issued and arrests, searches and seizures made under that Act - Hence, the provisions of ss. 100 and 165 CrPC which were not inconsistent with the provisions of the NDPS Act were applicable for effecting search, seizure or arrest under the NDPS Act also - Provisions of the CrPC should be applicable in so far as they were not inconsistent with the NDPS Act to all warrants, searches, seizures or arrest made under the Act - If a police officer without any prior information as contemplated under the provisions of the NDPS Act made a search or arrested a person in the normal course of investigation into an offence or suspected offence as provided under the provisions of CrPC and when such search was completed at that stage s. 50 of the NDPS Act would not be attracted and the question of complying with the requirements there under would not arise - If during such search or arrest there was a chance recovery of any narcotic drug or psychotropic substance then the police officer, who was not empowered, should inform the empowered officer who should thereafter proceed in accordance with the provisions of the NDPS Act If he happened to be an empowered officer also, then from that stage onwards, he should carry out the investigation in accordance with the other provisions of the NDPS Act - Further, u/s. 41(1) only an empowered Magistrate should issue warrant for the arrest or for the search in respect of offences punishable under Chapter IV of the Act etc. otherwise such search or arrest if carried out would be illegal - Only empowered officers or duly authorized officers as enumerated in ss. 41(2) and 42(1) should act under the provisions of the NDPS Act otherwise the same would be illegal - U/s. 42(1) of the Act empowered officer if has a prior information given by any person, that should necessarily be taken down in writing but if he had reason to believe from personal knowledge that offences under Chapter IV had been committed or materials which might furnish evidence of commission of such offences were concealed in any building, etc., he should carry out the arrest or search without a warrant between sunrise and sunset and this provision did not mandate that he should record his reasons of belief - However, under the proviso to s. 42(1) if such officer was required to carry out such search between sunset and sunrise, he should record the grounds of his belief and to that extent those provisions were mandatory and contravention of the same would affect the prosecution case and vitiate the trial - U/s. 42(2) such empowered officer who took down any information in writing or records the grounds under proviso to s. 42(1) should forthwith send a copy thereof to his immediate official superior - If there was total non-compliance of s. 42(1) the same affected the prosecution case and hence it was mandatory - If a police officer, even if he happened to be an 'empowered' officer while effecting an arrest or search during normal investigation into offences purely under the provisions of CrPC failed to strictly comply with the provisions of ss. 100 and 165 CrPC including the requirement to record reasons, such failure would only amount to an irregularity - In such a situation, the court was required to consider whether any prejudice had been caused to the accused and also examine the evidence in respect of search in the light of the fact that those provisions had not been complied with and further consider whether the weight of evidence was in any manner affected because of non-compliance - If an empowered officer or an authorized officer u/s. 41(2) of the Act carried out a search, he would be doing so under the provisions of CrPC namely ss. 100 and 165 CrPC and if there was no strict compliance with the provisions of CrPC then such search would not per se be illegal and would not vitiate the trial - Effect of such failure was required to be borne in mind by the courts while appreciating the evidence in the facts and circumstances of each case - On prior information, the empowered officer or authorized officer while acting u/s. 41(2) or 42 should comply with the provisions of s. 50 before the search of the person was made and such person should be informed that if he so required, he shoul be produced before a gazetted officer or a magistrate as provided there under - It was obligatory on the part of such officer to inform the person to be searched - Failure to inform the person to be searched and if such person so required, failure to take him to the gazetted officer or the magistrate, would amount to non-compliance of s. 50 which was mandatory and thus it would affect the prosecution case and vitiate the trial - After being so informed whether such person opted for such a course or not would be a question of fact - Provisions of s. 50 were mandatory and it was imperative on the part of the officer to inform the person to be searched of his right that if he so required to be searched before a gazetted officer or a magistrate - It was a valuable right given to the person to be searched in the presence of a gazetted officer or a magistrate if he so required, since such a search would impart much more authenticity and credit worthiness to the proceedings while equally providing an important safeguard to the accused - To afford such an opportunity to the person to be searched, he should be aware of his right and that could be done only by the authorized officer informing him - Ss. 52 and 57 came into operation after the arrest and seizure under the Act - While determining whether the provisions of the Act to be followed after the arrest or search were directory or mandatory, it would have to be kept in mind that the provisions of a statute creating public duties were generally speaking directory - Provisions of ss. 52 and 57 contained certain procedural instructions for strict compliance by the officers - However, if there was no strict compliance of any of these instructions that by itself should not render the acts done by these officers null and void and at the most it would affect the probative value of the evidence regarding arrest or search and in some cases it would invalidate such arrest or search - Such violation, however, by itself did not invalidate the trial or the conviction if otherwise there was sufficient material - Hence, it was required to be shown that such non-compliance had caused prejudice and resulted in failure of justice - Officers, however, should not totally ignore the said provisions and if there was no proper explanation for non-compliance or where the officers totally ignore the provisions then that would definitely have an adverse effect on the prosecution case and the courts had to appreciate the evidence and the merits of the case bearing those aspects in view - However, a mere non-compliance or failure to strictly comply by itself would not vitiate the prosecution - Since the alleged offences had been taken place long time back, it was not expedient to order for retrial in the instant case in view of most of the incriminating evidence would have been disappeared - Appeal dismissed.
