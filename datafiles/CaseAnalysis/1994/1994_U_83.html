Supreme Court Legal and Committee Representing v Union Of India

Supreme Court of India

07 October 1994

Case Analysis

Bench

A.M. Ahmadi, B.L. Hansaria

Where Reported

1994 Indlaw SC 833; (1994) 6 SCC 731; (1995) SCC (Cr) 39; AIR 1994 SCW 5115; 1994 (4) CCR 732; 1994 (3) Crimes 644; JT 1994 (6) SC 544; 1994(4) SCALE 452; 1995 (2) SCJ 36; [1994] Supp4 S.C.R. 386

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985, s. 2 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 190 and 309 - Constitution of India, arts. 14, 19 and 21 - Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances (Amendment) Act, 1988, ss. 31-A, 36 and 36-A,B,C,D, - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 4(1), 4(2), 6 and 209 - A large number of cases in relation to offences under the NDPS Act, 1988 were detected in Bombay and prosecutions were launched against the offenders - These cases were being dealt with by courts having jurisdiction under the Code - After the insertion of ss. 36, 36-A and 36-D the State Govt. constituted two Special Courts for Greater Bombay - (A) Whether in cases in which the Court of Session had taken cognizance before the constitution of the Special Court, the latter had not jurisdiction? - S. 36 provides for the constitution of Special Courts and s. 36-A(1)(a) says that notwithstanding anything contained in CrPC, 1973 all offences under the NDPS Act, 1988 shall be triable only by Special Court - Once a Special Court has been constituted for an are or areas in which the offence has been committed, then notwithstanding anything contained in the Code, the Special Court alone will have jurisdiction and all other courts exercising jurisdiction prior to the constitution of the Special Courts will cease to have jurisdiction - S. 36-A(1)(a) and (d) also begin with a non-obstante clause - A Special Court may take cognizance of an offence without the accused being committed to it for trial - U/s. 36-D(1) any offence committed under the NDPS Act, 1988 on or after the commencement of the Amendment Act, 1988, until a Special Court is constituted u/s. 36, shall, notwithstanding anything contained in the Code, be tried by a Court of Session - (B) When can a Special Court be said to have been constituted? - The mode of constitution of Special Courts is by issuance of a notification in the Official Gazette specifying the area for which each Special Court is constituted - The likelihood of a time-gap between the issuance of a notification u/sub-s. (1) of s. 36 and the appointment of a Judge to man the court has to be countenanced - The words 'court' and 'Judge' are frequently used interchangeably because a Judge is an essential constituent of a court since there can be no dispensation of justice without a Judge - Thus when complete in its organised aspect with all the constituent elements of time, place and officers, that 'court' is constituted in the general legal acceptation of the term - Held that the provision as to the constitution of the court at the "initial stage" as contemplated by s. 36, must, be understood to mean that all its constituent elements of time, place and officers are complete and unless the Judge expected to man the court is appointed the constitution cannot be said to be complete because the court cannot take off till then - The initial constitution becomes complete only when the requirements of ss. 36(1) and (2) have been completed - Whether further detention of foreigners, languishing in jails as undertrials under the NDPS Act, 1988 for a period exceeding two years, can be void or in any case they be released on bail their cases be given priority over others? - To refuse bail on the one hand and to delay trial of cases on the other is clearly unfair and unreasonable and contrary to the spirit of s. 36(1) NDPS Act, 1988, s. 309 CrPC, 1973 and arts. 14, 19 and 21, Constitution - Not inclined to accept the extreme submission of quashing the proceedings and setting free the accused whose trials have been delayed beyond reasonable time - However, if the period of deprivation pending trial becomes unduly long, the fairness assured by art. 21 would receive a jolt - Thus, held that after accused persons have suffered imprisonment which is half of the maximum punishment provided for the offence, any further deprivation of personal liberty would be violative of art.21 - Therefore, various directions set out to address this issue - The directives subject to the general conditions - They are intended to operate as one-time directions for cases in which the accused persons are in jail and their trials are delayed - Not intended to interfere with the Special Court's power to grant bail u/s .37 NDPS Act, 1988 - Petition insofar as it relates to Maharashtra disposed.
