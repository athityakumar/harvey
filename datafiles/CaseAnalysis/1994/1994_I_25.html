 Ismail Faruqui v Union of India

Supreme Court of India

24 October 1994

Case Analysis

Bench

M.N. Venkatachaliah, A.M. Ahmadi, G.N. Ray, S.P. Bharucha, J.S. Verma

Where Reported

1994 Indlaw SC 516; (1994) 6 SCC 360; AIR 1995 SC 605; JT 1994 (6) SC 632; 1994 SCALE 100; [1994] Supp5 S.C.R. 1

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Land & Property

Keywords: communal harmony, communal violence, communalism

Summary: (A) Land & Property - Criminal - Acquisition of Certain Area At Ayodhya Act, 1993 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - Maintenance - Immovable property - Land acquisition - Communal violence - Ayodhya a State in North-India in Uttar Pradesh had been a place of holy pilgrimage - Mosque was erected on a land where it was believed was the birth spot of Sri Ram where a temple stood earlier - Controversy entered a new phase when the idols were placed in the disputed structure - Premises were attached u/s. 150, CrPC - Movement to construct Ram Temple at the site of disputed structure gathered momentum in recent years which became a matter of great controversy and tension - Many political parties were involved - Entire structure was demolished and razed to the ground which was an act of 'national shame' - Minorities lost their faith in the rule of law and constitutional process - Five-hundred-year-old structure which was defenseless and whose safety was a sacred trust in the hands of the State Govt. was demolished - Civil administration attached the premises u/s. 145 of the CrPC after the idols were placed on the disputed structure - Two civil suits were filed by Hindu plaintiffs seeking to restrain the Administration from removing the idols from the disputed structure or placing any restrictions in the way of devotees intending to offer worship - injunctions were confirmed by the Allahabad HC - Whether a Hindu temple or any Hindu religious structure existed prior to the construction of the Ram Janma Bhumi-Babri Masjid (including the premises of the inner and outer courtyards of such structure) in the area on which the structure stood - Held, it was stated on behalf of the Muslim organizations, as well as by certain eminent historians, that there was no evidence in favour of either of these two assertions - It was also stated by certain Muslim leaders that if these assertions were proved, the Muslims would voluntarily handover the disputed shrine to the Hindus - Hindu structures of worship already existed in the outer courtyard of the structure - Ayodhya is said to be of particular significance to the Hindus as a place of pilgrimage because of the ancient belief that Lord Ram was born there, the mosque was of significance for the Muslim community as an ancient mosque built by Mir Baqi in 1528 AD - Many case laws were referred - Also many articles, ss. and acts were cited - But it was not clear whether a Hindu temple or any Hindu religious structure existed prior to the construction of the Ram Janma Bhumi-Babri Masjid (including the premises of the inner and outer courtyards of such structure) in the area on which the structure stood - Order accordingly.

(B) Land & Property - Criminal - Acquisition of Certain Area At Ayodhya Act, 1993 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894 - Maintenance - Immovable property - Land acquisition - Communal violence - Whether the provision in s. 7 containing the mandate to maintain the status quo existed at the disputed site was a slant in favour of the Hindu community, intended to perpetuate an injustice done to the Muslim community by demolition of the mosque and, therefore, it amounted to an anti-secular or discriminatory act which rendered the provision unconstitutional - Held, it was necessary to recall situation as it existed on the date when the significant events took place - S. 7 is a transitory provision, intended to maintain status quo in the disputed area, till transfer of the property is made by the Central Govt. on resolution of the dispute - Unless status quo was ensured, the final outcome on resolution of the dispute may be frustrated by any change made in the disputed area which may frustrate the implementation of the result in favour of the successful party and render it meaningless - Exercise made was to find out the true owner of the disputed area, to maintain status quo therein during the interregnum and to hand it over to the true owner found entitled to it - S.7 related to the management and administration of the whole bundle of property and rights - S.7(1) stated that it shall be managed by the Central Govt. or body of persons or trustees of any trust authorized by the Govt. on this behalf - Act itself stated that the idols shall remain on the disputed site for an indefinite period of time and puja shall continue to be performed before them - Order accordingly.
