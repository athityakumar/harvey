Jay Laxmi Salt Works Private Limited v State of Gujarat

Supreme Court of India

04 May 1994

Case Analysis

Bench

R.M. Sahai, Kuldip Singh

Where Reported

1994 Indlaw SC 325; (1994) 4 SCC 1; 1994 ACJ 902; 1994 (3) CCC 8; JT 1994 (3) SC 492; [1994] 3 S.C.R. 866

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Constitution; Practice & Procedure; Tort

Keywords: Act Of God, Breach Of Duty, Tortious Liability, Wrongful, Wrongs, Malice, Malfeasance

Summary: Tort - Indian Limitation Act, 1908, arts. 36, 115 and 120 - Construction of bundh by State - Appellant objected and referred the matter to authorities - Area flooded and bundh dismantled - Factory premises of appellant inundated - Damage caused to the factory premises of appellant - Appellant claimed damages - Trail Court held that it was an act of God - High Court found the respondent-state negligent in it duty yet the suit barred by limitation u/art. 36 of the Limitation Act - (A) Legality - Held, since the appellant suffered loss on facts found due to action of respondent's officers both at the stage of construction and failure to take steps even at the last moment it was liable to be compensated - "Fault liability" can be inferred even from the circumstances - The view of the HC, therefore, that the rule of strict liability laid down in Rylands v. Fletcher' s case was modified by Supreme Court in Modern Cultivator's case (1982 Indlaw QBD 57) does not appear to be correct - 'Absolute liability', or 'strict liability' and 'fault liability' do not go together - (B) Tort and Contract - Liabilities - Nature of - Held, Injury and damage are two basic ingredients of tort - In tort, violation of duty fixed by law but in contract, duty fixed by party themselves - In tort, duty towards general but in contract it is towards only to specific persons - Action of tort is for pecuniary compensation - "Strict liability" and "fault liability" arises from presence and absence of mental element - A breach of legal duty willfully, or deliberately or even maliciously is negligence emanating from fault liability but injury or damage resulting without any intention yet due to lack of foresight etc. is strict liability - Duty is the primary yard stick to fix tortious liability - (C) Construction of Dam by State - Injury to the public - Tortious liability of State - Held, construction of dam or bundh for the sake of community is essential function and use of land or accumulation of water for the benefit of society cannot be non-natural user - But State cannot absolve from its duty of being responsible to its citizens for such violations as are actionable and result in damage, loss or injury - Once the occasion for loss or damage is failure of duty, general or specific, the cause of action under tort arises - (D) "Malfeasance" - Meaning of - Word has very wide import and strong expression - It brings into motive, intention, malice etc. - Law of torts, however, is not confined and cannot be strictly categorised - (E) Construction of bundh - Nature of the duty of State - Held, if state undertakes a common law duty its actions may give rise to common law tort - Negligence in performance of duty is only a step to determine if action of Govt resulting in loss or injury to common man should not go uncompensated - If construction of bundh is a common law or public duty, it give rise to tortious liability in the modern developing sense - (F) Art. 36 of Limitation Act - Interpretation of - Held, all violations demarcates as 'ex delicto' or 'ex contractu' by HC is erroneous - Art. 36 is not a residuary article, applied to all tortious liability - Take care by using the word "often" in the article - HC's interpretation was too rigid and not in conformity with principle of interpretation nor the scheme and purpose of the enactment - (G) Construction of bundh - Negligence of authorities - Tortious liability - Suit for damages - Limitation - Held, it was not only negligence but mistake, defective planning, failure to discharge public duty - Art. 36 not applicable but art. 120 applicable in broader sense wherein limitation starts to run only after 6 years - Therefore, the present suit for damage could not be thrown out as it was filed beyond two years from the date the incident took place - (H) Limitation u/art. 36 - Computation of time - Held, it commences either from the date when malfeasance, misfeasance or nonfeasance occurred or from the date when the damage took place or where claim is lodged within period allowed by law - Damage can be ascertained from the date of the claim rejected - If the authorities improperly or arbitrarily rejects the claim of damage found by the Official Committee, limitation starts from the date the Govt. refused to pay the amount determined by the Committee - Since the rejection was not communicated nor the copy of the report was supplied despite request the suit could not be said to be barred by time - Appeal allowed.
