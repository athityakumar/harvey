Entry Tax Officer v Chandanmal Champalal and Company

Supreme Court of India

18 April 1994

Case Analysis

Bench

B.P. Jeevan Reddy, N. Venkatachala

Where Reported

1994 Indlaw SC 1870; (1994) 4 SCC 463; JT 1994 (3) SC 334; 1994(2) SCALE 627; [1994] 3 S.C.R. 545; 1994 (95) STC 5

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Summary: Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Karnataka Tax On Entry Of Goods Into Local Areas For Consumption Use Or Sale Therein Act, 1979 - Appellant in the instant case alleged that entry 52 in list 11 of the seventh schedule to the Constitution should not be confined to an impost, formerly known as 'octroi' and secondly, since it was a legislative entry, it should be liberally construed and full effect should be given to the words used therein - He further alleged that when entry 52 speaks of a tax on the entry of goods into a local area for consumption, use or sale therein, the words 'sale therein' should be given their due and natural meaning and should not be restricted to a sale for the purpose of consumption or use of the goods sold within the limits of the local area concerned - The submission was urged with reference to the scope and ambit of the 1979 Act - Whether, levy created by s. 3 of the 1979 Act on the sales effected within a local area was confined only to those sales of goods which were meant for consumption or use within such local area and whether, the refund of the amount should be allowed to the respondent which had been paid in excess of the legal liability - Held, relying on the view taken by instant court in Burmah Shell Oil Storage & Distributing Co. India Ltd. v. Belgaum Borough Municipality 1962 Indlaw SC 295, it was concluded that, levy created by s. 3 of the 1979 Act on the sales effected within a local area was confined only to those sales of goods which were meant for consumption or use within such local area - In other words, where the goods were sold within a local area for the purpose of being taken out of that local area and were actually taken out, no levy was permissible - However, refund was not allowed to the respondent, holding the view that such direction for the refund would have been amounted to unjust enrichment of the respondents who were merely dealers and had passed on the burden to the purchasers or the consumers - Appeal partly allowed.
