Jai Mangal Oraon v Mira Nayak And Ors.

Supreme Court of India

05 May 2000

Case Analysis

Bench

Doraiswamy Raju, M.B. Shah

Where Reported

2000 Indlaw SC 332; (2000) 5 SCC 141; AIR 2000 SC 2276; JT 2000 (7) SC 1; 2000(4) SCALE 491; [2000] 3 S.C.R. 1128; 2000 (5) SLT 309; 2000 (4) Supreme 583

Case Digest

Subject: Municipalities & Local Governments; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 420, 466, 467, 468, 471 and 120-B - Chota Nagpur Tenancy Act, 1908, ss. 71-A, 46 and 72 - Chota Nagpur Tenancy (Amendment) Act, 1947 - Land Acquisition Act - Respondent 1 claimed to have purchased land - She got her name mutated - Obtained necessary sanction, raised construction - Appellant filed an application alleging that respondent 1 had forcibly started occupying land belonging to him - Appellant approached Deputy Commr. - Special Officer, Scheduled Area Regulation directed respondent to stop construction - Appellant's claim was that under a registered adoption deed dated he succeeded to interest of his predecessor-interest in property - Respondent challenged jurisdiction and authority - Special Officer held in favour of appellant - Writ Petition allowed by High Court - (A) What effect do subsequent developments have? - Subsequent developments or facts and turn of events coming into existence but found really relevant, genuine and vitally important in effectively deciding issues raised and necessary to do real, effective and substantial justice or prevent a miscarriage of justice not only can but ought to be taken into consideration by courts even at appellate - Held, vital facts now coming to light, which are not only grave and serious but also go to root of matter, undermining very basis of appellant claims and even locus standi or right to agitate before courts in relation to property in question, cannot be totally ignored to permit perpetuation of grave injustice and abuse of process of court - Facts themselves constitute sufficient ground to dismiss these appeals - (B) Whether s. 71-A applies to case? - Statutory provisions as they stood in force on 15-1-1942 neither envisaged obtaining of prior sanction of Deputy Commr. before a surrender by a tenant could be made of his interest in favour of landlord nor could such surrender be held bad merely because it was not at end of agricultural year but immediately before - HC held that claim of forcible dispossession of appellant will not amount to a transfer within meaning of s. 71-A - Amendment Act, 1947 amending ss. 46 and 72 was prospective in operation, there was no obligation or necessity to obtain previous sanction of Deputy Commr. for effecting surrender in 1942 - Nothing illegal or wrong in said reasoning and conclusion by HC well merited - Appeal dismissed.
