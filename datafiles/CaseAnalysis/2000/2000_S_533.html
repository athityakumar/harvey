Joseph S/O Kooveli Poulo v State of Kerala

Supreme Court of India

27 April 2000

Case Analysis

Bench

Doraiswamy Raju, K.T. Thomas, S.N. Variava

Where Reported

2000 Indlaw SC 309; (2000) 5 SCC 197; (2000) SCC (Cr) 926; AIR 2000 SC 1608; AIR 2000 SCW 1682; 2000 (2) CCR 122; 2000 (2) Crimes 230; 2000 CRLJ 2467; 2000 (2) JCC 592; JT 2000 (6) SC 195; 2000 (2) RCR(Criminal) 738; 2000(4) SCALE 92; [2000] 3 S.C.R. 729; 2000 (4) SLT 382; 2000 (3) Supreme 588

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302, 376 and 392 - Rape - Murder - Conviction - Challenged - Appeal was charged for offences punishable u/ss. 376, 392 and 302 of IPC - Trial Court held that there was no evidence to show that the appellant committed rape on deceased, or that it was the appellant who sold the ornaments of deceased and could not, therefore, be responsible in any manner for the death of deceased and acquitted appellant - On appeal, HC after specifically noticing the several incriminating facts which inevitably and necessarily led to an hypothesis of the appellant being guilty of the charges levelled against him convicted him of the offences, charged with - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, the deceased was last seen alive only with the appellant and thereafter she neither returned to the Convent nor her home, alive and not found anywhere else also by any one, outside the company of the appellant. The incriminating circumstances inevitably lead to the guilt of the appellant and nothing has been highlighted or brought on record to make the facts proved or the circumstances established to be in any manner in consonance with the innocence at any rate of the appellant. The findings of the HC holding the appellant guilty of offences u/s. 302 of IPC for committing the murder and for robbing deceased of her jewellery worn by her, u/s. 392 of IPC upheld. However, benefit of doubt given to the appellant regarding commission of rape and appellant acquitted of the offence u/s. 376 of IPC. Appeal disposed of.

Ratio - Unless the discrepancies and contradictions are so material and substantial and that too are in respect of vitally relevant aspects of the facts deposed, the witnesses cannot be straightaway condemned and their evidence discarded in its entirety.
