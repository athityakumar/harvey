State of Uttar Pradesh v Hari Mohan and Others

Supreme Court of India

07 November 2000

Case Analysis

Bench

R.P. Sethi, K.T. Thomas

Where Reported

2000 Indlaw SC 3137; (2000) 8 SCC 598; (2001) SCC (Cr) 49; AIR 2001 SC 142; 2000 (4) CCR 209; 2000 (4) Crimes 234; 2001 CRLJ 170; JT 2000 (Supp2) SC 467; 2000 (4) RCR(Criminal) 667; 2000(7) SCALE 348; 2000 (8) SLT 168; [2000] Supp4 S.C.R. 440; 2000 (7) Supreme 516

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34, 201 and 302 - In case there are no direct evidence, can case of prosecution be based on circumstantial evidence? - Held, to convict a person on basis of circumstantial evidence all circumstances relied upon by prosecution must be clearly established - Circumstances must be such as would reasonably exclude possibility of innocence of accused - Arriving at conclusion about guilt of accused charged with commission of a crime, Court has to judge evidence by yardstick of probabilities, its intrinsic worth and animus of witnesses - Further held, a wrong statement on a very vital aspect concerning death of deceased can also be counted, among other circumstances, as a link in chain of circumstances to connect accused with commission - Relied on Rajinder Kumar v State of Punjab, 1962 Indlaw SC 163 - Court counted contradictory statement made by accused as one of links in chain - Circumstances fully established by prosecution - Circumstances are a chain, complete in itself and inconsistent with innocence of accused - Appeal partly allowed.
