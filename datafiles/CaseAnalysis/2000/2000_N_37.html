Narayan Chetanram Chaudhary and Another v State of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

05 September 2000

Case Analysis

Bench

R.P. Sethi, K.T. Thomas

Where Reported

2000 Indlaw SC 2831; (2000) 8 SCC 457; (2000) SCC (Cr) 1546; AIR 2000 SC 2000; JT 2000 (10) SC 78; 2000 (4) RCR(Criminal) 75; 2000(6) SCALE 230; [2000] Supp3 S.C.R. 104

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Approver

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120-B, 302, 34, 342, 392, 297, 449 - Criminal Procedure Code, 1973, ss. 306, 307, 313 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 114, 133 - Grant of pardon - Held, Illustration (b) to s. 114 of Evidence Act observed that whilst it is not illegal to act on uncorroborated evidence of an accomplice, it is a rule of prudence so universally followed as to amount almost to a rule of law that it is unsafe to act on evidence of an accomplice unless it is corroborated in material respects so as to implicate accused; and further that evidence of one accomplice cannot be used to corroborate evidence of another accomplice - Rule of prudence was based on interpretation of phrase 'corroborated in material particulars' in Illustration (b) - For corroborative evidence court must look at broad spectrum of approver's version and then find out whether there is other evidence to corroborate and lend assurance to that version - Nature and extent of such corroboration may depend upon facts of different cases - Corroboration need not be in form of ocular testimony of witnesses and may even be in form of circumstantial evidence - The Trial Court felt that corroborations assure correctness and trueness of approver and therefore from evidence of approver corroborated by other circumstantial evidence, prosecution had proved offences against accused persons beyond reasonable doubt - The High Court upheld and confirmed conviction passed by Trial Court - No merit - Conviction upheld - Appeals dismissed.
