 Bijay Cotton Mills Limited v State of Ajmer

Supreme Court of India

14 October 1954

Case Analysis

Bench

Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, T.L. Venkatarama Aiyyar, Mehr Chand Mahajan, Vivian Bose, B. Jagannadhadas

Where Reported

1954 Indlaw SC 55; AIR 1955 SC 33; 1955 (1) LLJ 129; 1955 (1) MLJ(SC) 10; 1955 SCJ 51; [1955] 1 S.C.R. 752

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 19, 19(1)(g) - Minimum Wages Act, 1948, s.8 - Wages - Capacity - Maintainability - There was industrial dispute between company and laborers regarding enhancement of wages and dispute was referred by Govt. to Tribunal - Tribunal made its award and held that present earning capacity of mill precludes award of higher rates of wages and higher dearness allowance - Employees took appeal against this award to Appellate Tribunal - Appellate Tribunal sent case back to Tribunal for further investigation and Tribunal made its final award by which it rejected basis upon which minimum wages of Rs. 56 were fixed by Chief Commissioner and fixed minimum wages including dearness allowance at Rs. 35 only - Petition had been filed by company challenging Constitutional validity of material provisions of the 1948 Act - Workmen who were willing to work at less than minimum wages fixed by State Govt. had filed other petition supporting all allegations of company - Hence, instant petitions - Whether order passed by Tribunal was maintainable - Held, procedure for fixing of minimum wages, 'appropriate Govt.' had undoubtedly been given very large powers s. 8 of the 1948 Act provided for appointment of Central Advisory Board for purpose of advising Central as well as State Govt both in matter of fixing and revision of minimum wages - Provisions constituted adequate safeguard against any hasty or capricious decision by 'appropriate Govt. - In suitable cases 'appropriate Govt.' had also been given power of granting exemptions from operation of provisions of the 1948 Act - There was no provision undoubtedly for further review of decision of 'appropriate Govt.', but we do not think that by itself would make provisions of the 1948 Act unreasonable - Thus, restrictions, though they interfere to some extent with freedom of trade or business guaranteed u/art. 19(1) (g) of Constitution, were reasonable and being imposed in interest of general public were protected by terms of cl. (6) of art.19 of Constitution - Petitions dismissed.
