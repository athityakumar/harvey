Nathoo Lal v Durga Prasad

Supreme Court of India

09 April 1954

Case Analysis

Bench

Mehr Chand Mahajan, Vivian Bose, Ghulam Hasan

Where Reported

1954 Indlaw SC 147; AIR 1954 SC 355; 1954 SCJ 557; [1955] 1 S.C.R. 51

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Constitution; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Family & Personal - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 133 - R died survived by his mother, S, his widow, B, and his two daughters, Bhuri and L and made an oral will bequeathed property in favour of L - S and B executed and registered a deed of gift in favour of L as per will - After her death X, her husband, mortgaged property to defendant-appellant N and later sold it to him - Plaintiff, son of Bhuri, filed a suit for possession - It was contended that X had no right either to mortgage or sell house and that L was not absolute owner of property but had only a limited estate in it, suit was dismissed by Civil Judge - On appeal, the decision was affirmed by District Judge - Plaintiff preferred and succeeded a second appeal to High Court, Jaipur - HC held that house was bequeathed to L by R under an oral will, there was no proof that it conferred upon her an absolute interest in property - Defendant applied for a review of this judgment meanwhile Jaipur HC had become defunct and review was heard by Rajasthan HC as successor and partly allowed and decree was accordingly amended - Whether respondent raised a objection as to maintainability of appeal and contended that according to CPC, 1908 of Jaipur State decision of Jaipur HC had become final as no appeal lay from it and the appeal was incompetent? - Held, the objection is not well founded and only operative decree in suit which finally and conclusively determines rights of parties is decree passed by Rajasthan HC and that having been passed after coming into force of Constitution of India, provisions of art. 133 are attracted to it and it is appealable to SC provided requirements of that article are fulfilled - Gift deed fully supports testimony of plaintiff on this point and states that according to will, gift deed was executed in favour of L and it further recites that L was entitled to deal with house in any manner she liked and who were directed to execute oral will must be presumed to have carried out his directions in accordance with his wishes - It seems clear that intention of testator was to benefit L and to confer upon her same title as he himself possessed - L therefore became absolute owner of property under terms of oral will of her father - Appeal allowed.
