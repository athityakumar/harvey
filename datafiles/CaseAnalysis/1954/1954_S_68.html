 Saghir Ahmad v State of Uttar Pradesh and Others

Supreme Court of India

13 October 1954

Case Analysis

Bench

Bijan Kumar Mukherjea, Mehr Chand Mahajan, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Vivian Bose, Ghulam Hasan

Where Reported

1954 Indlaw SC 121; AIR 1954 SC 728; 1955 ALJ 38; 1954 (2) MLJ(SC) 622; 1954 SCJ 819; [1955] 1 S.C.R. 707

Case Digest

Subject: Carriers & Transportation

Keywords: Common Carriers, Uttar Pradesh Road Transport Act, 1951

Summary: Carriers & Transportation - Constitution - Excise - Municipalities & Local Govts. - Motor Vehicles Act, 1939 - Uttar Pradesh Road Transport Act, 1951 - Public highway - Right to use - State control - Public utility - Constitutional rights - (A) whether right to use public highway for trade is in nature of easements Act or not? - Held, that right to use a public highway for purposes of trade is not in nature of an easement - It is provided by Constitution under art. 31 and as such cannot be reckoned as property in law -(A1) Whether right of public to use motor vehicles on public road is a special right given by Motor Vehicles Act or guaranteed under Constitution?- Further held, right of public to use motor vehicles on public road is not a right created by Motor Vehicles Act - Right exists in art. 19(1) (g) of Constitution - State only controls and regulates it - (A2) Whether doctrine of franchise applicable in India or not? - Further held, that American doctrine of franchise has no place in Indian Constitution - Under Indian Constitution contract carriers as well as common carriers would occupy same position so far as guaranteed right under art. 19(1)(g) is concerned - English law is enshrined in Indian Constitution with regard to nature and extent of right of public to use public highways -(B)Whether Constitution of India, 1953 art. 19(6) as amended by Constitution (First) Amendment Act, 1951 has retrospective effect or not?- Further held, that Constitution of India, 1953 art. 19(6) as amended by Constitution (First) Amendment has retrospective effect - A statute void for unconstitutionality is dead and cannot be vitalised by a subsequent amendment of Constitution removing constitutional objection - Validity of law in question is not to be decided by applying new clause introduced by Amendment - (C)Whether word 'restrictions' in various clauses of art. 19 of Constitution includes total deprivation of right or not? - Further held, that word 'restrictions' in art. 19 is not only used in sense of limitation or extinction but also includes total deprivation of right as well - (C1) Whether restrictions imposed by a particular legislation on exercise of fundamental right under art. 19(1)(g) are reasonable or not? - Further held, that restrictions imposed by a legislation on exercise of fundamental right under art 19(1)(g) are reasonable or not would depend on nature of trade and conditions prevalent in it - It is not enough that restrictions are for benefit of public - They must be reasonable as well and reasonableness could be decided only on a conspectus of all relevant facts and circumstances -(C2)Whether an enactment on face of it is found to violate a fundamental right guaranteed under art. 19(1)(g) of Constitution is valid or not? - Further held, that it must be held to be invalid - Unless those who support legislation can bring it within purview of exception laid in Constitution - There is undoubtedly a presumption in favour of constitutionality of a legislation - (C3)Whether Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19(6) as amended by Constitution (First) Amendment Act 1951 creates state monopoly in trade or not? - Further held, that a State can create a monopoly in its own favour in respect of any trade - But amendment does not make establishment of such monopoly a reasonable restriction within meaning of cl. 1 of art. 19(6) - State would not have to justify such action as reasonable at all in a court of law and no objection could be taken to it on ground that it is an infringement of right guaranteed under art. 19(1)(g) of Constitution - (C4) Whether U P State Road Transport Act,1951, s. 3 infringes art. 14 of Constitution? - Further held, that U P State Road Transport Act does not infringe art. 14 of Constitution - S. 3 of Act merely authorizes State Govt. to declare that road transport service in general or on particular routes should be run and operated by State Govt. - On a proper construction of this section State is only to choose routes or portions of routes on which private citizens would be allowed to operate and number of persons to whom permits should be given - Granting of permits would necessarily be regulated by provisions of Motor Vehicles Act - (C5) Whether Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 301 and 19(1)(g) and U P State Road Transport Act,1951 conflicts with guarantee of freedom of inter-state and intra-state trade, commerce and intercourse provided u/art. 301 ?-Further held, that points, raised and different views that are possible in respect of issue is indicated for purposes of future legislation -Appeal allowed.
