 Supreme Court Advocates-On-Record Association and Others v Union of India

Supreme Court of India

06 October 1993

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Ratnavel Pandian, A.M. Ahmadi, A.S. Anand, Kuldip Singh, M.M. Punchhi, S.P. Bharucha, Yogeshwar Dayal, G.N. Ray, J.S. Verma

Where Reported

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution

Summary: Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950 - Primacy of the opinion of the Chief Justice of India in regard to the appointments of Judges to the SC and the HC, and in regard to the transfers of HC Judges/Chief Justices - Whether the opinion of the Chief Justice of India in regard to the appointment of Judges to the SC and HCs as well as in regard to the transfer of HCs Judges, is entitled to primacy.

Held, art. 124(2) and 217(1) of the Constitution impose a mandate on the highest functionaries drawn from the Executive and the Judiciary to perform the constitutional obligation of making appointments of Judges to the SC and the HCs collectively in consultation with each other. In the event of disagreement in the process of consultation, the viewpoint of Judiciary being primal, has to be preferred. The majority view in S.P. Gupta v Union Of India & Anr. 1981 Indlaw SC 599 giving primacy to the Central Govt. in the matter of appointment of Judges to the superior courts does not lay down correct law and is over-ruled to that extent. The expression 'President' in arts. 124(2) and 217(1) of Constitution when read with art. 74(1) of Constitution makes the President to act on the advice of the Council of Ministers with the Prime Minister as the head. The Prime Minister and the Council of Ministers are bound to tender the advice in accordance with the interpretation given by this Court to arts. 124(2) and 217(1) of the Constitution. The Process of consultation under art. 124(2) of Constitution means consultation with the Chief Justice of India as head of the Judiciary. The opinion of the Chief Justice of India is not his individual but formed collectively by a body of men at the apex level of the Judiciary. Such collectively shall consist of the Chief Justice of India, two senior-most Judges of the SC and the senior SC Judge who comes from the State. The Process of appointment under art. 217(1) of Constitution is to begin with the recommendation of the Chief Justice of the HC. He must ascertain the views of the two senior-most Judges of the HC and incorporate the same in his recommendation. The Chief Justice of India while examining the recommendation must take into account the views of two senior-most Judges of the SC and also the opinion of the senior Judge conversant with the affairs of the concerned HC. The opinion of the Chief Justice of India, forwarded in the manner shall be primal. No appointment can be made by the President under arts. 124(2) and 217(1) of the Constitution unless it is in conformity with the opinion of the Chief Justice of India. The Chief Justice of India shall be appointed on the basis of 'selection by merit' and 'seniority alone' rule shall not be applicable. The appointment to the SC shall be by 'selection on merit'. Inter-se seniority amongst Judges in their respective HCs has to be kept in view while considering the Judges for elevation to the Supreme Court. The combined seniority on all India basis shall be relevant in the process of consideration. The outweighing factor of merit would justify the elevation of a junior Judge from the same HC. The Executive may not appoint a recommendee of the Judiciary if considered unsuitable for good reasons based on the material available on record and placed before-the Chief Justice of India. However, if after due consideration the recommendation is reiterated by the Chief Justice of India with the unanimous agreement of other judicial consultees then the Executive is bound by the recommendation. A Chief Justice/Justice may be transferred from one HC to another art. 222 of Constitution in public interest. A transferred Chief Justice/Judge can he transferred again and the power is not exhausted after the first transfer. The consent of the Chief Justice/Judge concerned is not required under the Constitution. S.P. Gupta's case stands overruled to the extent. A proposal for transfer of a Chief Justice/Judge under art. 222 of Constitution has to be initiated by the Chief Justice of India and the ultimate recommendation in that respect is binding on the Executive. The transfer of a Chief Justice/Judge is not justifiable in the court of law except on the ground that the transfer was made without the recommendation of the Chief Justice of India. Strength in the HCs is justifiable. The proposal made by the Chief Justice of a State for increasing the strength of the High Court, if it has the concurrence of the Chief Justice of India, is binding on the Executive. S.P. Gupta's case overruled to the extent. Reference disposed of.
