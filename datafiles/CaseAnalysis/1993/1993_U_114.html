T. Velayudhan Achari and Another v Union of India and Others

Supreme Court of India

05 February 1993

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Mohan, L.M. Sharma, N. Venkatachala

Where Reported

1993 Indlaw SC 1126; (1993) 2 SCC 582; AIR 1993 SCW 1201; 1993 (1) BC 564; [1993] 77 Comp Cas 197; JT 1993 (1) SC 580; 1993 (2) MLJ(SC) 90; 1993(1) SCALE 586; [1993] 1 S.C.R. 832; 1993 (1) UJ 608

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance

Keywords: Banking Companies (Amendment) Act, 1959, Banking Laws (Amendment) Act, 1983, Banking Laws (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 1963, Banking Laws (Amendment) Act, 1968

Summary: Banking & Finance - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 19 and 20(1) - Reserve Bank of India Act, 1934, Chap III-C, ss. 45-I(bb), 45-SS, 58-A and 58B(5-A) - Banking Laws (Amendment) Act, 1983 - Banking Companies (Acceptance of Deposits) Rules, 1975 - Kerala Money-Lenders Act, 1958, s. 4(2)(iii) - (A) Whether Chapter III-C r/w s. 58-A of RBI Act, 1934, introduced by Banking Laws (Amendment) Act, 1983 constitutionally valid in as much as s. 45-B restricts number of depositors and rate of interest u/s. 4(2)(iii) of Kerala Money-Lenders Act? - U/s. 45-I(bb) deposit has been defined; if as per definition there are enough sources of deposit there is no reason why appellants cannot reduce deposits; therefore, if package is reasonable there is no justification to dilute effect of s. 58-A - Held, approving reasoning of High Court in Kanta Mehta's case [ 1985 Indlaw DEL 149 (Del)], no doubt, impugned legislation places restrictions on right of appellants to carry on business, but what is essential is to safeguard rights of various depositors and to see that they are not preyed upon; a ceiling for acceptance of deposits and to require maintenance of certain liquidity of funds as well as not to exceed borrowing's beyond a particular percentage of net-owned funds have been provided in corporate sector, but for these requirements, depositors would be left high and dry without any remedy; maybe, Kerala Act restricts rates of interest u/s. 4(2)(iii) but that cannot enable Writ Petitioners in W P No's 508 and 534 of 1988 to disregard these provisions, being non-banking financial institution - (B) Whether s. 58-B(5-A) is violative of art. 20(1) of Constitution? - When a similar argument was raised against s. 58-A of Companies Act, that was repelled by Supreme Court in Delhi Cloth and General Mills Co. Ltd. [1983 Indlaw SC 270] holding that insertion of proviso to r. 3 does not make rule retroactive; as to plight of these depositors, it is said in Peerless General Finance and Investment Co. Ltd. case [ 1987 Indlaw SC 175] that while on one hand these schemes encourage two vice affecting public economy, desire to make quick and easy money and habit of excessive and wasteful consumer spending, on other hand of investors who generally belong to gullible and less affluent classes have no security whatsoever, Action appears imperative - Appeals and Petitions dismissed.
