Jiwan Dass v Life Insurance Corporation of India and Another

Supreme Court of India

28 September 1993

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Ramaswamy, N.P. Singh

Where Reported

1993 Indlaw SC 676; (1994) Supp3 SCC 694; 1995 (1) RCJ 541; 1995 (1) RCR(Rent) 530; 1995 (2) RentLR 533

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Summary: Civil Procedure - Transfer of Property Act, 1882, s.106 - Public Premises (Eviction of Unauthorised Occupants) Act, 1971, s.5(1) - Unauthorized occupant - Eviction - Legality - Appellant was inducted in 1949 as a tenant on the ground floor of the premises on monthly tenancy at a rent of Rs. 15 excluding electricity and water charges - Notice was issued to the appellant determining the tenancy u/s.106 of 1882 Act and, therefore, action was initiated u/s.5(1) of 1971 Act for eviction of the appellant from unauthorised occupation - Appellant filed writ petition in HC - DB of HC summarily dismissed it - Appellant contended that the respondent/Corporation being a public authority, before initiating the action u/s.5 of the Act, was enjoined to assign reasons which should be just and germane for the purpose of its exercise and its reasonableness should be tested on the touchstone of art.14 of the Constitution - Appellant further contended that unless the public authority justified its action taken u/s.5(1) of 1971 Act before determining the tenancy u/s.106 of 1882 Act, it was violative of art.14 of the Constitution - Held, s.106 of 1882 Act did indicate that the landlord was entitled to terminate the tenancy by giving 15 days' notice, if it was a premises occupied on monthly tenancy and by giving 6 months' notice if the premises were occupied for agricultural or manufacturing purposes, and on expiry thereof proceedings could be initiated - S.106 of the 1882 Act did not contemplate of giving any reason for terminating the tenancy - Equally the definition of the public premises 'unauthorised occupation' u/s.2(g) of 1971 Act postulated that the tenancy was determined for any reason whatsoever - When the statute had advisedly given wide powers to the public authorities under 1971 Act to determine the tenancy, it was not permissible to cut down the width of the powers by reading into it the reasonable and justifiable grounds for initiating action for terminating the tenancy u/s.106 of 1882 Act - If it was so s.106 of 1882 Act and s.2(g) of 1971 Act would become ultra vires - Statute advisedly empowered the authority to act in the public interest and determine the tenancy or leave or licence before taking action u/s.5 of 1971 Act - If the contention of the appellant was given acceptance he would be put on a higher pedestal than a statutory tenant under the 1977 Act - For example that a premises was let out at a low rent years back like the instant case - Rent would be unrealistic - With a view to revise adequate market rent, tenant would become liable to ejectment - Action would be violative of art.21 of Constitution offending right to livelihood - Such contention too was devoid of any substance - Owner was entitled to deal with his property in his own way profitable in its use and occupation - Public authority was equally entitled to use the public property to the best advantage as a commercial venture - As an integral incidence of ejectment of a tenant/licensee was inevitable - So the doctrine of livelihood could not indiscriminately be extended to the area of commercial operation - Appeal dismissed.
