Union of India and Others v Hindustan Development Corporation and Others

Supreme Court of India

15 April 1993

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Jayachandra Reddy, G.N. Ray

Where Reported

1993 Indlaw SC 1085; (1993) 3 SCC 499; AIR 1994 SC 988; AIR 1993 SCW 494; JT 1993 (3) SC 15; 1993(2) SCALE 506; [1993] 3 S.C.R. 128; 1993 (2) UJ 365

Case Digest

Subject: Contract & Commercial

Keywords: Freedom Of Speech And Expression

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Constitution of India, 1950 - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Freedom of speech and expression - Railway Board - Manufacturers - Tender Committee - Railway Board entered into contracts with the manufacturers for the supply of cast steel bogies every year which were used in turn for building the wagons - There were 12 suppliers in the field who had been regularly supplying these items - Two new firms entered into the field -Limited tender notice for procurement of 19000 cast steel bogies were issued to the regular suppliers as well as the above two new entrants - It was also stated therein that the price was subject to the price variation clause and that the Railways reserved the right to order additional quantity upto 30% of the ordered quantity during the currency of the contract on the same price and terms and conditions with suitable extensions in delivery period - Offers were to remain open for a period of 90 days - On that day the tenders were opened in the presence of all parties - Price were quoted by three manufacturers - After the tenders were opened and before the same could be finalised, the Govt. of India announced two major concessions namely reduction of custom duty on the import of steel scrap and dispensation of freight equalisation fund for steel - Tenders were put up and placed before the Tender Committee of the Railways which considered all the aspects - HC at an interlocutory stage pending the W.P.S. passed an order directing the Ministry to accept the allocation of bogies recommended by the Tender Committee and to pay a price at the rate of Rs. 67,000 only per bogie and that would be subject to the final decision of the W.P.S - Being aggrieved by this order, the Railways filed a petition for special leave to appeal and this Court while refusing to interfere at that interlocutory stage - Whether such a stand taken by the Govt. as a policy, was unfair and arbitrary as to warrant interference by the courts - Held, though ordinarily a private individual would be guided by economic considerations of self- gain any action taken by him, it was always open to under the law to act contrary to his self-interest or to oblige another in entering into a contract or dealing with his property - But the Govt. was not free to act as it likes in granting largess such as awarding a contractor selling or leasing out its property - Whatever be its activity, the Govt. was still the Govt. and was, subject to restraints inherent in its position in a democratic society - Every action taken by the Govt. must be in public interest; the Govt. cannot act arbitrarily and without reason and if it does, its action would be liable to be invalidated - If the Govt. awarded a contract of leases out or otherwise dealt with its property or grants any other largess, it would be liable to be tested for its validity on the touch-stone of reasonableness and public interest and if it fails to satisfy either test, it would be unconstitutional and invalid - Petitions dismissed.
