Dharampal and Others v Ramshri and Others

Supreme Court of India

07 January 1993

Case Analysis

Bench

P.B. Sawant, R.M. Sahai

Where Reported

1993 Indlaw SC 1325; (1993) SCC (Cr) 333; (1993) 1 SCC 435; AIR 1993 SC 1361; 1993 ALJ 691; 1993 (1) AnWR 60; 1993 (1) BLJR 361; 1993 (1) CCR 47; 1993 (1) Crimes 304; 1993 CRLJ 1049; 1993 CrLR(SC) 189; JT 1993 (1) SC 61; 1993 (1) RCR(Rent) 696; 1993(1) SCALE 12; [1993] 1 S.C.R. 1

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Criminal

Summary: Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 145, 146, 397(3) and 482 - Order of attachment - Withdrawal by the Magistrate - Jurisdiction of - Whether Magistrate can withdraw order of attachment passed by him even during pendency of dispute in Civil Court? - Held, Magistrate can withdraw the order of attachment passed by him even during the tendency of the dispute in the Civil Court is made further clear by the provisions of sub-section (2) of s. 146 - When the Civil Court appoints a receiver, the order of attachment passed by the Magistrate necessarily gives way to the order of the Civil Court appointing the receiver - It is only when the Civil Court does not appoint the receiver that the Magistrate may make arrangements for looking after the property or even appoint a receiver himself - However, even when such a receiver is appointed and the Civil Court subsequently appoints a receiver of its own the Magistrate has to order the receiver appointed by him to hand over the possession of the subject in dispute to the receiver appointed by the Civil Court and discharge the receiver appointed by him - Magistrate has also the pass such other incidental or consequential orders as he thinks just - Such order may include an order of withdrawal of the attachment in views of the seizure of the matter by the Civil Court and the consequence want of apprehension of breach of the peace - It is, therefore, not correct to say as held by the High Court that the property continues to remain under attachment of the Magisterial order till the rights of the parties are decided finally by the competent court of law - That appears to be the purport of the HC's order since in the present case the Appellate Civil Court has already passed an order of injunction against respondent 1 and her husband by virtue of which the possession on continues to be with the appellants - Therefore, the Magistrate had not erred in withdrawing the attachment by his order - The impugned order of the HC is set aside - Appeal allowed.
