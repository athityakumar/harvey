Kundula Bala Subrahmanyam and Another v State of Andhra Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

26 March 1993

Case Analysis

Bench

A.S. Anand, N.P. Singh

Where Reported

1993 Indlaw SC 194; (1993) SCC (Cr) 655; (1993) 2 SCC 684; AIR 1993 SCW 1321; 1993 CRLJ 1635; JT 1993 (2) SC 559; [1993] 2 S.C.R. 666

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Bride Burning

Summary: Criminal - Supreme Court (Enlargement of Criminal Appellate Jurisdiction) Act, 1970, s. 2(a) - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 302 and 34 - Bride burning - Circumstantial evidence - Appellants acquitted by Trial Court but convicted by High Court on appeal by Govt. - Appellants surrendered before court themselves and claimed it as a ground of innocence - Hence, present appeal - (A) Motive - In a case based on circumstantial evidence, motive assumes great significance as its existence is an enlightening factor in a process of presumptive reasoning - Held, motive in this case is alleged to be greed of dowry - Deceased not transferring land in name of appellants was enough motive to commit offence - Prosecution succeeded in establishing existence of motive for both appellants to commit crime conclusively and positively - (B) Dying declaration - Dying declaration made by person on the verge of his death has a special sanctity as at that solemn moment, a person is most unlikely to make any untrue statement - Once statement of dying person and evidence of witnesses testifying to same passes the test of careful scrutiny of the courts, it becomes a very important and a reliable piece of evidence - If court is satisfied that dying declaration is true and free from any embellishment such a dying declaration, by itself, can be sufficient for recording conviction even without looking for any corroboration - Held, prosecution successfully established a very crucial piece of circumstantial evidence in the case that the deceased had voluntarily made dying declaration implicating both appellants and disclosing the manner in which she had been put on fire shortly before her death - Dying declaration given by deceased believable - Evidence of witnesses corroborate each other - No attempt by appellant husband to save his deceased wife - Chain of circumstantial evidence complete - Case against appellants proved beyond reasonable doubt - Appeal dismissed.
