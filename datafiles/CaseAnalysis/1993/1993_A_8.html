Ferro Alloys Corporation Limited v A. P. State Electricity Board and Another

Supreme Court of India

15 April 1993

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Mohan, K. Jayachandra Reddy

Where Reported

1993 Indlaw SC 1084; (1993) Supp4 SCC 136; AIR 1993 SC 2005; AIR 1993 SCW 2025; JT 1993 (3) SC 82; 1993(2) SCALE 593; [1993] 3 S.C.R. 199

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Electricity & Energy

Summary: (A) Constitution - Electricity & Energy - Constitution of India, 1950 - Electricity (Supply) Act, 1948 - Electricity Act, 2003 - Interest Act, 1978 - Security Deposit - Interest rate - Condition of supply - Tariff - State electricity boards (Electricity Board) supplied power to high tension consumers - Each of the Electricity Boards framed terms and conditions of supply of electricity to high tension consumer - One such condition related to security deposits, which varied from Electricity Board to Electricity Board of each State -Consumers contended that s. 49 of the 1948 Act was not valid as it did not contain any norm of guideline with regard to framing of terms and conditions for the supply of electricity and in particular, the demand of payment of interest on the amounts due to the Electricity Board - They also urged that the consumption deposit should not exceed two months average consumption charges - And that interest paid on the deposit should be at the same rate as was paid by the schedule bank on fixed deposits - Electricity Boards contended that while it was required to make colossal advances to generate electricity and supply to consumers, the consumers also used and consumed electricity on credit ranging from two to three months depending upon the category of consumers - To off-set part of the amount that the consumer owed to the Electricity Board constantly and also to ensure timely payment of advances by the Electricity Board to its suppliers an advance consumption deposit was insisted upon before commencing supply to the consumer - If this was not so taken the Electricity Board would be left with no other option than to increase the tariff - The advance deposit could not be termed as a fixed deposit as the amount could not be utilized against non-payment of dues from consumers - And the consumers could also ask for the refund -It was submitted that ss. 49(1) and 79 (j) of the 1948 Act could not be termed as arbitrary - Whether s. 49 of the 1948 Act was bad for want of guidelines - Held, the Electricity Board was conferred statutory powers u/s. 49(1) of the 1948 Act to determine the terms and conditions for supplying electricity - Terms and conditions had to be reasonable in that they should be related to object and purpose for which they were issued - Power u/s. 49 of the 1948 Act could not be used for oblique purposes or for purposes unrelated to the ones sought to be achieved by a given condition - In exercise of this power the Electricity had initially introduced the condition regarding security and each of the petitioners had accepted the terms - Demand for security from the consumer was in accordance with the regulations framed by the Electricity Board and could not be said to unreasonable merely because no interest was paid on security deposited by consumer - Thus, the terms and conditions notified u/. 49 of the 1948 Act related to the object and purpose for which they were issued - That power was not exercised for a collateral purpose - Accordingly, s. 49 of the 1948 Act was valid - Petitions disposed of.

(B) Constitution - Electricity & Energy - Constitution of India, 1950 - Electricity (Supply) Act, 1948 - Electricity Act, 2003 - Interest Act, 1978 - Security Deposit - Interest rate - Condition of supply - Tariff - What was the nature of consumption security deposit - Held, the nature of consumption deposit was to secure prompt payment and was intended for appropriation - It was clear that while the Electricity Board was required to make colossal advances to generate electricity and supply to consumers, the consumers used and consumed electricity on credit ranging from two to three months depending upon the category of consumers - To off-set part of the amount the consumer owed to the Electricity Board continually to ensure timely payment of bills by the Electricity Board to its suppliers, the advance consumption deposit was required to be kept with the Electricity Board before commencing supply to the consumer - Clauses in the contract in relation to conditions of supply of electric energy enabled the Electricity Board to adjust the bill against such deposits - Therefore, this was not a case of mere deposit of money as in commercial transaction - Petitions disposed of.

(C) Constitution - Electricity & Energy - Constitution of India, 1950 - Electricity (Supply) Act, 1948 - Electricity Act, 2003 - Interest Act, 1978 - Security Deposit - Advance Consumption deposit - Interest rate - Condition of supply - Tariff - Whether interest was payable on security deposit or advance consumption deposit - Held, there was no liability on the Electricity Board either under the statute or common law or equity to pay interest - There was no statutory provision which caste an obligation on the Electricity Board to pay interest on the security deposit either under the scheme of the 1947 Act or under Schedule VI of the 1948 Act - Further, the 1978 Act was also not applicable - The deposit made could not be equated to a fixed deposit - In the case of daily supply of electricity, there was a consequential liability to pay for each day's consumption of electricity - To ensure that payment, the security deposit was furnished - Hence, it could not be equated to a deposit at all - It was in the nature of a running current account - Petitions disposed of.

(D) Constitution - Electricity & Energy - Constitution of India, 1950 - Electricity (Supply) Act, 1948 - Electricity Act, 2003 - Interest Act, 1978 - Security Deposit - Advance Consumption deposit - Interest rate - Condition of supply - Tariff - Whether the clause providing for non-payment of interest was unconstitutional or arbitrary - Held, clause not providing for interest on the security deposit was neither arbitrary nor palpably unreasonable, nor even unconscionable - Consumer made the security deposit in consideration of the performance of its obligation for obtaining the service which was essential to it - Electricity supply was made to the consumers on credit - Billing time taken by the Electricity Board was to the advantage of the consumer - Moreover, consumer with open eyes entered into the agreement and undertook to abide by the terms regarding non-payment of interest - He could not resile from the condition because there was nothing inherently objectionable about such a condition nor was such a condition per se illegal or void as opposed to public policy - It was not uncommon in commercial transaction, such a provision is entered into - U/s. 49 of the 1948 Act, the Electricity Board was entitled to insist upon terms and conditions that it sought fit - Consumption security deposit whether or not it carried interest was a condition precedent for the supply of electric energy - And in imposing such a condition the Electricity Board had not acted as a private trader - Nature of deposit had a rational relationship to the object which was incorporated as a condition of supply - And insistence of a security deposit had a direct bearing on the operations of the Electricity Board - Also, the Electricity Board was not a commercial organization to which the consumer had furnished the security to earn interest - Petitions disposed of.

(E) Constitution - Electricity & Energy - Constitution of India, 1950 - Electricity (Supply) Act, 1948 - Electricity Act, 2003 - Interest Act, 1978 - Security Deposit - Advance Consumption deposit - Interest rate - Condition of supply - Tariff - Additional consumer deposit - Whether the demand for additional consumer deposit was valid - Held, no reason needed to be given for enhancement of additional security deposit - It stood to reason that if there was a revision in the rate of tariff there would also be an upward revision in the consumption security deposit since it had direct bearing to the level of supply in consumption of electricity - This being a condition of supply, no reason needed to be given at the time of upward revision in the consumption security deposit since it had direct bearing to the level of supply in consumption of electricity - Petitions disposed of.
