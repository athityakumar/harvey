Agnigundala Venkata Ranga Rao v Indukuru Ramachandra Reddy (Dead) by Lrs. and others

Supreme Court of India

13 April 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Abhay Manohar Sapre, Navin Sinha

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 313; (2017) 7 SCC 694; AIR 2017 SC 2042; 2017 (4) ALD(SC) 67; 2017(4) ALL MR 936; JT 2017 (6) SC 11; 2017(4) SCALE 530

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Land & Property

Keywords: Andhra Pradesh Land Reforms (Ceiling on Agricultural Holdings) Act, 1973, Andhra Pradesh Rights in Land and Pattadar Pass Books Act, 1971, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, Constitution of India, 1950

Summary: Land & Property - Administrative - Andhra Pradesh Land Reforms (Ceiling on Agricultural Holdings) Act, 1973, ss. 10, 11, 17, 8, 3(o), 7, 7(2) - Ceiling on Land - Declaration - Challenged - Appellant/plaintiff owner of suit land filed application u/s. 7 of the Act for declaration in respect of excess holding - Trial Court in suit by plaintiff held sale of suit land as null and void and decreed suit against defendant - On appeal, HC allowed appeal and set aside order of Trial Court - Hence, instant appeal - Whether appellant was entitled to claim permanent injunction against respondents in relation to suit land.

Held, appellant simply abused the process of law in filing suit for permanent injunction in relation to suit land against respondents. Further, it was a settled principle of law that in order to claim prohibitory (temporary or permanent) injunction, it was necessary for plaintiff to prima facie prove apart from establishing other two ingredients, namely, irreparable loss and injury that his possession over suit land was "legal". In instant case, Tribunal had already declared the land held by plaintiff to be in excess of ceiling limits prescribed under the Act. In those circumstances, plaintiff was neither holding the land nor could he be held to be in its lawful possession so as to enable him to exercise any ownership rights against any other private party over suit land. SC consider it apposite to state that appellant and respondents made frantic efforts to retain suit land to them and keep the land away from the clutches of the Act. With that aim they got the suit land involved in litigation since 1976. All that was done without notice to State Authorities. It was a collusive suit filed to frustrate the rights of State which had accrued in State's favour in suit land by virtue of the order read with the provisions of the Act. Such frivolous suit deserved rejection at its threshold. SC direct Tribunal to take up the case of appellant on its Board and pass appropriate consequential order, Thus, conclusion arrived at by HC was upheld. Appeal dismissed.
