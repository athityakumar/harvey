Vipulbhai Mansingbhai Chaudhary v State of Gujarat and another

Supreme Court of India

17 April 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

J. Chelameswar, Abhay Manohar Sapre

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 355; AIR 2017 SC 2340; JT 2017 (6) SC 422; 2017 (4) MLJ 597; 2017(4) SCALE 637

Case Digest

Subject: Trusts & Associations

Keywords: Customs Act, 1962, Central Excises And Salt Act, 1944, General Clauses Act, 1897, Gujarat Co-operative Societies Act, 1961, Gujarat General Clauses Act, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Gujarat Co-operative Societies (Amendment) Act, 2015

Summary: (A) Trusts & Associations - Gujarat Co-operative Societies Act, 1961, ss. 2(14), 2(19), 2(5), 73, 76(B)(1)(2), 76B, 76B(1), 76B(2), 81 - Show cause notice - Challenged - Appellant was elected as Chairman of Society for a period of 3 years - Show-cause notice was issued on 12-1-2015 (Show-Cause Notice-I) by Registrar to remove appellant from the office - Appellant challenged show-cause notice by way of a writ petition before HC - HC upheld action of Registrar pertaining to removal of appellant however set aside order of disqualification of appellant for a period of 3 years - Registrar issued a fresh show-cause notice dt. 3-10-2015 (Show-Cause Notice-II) calling upon appellant to explain as to why he should not be disqualified for a future period u/s. 76B(2) of the Act - Appellant challenged Show-Cause Notice-II before HC - HC dismissed writ petition holding that Show-Cause Notice-II was untenable on ground that when HC did not record that Registrar was permitted to issue a fresh show-cause notice proposing action u/s. 76B(2) of the Act - Hence, instant appeals - Whether Show-Cause Notice-II could not have been issued in the absence of grant of any leave by HC to issue a fresh show-cause notice u/s. 76B(2) of the Act.

Held, both in show-cause notice-II and order Registrar repeated all the allegations which formed the basis for the order u/s. 76B(1) of the Act which was not really necessary. It would have sufficed if Registrar mentioned the fact that appellant was removed from the office of Chairman of Society in exercise of power u/s. 76B(1) of the Act the mention of such a fact was not mandatory. However, mentioning of past history though avoidable does not in any way vitiate show-cause notice or final order if they were in accordance with law. Therefore, there was no legal infirmity either in the logic adopted by Registrar or action taken by him u/s. 76B(2) of the Act on ground that Registrar relied upon the same set of facts and conclusions drawn for taking action both u/ss. 76B(1) and 76B(2) of the Act. The final order disqualifying appellant was passed after due compliance with the principles of natural justice. If Registrar came to the conclusion that appellant should not be permitted to contest any election or hold any office in any society functioning under the Act, the same, can't be found fault with. As appellant was found guilty of conduct which resulted in a large scale financial irregularities in the conduct of business of society and financial loss to society. Disqualifying appellant only from the holding post in Society was to give him a chance to meddle with the affairs of societies which were members of UNION. Appeals disposed of.

(B) Trusts & Associations - Gujarat Co-operative Societies Act, 1961, ss. 2(14), 2(19), 2(5), 73, 76(B)(1)(2), 76B, 76B(1), 76B(2), 81 - Disqualification - Applicability - Whether the period of disqualification of 6 years was consistent with law.

Held, s. 76B(2) of the Act provides for disqualification of an officer for a period not exceeding 6 years. Repeal could be either of the entire enactment or a part of it. Substitution of parts of an enactment is nothing but pro tanto to repeal those parts. No right or liability could be created by a repealing enactment, which was inconsistent with the rights and obligations conferred under the repealed Act unless the repealing enactment makes an express declaration to that effect or adopts some other technique known to law to achieve that purpose. Giving retrospective effect to the repealing enactment is one of the techniques by which the legislature seeks to achieve that purpose. There was nothing in the Act which warrants an interpretation that legislature intended to create a disqualification which would run for a maximum period of 6 years with retrospective effect. Thus, the disqualification of 6 years on appellant was not tenable and at best appellant could be disqualified for a maximum period of 4 years. Appeals disposed of.
