 Krishna Kumar Singh and another v State of Bihar and others

Supreme Court of India

02 January 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, Sharad Arvind Bobde, Adarsh Kumar Goel, Uday Umesh Lalit, D.Y. Chandrachud, T.S. Thakur, L. Nageswara Rao

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 10; (2017) 3 SCC 1; 2017 (1) AWC 639; 2017 (1) CLT(SC) 159; JT 2017 (1) SC 67; 2017 (1) MLJ 698; 2017(1) SCALE 178

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: Limitation, Special Provision, Subordinate Legislation, State Legislature, General Clauses Act, 1897, Legislative Power, Government of India Act, 1935, Elections, Conduct Of Business, Constitution (Forty-Second Amendment) Act, 1976, Rules Of Procedure, Proclamation Of Emergency, Legislative Assembly, Government of India Act, 1915, Preventive Detention Act, 1950, Preventive Detention (Amendment) Act, 1951, Emergency Powers (Defence) Act, 1939, Constitution (Thirty-Eighth Amendment) Act, 1975, Indian Councils Act, 1861, Constitution of India, 1950, Orissa Municipal Elections Validation Ordinance, 1959, Governor General's Act

Summary: Madan B. Lokur, J.

(A) Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 123, 14, 154, 168, 200, 213(1), 213(2), 213(2)(a), 213(2)(b), 213(3), 249, 250, 304(b), 32, 352(1), 352(4), 356, 356(1), 356(3), 357(2), 359(1A), 360, 360(2), 367(2) - Rules of Procedure and Conduct of Business in the Bihar Vidhan Sabha, r. 10 - Ordinances issued - Legislature approval - Validity - Governor promulgated the first of Ordinances providing for taking over of 429 Sanskrit schools in State and services of teachers and other employees of school were transferred to State - However, none of the Ordinances issued in exercise of power of Governor u/art. 213 of Constitution were placed before State legislature as mandated - Petitioners/teachers filed writ petitions before HC seeking for payment of salaries which was dismissed holding that the repeated repromulgation of ordinances was unconstitutional - On appeal, two bench judge of SC had difference of opinion in their assessment of Constitutional validity of first Ordinance, one of them holding that it was invalid while the other held it to be constitutional - On further appeal before three bench judges of SC it was referred to a Bench of five judges on the ground that it raised substantial questions relating to constitution - Hence, instant reference - Whether it was mandatory for an Ordinance to be laid before Legislative Assembly and the consequence if it was not so laid.

Held, on a reading of art. 213(2) of Constitution it was not mandatory that an Ordinance should be laid before Legislative Assembly of State Legislature. Constitution does not make it mandatory for Executive to lay an Ordinance promulgated by Governor of State before Legislative Assembly however, State Legislatures would need to be more vigilant and proactive in keeping a check on Executive riding roughshod over democratic requirements and exert their constitutional supremacy over Executive. When an Ordinance was promulgated it was printed in the Official Gazette and every legislator was aware of its promulgation. As far as State Legislature was concerned, u/r. 10 of the Rules a printed copy of the Ordinance was required to be made available to all Members of Legislative Assembly by its Secretary. Therefore, on reassembly of Legislative Assembly, any Member might move a resolution for disapproving Ordinance either on the basis of Official Gazette or on the basis of a printed copy of Ordinance made available by Secretary of Legislative Assembly. Consequently, even if the Executive does not lay the Ordinance before State Legislature or if Secretary of Legislative Assembly does not supply a printed copy of Ordinance, a Member of Legislative Assembly was not helpless. Surely, his right to move a Resolution for disapproving the Ordinance cannot be taken away by that subterfuge. Such right of a Member of Legislative Assembly cannot be made dependent on Executive laying the Ordinance before State Legislature, nor can such right be taken away by the Executive by simply not laying the Ordinance before Legislative Assembly. Thus, even without making the laying of an Ordinance before State Legislature mandatory, Constitution does provide adequate checks and balances against a possible misuse of power by Executive. Reference answered.

(B) Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 123, 14, 154, 168, 200, 213(1), 213(2), 213(2)(a), 213(2)(b), 213(3), 249, 250, 304(b), 32, 352(1), 352(4), 356, 356(1), 356(3), 357(2), 359(1A), 360, 360(2), 367(2) - Orissa Municipal Elections Validation Ordinance, 1959 - Withdrawal of ordinance - Reassembly of Legislature - Relevancy - Whether Executive might in view of the provisions of art. 213(2)(b) of Constitution advise Governor of State to withdraw an Ordinance at any time before reassembly of State Legislature or even after reassembly.

Held, Executive was entitled to in view of the provisions of art. 213(2)(b) of Constitution advise Governor of State to withdraw an Ordinance at any time before reassembly of State Legislature or after its reassembly but before it was laid before Legislative Assembly. In such situations, no purpose would be served by laying a withdrawn Ordinance before State Legislature except perhaps completing an empty formality. Constitution was not been framed for the sake of completing empty formalities. That was an additional reason for holding that there was no mandatory requirement that regardless of circumstances, an Ordinance should mandatorily be placed before State Legislature. The reasons for withdrawal of an Ordinance by Governor at the instance of Executive, whether before or after reassembly of State Legislature were not relevant for instant discussion and it was not necessary to go into them. Reference answered.

(C) Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 123, 14, 154, 168, 200, 213(1), 213(2), 213(2)(a), 213(2)(b), 213(3), 249, 250, 304(b), 32, 352(1), 352(4), 356, 356(1), 356(3), 357(2), 359(1A), 360, 360(2), 367(2) - Orissa Municipal Elections Validation Ordinance, 1959 - Ordinance before Legislature - Mandatory requirement - Whether Executive might u/art. 213(2)(a) of Constitution lay an Ordinance before Legislative Assembly of State Legislature and what could happen thereafter.

Held, there was no mandatory requirement that an Ordinance should be laid before Legislative Assembly on its reassembly. The fate of an Ordinance, whether it was laid before Legislative Assembly or not, was governed entirely by the provisions of art. 213(2)(a) of Constitution and by Legislative Assembly. The limited control that Executive has over the fate of an Ordinance after it was promulgated was that of its withdrawal by Governor of State u/art. 213(2)(b) of Constitution the rest of control was with State Legislature which was the law making body of State. In the absence of any challenge to first three Ordinances and that those three Ordinances were valid, the benefit given to employees (such as salary and perks) by those Ordinances till they ceased to operate were justified. However, those three Ordinances did not and could not grant any enduring or irreversible right or benefits to the employees and the employees did not acquire any enduring or irreversible right or benefits under those three Ordinances. Any right or benefits acquired by them terminated when the Ordinances ceased to operate. Despite a specific challenge made to fourth and subsequent Ordinances, State was not justified their promulgation and were struck down. Thus, the directions given by HC for payment of salary (if not already paid) and interest thereon need not be disturbed. Reference answered.

T.S. Thakur, J.

(D) Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 123, 14, 154, 168, 200, 213(1), 213(2), 213(2)(a), 213(2)(b), 213(3), 249, 250, 304(b), 32, 352(1), 352(4), 356, 356(1), 356(3), 357(2), 359(1A), 360, 360(2), 367(2) - The Bihar Non-Government Sanskrit Schools (Taking Over of Management and Control) Ordinance, 1989 - Ordinance repromulgation - Validity - Whether seven successive repromulgations of the Ordinance suffer from any illegality or constitutional impropriety.

Held, repeated repromulgation of the ordinances was a fraud on Constitution especially when Govt. of the time appears to have persistently avoided the placement of ordinances before legislature. In light of the pronouncement of SC in Dr. D.C. Wadhwa And Ors. v. State Of Bihar And Ors., 1986 Indlaw SC 247 such repeated repromulgations were legally impermissible which was rightly declared by HC. The first, second and third ordinances stood repealed by subsequent ordinances issued by Govt. At any rate, since the process of issuing the ordinances and repromulgation thereof was in the nature of a single transaction and a part of a single series on the same subject the vice of invalidity attached to any such exercise of power would not spare the first, second and third ordinances which would like the subsequent ordinances be unconstitutional on the same principle. Those ordinances provided foundation for edifice of subsequent repromulgations. If edifice was affected, there was no way the foundation could remain unaffected by the vice of unconstitutionality. SC agree that the ordinances in question starting with Ordinance 32 of 1989 and ending with Ordinance 2 of 1992 were all constitutionally invalid, the fact that none of them was ever placed before State legislature as required u/art. 213 (2) of Constitution lending support to that conclusion. Reference answered.

(E) Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 123, 14, 154, 168, 200, 213(1), 213(2), 213(2)(a), 213(2)(b), 213(3), 249, 250, 304(b), 32, 352(1), 352(4), 356, 356(1), 356(3), 357(2), 359(1A), 360, 360(2), 367(2) - The Bihar Non-Government Sanskrit Schools (Taking Over of Management and Control) Ordinance, 1989 - Ordinances issued - Enduring rights - Effect of - Whether ordinances issued by Govt. in exercise of its powers u/arts. 213 or 123 of Constitution could create enduring rights in favour of individuals affected thereby.

Held, nature of power invoked for issuing ordinances does not admit of creation of enduring rights in favour of those affected by such ordinances. The Constitution Bench decision in State of Orrissa v. Bhupendra Kumar Bose, 1961 Indlaw SC 395 and T. Venkata Reddy Etc. Etc. v. State Of Andhra Pradesh, 1985 Indlaw SC 490, to the extent the same extended the theory of "creation of enduring rights" to legislation by ordinances have not been correctly decided and should stand overruled. It follows that the ordinances issued in instant case could not have created any enduring rights in favour of Sanskrit school teachers particularly when the ordinances themselves were a fraud on Constitution. HC and also the views expressed by esteemed brothers Madan B. Lokur and Chandrachud, JJ. on that aspect were legally unexceptionable. Reference answered.

(F) Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 123, 14, 154, 168, 200, 213(1), 213(2), 213(2)(a), 213(2)(b), 213(3), 249, 250, 304(b), 32, 352(1), 352(4), 356, 356(1), 356(3), 357(2), 359(1A), 360, 360(2), 367(2) - The Bihar Non-Government Sanskrit Schools (Taking Over of Management and Control) Ordinance, 1989 - Benefit of salaries - Reversed - Entitled to - Whether the benefit of salaries drawn by Sanskrit school teachers covered by the ordinances can be reversed and the amount so received by them, recovered by State Govt.

Held, teachers who were paid their salaries under the ordinances and who organised their lives and affairs on the assumption and in the belief that the amount paid to them was legitimately due and payable cannot at this distant point of time be asked to cough up the amount disbursed to them. Payments already made should not be recoverable from those who have received the same. There might indeed be situations in which a repromulgation may be necessary without the ordinances having been placed before legislature. Equally plausible was that Constitution provides for the life of ordinances to end six weeks from the date of re-assembly of legislature, regardless whether the ordinances has or has not been placed before the house. Non-presentation of the ordinances before State Legislature was, at any rate, only a circumstance to show that the executive had invoked the power vested in it without complying with the concomitant obligation of placing the ordinances before legislature even when it had the opportunity to do so. HC was right in holding that no relief on that basis could be granted to petitioners. SC would leave the question of interpretation of arts. 123(2) and 213(2) of Constitution in so far as the obligation of Govt. to place the ordinance before Parliament/legislature open. Reference answered.

Dr D.Y. Chandrachud, J.

(G) Parliament & Legislature - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 123, 14, 154, 168, 200, 213(1), 213(2), 213(2)(a), 213(2)(b), 213(3), 249, 250, 304(b), 32, 352(1), 352(4), 356, 356(1), 356(3), 357(2), 359(1A), 360, 360(2), 367(2) - The Bihar Non-Government Sanskrit Schools (Taking Over of Management and Control) Ordinance, 1989 - Promulgated ordinance - Constitutional requirement - Applicability - Whether a re-promoulgated ordinance would meet the basic constitutional requirement of existence of circumstances bearing upon the satisfaction of Governor on the need to take immediate action.

Held, Constitutional fiction, attributing to an Ordinance the same force and effect as a law enacted by legislature comes into being if the Ordinance was validly promulgated and complies with the requirements of arts. 123 and 213 of Constitution. The Ordinance making power does not constitute President or Governor into a parallel source of law making or an independent legislative authority. Every one of the ordinances at issue commencing with Ordinance 32 of 1989 and ending with the last of the ordinances, Ordinance 2 of 1992 constituted a fraud on constitutional power. Those ordinances which were never placed before State legislature and were re-promulgated in violation of the binding judgment of SC in Dr. D.C. Wadhwa And Ors., 1986 Indlaw SC 247 case were bereft of any legal effects and consequences. The ordinances do not create any rights or confer the status of govt. employees. However, it would be necessary for SC to mould the relief (which we do) by declaring that no recoveries should be made from any of the employees of salaries which were paid during the tenure of ordinances in pursuance of the directions contained in the judgment of HC. Reference answered.
