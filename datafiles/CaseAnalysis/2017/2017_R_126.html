State of Maharashtra and others v Reliance Industries Limited and others

Supreme Court of India

15 September 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Arun Mishra, Mohan M. Shantanagoudar

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 759; 2017 (5) ALT(SC) 49; 2017 (5) AWC 5106

Case Digest

Subject: Land & Property

Keywords: Acquisition Of Land, Accommodation, Compulsory Acquisition, Public Purpose, Land Acquisition Officer, Attached To The Earth, Secretary Of State, Requisition, Additional Compensation, Permanently Fastened, Trees, Earth, Manufactory, Bombay Land Requisition Act, 1948, Constitution of India, 1950, Land Acquisition Act, 1894, A.P. Scheduled Area Land Transfer Regulations, 1959

Summary: (A) Land & Property - Administrative - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 16, 23, 3(a), 4, 49, 49(1), 5A, 6, 9 - Notification - Floor acquisition - Challenged - Appellants/State Govt. issued notification u/s. 4 of the Act to acquire third floor premises in suit property - Notice was issued for the purpose of an inquiry u/s. 5A of the Act to respondents - Respondents filed writ petition before HC challenging acquisition proceedings - HC allowed petition and quashed acquisition proceedings as unsustainable - Hence, instant appeal - Whether under the Act acquisition of part of building could be made without acquiring land underneath to such building.

Held, provisions of s. 49 of the Act made it clear besides the inclusive definition u/s. 3(a) of the Act that there could be acquisition of part of building or house and owner has the option to express his desire that whole of it should be acquired and not the part, as the case might be. Court has to take into consideration the question whether land proposed to be taken is reasonably required for the full and unimpaired use of the house, manufactory or building. If the court holds otherwise, possession of land should not be taken. There could be acquisition of house or building or manufactory u/s. 49(1) of the Act or acquisition of part. It was not a case where any of the owners of building has desired that whole of building be acquired. In case such intention would have been expressed, it would have been incumbent to acquire the whole of building. The definition of land was of wide connotation. It cannot be construed in narrow sense to render provisions of the Act otiose or impracticable. Appeal allowed.

(B) Land & Property - Administrative - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 16, 23, 3(a), 4, 49, 49(1), 5A, 6, 9 - Meaning of land - Interpretation - Validity - Whether interpretation of term 'includes' under the Act.

Held, the only interpretation possible in the context was interpretation made by SC inasmuch as such interpretation would not take away the very meaning of land. In instant case, owner of land was State whereas owner of building was respondent. Since, building cannot stand without the land, building also becomes part of the land. However, since the owner of building was different from the owner of land, and if a portion of building was required for public purpose, it was open for State to acquire that portion of building by paying adequate compensation in respect of that portion of building as well as in respect of proportionate diminution of the user if any of the land u/s. 23 of the Act in accordance with law. Appeal allowed.

(C) Land & Property - Administrative - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 16, 23, 3(a), 4, 49, 49(1), 5A, 6, 9 - Scheme of Act - Public purpose - Applicability - Whether to consider the context of definition of land was it mandatory to refer object and scheme of the Act.

Held, the object was to compensate the owner adequately. There was no doubt that pendency of acquisition proceedings were not to cause hardship to the affected parties. The purpose of the Act was to make additions for public purpose and to award to owners/interested persons compensation in accordance with the provisions of the Act. The acquisition was made for public purpose in instant case. The entire floors are being acquired for the purpose of housing of offices and there was acute paucity of such spaces in City and nearby places. When flats could be sold independently, they can be acquired as well. As all the rights in the floor are being acquired and the land beneath it need not be acquired more so it belongs to Govt. there could be valid acquisition of such floors independently without land in such cases. Appeal allowed.

(D) Land & Property - Administrative - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 16, 23, 3(a), 4, 49, 49(1), 5A, 6, 9 - Acquisition proceedings - Restriction - Legality - Whether State to acquire its own land underneath the buildings or other interest.

Held, Notification was issued which was served upon proprietors of bungalows in which it was set out that Govt. claimed to be the owner of land upon which various bungalows and outhouses were selected. They were desirous of acquiring building under the Act. An objection was raised that notification was bad because it was not a notification for acquisition of land but notification with intention for acquiring building on land. As such proceedings under the Act were fundamentally bad because notification upon which proceedings started was invalid. The Act was silent as to acquisition of partial interests in land but it cannot be inferred therefrom that interest in the land was restricted because of existence of rights of State in land cannot be acquired. Where interest of owner was clogged by right of State, compensation payable was only the market value of that interest subject to clog. State in a proceeding for acquisition does not acquire its own interest in land. Appeal allowed.

(E) Land & Property - Administrative - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 16, 23, 3(a), 4, 49, 49(1), 5A, 6, 9 - Acquisition of building - Determination - Whether acquisition of part of building without land u/s. 49 of the Act.

Held, land on which building was standing need not be acquired and there was no necessity to acquire it. There could be acquisition of part of building or the house or manufactory as the owners have not exercised their option to insist for acquisition for whole of building as such only the rights which they have in particular floors were being acquired. U/s. 49(1) of the Act there could be acquisition of land beside the part of building, house or manufactory and when land was proposed to be taken, dispute as to whether it does or does not form part of house, manufactory or building, Collector should refer the determination of such question to Court. There was no dispute with aforesaid proposition but where part of building that too a multi-storied building was being acquired, land need not be acquired more so when owner of building was not the owner of land and his entire interest in part of building could be acquired. Appeal allowed.

(F) Land & Property - Administrative - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 16, 23, 3(a), 4, 49, 49(1), 5A, 6, 9 - Valuation process - Right of Govt. - Validity - Whether valuation method of building mandates acquiring of land.

Held, when notification was of a single unit whatever might be the interest of owners thereof might have therein the purpose of acquisition was to acquire all interest which clog the right of Govt. to full ownership of land. In instant case, ownership of land does not lie with respondents, it was not necessary to acquire land. The entire interest of the owner has to be acquired and that was precisely done in instant case. That would not come in the way of determining the valuation of a particular floor, all the aspects of owners interest and bundle of other rights could be taken into consideration including support provided by land and value of land in the locality etc. Thus, SC found that acquisition process to be legal and valid and notifications in question are valid and let it be taken to a logical end. Impugned judgment and order passed by HC was set aside. Appeal allowed.
