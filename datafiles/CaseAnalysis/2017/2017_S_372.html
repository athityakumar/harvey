State of Karnataka and another v Selvi J. Jayalalitha and others

Supreme Court of India

14 February 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Pinaki Chandra Ghose, Amitava Roy

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 111; (2017) 6 SCC 263; [2017] 201 Comp Cas 230; [2017] 392 ITR 97; JT 2017 (4) SC 14; 2017 (1) MLJ(Crl) 504; 2017 (1) RCR(Criminal) 802; 2017(2) SCALE 375

Summary: Pinaki Chandra Ghose, J:

(A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 109, 120A, 120B, 161, 162, 163, 164, 165, 165A, 21, 302, 34, 409, 420, 467, 471 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 11, 13(1)(e), 13(2), 17, 3, 4(3), 5 - Business enterprises acquisition - Illegal possession - Permissability - Charges were framed against A1/former Chief Minister of State and co-accused viz. A2, A3 and A4/respondents for commission of alleged offences punishable u/ss. 13(1)(e) r/w s. 13(2) of the 1988 Act and u/ss. 120B and 109 IPC - Trial Court convicted A1 for the offence punishable u/s. 13(1)(e) r/w s. 13(2) of the 1988 Act and sentenced to undergo imprisonment for a period of 4 years - Trial Court further convicted A1 to A4 for the offence punishable u/s. 120B IPC r/w s. 13(1)(e) r/w s. 13(2) of the 1988 Act and sentenced to undergo simple imprisonment for 6 months - On appeal, HC set aside impugned judgment of Trial Court and acquitted accused - Hence, instant appeals - Whether prosecution proves beyond all reasonable doubt that A1, being a public servant acquired and possessed in her name and in the names of A2 to A4 and in the names of business enterprises floated in their names, income of 64 heads was Rs.9,34,26,053.56 which Trial Court on a consideration of evidence found to be Rs.9,91,05,094.75.

Held, in view of the fact that respondents accept the income of Rs.9,34,26,053.56 as cited by Director of Vigilance and Anti Corruption (DVAC) and there was no dispute with regard thereto and it was not necessary to examine the evidence of prosecution in support of its figures. Evidence of A1 was held to be vague and ambiguous being not supported by acceptable evidence. Further, it was held that though all those aspects were within the exclusive knowledge of A1, she had failed to produce the best evidence available to her. Further, approach of HC on that aspect appears to be summary in nature without reference to the other evidence on record as analysed by Trial Court, in law the income tax returns/orders passed thereon qua the issue are not final and binding on a criminal court, and at best only are relevant and always subject to its independent appraisal on merits. Thereby HC has accorded unassailable primacy to such income tax returns/orders and have made those final and binding on criminal court without any appreciation of probative potential thereof. HC had proceeded not only in disregard of the evidence as a whole but also being oblivious of legal postulations enunciated by SC that income tax returns/orders passed thereon were not binding on criminal Court and that the facts involved were to be proved on the basis of independent evidence and that the income tax returns/orders were only relevant and nothing further. Appeals allowed.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 109, 120A, 120B, 161, 162, 163, 164, 165, 165A, 21, 302, 34, 409, 420, 467, 471 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 11, 13(1)(e), 13(2), 17, 3, 4(3), 5 - Bank loans - Duplication - Validity - Whether loans in addition to the bank loans cited by DVAC as taken note by HC to add Rs.18,17,46,000 to the income of respondents.

Held, according to prosecution there were two aspects, firstly there was a duplication as DVAC has included those loans in its chart which were considered by Trial Court and secondly, even otherwise, HC committed a mistake in adding up the loan amounts of those additional 10 heads to arrive at a figure of Rs.24,17,31,274 which on a correct calculation, ought to be Rs.10,67,31,224/-. Trial Court did take note of such aspect in its judgment while dealing with those items of loan on the basis of evidence adduced more particularly while dealing with the heads of expenditure. HC was in error in including such item of loan in the income of respondents. HC has not only erred in including the entire amount of loan encompassed but also premised its finding on income on an inflated and patently incorrect figure of Rs.24,17,31,274/-. That addition of Rs.18,17,46,000/- to the income of respondents as done by HC was erroneous and cannot be sustained. Appeals allowed.

(C) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 109, 120A, 120B, 161, 162, 163, 164, 165, 165A, 21, 302, 34, 409, 420, 467, 471 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 11, 13(1)(e), 13(2), 17, 3, 4(3), 5 - Assets computation -Disproportionate - Illegal - Whether HC erred in computing the assets which were categorized as "land and building, shares, acquisition of Indo Doha Chemicals and Pharmaceutical Ltd., new/additional constructions, renovation, vehicles, fixed deposits, jewellery and machinery.

Held, prosecution has jettisoned the disclosures in income tax returns and orders/opinions expressed thereon by departmental authorities as wholly inconsequential. HC erred in taking into account only five special items by excluding the other expensive furnishings/attachments in computing the cost of construction. Further, though in all 146 sale deeds were involved qua the immovable properties figuring therein, HC limited its attention only to 97 such deeds and left out from its consideration, the remaining 49 sale deeds, while arriving at its quantification of such item of the asset to be Rs.6,24,09,120/-. HC though did accept the valuation of assets for the purpose of computation to be Rs.66,44,73,573/- as valued by prosecution for the purpose of ascertaining disproportionate assets, it took into account only the cost of new or additional construction of buildings as assessed by it at Rs. 5,10,54,060/- thus reducing the value thereof as made by the prosecution by a sum of Rs.22,69,34,885/-. The omission on the part of HC to exclude the other four items of assets on which as well it had scaled down the value, in working out the extent of disproportionate assets however had not been questioned by respondents before SC. The judgment of Trial Court does not demonstrate as well, the alleged total non-consideration of evidence adduced on behalf of respondents. Therefore, reduction of cost of new or additional construction of buildings to Rs.5,10,54,060/- as effected by HC has to be held as patently erroneous. Thus, quantification of disproportionate assets was also visibly wrong. Appeals allowed.

(D) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 109, 120A, 120B, 161, 162, 163, 164, 165, 165A, 21, 302, 34, 409, 420, 467, 471 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 11, 13(1)(e), 13(2), 17, 3, 4(3), 5 - Marriage expenditure - Evidence evaluation - Unacceptable - Whether acceptance of HC the expenditure assessed by prosecution in respect of all items except item nos. 2 to 6 i.e. the marriage of A3 and reduced the sum of Rs.6,45,04,222/- as fixed by DVAC to Rs.28,68,000/- occasioning a drop of Rs.6,16,36,222/- was valid.

Held, evaluation of evidence of other witnesses touching upon the remaining aspects of expenditure incurred on the marriage, as conducted by Trial Court also does not merit rejection in toto. Trial Court did not accept the expenditure quoted by prosecution on the cost of Titan watches as the evidence to that effect was construed to be inadequate to lay the same in the account of respondents. Trial Court did not erred in not accepting the figure of Rs.28,68,000/-, as the expenditure incurred by A1 on the basis of her reply to the queries made by Income Tax Department. Though it was not unknown that very often the bride's family shares or bears the expenditure of marriage, dependent on the practice prevalent, it was not an invariable phenomenon and permits of exception in varying fact situations and no rigid assumption either way was tenable. As the investigation into the expenditure on marriage had to be conducted more than 2 years after the event, it was logical that the exercise involved was expected to be informed with some assumptions, which if realistic and logical, would not as such vitiate the assessment as incurably infirm or non-est. SC inclined to hold that the computation of expenditure incurred by A1 in the marriage as made by HC was unacceptable in the teeth of the materials on record to the contrary. There being concurrent findings of the two forums on the rest of the items of expenditure, SC construe it to be inessential to undertake a fresh exercise with regard thereto in instant proceedings. Appeals allowed.

(E) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 109, 120A, 120B, 161, 162, 163, 164, 165, 165A, 21, 302, 34, 409, 420, 467, 471 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 11, 13(1)(e), 13(2), 17, 3, 4(3), 5 - Disproportionate income - Conspiracy - Liability - Whether respondents i.e. A1 to A4 had entered into a conspiracy and in furtherance thereof, A1 who was a public servant, had come to possess assets to the tune of Rs.66.65 crores, disproportionate to her known sources of income, during the period 1991 to 1996 when she held the office of Chief Minister of State and that A2 to A4 had abetted her i.e. A1 in the commission of offence.

Held, the unimpeded, frequent and spontaneous inflow of funds from the account of A1 to those of other co-accused and firms/companies involved overwhelmingly demonstrate the collective culpable involvement of respondents in the transactions in the face of their overall orientations so as to render the same to be masked banking exchanges though involving several accounts but mostly of the same bank. Trial Court was meticulous, sensitive, vigilant and judicious in appraisal, stands authenticated by the fact that in valuing the assets, as warranted, it excluded a sum of Rs.32 lakhs towards the price of sarees and further reduced the value of gold and diamond to the extent of Rs.2 crores. Therefore, the course adopted by Trial Court cannot be faulted with. A1 to A4 have entered into a conspiracy and in furtherance of the same, A1 who was a public servant at the relevant time had come into possession of assets disproportionate to the known sources of her income during the check period and had got the same dispersed in the names of A2 to A4 and the firms & companies involved to hold these on her behalf with a masked front. Further, charge of abetment laid against A2 to A4 in commission of offence by A1 also stands proved. Thus, direction of Trial Court towards confiscation/forfeiture of attached property was restored and would be construed to be an order by SC. The decisions cited on behalf of the respondents on this issue, are distinguishable on facts and are of no avail to them. Appeals allowed.

(F) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 109, 120A, 120B, 161, 162, 163, 164, 165, 165A, 21, 302, 34, 409, 420, 467, 471 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 11, 13(1)(e), 13(2), 17, 3, 4(3), 5 - Pecuinary resources -Conviction - Sustainability - Whether prosecution proves beyond reasonable doubt that A2 to A4 abetted the commission of offence by intentionally aiding A1 in acquisition and possession of pecuniary resources and properties disproportionate to her known source of income by holding substantial portion thereof in their names and in the names of 32 business enterprises floated in the names of A2 to A4, rendering them liable for conviction for the offence punishable u/s. 109 IPC r/w s. 13(2) r/w s.13(1)(e) of the Act.

Held, A1 had executed a General Power of Attorney in favour of A2 in respect of Jaya Publications marked as Ex.P-995. The circumstance of executing the power of attorney in favour of A2 indicates that with a view to keep herself secured from legal complications, A1 executed the said power of attorney knowing fully well that under the said powers, A2 would be dealing with her funds credited to her account in Jaya Publications. Constitution of various firms during the check period was another circumstance establishing the conspiracy between the parties. It has come in evidence that 10 firms were constituted on a single day. In addition, A2 and A3 started independent concerns and apart from buying properties, no other business activity was undertaken by them. The aforesaid firms and companies were operating from the residence of A1 and it cannot be accepted that she was unaware of the same even though she feigned ignorance about the activities carried on by A2 to A4. They were residing with A1 without any blood relation between them. Further, A2 to A4 were accommodated in the house of A1 pursuant to the criminal conspiracy hatched by them to hold the assets of A1. The flow of money from one account to the other proves that there existed active conspiracy to launder the ill-gotten wealth of A1 for purchasing properties in the names of the firms. The conspiracy among accused persons was also proved by the evidence of Sub-Registrar/PW159 and evidence of PW71/Horticultural officer. Trial Court correctly came to the conclusion on such reasoning and SC upholds the same. Impugned judgment and order of HC was set aside and restore the judgment of Trial Court in toto against A2 to A4. Appeals allowed.

Amitava Roy, J.

(G) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 109, 120A, 120B, 161, 162, 163, 164, 165, 165A, 21, 302, 34, 409, 420, 467, 471 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 11, 13(1)(e), 13(2), 17, 3, 4(3), 5 - Undisclosed income - Legality - Whether the receipt was an income from undisclosed sources or not and was unrelated to the lawfulness of sources or of receipt.

Held, judicial adjudication of a charge based on an anti-corruption law motivated by the impelling necessities of time, has to be informed with the desired responsibility and the legislative vision therefor. Any interpretation of the provisions of such law has to be essentially purposive, in furtherance of its mission and not in retrogression thereof. Innovative nuances of evidential inadequacies, processual infirmities and interpretational subtleties, artfully advanced in defence, otherwise intangible and inconsequential, ought to be conscientiously cast aside with moral maturity and singular sensitivity to uphold the statutory sanctity, lest the coveted cause of justice was causality. Such is the militant dominance of this sprawling evil, that majority of the sensible, rational and discreet constituents of the society imbued with moral values and groomed with disciplinal ethos find themselves in minority, besides estranged and resigned by practical compulsions and are left dejected and disillusioned. Corruption is a vice of insatiable avarice for self- aggrandizement by the unscrupulous, taking unfair advantage of their power and authority and those in public office also, in breach of the institutional norms, mostly backed by minatory loyalists. A collective, committed and courageous turnaround is thus the present day imperative to free the civil order from the suffocative throttle of this deadly affliction. Every citizen has to be a partner in this sacrosanct mission, if we aspire for a stable, just and ideal social order as envisioned by our forefathers and fondly cherished by the numerous self-effacing crusaders of a free and independent Bharat, pledging their countless sacrifices and selfless commitments for such cause. Appeals allowed.
