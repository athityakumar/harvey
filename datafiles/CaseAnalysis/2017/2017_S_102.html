Securities and Exchange Board of India and others v Kanaiyalal Baldevbhai Patel and others

Supreme Court of India

20 September 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

N.V. Ramana, Ranjan Gogoi

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 734

Case Digest

Subject: Securities Exchange Board of India

Keywords: Misrepresentation, Good Faith, Companies, Stock, Trading, Contract, Specific Relief Act, 1963, Consumer Protection Act, 1986, Monopolies And Restrictive Trade Practices Act, 1969, Indian Contract Act, 1872, Subsequent, Securities, Securities And Exchange Board Of India Act, 1992, Usurious Loans Act, 1918, Investors, Issuer, Securities And Exchange Board Of India (Prohibition Of Fraudulent And Unfair Trade Practices Relating To Securities Markets) Regulations, 2003, Competition Act, 2002, Securities Exchange Act, 1934, Code of Federal Regulations, Food Safety and Standards Act, 2006

Summary: Securities Exchange Board of India - Securities and Exchange Board of India (Prohibition of Fraudulent and Unfair Trade Practices Relating to Securities Markets) Regulations, 2003, regns. 3(a)(b)(c)(d), 4(1) - Non-intermediary front running - Prohibition - Validity - Appellants/SEBI investigated activities of respondent no. 1/individual trader - Respondent no. 2 provided information to respondent no. 1 regarding forthcoming trading activity of Passport India Investment (PII) - Respondent no. 1 indulged in trading before PII and squared off the position when order of PII were placed in the market and earned a total profit of Rs. 1,56,32,364.01/- - Tribunal took two different views in different cases appealed by appellants and private individuals who were alleged to have been involved in front running - Hence, instant appeals - Whether 'front running by non-intermediary' was a prohibited practice u/regns, 3(a)(b)(c) and (d) and 4(1) of the Regulations.

Held, in order to establish charges against respondents u/regns. 3(a)(b)(c) and (d) and 4(1) of the Regulations, one needs to prove that a person who had provided the tip was under a duty to keep the non-public information under confidence, further such breach of duty was known to tippee and he still trades thereby defrauding the person, whose orders were front-runned, by inducing him to deal at the price he did. It should be noted that the provisions of regns. 3(a)(b)(c) (d) and 4(1) of the Regulations are couched in general terms to cover diverse situations and possibilities. Once a conclusion, that fraud was committed while dealing in securities, as arrived at, all those provisions get attracted in a situation like the one under consideration. SC was not inclined to agree with the contention that appellants should have identified as to which particular provision of the Regulations was violated. A pigeon-hole approach might not be applicable in instant case. Concerned parties to the transaction were involved in an apparent fraudulent practice violating market integrity. The parting of information with regard to an imminent bulk purchase and subsequent transaction thereto were so intrinsically connected that no other conclusion but one of joint liability of both the initiator of fraudulent practice and the other party who had knowingly aided in the same was possible. Appeals disposed of.
