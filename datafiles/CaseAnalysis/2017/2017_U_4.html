Shayara Bano and others v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

22 August 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Jagdish Singh Khehar, Kurian Joseph, Rohinton Fali Nariman, Uday Umesh Lalit, S. Abdul Nazeer

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 629; (2017) 9 SCC 1; AIR 2017 SC 4609; 2017 (5) AWC 4353; 2017 (3) CLT(SC) 66; JT 2017 (8) SC 313; 2017 (6) MLJ 378

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Family & Personal; Women & Children

Keywords: Maintenance, Welfare, Delegated Legislation, divorce, Arbitrariness, Freedom Of Religion, Equality of opportunity, Jurisdiction, Hindu Marriage Act, 1955, Health, Hindu Minority And Court Rules, 1966, Family Courts Act, 1984, Muslim Law, Government of India Act, 1915, Muslim Women (Protection of Rights on Divorce) Act, 1986, Special Marriage Act, 1872, Bombay Rent Act, Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937, Parsi Marriage and Divorce Act, 1936, Foreign Marriage Act, 1969, Hindu Widows' Remarriage Act, Madras Devadasis (Prevention of Dedication) Act, 1947, Hindu Marriage Validity Act, 1949, Prisons Act, 1894, Dissolution of Muslim Marriages Act, 1939, Arya Marriage Validation Act,1937, Roerich and Devikarani Roerich Estate (Acquisition and Transfer) Bombay Devadasi Protection Act, 1934, Indian Sati Prevention Act, 1988, Muslim Marriage and Divorce Act, 1951, Muslim Family Laws Ordinance, 1961, Constitution of the United States of America, Islamic Summary: Jagdish Singh Khehar J. and S. Abdul Nazeer J.

(A) Family & Personal - Constitution - Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937, s. 2 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Divorce pronounced - Challenged - Petitioner/wife filed instant writ petition assailing the divorce pronounced by her husband/respondent no. 5 seeking declaration that 'talaq-e-biddat' pronounced by her husband be declared as void ab initio contending that such a divorce which abruptly,unilaterally and irrevocably terminates the ties of matrimony u/s. 2 of the Act be declared unconstitutional - Whether judgment of Privy Council in Rashid Ahmad v. Anisa Khatun and Others, 1931 Indlaw PC 47, upholding 'talaq-e-biddat' require a relook.

Held, opinion expressed by Privy Council with reference to 'talaq-e-biddat', in Rashid Ahmad case, holding that 'talaq e-biddat' results in finally and irrevocably severing the matrimonial tie between spouses the very moment it was pronounced, needs to be examined afresh. More particularly, because the validity of the same as an approved concept, of Muslim 'personal law' 'Shariat', was not evaluated at that juncture (as it indeed could not have been, as the legislation was not available, when the Privy Council had rendered its judgment) in the backdrop of the Act and also the provisions of the Constitution. Order accordingly.

(B) Family & Personal - Constitution - Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937, s. 2 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Talaq-e-biddat - Sanction - Validity - Whether 'talaq-e-biddat' was concededly sinful, sanction of law.

Held, practice of 'talaq-e-biddat' was in vogue since the period of Umar, which was roughly more than 1400 years ago. Secondly, that each one of counsel, irrespective of who they represented (petitioners or respondents) acknowledged in one voice, that 'talaq-e-biddat' though bad in theology, was considered as “good” in law. Counsel representing petitioners were also unequivocal, that 'talaq-e-biddat' was accepted as a “valid” practice in law. That being so, it was not possible for SC to hold, the practice to be invalid in law, merely at the asking of petitioners, just because it was considered bad in theology. Order accordingly.

(C) Family & Personal - Constitution - Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937, s. 2 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Hadiths approval - Validity - Whether the practice of 'talaq-e-biddat', approved/disapproved by “hadiths”.

Held, it would not be appropriate for SC to record a finding, whether the practice of 'talaq-e-biddat' was, or not affirmed by 'hadiths' in view of the enormous contradictions in the 'hadiths', relied upon by the rival parties. Order accordingly.

(D) Family & Personal - Constitution - Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937, s. 2 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Talaq practice - Personal law - Applicability - Whether the practice of 'talaq-e-biddat', a matter of faith for Muslims, if yes, whether it was a constituent of their 'personal law'.

Held, the practice of 'talaq-e-biddat' has to be considered integral to the religious denomination in question Sunnis belonging to the Hanafi school. The practice of 'talaq-e-biddat' had the sanction and approval of religious denomination which practiced it and as such, there could be no doubt that the practice was a part of their 'personal law'. Order accordingly.

(E) Family & Personal - Constitution - Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937, s. 2 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 13 - Legislation - Statutory status - Whether the Act confer statutory status to the subjects regulated by the said legislation.

Held, it was not possible for SC to accept the contention of petitioners that questions/subjects covered by the Act ceased to be 'personal law' and got transformed into 'statutory law'. The practices of Muslim 'personal law' 'Shariat' cannot be required to satisfy the provisions contained in Part III - Fundamental Rights, of Constitution applicable to State actions, in terms of art. 13 of Constitution. Order accordingly.

(F) Family & Personal - Constitution - Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937, s. 2 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 15, 21, 25 - Constitutional parameters - Violation - Whether 'talaq-e-biddat' violate the parameters expressed in art. 25 of Constitution.

Held, Muslim 'personal law' 'Shariat', in SC view was a matter of 'personal law' of Muslims, to be traced from four sources, namely, Quran, the 'hadith','ijma' and the 'qiyas'. None of those can be attributed to any State action. A practice which was a component of 'faith' of those belonging to that school. 'Personal law' being a matter of religious faith, and not being State action, there was no question of its being violative of the provisions of Constitution more particularly, the provisions relied upon by the petitioners, to assail the practice of 'talaq-e-biddat', namely, arts. 14, 15 and 21 of Constitution. Order accordingly.

(G) Family & Personal - Constitution - Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937, s. 2 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Personal law - Judicial intervention - Legality - Whether there was any Constitutional morality and 'talaq-e-biddat'.

Held, the practice of 'talaq-e-biddat' being a constituent of 'personal law' has a stature equal to other fundamental rights, conferred in Part III of Constitution. The practice cannot be set aside, on ground of being violative of the concept of Constitutional morality, through judicial intervention. Order accordingly.

(G) Family & Personal - Constitution - Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937, s. 2 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 25, 44 - Personal law Reforms - Acceptability - Whether there was any Reforms to 'personal law' in Country.

Held, Reforms to 'personal law' in Country, with reference to socially unacceptable practices in different religions, have come about only by way of legislative intervention. Such legislative intervention was permissible u/arts. 25(2) and 44, r/w entry 5 of the Concurrent List in Seventh Schedule of Constitution. The said procedure alone need to be followed with reference to the practice of 'talaq-e-biddat', if the same was to be set aside. Order accordingly.

(H) Family & Personal - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 25 - Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937, s. 2 - International conventions - Declaration - Whether there was any impact of international conventions and declarations on 'talaq-e-biddat'.

Held, International conventions and declarations were of no avail in instant controversy because the practice of 'talaq-e-biddat', was a component of 'personal law'and has the protection of art. 25 of Constitution. SC direct respondents to consider appropriate legislation with reference to 'talaq-e-biddat'. SC would implore legislature to bestow its thoughtful consideration to instant issue of paramount importance. SC injunct Muslim husbands, from pronouncing 'talaq-e-biddat'as a means for severing their matrimonial relationship for 6 months. Order accordingly.

KURIAN, J

(I) Family & Personal - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950 - Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937, s. 2 - Tenets of Quran - Permissibility - Whether what was Quranically wrong could be legally right in instant case.

Held, on the statement that triple talaq was an integral part of the religious practice, SC disagree. Merely because a practice has continued for long, that by itself cannot make it valid if it was expressly declared to be impermissible. The whole purpose of the Act was to declare Shariat as the rule of decision and to discontinue anti-Shariat practices with respect to subjects enumerated in s. 2 of the Act which include talaq. Therefore, after the introduction of the Act no practice against the tenets of Quran was permissible. There cannot be any Constitutional protection to such a practice. When issues of such nature come to the forefront, the discourse often takes the form of pitting religion against other Constitutional rights. A reconciliation between the same was possible, but the process of harmonizing different interests was within the powers of legislature. Such power has to be exercised within the Constitutional parameters without curbing the religious freedom guaranteed under the Constitution. However, it was not for Courts to direct for any legislation. Order accordingly.

R.F. Nariman, J.

(J) Family & Personal - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13(1), 14 - Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937, s. 2 - Triple talaq - Legality - Whether the Act could be said to recognize and enforce Triple Talaq as a rule of law to be followed by Courts and if not whether personal laws are outside art. 13(1) of Constitution was correct in law.

Held, given the fact that Triple Talaq was irrevocable, it was obvious that any attempt at reconciliation between husband and wife by two arbiters from their families, which is essential to save the marital tie, cannot ever take place. It was clear that such form of Talaq was manifestly arbitrary in the sense that the marital tie could be broken capriciously and whimsically by a Muslim man without any attempt at reconciliation so as to save it. Such form of Talaq should be held to be violative of fundamental right contained u/art. 14 of Constitution. Thus, the Act as it seeks to recognize and enforce Triple Talaq was within the meaning of the expression “laws in force” in art. 13(1) of Constitution and should be struck down as being void to the extent that it recognizes and enforces Triple Talaq. Impugned practice of 'talaq-e-biddat' triple talaq was set aside. Order accordingly.

Ratio- Triple Talaq is unconstitutional.
