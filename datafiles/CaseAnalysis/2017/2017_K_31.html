State of Madhya Pradesh and others v Kallo Bai

Supreme Court of India

08 May 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

N.V. Ramana, Prafulla C. Pant

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 406; AIR 2017 SC 2516; 2017 CRLJ 3233; 2017 (3) MLJ(Crl) 282; 2017(6) SCALE 311

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Criminal Case, Criminal Prosecution, Jurisdiction, Vehicle, Confiscation, Agent, Opportunity, Transport, Search, Rank, Dealing, Distinct, Indian Forest Act, 1927, Andhra Pradesh Forest Act, Madhya Pradesh Van Upaj (Vyapar Viniyaman) Adhiniyam, 1969, Registered, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Seizure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 102, 103, 482 - Indian Forest Act, 1927, ss. 15A, 26, 41, 53, 55 - Madhya Pradesh Van Upaj (Vyapar Viniyaman) Adhiniyam, 1969, ss. 10, 11, 15, 15(1), 15(5), 15B, 15C, 15D, 2, 3, 4, 5, 7, 8, 9 - Confiscation of goods - Challenged - Respondent/accused allegedly carried teak wood without requisite documents for transportation - Project Range Officer seized vehicle, registered the offence u/ss. 5 and 15 of the 1969 Act r/w ss. 26 and 41 of the 1927 Act - Authorized Officer ordered confiscation of vehicle and teak wood - On appeal, Appellate Authority dismissed appeal and confirmed the order of authority below - Respondent filed revision petition before Trial Court - Trial Court allowed revision and quashed the order of confiscation and directed to release the vehicle - Appellants/State challenged order of Trial Court before HC - HC dismissed petition and affirmed the order of Trial Court - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, s. 15 of the 1969 Act gives independent power to the concerned authority to confiscate the articles as mentioned there under, even before the guilt was completely established. Such power could be exercised by concerned officer if he was satisfied that the said objects were utilized during the commission of a forest offence. A protection was provided for the owners of the vehicles/articles, if they were able to prove that they took all reasonable care and precautions as envisaged u/s. 15(5) of the 1969 Act and said offence was committed without their knowledge or connivance. Criminal prosecution was distinct from confiscation proceedings. The two proceedings were different and parallel, each having a distinct purpose. The object of confiscation proceeding was to enable speedy and effective adjudication with regard to confiscation of the produce and the means used for committing the offence while the object of prosecution was to punish the offender. The scheme Adhiniyam prescribes an independent procedure for confiscation. The intention of prescribing separate proceedings was to provide a deterrent mechanism and to stop further misuse of the vehicle. HC as well as revisional Court erred in coming to a conclusion that confiscation under the law was not permissible unless the guilt of accused was completely established. Appeal allowed.
