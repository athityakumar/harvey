Bimolangshu Roy (Dead), Through Lrs v State of Assam and another

Supreme Court of India

26 July 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

J. Chelameswar, R.K. Agrawal, Abhay Manohar Sapre

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 688; 2017 (4) AWC 3743

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: Speaker, State Legislature, Legislative Power, Government of India Act, 1935, Privileges, Proclamation Of Emergency, Inter-State Water Disputes Act, 1956, House Of The People, Legislative Assembly, Constitution (Forty-Fourth Amendment) Act, 1978, Constitution (Seventy-Third Amendment) Act, 1992, Central Provinces and Berar Sales of Motor Spirit and Lubricants Taxation Act, 1938, American Constitution, Constitution (Ninety-First Amendment) Act, 2003, US Constitution, Constitution of India, 1950, Assam Parliamentary Secretaries (Appointment, Salaries, Allowances and Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 2004, Assam Parliamentary Secretaries (Appointment, Salaries, Allowances and Miscellaneous Provisions) Ordinance, 2004, Assam Ministers, Ministers of State and Deputy Ministers Salaries and Allowances Act, 1958, Gold (Control) Act (45 of 1968), Constitution 91st Amendment Bill, 2003

Summary: Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 164, 194, 178, 179, 187 and Entry 39 of List II - Constitution (Ninety-first Amendment) Act, 2003 - Assam Parliamentary Secretaries (Appointment, Salaries, Allowances and Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 2004 - Appointment of Parliamentary Secretaries - Power of Legislature - At time of coming into force of 2003 Act, strength of Council of Ministers in respondent State was at 36 out of a total 126 members, amounting to 28.57% of strength of Legislative Assembly - However, mandate contained in art. 164(1A) the strength of Council of Ministers was to be brought down to 19 to be consistent with ceiling of 15% imposed by art.164(1A) - Meanwhile, respondent enacted 2004 Act - Petitioner challenged Constitutional validity of 2004 Act before HC - Later, matter transferred to SC - Hence, instant transferred petition - Whether, the text of art. 194(3) and Entry 39 is wide enough to authorize the legislature to make 2004 Act.

Held, it can be seen from the scheme of art. 194 that it does not expressly authorize the State Legislature to create offices such as the one in question. Further, art. 178 speaks about the offices of Speaker and Deputy Speaker. Art. 179 deals with the vacation of those offices or resignations of incumbents of those offices whereas art. 182 and 183 deal with the Chairman and Deputy Chairman of the Legislative Council wherever the Council exists. The most crucial article in this Chapter is art. 187 which makes stipulations even with reference to the secretarial staff of the Legislature. On the face of such elaborate and explicit Constitutional arrangement with respect to the Legislature and the various offices connected with the legislature and matters incidental to them to read the authority to create new offices by legislation would be a wholly irrational way of construing the scope of art. 194(3) and Entry 39 of List II. Such a construction would be enabling the legislature to make a law which has no rational connection with the subject matter of the entry. "The powers, privileges and immunities" contemplated by art. 194(3) and Entry 39 are those of the legislators qua legislators. Thus, the Legislature of respondent State lacks the competence to make the impugned Act. The impugned Act is declared unconstitutional. Petition allowed.
