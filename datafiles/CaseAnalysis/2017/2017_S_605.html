Vijendra Singh and another v State of Uttar Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

04 January 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, R.F. Nariman

Where Reported

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Common Intention, Juvenile, Proof, Criminal Liability, Spot, Version, Material Witness, Reliance, Inference, Adverse Inference, Interested Witnesses, Enmity, Lathi, Juvenile Justice (Care And Protection Of Children) Act, 2000, Constructive Liability, Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Rules, 2007, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Plea

Summary: Criminal Appeal No. 1448 of 2010

Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973,s. 161 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 149, 302, 34 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellants/accused who were armed with pistols, lathi and ballam allegedly fired a gun shot to deceased causing instant death - Charge sheet was filed against accused u/s. 302 r/w s. 34 IPC - Trial Court found accused guilty of the offence u/s. 302 r/w s. 34 IPC and sentenced them to suffer RI for life - On appeal, HC dismissed appeal and upheld order of Trial Court - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, it was manifest from the evidence that accused had accompanied the other accused who were armed with gun and they themselves carried lathi and ballam. The carrying of weapons, arrival at a particular place and at the same time, entering into the shed and murder of deceased definitely attract the constructive liability as engrafted u/s. 34 IPC. Since the witnesses examined by prosecution were trustworthy and Court could safely act on their testimony. There was no justification in instant case to draw any adverse inference against prosecution. Further, PW6/ Doctor who conducted the post mortem had opined that death of deceased was caused by gunshot injury. Accused was carrying a ballam and accompanying others. Their intention was to go to the shed where deceased was studying because of availability of electric light was established. The evidence of ocular witnesses was found to be satisfactory, reliable, consistent and credible by Trial Court and nothing tangible could be elicited from their evidence in cross-examination to create any speck of doubt in their version or to treat their testimony as infirm or perverse. Appeals disposed of.

Criminal Appeal No. 1452 of 2010

Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 161 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 149, 302, 34 - Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2000, ss. 20, 7A -Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Rules, 2007 - Conviction of juvenile - Release - Whether appellant no. 1 being a juvenile should be released from custody as he sustained conviction for maximum period.

Held, as far as appellant no. 1 was concerned, a report was called for and he was found to be a juvenile being 16 years 3 months 10 days old on the date of offence. The said report has gone unchallenged and counsel for State, has fairly stated that he was a juvenile on the date of offence. SC found that Court relying on s. 7A of the Act and the amendments introduced in s. 20 of the Act whereby the proviso and Explanation were added to s. 20 of the Act and the Rules, 2007 remitted the matter to Juvenile Justice Board with the observation that if appellant no. 1 was detained for more than the maximum period for which a juvenile might be confined to a special home, Board should release him from custody forthwith. In instant case appellant no. 1 remained in custody for more than the maximum period for which he could have been confined to a special home, while sustaining the conviction, SC release him from custody. Appeals disposed of.
