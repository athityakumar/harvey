Commissioner of Customs, Mumbai v Aban Loyd Chiles Offshore Limited and others

Supreme Court of India

02 February 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Prafulla C. Pant

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 64; (2017) 3 SCC 211; 2017 (346) E.L.T. 513; JT 2017 (2) SC 248; 2017 (2) MLJ 610; 2017(2) SCALE 183

Case Digest

Subject: Customs; Indirect Tax

Keywords: CEGAT, Vehicle, Customs Act, 1962, Bill Of Entry, Importation, Imported Goods, Part, Customs Tariff Act, 1975, Any Goods, Confiscation, Proper Officer, Luggage, Import Licence, Importer, Vessel, Conveyance, Territorial Waters Of India, Time, India, Stores, Territorial Waters, Continental Shelf, Exclusive Economic Zone and Other Maritime Zones Act, 1976

Summary: Customs - Indirect Tax - Territorial Waters, Continental Shelf, Exclusive Economic Zone and other maritime Zones Act, 1976 - Penalty - Challenged - Respondent obtained approval of GOI for import of Rig for oil field services - On investigations by Customs authorities, it was found that there was contravention of certain provisions by Assessee and others with regard to these two acts of bringing rig into India - Commissioner passed order holding that that rig was imported for home consumption and Assesses were liable to pay duty - On appeal, Tribunal opined that rig was liable for confiscation but as there was no deliberate intention on part of Asessee/importer to contravene regulations although there was clear negligence and rules were not followed, fine for redemption of rid was reduced - Hence, instant appeals - Whether, order of Tribunal is sustainable. Held, it cannot be said that vessel, i.e., rig, was imported into India when it had anchored twice in 1996 and once in 1998 for purpose of repair, for element of home consumption is missing even when vessel, i.e., rig, had entered territorial waters. Thus, it would be incorrect to hold that mere repair of vessel in 1996 or in 1998 would constitute taxable import. Authorities have laid emphasis on factum that rig was purchased for being used in oil field of ONGC and for this purpose owner had made application and permission/licence for import was granted by Ministry of Industry. Rig was purchased from foreign exchange released by Govt. on basis of import licence for rig. If rig was not to be used in India, foreign exchange would not have been released and import licence would not have been granted. This argument on behalf of Department does not further the stand. It cannot be regarded as conclusive. Adjudication order does not hold that import had taken place in 1987 when rig first put into operation in high seas. This was not treated as date of import or home consumption. It cannot be held that owner had not violated provisions of Act, which are much broader and wider in scope. Act regulates and mandates compliance by foreign going vessels when they enter territorial waters. Provisions of Act are required to be met and complied with even when no goods are to be unloaded for import into India or vessel is not 'good' meant for home consumption. Thus, violations recorded by Tribunal cannot be found fault with. Appeals dismissed.
