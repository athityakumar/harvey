Rasiklal Kantilal and Company v Board of Trustee of Port of Bombay and others

Supreme Court of India

28 February 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

J. Chelameswar, A.M. Sapre

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 146; AIR 2017 SC 1283; 2017 (2) Bom.C.R. 823; 2017 (348) E.L.T. 3; JT 2017 (3) SC 25; 2017 (3) MLJ 844; 2017(3) SCALE 195

Case Digest

Subject: Customs; Indirect Tax

Keywords: Bill Of Lading, Services, Customs Act, 1962, Part, Specific Relief Act, 1963, Port Trust, Any Goods, Demurrage Charges, Indian Contract Act, 1872, Any Person, Importer, Exporter, Vessel, Detention Certificate, Issued, Time, India, Give, Steamer Agent, Such Goods, Central Boards of Revenue Act, 1963, Retention, Seize, Major Port Trusts Act, 1963, Indian Ports Act, 1908, Madras Port Trust Act, 1905, International Airports Authority Act, 1971, Indian Bills of Lading Act, 1856, Indian Ports Act, 1889, International Airports Authority (Storage and Processing of Goods) Regulation, 1980, Indian Ports Act, 1855, Indian Ports Act, 1875, Indian Ports Act, 1879, Indian Ports Act, 1890, Indian Ports Act, 1905

Summary: (A) Customs - Indirect Tax - Customs Act, 1962, ss. 2(12)(3), 292 - Major Port Trusts Act, 1963, ss. 3(1), 59, 59(1), 61, 62, 63 - Detention certificate - Demurrage charges - Restriction - Appellant applied to respondent/Customs Authorities to have the Bills of Entry substituted in their name for 41 consignments lying unclaimed - Further, petitioner wrote to Customs Authorities seeking an amendment of IGM so that the goods could be cleared - Customs Authority granted permission of detention certificate for 41 consignments from 23-03-1992 to 09-09-1992 - Meantime, respondent/authorities levied demurrage charges on ground that no remission could be granted prior to date of noting - Aggrieved, appellant filed writ petition before HC stating that appellant cleared the goods after making payment of amount claimed by respondent no. 1 which was dismissed - Hence, instant appeal - Whether action of respondents in not granting the remission of demurrage charges in respect of said consignments and restricting granting of remission of demurrage charges only from the date of filing of bills of entry in the name of petitioners was valid.

Held, if the 1963 Act authorises respondent no. 1 to recover its dues by bailing the goods under bailment, in those cases where the consignee does not turn up to take the delivery of goods within the time stipulated u/ss. 61 or 62 of the 1963 Act, to deny the right to demand and recover the amounts due from consignee when he seeks delivery of goods under bailment would be illogical and inconsistent with the scheme of the Act. Such right, in SC view, enables respondent no. 1 to claim various amounts due to it, from any person claiming delivery of goods either the bailor or a person claiming through bailor for the services rendered w.r.t. the goods. Denying such a right on the ground that the person claiming delivery of goods acquired title to the goods only towards the end of the period of bailment of goods with respondent no. 1 would result in driving respondent no. 1 to recover the amount due to it from bailor or his agent who might or might not within the jurisdiction of municipal Courts of Country. Appeal partly allowed.

(B) Customs - Indirect Tax - Customs Act, 1962, ss. 2(12)(3), 292 - Major Port Trusts Act, 1963, ss. 3(1), 59, 59(1), 61, 62, 63 - Clearance of goods - Delayed - Whether appellant was entitled for complete remission of amount claimed towards demurrage on account of delayed clearance of goods.

Held, delay in taking delivery was attributable purely to the failure of original consignee. Appellant clearly knew or at least ought to have known, when he purchased the goods that respondent no. 1 would demand demurrage. Appellant as a person claiming through consignor was not entitled in law to claim any right of remission on ground that he did not have any interest or title in the goods for such period. Such delay occurred because of the time taken in ensuring that appellant complied with various statutory obligations to import goods such as amendment of the IGM etc. The fact that appellant was not permitted to clear the goods because of pendency of some proceedings initiated by customs authorities by itself does not create a right of remission in favour of appellant. Though it might constitute a relevant circumstance for considering granting remission if respondent no. 1 so chooses as a matter of policy. As a matter of fact, remission of a part of demurrage was granted by respondent no. 1. Appeal partly allowed.

(C) Customs - Indirect Tax - Customs Act, 1962, ss. 2(12)(3), 292 - Major Port Trusts Act, 1963, ss. 3(1), 59, 59(1), 61, 62, 63 - Remission - Statutory obligation - Denial - Whether respondent no. 1 granted complete remission of the amount payable towards demurrage in case of another importer who was similarly situated and action of respondent no. 1 in declining remission to appellant was discriminatory.

Held, cases of another importer and appellant were not identical. It was the case where original consignee sold the goods to a third party on high seas even before their arrival into India. It so transpired that the purchaser did not have an appropriate license under the relevant law to import the goods. In view of the said problem, the goods were detained for some time and eventually original consignee himself cleared the goods. It is in the said circumstances that the other importer was granted remission. However, authority of respondent no. 1 to grant or decline remission of any amount due towards any rate payable under the 1963 Act should be based on rational consideration and a sound policy. Such a requirement was inherent in the fact that respondent no. 1 was a statutory body discharging important statutory obligations. Respondent no. 1 could not bring anything on record which demonstrates the reasons for declining remission as claimed by appellant nor any clear policy of respondent which regulates the discretion. Thus, SC deem it appropriate to set aside the decision of respondent no. 1 in declining the remission and leave it open to respondent to take appropriate decision on application duly recording the reasons for such decision. Impugned judgment of HC was set aside. Appeal partly allowed.
