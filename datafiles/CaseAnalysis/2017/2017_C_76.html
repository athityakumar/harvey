Commissioner Central Excise, Bangalore v United Spirits Limited and another

Supreme Court of India

05 January 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, N.V. Ramana

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 27; (2017) 3 SCC 166; AIR 2017 SC 1266; 2017 (345) E.L.T. 417; JT 2017 (3) SC 90; 2017(1) SCALE 350

Case Digest

Subject: Excise; Indirect Tax

Keywords: Show Cause Notice, Assessable Value, Central Excise Act, 1944, Packing, Sales Tax, Central Excise Rules, 2002, Karnataka Sales Tax Act, 1957, Kerala General Sales Tax Act, 1963, Administrative

Summary: (A) Excise - Indirect Tax - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11A, 11A(1), 2(f), 4 - Central Excise Rules, 2002, r. 26 - Manufacture - Food flavours - Differential duty levied - Respondent/assessee was manufacturer of Indian Made Foreign Liquor (IMFL) and food flavours - Revenue issued a show cause notice on ground that respondent received additional consideration from its franchisees in the form of royalty for supplying food flavours - Adjudicating Authority u/s. 11A of the 1944 Act demanded differential duty for April, 1997 to March, 2009 of Rs. 35,45,865,860/- Aggrieved, respondent approached Tribunal which remanded the matter to Commissioner - Commissioner held that the royalty from various units under the manufacturing agreement deserve to be included in assessable value of food flavour supplied to them and confirmed demand u/s. 11A of the 1944 Act - On appeal, Tribunal allowed appeal holding that food flavour was one of the blending materials and not the sole blending materials sold by assessee and there does not appear to be any close nexus between royalty and food flavor - Hence, instant appeal - Whether there was 'manufacture' of food flavor.

Held, contention of respondent that they were mixing essences and in some cases merely selling food flavours purchased from third parties without any processing and in any case mixing of essences under no circumstances could amount to manufacture. by such process of mixing change takes place and no separate and marketable commodity comes into existence. It was well settled in law that 'manufacture' implies change, but every change was not manufacture, such change was normally a result of treatment, labour and manipulation. It is noticeable that respondent had pleaded a different factual matrix which was accepted by Tribunal, albeit, without referring to specific details. General observation and broad brush approach need not reflect true consideration paid for all transactions. Invoice would not be determinative, as the royalty payment was inseparably linked with the sale consideration paid for concentrate. The indelible nexus and connect was established to club the two considerations. A far greater and deeper scrutiny of facts was required before forming any opinion, one way or the other. It would be wrong to be assumptuous without full factual matrix being lucent and absolutely clear. Appeal allowed.

(B) Excise - Indirect Tax - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11A, 11A(1), 2(f), 4 - Central Excise Rules, 2002, r. 26 - Price paid - Royalty received - Nexus - Whether there was nexus in royalty received and the price paid for the food flavour sold.

Held, in instant case order of Tribunal the exact nature of the process undertaking and how mixing was undertaken and the process involved was not discernible and was not ascertained and commented. It remains ambiguous and inconclusive. Respondent claims that about 26% of the sales of odoriferous substances were brought from third party and sold without any modification or process. Those were all questions of fact which should be first authenticated and the actual factual position validated. Tribunal has answered the question in favour of respondent without the background check as to the Actual process involved and undertaken. Different flavours might have different processes. Appeal allowed.

(C) Excise - Indirect Tax - Central Excise Act, 1944, ss. 11A, 11A(1), 2(f), 4 - Central Excise Rules, 2002, r. 26 - Show cause notices - Barred - Validity - Whether two show cause notices were correctly determined to be barred by limitation by Tribunal.

Held, Tribunal has held that certain show cause notices were barred by limitation. Tribunal on such score has also not scrutinized the dates appropriately, but has returned a cryptic finding. SC constrained to remit the matter to Tribunal for reconsideration of aforesaid aspects on the basis of observations made and the law in the field. However, SC might proceed to state that SC have not expressed anything on the merits of the case including the imposition of penalty and interest. SC expects Tribunal should advert to each and every facet in detail so that SC could appropriately appreciate the controversy. Matter was remitted to Tribunal for fresh determination. Appeal allowed.
