Rakesh Kumar Paul v State of Assam

Supreme Court of India

16 August 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, Deepak Gupta, Prafulla C. Pant

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 871; 2017 (242) DLT 79

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Life Imprisonment, Habeas Corpus, Jurisdiction, Production, Accused Person, Investigating Agency, Entries, Telegram, Prayer, Personal Liberty, Rank, Diary, Imprisonment For Life, Proviso, Legal Aid, Prosecutor, Public Prosecutor, Narcotic Drugs And Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985, Expiry, Minimum Sentence, Specially Empowered, Dealing, Commissions Of Inquiry Act, 1952, Terrorist And Disruptive Activities (Prevention) Act, 1987, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898, Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961, Code of Criminal Procedure (Amendment) Act, 1978, Delhi Special Police Establishment Act, 1946, Central Vigilance Commission Act, 2003, Forthwith, Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Husband, Constitution of India, 1950, Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, Corruption, Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 2013

Summary: Madan B. Lokur, J.

(A) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 164, 167, 167(2), 167(2)(a), 167(2)(a)(i), 167(2)(a)(ii), 173(8), 439 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 13(1), 13(1)(a), 13(1)(b), 13(1)(b)(ii), 13(1)(d), 13(2), 7 - Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 2013 - FIR lodged - Bail application rejection - Challenged - FIR was lodged against petitioner under IPC and the 1988 Act - Petitioner applied for bail before Special Judge before expiry of 60 days which was rejected - Subsequently, petitioner before expiry of 90 days of detention applied for bail before HC which was dismissed - Hence, instant appeals - Whether petitioner was entitled to 'default bail' when his application for "regular bail" was rejected by HC.

Held, petitioner had satisfied all the requirements of obtaining 'default bail' which was that he had put in more than 60 days in custody pending investigations into an alleged offence not punishable with imprisonment for a minimum period of 10 years, no charge sheet was filed against him and he was prepared to furnish bail for his release, as such, he ought to have been released by HC on reasonable terms and conditions of bail. Even if petitioner was not within the purview of the 2013 Act he was certainly not outside the purview of the 1988 Act and could be prosecuted and punished for a violation of s. 13(1) of the 1988 Act. There was no cogent reason for excluding petitioner from the rigours of the 1988 Act as amended by the 2013 Act. Thus, petitioner was entitled to the grant of 'default bail'. Impugned judgment of HC was set aside. Appeals disposed of.

Prafulla C. Pant, J.

(B) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 164, 167, 167(2), 167(2)(a), 167(2)(a)(i), 167(2)(a)(ii), 173(8), 439 - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 13(1), 13(1)(a), 13(1)(b), 13(1)(b)(ii), 13(1)(d), 13(2), 7 - Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 2013 - Charge sheet filed - Punishment extension - Permissibility - Whether in a case regarding offence for which punishment imposable might extend upto 10 years accused was entitled to bail u/s. 167(2) CrPC due to default on part of investigating agency in not filing charge sheet within 60 days.

Held, intention of legislature was that if an offence was punishable with imprisonment upto 10 years, then it falls within the provision of s. 167(2)(a)(i) CrPC and permissible period for investigation was 90 days. The intention of Legislature in extending the permissible time period from 60 days to 90 days for investigation was to include the offences in which sentence awardable was at least 10 years or more. Therefore, though the expression "not less than 10 years" used in s. 167(2)(a)(i) CrPC has created some ambiguity, real intention of legislature seems to include all such offences wherein an imprisonment which might extend to 10 years was an awardable sentence. Thus, for offences wherein punishment might extend to 10 years imprisonment, permissible period for filing charge sheet should be 90 days and only after the period of 90 days accused should be entitled to bail on default for non-filing of charge sheet. Since the expression "not less than ten years" has caused ambiguity in interpretation, the best course for legislature would be to clear its intention by using appropriate words. Appeals disposed of.

(C) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 164, 167, 167(2), 167(2)(a), 167(2)(a)(i), 167(2)(a)(ii), 173(8), 439 - Default bail - Entitled to - Whether appellant was entitled to default bail u/s. 167(2) CrPC though he has not made any application (oral or written) u/s. 167(2) CrPC before Magistrate instead argued orally without pleadings in a pending regular bail application filed u/s. 439 CrPC before HC.

Held, requirement of an application claiming statutory right u/s. 167(2) CrPC was prerequisite for the grant of bail on default. Such application has to be made before Magistrate for enforcement of statutory right. In the cases under the 1988 Act or other Acts where Special Courts were constituted by excluding the jurisdiction of Magistrate, it has to be made before such Special Court. In instant case, since appellant never sought default bail before Court concerned as such not entitled to the same. Appeals disposed of.

(C) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 164, 167, 167(2), 167(2)(a), 167(2)(a)(i), 167(2)(a)(ii), 173(8), 439 - Grant of bail - Constitutional right - Whether appellant was entitled to bail on merits.

Held, in instant case, allegations do not disclose merely an economic offence but it shows a transgression of constitutional rights of the victims of crime. Chairman of APSC has the responsibility on behalf of State for enforcement of Fundamental Rights of equality in matters of public employment. If the allegations were found to be true, then the offence cannot merely be considered as an economic offence but a fraud on Constitution itself by the persons appointed to enforce it. The role attributed to appellant, the fact that further investigation regarding the offence was underway, possibility of tampering evidence and influencing witnesses, it was not a fit case for grant of bail at such stage even on merits. Appeals disposed of.

Deepak Gupta, J.

(D) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 164, 167, 167(2), 167(2)(a), 167(2)(a)(i), 167(2)(a)(ii), 173(8), 439 - Punishable offence - Scientific investigation - Validity - Whether accused was entitled to grant of bail in terms of s. 167(2) CrPC if investigating agency does not file the charge-sheet within 60 days.

Held, s. 167(2)(a)(i) CrPC was applicable only in cases where accused was charged with offences punishable with death and any lower sentence, offences punishable with life imprisonment and any lower sentence and offences punishable with minimum sentence of 10 years. In all cases where minimum sentence was less than 10 years but the maximum sentence was not death or life imprisonment then s. 167(2)(a)(ii) CrPC would apply and accused would be entitled to grant of 'default bail' after 60 days in case charge-sheet was not filed. Therefore, if investigation was done scientifically and efficiently by police officials, who were earmarked and trained to do investigation work, then SC see no reason why investigation cannot normally be completed even within a period of 15 days, as envisaged in the year 1898. Appeals disposed of.

(E) Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, ss. 164, 167, 167(2), 167(2)(a), 167(2)(a)(i), 167(2)(a)(ii), 173(8), 439 - Bail application - Indefeasible right - Applicability - Whether accused could be enlarged on bail u/s. 167(2) CrPC even though he might not have made an application in writing u/s. 167(2) CrPC but has orally argued that he was entitled to grant of 'default bail'.

Held, though the period had expired accused would be deemed to be in legal custody till he does not furnish bail. The requirement was of furnishing of bail. Accused does not have to make out any grounds for grant of bail. He does not have to file a detailed application. All he has to aver in the application was that since 60/90 days have expired and charge-sheet has not been filed, he was entitled to bail and was willing to furnish bail. Such indefeasible right cannot be defeated by filing charge-sheet after accused has offered to furnish bail. SC in a large number of judgments has held that right to legal aid is also a fundamental right. It is the duty of counsel representing accused whether they are paid counsel or legal aid counsel to inform accused that on the expiry of statutory period of 60/90 days, they were entitled to 'default bail'. Magistrate should also not encourage wrongful detention and should inform accused of his right. In case accused still does not want to exercise his right then he should remain in custody but if he chooses to exercise his right and was willing to furnish bail he should be enlarged on bail. Appeals disposed of.
