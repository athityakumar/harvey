Energy Watchdog and others v Central Electricity Regulatory Commission and others

Supreme Court of India

11 April 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, Pinaki Chandra Ghose

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 275; 2017 (3) AWC 2692; JT 2017 (6) SC 161; 2017(4) SCALE 580

Case Digest

Subject: Electricity & Energy

Keywords: Consumer, Indian Electricity Act, 1910, Indian Contract Act, 1872, Tariff, Electricity (Supply) Act, 1948, Arbitration And Conciliation Act, 1996, Licensee, Arbitration, State Electricity Board, Domestic, Surcharge, Energy, Electricity Act, 2003, Works, Generating Company, Station, System, Electricity Regulatory Commissions Act, 1998, Income-tax Act, 1961

Summary: Electricity & Energy - Contract & Commercial - Electricity Act, 2003, ss. 3, 3(3), 63, 79, 79(1)(b) - Doctrine of frustration - Applicability of - Petitioner was selected by Gujarat Urja Vikas Nigam Limited (GUVNL) as successful bidder and on 2-2-2007, a Power Purchase Agreement (PPA) was entered between them - Petitioner filed petition before Commission seeking relief on impact of Indonesian Regulation to either discharge them from PPA or to restore petitioners to the same economic condition prior to change in law - Commission held that in exercise of regulatory powers provided u/s. 79 of the Act Commission could provide redressal of grievances to generating companies and constituted a committee to look into the alleged difficulties faced by petitioner - On appeal, Tribunal held that change in law provisions do not apply to foreign law and remanded matter to Commission to grant compensatory tariff - Commission by its order has arrived at a certain determination as to compensatory tariff to be granted on account of force majeure - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, doctrine of frustration cannot apply to those cases as the fundamental basis of PPAs remains unaltered. Nowhere do PPAs state that coal was to be procured only from Indonesia at a particular price. The fact that the fuel supply agreement has to be appended to PPA was only to indicate that the raw material for working of plant was there and was in order. It was clear that an unexpected rise in the price of coal would not absolve generating companies from performing their part of contract for the very good reason that when they submitted their bids, this was a risk they knowingly took. SC was of view that mere fact that bid might be non-escalable does not mean that respondents were precluded from raising the plea of frustration, if otherwise it was available in law and could be pleaded by them. However, fact that a non-escalable tariff was paid for was a factor which might be taken into account only to show that the risk of supplying electricity at the tariff indicated was upon generating company. Further, for the operation period of PPA, compensation for any increase/decrease in cost to seller should be determined and be effective from such date as decided by Commission. Though change in Indonesian law would not qualify as a change in law under the guidelines read with PPA, change in Indian law certainly would. It was to such limited extent that change in law was held in favour of respondents. Impugned judgments of Tribunal and Commission's orders were set aside. Commission was directed to go into the matter afresh and determine what relief should be granted to those power generators who fall within cl. 13 of PPA. Appeals disposed of.
