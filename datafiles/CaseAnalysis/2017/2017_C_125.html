Excel Crop Care Limited v Competition Commission of India and another

Supreme Court of India

08 May 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, N.V. Ramana

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 986; 2017 (2) CPJ(Sc) 20

Case Digest

Subject: Consumer Protection

Keywords: Airlines, Co-operative Bank, companies, comprehensive, consumer, illegal, industries, lic, manufacturer, Restrictive Trade Practice, sale-deed, shed, tender, tractor, Trade Marks, trader, van

Summary: (A) Competition – Competition Act, 2002, ss.3, 3(3)(a), 3(3)(b), 3(3)(d) – Anti-competitive agreement – Collusive bidding – FCI complained to CCI of an anti-competitive agreement alleged to be arrived at among appellants and Agrosynth Chemicals in relation to tenders issued by FCI for Aluminium Phosphide Tablets of 3 gms, between the years 2007 and 2009 – Later, upon hearing parties, CCI passed order concluding that appellants entered into anti-competitive agreement in a concerted manner offending the provisions of s.3 of the Act and as such, imposed penalty of 9% on average total turnover of their establishments for last three years – Aggrieved, appellants filed three separate appeals before COMPAT and vide common judgment, COMPAT rejected all contentions raised by appellants except qua penalty of the appellants – Hence, instant appeals by appellants against the findings of COMPAT holding that there was violation of ss.3(3)(a), 3(3)(b) and 3(3)(d), of the Act on the part of appellants – Further, CCI also field appeals against that part of the impugned order whereby penalty imposed upon appellants is restricted to ‘relevant turnover’ instead of ‘total turnover’ – Whether conclusion of CCI that appellants contravened ss. 3(3)(a), 3(3)(b) and 3(3)(d) of the Act is justified. Held, there are clear cut examples of identical pricing by three appellants. Further, though, appellants could not be penalized in respect of 7 tenders from 14-3-2007 to 27-2-2009 as there was no provision like s.3 at that time, yet, such illustrations are important in finding out the mens rea of appellants, i.e., arriving at an agreement to enter into collusive bidding which continued with impunity right upto 2011. Further, such trend of quoting identical price in respect of so many tenders, not only of FCI but other Govt. bodies as well, is sufficient to negate all explanations given by appellants taking the pretext of coincidence or economic forces. Further, there could not be coincidence to such an extent that almost at all times price quoted by three appellants is identical, not even few paisa more or less from each other. Further, there was a 10 years history of quoting identical prices; there were only four suppliers of the product in the market out of which three are the appellants; even when the cost of production is different, they have quoted identical prices; even when the geographical location of the three suppliers is different, strange coincidence of identical pricing is found, that too repeatedly; profit margin would be different, still quotations are same; and to different parties, in respect of different tenders, different rates are quoted but whatever price is quoted in respect of one particular tender, that is identical but it is too much of a coincidence, difficult to believe. Hence, onus was on appellants in view of s.3 of the Act to justify the trend, but they have failed to discharge the burden and as such, ingredients of s.3 are satisfied. Further, as collusion is proved by conduct of the appellants in abstaining from the bidding in respect of May 2011 tender, requirement of s.3(3)(d) of the Act read with ‘explanation’ thereto is satisfied, viz., concerted action based on an agreement/arrangement between the appellants resulted in restricting or manipulating competition or process of bidding since the said act was collusive in nature. As such, COMPAT rightly held that there was violation of ss.3(3)(a),3(3)(b) and 3(3)(d) of the Act by appellants. Appeals dismissed. (B) Competition – Competition Act, 2002, ss.2(y),27(b) – Penalty – Turnover – Whether penalty u/s.27(b) of the Act has to be on total/entire turnover of the offending company or it could be only on ‘relevant turnover’. Held, s.2(y) of the Act which defines ‘turnover’ does not provide any clarity to the clause and it only mentions that turnover includes value of goods or services. Thus, there is absence of certainty as to what precise meaning should be ascribed to the expression ‘turnover’. However, in the absence of specific provision as to whether such turnover has to be product specific or entire turnover of the offending company, the criteria of ‘relevant turnover’ for the purpose of imposition of penalty is more in tune with ethos of the Act and the legal principles which surround matters pertaining to imposition of penalties. Hence, there is no error in the approach of order of COMPAT interpreting s.27(b) of the Act. Appeals dismissed.
