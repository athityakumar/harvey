J. Balaji Singh v Diwakar Cole and others

Supreme Court of India

24 April 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

A.M. Sapre, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 296; AIR 2017 SC 2402; 2017 (4) ALD(SC) 180; 2017 (123) ALR 817; 2017 (4) Bom.C.R. 65; 2017 (2) CLT(SC) 427; 2017 (4) CTC 690; JT 2017 (4) SC 444; 2017 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1761; 2017 (3) MLJ 839; 2017 (3) RCR(Civil) 85; 2017(5) SCALE 194

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Keywords: Additional Evidence, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908

Summary: Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O. 41 r. 27, O. 43 r. 1(u), ss. 100, 151, 96 - Suit for declaration - Additional evidence - Dismissal - Appellant filed suit before Trial Court against respondents for declaration and injunction restraining them from interfering in his possession over suit property which was dismissed - Appellant on appeal filed an application under O. 41 r. 27 r/w s. 151 CPC seeking permission to file additional evidence - Appellate Court allowed appeal and remanded case to Trial Court for deciding the suit afresh - Aggrieved, respondents filed appeal before HC under O. 43 r. 1(u) CPC - Single Judge allowed appeal and restored judgment and decree of Trial Court - Hence, instant appeal - Whether HC was justified in allowing respondent's appeal and thereby justified in restoring judgment/decree of Trial Court.

Held, once Appellate Court allowed application under O. 41 r. 27 CPC and took on record additional evidence, it rightly set aside judgment/decree of Trial Court giving liberty to parties to lead additional evidence in support of their case which enabled Trial Court to decide suit afresh. Appellate Court was justified in taking recourse to powers conferred on Appellate Court under O. 41 r. 23A CPC for remanding the case to Trial Court. SC found no fault in exercise of such power by Appellate Court. HC committed jurisdictional error when it examined the case on merits and set aside judgment of Appellate Court and restored judgment of Trial Court. HC should not have done that for the simple reason that it was only examining the legality of remand order in an appeal filed under O. 43 r. 1(u) CPC. Once HC failed to see that when Appellate Court itself did not decide appeal on merits and considered it proper to remand the case to Trial Court, a fortiori, HC had no jurisdiction to decide appeal on merits. HC should have seen that O. 43 r. 1(u) CPC gives a limited power to examine the issue relating to legality of remand order. Thus, Appellate Court rightly allowed appellant to file additional documents in appeal which satisfied the requirements of O. 41 r. 27 CPC. Impugned judgment of HC was set aside and restore that of Appellate Court. Appeal allowed.
