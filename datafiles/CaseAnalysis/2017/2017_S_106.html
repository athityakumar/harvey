National Securities Depository Limited v Securities and Exchange Board of India

Supreme Court of India

07 March 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, Pinaki Chandra Ghose

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 189; (2017) 5 SCC 517; AIR 2017 SC 1714; 2017 (2) BC 617; [2017] 202 Comp Cas 22; 2017 (2) CompLJ 415; 2017 (348) E.L.T. 601; JT 2017 (6) SC 370; 2017(4) SCALE 79

Case Digest

Subject: Securities Exchange Board of India

Keywords: Jurisdiction, Stock, Trading, Registration, Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, Certificate Of Registration, Stock Exchange, Securities, Securities And Exchange Board Of India Act, 1992, Electricity Act, 2003, Telecom Regulatory Authority of India Act, 1997, Public Company, Muslim Wakfs Act, 1954, Investors, Securities Appellate Tribunal, Representation Of The People Act, 1951, Bombay Tenancy and Agricultural Lands Act, 1948, Mineral Concession Rules, 1949, Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, Constitution of India, 1950, Land Acquisition Act, 1894

Summary: Securities Exchange Board of India - Securities and Exchange Board of India Act, 1992, ss. 11(1), 11(4), 11(b), 11(d), 12(3), 15T - Representation of the People Act, 1951, s 29A - Administrative circular - Restriction - SEBI/respondents issued administrative circular u/s. 11(1) of the 1992 Act to protect the interests of investors and regulate securities market - Appellants raised objections before Tribunal under the 1992 Act - Tribunal dismissed appeals holding that there being nothing in the 1992 Act to restrict an appeal only against quasi-judicial orders, appeals would lie against all three types of orders under the 1992 Act - Hence, instant appeals - Whether an administrative circular that was issued by SEBI u/s. 11(1) of the 1992 Act could be the subject matter of appeal u/s. 15T of the 1992 Act.

Held, in addition to the tests laid down, the absence of a lis between the parties would not necessarily lead to conclusion that the power conferred on an administrative body would not be quasi-judicial, so long as the tests were followed, the power was quasi-judicial. A quasi-judicial function lying somewhere in between was an administrative function which the law requires to be exercised in some respects as if it were judicial. A quasi-judicial decision is, therefore, a decision which is subject to a certain measure of judicial procedure. It might be stated that both Rules made u/s. 29 of the 1951 Act as well as Regulations have to be placed before Parliament u/s. 31 of the Act. It was clear on a conspectus of the authorities that it was orders referable to ss. 11(4), 11(b), 11(d), 12(3) and 15T of the Act, being quasi-judicial orders, and quasi-judicial orders made under the Rules and Regulations that were subject matter of appeal u/s. 15T of the Act. Administrative orders such as circulars issued in instant case referable to s. 11(1) of the Act were outside the appellate jurisdiction of Tribunal. Impugned judgment of Tribunal was set aside. Appeals disposed of.
