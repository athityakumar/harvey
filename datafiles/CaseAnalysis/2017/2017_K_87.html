Mobilox Innovations Private Limited v Kirusa Software Private Limited

Supreme Court of India

21 September 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, Sanjay Kishan Kaul

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 1041; 2017 (6) AWC 5658

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance

Keywords: affected, arbitration, auction, balance sheet, Bankrupt, cheque, contract, conversion, financial institution, foreign exchange, gic, good faith, guarantor, in addition to, indemnity, Insolvency, insolvent, interlocutory, jurisdiction, probate, secured debt, Tribunal, uti, Without prejudice

Summary: Corporate - Banking & Finance - Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016, ss. 8, 9 - Operational debt - NDA breach - Denial - Appellant sub-contracted tele-voting work to respondent and issued purchase orders - Respondent followed up with appellant for payment of pending invoices - Appellant withheld payments against invoices raised as respondent had breached non-disclosure agreement (NDA) - Respondent filed an application before Tribunal against appellant u/ss. 8 and 9 of the Code stating that an operational debt was owed to respondent which was dismissed - On appeal, Appellate Tribunal allowed appeal and remitted the case to Adjudicating authority - Hence, instant appeal - Appellant contended that application should have been dismissed on ground that operational creditor did not furnish a copy of the certificate from a financial institution.

Held, confirmation from a financial institution that there was no payment of an unpaid operational debt by corporate debtor is an important piece of information that needs to be placed before Adjudicating Authority u/s. 9 of the Code, however given the fact that Adjudicating authority has not dismissed the application on that ground and appellant has raised such ground only at the appellate stage, SC was of view that application cannot be dismissed at the threshold for want of that certificate alone. The correspondence between the parties would show that appellant clearly informed respondent that they had displayed appellants confidential client information and client campaign information on a public platform which constituted a breach of trust and a breach of NDA between the parties. Respondents were further told that all amounts that were due to them were withheld till the time the matter was resolved. Further, without going into the merits of the dispute, appellant has raised a plausible contention requiring further investigation which was not a patently feeble legal argument or an assertion of facts unsupported by evidence. The defence is not spurious, mere bluster, plainly frivolous or vexatious. Respondent itself has not commenced any legal proceedings except for the present insolvency application which was filed almost 2 years after the said e-mail. A dispute does truly exist in fact between the parties, which may or may not ultimately succeed, and Appellate Tribunal was wholly incorrect in characterising the defence as vague, got-up and motivated to evade liability. Impugned judgment of Appellate Tribunal was set aside. Appeal allowed.
