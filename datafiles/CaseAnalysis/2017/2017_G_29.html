Gautam Jain v Union of India and another

Supreme Court of India

04 January 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, A.M. Sapre

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 12; (2017) 3 SCC 133; AIR 2017 SC 230; 2017 CRLJ 965; 2017 (345) E.L.T. 586; JT 2017 (1) SC 346; 2017 (1) RCR(Criminal) 546; 2017(1) SCALE 310

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Gold, Opportunity, Communication, Confessional Statement, Search, Detenu, Reliance, Detaining Authority, Subjective Satisfaction, Inference, Conservation Of Foreign Exchange And Prevention Of Smuggling Activities Act, 1974, Vague, Inadmissible, Constitution of India, 1950, Seizure, Plea

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Conservation of Foreign Exchange and Prevention of Smuggling Activities Act, 1974, ss. 3, 3(1), 5A - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 22(5), 32 - Detention order - Principle of segregation - Challenged - Respondent no. 2 passed a detention order dt. 23-09-2009 against appellant u/s. 3(1) of the Act whereby appellant was directed to be detained - Appellant filed writ petition before HC seeking direction to respondents to set appellant to liberty and for quashing of detention order - HC dismissed writ petition holding that if the documents pertaining to one particular ground were not furnished that ground could be ignored applying the principle of segregation and on remaining grounds detention order was sustainable - Hence, instant appeal - Whether detention order was based on one ground alone or it was a case of multiple grounds on which impugned detention order was passed.

Held, SC inclined to agree with the conclusion of HC that the order of detention was based on multiple grounds inasmuch as various different acts, which form separate grounds were mentioned on the basis of which detaining authority formed the opinion that it was desirable to put appellant under detention. Once it was found that the detention order contains many grounds, even if one of them was to be rejected, principle of segregation contained in s. 5A of the Act gets attracted. That provision commands communication of grounds on which the order of detention was passed and to afford him the earliest opportunity of making a representation against the order. In instant case, the documents containing the statement were not given and for that reason, HC rightly held that such a ground cannot be relied upon by respondents in support of the order. However, that would not mean that if there were other grounds on which the detention order could be sustained, principle of severability would become inapplicable. If that was accepted, it would mean that provisions of s. 5A of the Act cannot be applied at all. While rejecting such a contention, it would be sufficient to point out that constitutional validity of s. 5A of the Act was challenged in SC and repelled in the case of Attorney General for India & Ors. v. Amratlal Prajivandas & Ors., 1994 Indlaw SC 400 after discussing the provisions of s. 5A of the Act in the light of art. 22(5) of Constitution. Appeal dismissed.
