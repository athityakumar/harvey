Parle Agro Private Limited and others v Ersus Commissioner of Commercial Taxes, Trivandrum and others

Supreme Court of India

09 May 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Ashok Bhushan, A.K. Sikri

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 853; 2017 (352) E.L.T. 113; JT 2017 (6) SC 445

Case Digest

Subject: Sales Tax

Keywords: Tribunal, Tax Liability, Central Excise Tariff Act, 1985, Competent Authority, Review, Turnover, Rate Of Tax, Rate, Punjab General Sales Tax Act, 1948, Kerala Value Added Tax Act, 2003, Processing, Import (Control) Order, 1955, Fruit Products Order, 1955, Kerala Finance Act, 2007, Food Safety and Standards Act, 2011, Food Safety and Standards (Food Safety & Standards and Food Additives) Regulations, 2011

Summary: (A) VAT - Indirect Tax - Kerala Value Added Tax Act, 2003, ss. 6, 6(1)(a), 6(1)(d), 94 - Entry 71 - Scope and ambit - Appellant/dealer classified fruit juice based drink under Entry 71 of notification issued u/s. 6(1)(d) of the Act - AO held that assesse was liable to pay tax @ 20% on the product - Appellant filed an application u/s. 94 of the Act seeking clarification of product which was not done - Appellant filed writ petition before HC seeking direction to Commissioner to consider and pass order on application for clarification - Single Judge directed Commissioner to consider and pass orders on clarification application within a month - Commissioner passed an order classifying the product as 'aerated branded soft drinks', at the rate of 20% - On appeal, DB dismissed appeal and upheld order of Commissioner - Hence, instant appeal - Whether there was inter-relation between ss. 6(1)(a) and 6(1)(d) of the Act and scope and ambit of Item 5 of Entry 71 as amended.

Held, when fruit juice based drinks were covered under Entry 71, State Govt. knew that fruit juice based drinks were not covered by s. 6(1)(a) of the Act. Applicability of the power of State to issue notification u/s. 6(1)(d) of the Act arises only when goods were not covered by s. 6(1)(a) of the Act. Fruit juice based drinks were never treated as 'aerated branded soft drinks' which was the understanding of State while issuing notification u/s. 6(1)(d) of the Act. Had fruit juice based drinks were also to be covered by aerated branded soft drinks, there was no occasion for subordinate legislative authority, i.e., State Govt. to include such products in notification u/s. 6(1)(d) of the Act. The Entry of fruit juice based drinks got subsumed in the residuary entry and the amendment did not change or affect the character and content of the products which were included in Entry 71. Thus, the delegated legislative power of issuing notification to State Govt. was restricted and could be exercised only when goods do not fall u/ss. 6(1)(a) or 6(1)(c) of the Act. Appeals disposed of.

(B) VAT - Indirect Tax - Kerala Value Added Tax Act, 2003, ss. 6, 6(1)(a), 6(1)(d), 94 - Commercial parlance test - Technical evidence - Whether common parlance test was the only test to be applied for understanding the different entries u/ss. 6(1)(a) and 6(1)(d) of the Act.

Held, in instant case, Entry 2 u/s. 6(1)(a) of the Act uses the word 'aerated'. Common parlance and commercial parlance test was not the only test which could have been applied for interpreting the entries in items mentioned in s. 6(1)(a) of the Act and the entries which contain scientific and technical word were also to be looked into in technical and scientific meaning. Both HC and Commissioner discarded the evidence of technical and scientific meaning of word. Appellant has rightly relied on the technical evidence brought on the record which indicate that use of carbon dioxide to the extent of 0.6 per cent was only for the purpose of preservative in packaging the commodities and the product was thermally processed and carbon dioxide was added to as preservative. Appeals disposed of.

(C) VAT - Indirect Tax - Kerala Value Added Tax Act, 2003, ss. 6, 6(1)(a), 6(1)(d), 94 - Principle applicability - Overlooked - Whether Principle of Noscitur a Sociis was applicable to instant case.

Held, applying the principle of construction of 'noscitur a sociis' on Entry 71 it was clear that cl. 5 of Entry 71 has to take colour and meaning from the other items included in Entry 71. Item 5 of Entry 71 uses the words "similar other products not specifically mentioned under any other entry in that list or any other schedule". Thus, products which are to be covered under Item No.5 are similar other products. When Item No.2 of the Entry 71 i.e. fruit juice, fruit concentrates, fruit squash, fruit syrup and pulp, and fruit cordial and item No.4 i.e. health drinks of all varieties, are kept in mind fruit juice based drink should fall in Item No.5. Both HC and Commissioner overlooked that principle while interpreting item No.5 of Entry 71. Appeals disposed of.

(D) VAT - Indirect Tax - Kerala Value Added Tax Act, 2003, ss. 6, 6(1)(a), 6(1)(d), 94 - Clarification application - Sustainability - Whether DB could preclude Commissioner to examine the materials filed by appellant along with Clarification Application u/s. 94 of the Act.

Held, order passed by DB was a case of assessment of another assessee which decision was based on the materials brought on the record by the said assessee and could not have precluded appellant from filing the application u/s. 94 of the Act and when DB dismissed appeal Commissioner ought to have followed the observation given by DB. Thus, judgment of DB did not conclude the issue and Commissioner was not absolved from its duty of deciding the same in accordance with the materials brought on the record by appellant and although Committee noticed all the pleadings and relying on the ruling dismissed clarification application which cannot be sustained. Appeals disposed of.

(E) VAT - Indirect Tax - Product classification - Validity - Whether Tribunal decision has any relevance with regard to the classification of product in question.

Held, though order of Tribunal did not conclude the controversy in favour of appellant but fact that Tribunal did not hold the product to be under the "aerated water" was a factor which necessitated a more deeper consideration by HC to find out as to whether the product is 'aerated branded soft drink' or not. HC in its judgment found that since the product charged with air or carbon dioxide was an aerated drink. From the manufacturing process which was on the record, it was clear that carbon dioxide to the extent of 0.6 percent was added as preservative. Technical note submitted on behalf of appellant clearly mentioned that use of carbon dioxide was only as a preservative of product. Appeals disposed of.

(F) VAT - Indirect Tax - Certifications - Expert opinion - Rejection - Whether Committee as well as HC rightly discarded technical and expert opinion relied by appellant.

Held, technical opinion clearly mentioned that carbon dioxide was used for preservation purpose only. Before Committee entire process of manufacture of the product was explained along with all relevant orders and certificates of Food Safety Authorities. It was stated that Experts in their opinions and certifications have mentioned that product was commercially and technically distinct from products which have classified as 'aerated branded soft drinks'. The certifications which were relied by the appellant indicate that product does not undergo aeration or carbonation product was thermally processed with CO2 which help in preserving the Apple Juice concentrate which is otherwise perishable in nature. The expert authority and its opinion which were relied by appellant were required to be adverted to Clarification Authority as well as HC and expert opinion and materials were erroneously discarded. Therefore, Food Safety Authority and expert opinion regarding process of manufacture relied by appellant were relevant materials and Clarification Authority and HC erred in law in discarding those materials. Appeals disposed of.
