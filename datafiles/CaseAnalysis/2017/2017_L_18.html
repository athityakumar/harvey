Lekh Raj (Dead), through Lrs. and others v Ranjit Singh and others

Supreme Court of India

16 August 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Abhay Manohar Sapre, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 624

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property

Keywords: Second Appeal, Punjab Custom (Power to Contest) Act, 1920

Summary: Land & Property - Civil Procedure - Punjab Custom (Power to Contest) Act, 1920 - Execution of Decree - Retrospective effect of the Act - Respondents-Decree holders filed execution application for execution of decree awarded in their favour, before Executing Court and upon hearing parties, Executing Court overruled all objections raised by petitioners-judgment debtors and allowed application - Aggrieved, petitioners-judgment debtors filed appeal before appellate court but the same was dismissed and order of Executing Court was affirmed - Aggrieved, they approached HC but HC also dismissed revision and orders of Executing Court and Appellate Court were affirmed and hence, instant appeal contending that decree in question was rendered nullity in the light of amendment made in 1973 in the Act - Whether objection raised for first time before SC could be allowed.

Held, objection raised before SC was not raised at any stage of proceedings, either before Executing Court or Appellate Court or HC and was raised for first time in instant appeal. Further, nothing prevented petitioners-judgment debtors to raise the objection along with several other objections to enable Courts to record their finding on such objection. Even otherwise, suit and appeal stood already decided much prior to date of amendment coming into force and so, suit or appeal remained unaffected with the amendment. Suit was filed in 1962 whereas Appellate Court passed decree in 1965 and no appeal was filed against orders of appellate court and so, order of Appellate Court attained finality. The amendment in the Act was introduced and came into force in 1973. Hence, as the rights of the parties had already been crystallized then, subsequent change in law would not take away such rights which had attained finality due to lis coming to an end inter se the parties prior to such change. Further, objections raised by petitioner-judgment debtors were all on facts but could not be enquired into in executing proceedings and also executing Court could not go behind the decree. Appeal dismissed.
