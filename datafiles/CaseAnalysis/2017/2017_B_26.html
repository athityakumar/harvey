Balakrishnan v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

11 January 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sikri, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 328; (2017) 3 SCC 634; [2017] 391 ITR 178; JT 2017 (2) SC 176; 2017(2) SCALE 1; [2017] 247 TAXMAN 16

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes; Land & Property

Keywords: Land Acquisition, Compulsory Acquisition, Income-tax Act, 1961, Land Acquisition Act, 1894, Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013

Summary: Land & Property - Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 18, 4, 4(1), 5A, 6, 9 - Income-tax Act, 1961, ss. 10(37), 148, 18 - Compulsory acquisition - Resettlement award - Validity - Appellant filed writ petition before HC challenging order of respondent no. 2 whereby respondent no. 2 held that appellant's case did not come under compulsory acquisition and directed AO to compute income - Single Judge dismissed writ petition relying upon judgment of HC in case of Info Park Kerala v. Assistant Commissioner of Income Tax, 2008 Indlaw KER 371 - On appeal, DB dismissed appeal affirming view of Single Judge - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, merely because the compensation amount agreed upon would not change the character of acquisition from that of compulsory acquisition to voluntary sale. It might be mentioned that such was now the procedure which is laid down even under the 2013 Act as per which Collector could pass rehabilitation and resettlement award with the consent of parties/land owners. Nonetheless, the character of acquisition remains compulsory. It was clear that for Notification and award u/ss. 4 and 9 of the 1894 Act, appellant would not have entered into any negotiations for compensation of consideration which he was to receive for the said land. As far as the acquisition of the land in question was concerned, there was no consent. Appellant was put in such a condition that he knew that his land was acquired and he cannot reiterate the same. Therefore, appellant only wanted to salvage the situation by receiving as much compensation as possible commensurate with the market value and in the process avoid the litigation so that appellant was able to receive compensation well in time. If for that purpose appellant entered into the negotiations, such negotiations would be confined to the quantum of compensation only and cannot change or alter the nature of acquisition which would remain compulsory. Appeal allowed.
