State of West Bengal v Sarkar and Sarkar

Supreme Court of India

19 April 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Jagdish Singh Khehar, Dr. D.Y. Chandrachud, Sanjay Kishan Kaul

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 836; 2017(6) SCALE 495

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR

Keywords: Arbitral Tribunal, Jurisdiction, Arbitration And Conciliation Act, 1996

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, ss. 11, 8 16, 37, 7(4)(c ) - Appointment of Arbitrator - Award Challenged - Respondent/firm and appellant/state entered into contractual agreement - By virtue of direction from HC, Arbitrator was appointed and refused to entertain dispute on ground of jurisdiction - HC set aside order of Arbitrator in appeal u/s. 37 of the Act by respondent on ground that cl.12 provided for settlement of disputes between parties arising out of contractual obligation by way of arbitration - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, the fact that the Arbitral Tribunal has the competence to rule on its own jurisdiction and to define the contours of its jurisdiction, only means that when such issues arise before it, the Tribunal can, and possibly, ought to decide them. This can happen when the parties have gone to the Arbitral Tribunal without recourse to s. 8 or 11 of the Act. But where the jurisdictional issues are decided under these sections, before a reference is made, s. 16 of the Act cannot be held to empower the Arbitral Tribunal to ignore the decision given by the judicial authority or the Chief Justice before the reference to it was made. The competence to decide does not enable the Arbitral Tribunal to get over the finality conferred on an order passed prior to its entering upon the reference by the very statute that creates it. Thus, u/s. 16, of the Act, the Arbitral Tribunal has the right to rule on its own jurisdiction and any objection, with respect to the existence or validity of the arbitration agreement. Once it is held that there is an adjudicatory function entrusted to the Chief Justice by the Act, obviously the right of the Arbitral Tribunal to go behind the order passed by the Chief Justice would take another hue and would be controlled by s. 11(7) of the Act. In instant case, 'X' was actually appointed as an arbitrator by the HC in exercise of the powers vested in the HC, u/s. 11 of the Act. Further, as against the claim raised by the respondent/firm before the arbitrator, the appellant/state had indeed raised a counter-claim. And having done so, it must be deemed to have submitted before the arbitrator, a request to adjudicate its claims as well. When both parties, had approached the arbitrator, and submitted themselves to the arbitrator's jurisdiction, independent of all other factual and legal considerations, the arbitrability of the disputes was clearly made out u/s. 7(4)(c) of the Act. Hence, the conclusion drawn by HC does not warrant any interference. Appeal dismissed.
