Mumbai Port Trust v Lakshmi Steels and others

Supreme Court of India

27 July 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

Deepak Gupta, Madan B. Lokur

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 581; 2017 (352) E.L.T. 401; JT 2017 (8) SC 13

Case Digest

Subject: Customs; Indirect Tax

Keywords: Services, Customs Act, 1962, Collector Of Customs, Imported Goods, Part, Port Trust, Any Goods, Demurrage Charges, Importer, Provisional, Bonds, Issued, Time, India, Samples, Or Otherwise, Such Goods, Shipping Line, Major Port Trusts Act, 1963, Indian Ports Act, 1908, International Airports Authority Act, 1971, Handling of Cargo in Customs Areas Regulations, 2009, Madras Port Trust Act

Summary: (A) Customs - Indirect Tax - Customs Act, 1962, ss. 157, 160(9), 18, 45, 45(1), 48 - Handling of Cargo in Customs Areas Regulations, 2009, regns. 2(1), 2(b), 6(1) - Major Port Trusts Act, 1963, ss. 128, 160(9), 3, 42, 43, 43A, 45, 47A, 48, 48(1), 53, 58, 59 - Consignments import - Demurrage charges - Waiver - Respondents imported consignments of cold rolled coils and sought Customs Authorities to release goods to avoid payment of demurrage and detention charges - However, Customs Authorities did not respond to the same - Aggrieved, respondents filed writ petitions before HC seeking detention of goods by Customs as illegal - HC directed Customs Authorities that goods imported by respondents be released to them on payment of custom duty and detention charges were ordered to be borne by Customs - Hence, instant appeals - Whether any direction could be given to appellants to waive demurrage charges.

Held, appellants has the power and authority to levy rates including demurrage as fixed by the Tariff Authority u/s. 47A of the 1963 Act. Such right of the Port Trust was not affected either by the provisions of the 1962 Act or by the Regulations. Neither the regulations nor the provisions of the 1963 Act could impinge or in any manner affect the statutory power of Major Port Trusts to levy rates under the 1963 Act. In fact, the Authority that framed the Regulations was itself aware of that because regn. 6(l) of the Regulations itself begins with the words subject to any other law for the time being in force. Therefore, those Regulations cannot in any manner affect the right of Port Trust. HC erred in holding that the law settled by SC was no longer applicable in view of the Regulations. Reliance placed by UOI on s. 128 of the 1963 Act was totally misplaced. That provision only deals with the right of Central Govt. to collect customs duties. It does not deal with the rights of Port Trust to collect rates including demurrage. Appeals allowed.

(B) Customs - Indirect Tax - Customs Act, 1962, ss. 157, 160(9), 18, 45, 45(1), 48 - Handling of Cargo in Customs Areas Regulations, 2009, regns. 2(1), 2(b), 6(1) - Major Port Trusts Act, 1963, ss. 128, 160(9), 3, 42, 43, 43A, 45, 47A, 48, 48(1), 53, 58, 59 - Imported goods - Detention charges - Validity - Whether the liability to pay demurrage/detention charges in respect of imported goods could be fastened upon Directorate of Revenue Intelligence (DRI)/Customs Authorities.

Held, respondents did not take the benefit of provisional assessment offered. All other importers took benefit of such offer given by DRI and got their goods released but respondents for the reasons best known to them did not take the benefit of such offer. Though there might be some delay on part of DRI and customs authorities, respondent have also been guilty of delaying the matter and they cannot claim that they are not liable to pay demurrage and detention charges. Respondent were free to approach appellants in terms of s. 53 of the 1963 Act for exemption and remission of demurrage and other charges and Board may take a sympathetic view while considering the case of respondent u/s. 53 of the 1963 Act. As far as detention charges of Shipping Line were concerned, HC could not in writ proceedings have directed DRI/Customs to pay detention charges to Shipping Line since those were to be paid on the basis of a contract between respondents and shipping line. Appeals allowed.
