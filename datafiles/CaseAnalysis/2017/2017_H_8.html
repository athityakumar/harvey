HRD Corporation (Marcus Oil and Chemical Division) v GAIL (India) Limited (Formerly Gas Authority of India Limited)

Supreme Court of India

31 August 2017

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, Sanjay Kishan Kaul

Where Reported

2017 Indlaw SC 658

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR

Keywords: Arbitral Award, Arbitral Tribunal, Legal Representative, Arbitration And Conciliation Act, 1996, De Jure, Arbitration and Conciliation (Amendment) Act, 2015

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, ss. 12, 14 - Arbitrator appointment - Award passed - Challenged - Appellant and respondent entered into an agreement - Disputes arose between parties and appellant invoked arbitration clause - Arbitral Tribunal passed an award for specific performance of contract - Appellant filed applications u/s. 12 of the Act seeking termination of mandate of Arbitrator - Applications were disposed of holding that they were entitled to continue with arbitration - Aggrieved, appellant filed writ petitions before HC challenging the same, which were dismissed - Hence, instant appeals - Whether once a Seventh Schedule challenge was presented before Court, arbitrator becomes ineligible and de jure u/s. 14 of the Act.

Held, fact that Arbitrator has already rendered an award in a previous arbitration between the parties would not, by itself, on the ground of reasonable likelihood of bias, render him ineligible to be an arbitrator in a subsequent arbitration. SC was not been shown anything to indicate that Arbitrator would be a person holding a pronounced anti-claimant. SC was satisfied that there was no real possibility that Arbitrator would not bring an open mind and objective judgment to bear on arguments made by the parties in fourth arbitration, which may or may not differ from arguments made in the third arbitration. The appointment of Arbitrator was also attacked on the ground that he had not made a complete disclosure, in that his disclosure statement did not indicate as to whether he was likely to devote sufficient time to the arbitration and would be able to complete it within 12 months. SC cannot allow appellant to raise such point at that stage as it was never raised earlier. However, if Justice did not indicate anything to the contrary, he would be able to devote sufficient time to arbitration and complete the process within 12 months. It was also faintly urged that arbitrator should without delay make a disclosure to the parties in writing. Justice disclosure was by a letter 10-31-2016 which was sent to Secretary General of the International Centre for Alternative Dispute Resolution (ICADR). It has come on record that for no fault of Arbitrator, ICADR, through oversight, did not handover the said letter or a copy thereof to appellant until 11-24-2016. Impugned judgment of Single Judge required no interference. Appeals dismissed.
