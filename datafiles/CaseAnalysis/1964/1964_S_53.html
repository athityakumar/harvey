 Special Reference No. 1 Of 1964 v Na

Supreme Court of India

30 September 1964

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, A.K. Sarkar, K. Subba Rao, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, Mohammad Hidayatullah, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1964 Indlaw SC 447; AIR 1965 SC 745; [1965] 1 S.C.R. 413

Case Digest

Subject: Advocates & Judges; Constitution; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: Freedom Of Speech And Expression

Summary: P. B. Gajendragadkar C.J., K. Subba Rao, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, K. N. Wanchoo, M. Hidayatullah, J. C. Shah, JJ (per)

(A) Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 143 - Reference u/art. 143(1) - Competency - State Legislature committed a person to prison for contempt of the House - Contemnor challenged the committal before the HC with a pray for grant of bail - Two Judges of the HC granted bail - State Legislature found that the contemnor and his advocate and the Judges of the HC who granted the bail had committed contempt of Legislature - State Legislature passed a resolution that all of them be produced before it in custody - Judges and Advocate, subsequently, filed a writ petition before the HC - Full Bench of the HC stayed the execution of resolution, however, State Legislature, in the meantime, amended the resolution and asked the Judges and Advocate to appear before it and to give explanation instead of being produced in the custody - President of India, subsequently, referred the matter to the SC for its opinion - Whether the present Reference to the SC u/art. 143(1) is competent one - Held, terms of art. 143(1) are very wide and all that they require is that the President should be satisfied that the questions to be referred are of such a nature and of such public importance that it would be expedient to obtain the SC opinion on them - President's order making the present Reference showed that he was satisfied with its public importance, and hence the Reference was competent one - Earlier References made by the President u/art. 143(1) showed no uniform pattern and that was consistent with the broad and wide words used in art. 143(1) - Further, SC has no obligation to answer a Reference u/art. 143(1), however, refusal to make answer is justifiable only in those cases where question referred to is relating to purely socio-economic or political character with no constitutional significance at all - Present Reference, however, raised questions of grave constitutional importance and the answers given by the Court would help the President to advise the Union and State Governments to take suitable legislative or executive action - Order accordingly.

(B) Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, arts. 194 - State Legislature - Power to exclude the jurisdiction of courts - Whether State Legislature has its own powers and privileges to exclude the jurisdiction of Courts - Held, State Legislatures could not by virtue of art. 194(3) claim to be the sole judge of their powers and privileges to the exclusion of the Courts - State Legislature's powers and privileges were to be found in art. 194(3) alone and nowhere else, and the power to interpret that Article lay under the scheme of the Constitution rests exclusively with the Judiciary of the country - Order accordingly.

(C) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 194 - Privileges of State Legislature - Fundamental Rights - Conflict between - Whether art. 194(3) is made subject to the provisions of fundamental rights - Held, although art. 194(3) has not been made expressly subject to the provisions of the Constitution, it would be unreasonable in construing it to ignore other provisions, if for valid reasons they were found to be relevant and applicable - Therefore wherever it appeared that there was a conflict between the provisions of art. 194(3) and the provisions relating to fundamental rights, an attempt had to be made to resolve the said conflict by the adoption of the rule of harmonious construction as was done in Pandit M. S. M. Sharma v. Shri Sri Krishna Sinha & Others, 1958 INDLAW SC 135 - View taken in Sharma's case that the laws defining the powers and privileges of the legislatures under the first part of art. 194(3) would be subjected to art.13 and therefore to the fundamental rights - Order accordingly.

(D) Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 21, 32, 194, 226 - Contempt of State Legislature - General Warrant - Interference by HC - Justifiability - Whether the issuance of general warrant for contempt by the State Legislature could be examined by the Courts in writ proceedings - Held, art. 194(3) did not confer on Indian State Legislatures the right to commit for contempt by a general warrant which could not be examined for its validity by Courts in habeas corpus proceedings - Indian State Legislatures were not at any time in their history, either under the Constitution Act, 1935, or under the Indian Independence Act, 1947, intended to be courts of record - Legal fiction in art. 194(3) could not transfer the history of England to India and confer on Indian State Legislatures the status of superior courts of record - Thus, the very basis on which the English Courts agreed to treat general warrant issued by the House of Commons on the footing that it was a warrant issued by a superior court of record, was absent in the case of State Legislatures in India, and so, it would be unreasonable to contend that the relevant power to claim a conclusive character for the general warrant which the House of Common, by agreement, was deemed to possess, became vested in the Indian Legislatures - Further, even if the power to commit by non-examinable general warrant were treated as forming an integral part of the privileges of the House of Commons it would not follow that the Indian State Legislatures could exercise that power by virtue of art.194(3) - Very existence of the powers of the Courts u/arts. 226 and 32 necessarily implies a right in the citizen to approach the HC or the SC for the protection of his fundamental rights - If a citizen moved to the SC and complained that his fundamental right u/art. 21 or any other applicable right had been contravened, it would be the duty of the SC to examine the merits of the said contention - In such circumstances, State Legislature cannot say that the warrant issued against the citizen was a general warrant and a general warrant must stop all further judicial enquiry and scrutiny - Order accordingly.

(E) Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Advocates & Judges - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 32, 194, 211, 208, 226 - Contempt of State Legislature - Power to summon advocates and judges - Whether the State Legislature has the power and privilege to summon an advocate or a Judge to make an explanation before it on the basis of a charge of contempt - Held, rules for regulating the procedure of the House were subject to the provision of the Constitution u/art. 208(1) - State Legislature was incompetent because of art. 211 to discuss the conduct of a Judge in the discharge of his duties, cannot have the power to summon a Judge in custody for alleged contempt committed in discharge of his duties - If the claim of the House were upheld it would mean that the House could issue a general warrant against a Judge and no judicial scrutiny could be held in respect of the validity of such a warrant and that would put the basic concept of judicial independence into grave jeopardy - S. 30 of the Advocates Act 1961, confers on all Advocates, the statutory right to practice in all courts, including the SC, before any Tribunal or person legally authorized to take evidence, and before any other authority or person before whom such Advocate is by or under any law for the time being in force entitled to practice - S.14 of the Bar Councils Act also recognizes similar right for Advocates - Just as the rights of the Judicature to deal with matters before them u/arts. 226 or 32 cannot be subjected to the powers and privileges of the House u/art. 194(3), so the rights of the citizen to move the Judicature and the right of the Advocates to assist that process must remain uncontrolled by art. 194(3) - Therefore, the rights to commit by a conclusive general warrant, which the State Assembly claimed to be an integral part of its powers or privileges, was inconsistent with the material provisions of the Constitution and could not be deemed to have been included under the latter part of art. 194(3) - Order accordingly.

(F) Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 226 - Contempt of State Legislature - Grant of bail by HC - Legality - Whether HC was competent to entertain the petition of contemnor of State Legislature and to grant bail for him - Held, HC was competent to entertain the petition of contemnor and to grant him bail pending, disposal of his petition - No contempt of the State Legislature was committed by contemnor or his Advocate in moving the application u/art. 226, or by the HC in entertaining the said petition and granting bail - State Legislature was not competent to direct the production of the two HC Judges and the Advocate before it in custody or to call for their explanation for their conduct - Full Bench of the HC, however, was competent to entertain and deal with the petitions of the Judges and the Advocate and to pass interim orders restraining the State Legislature to execute the resolution - A Judge of a HC who entertains or deals with a petition challenging any order or decision of State Legislature imposing any penalty on the petitioner (who is not a member of the Legislature) or issuing any process against the petitioner for its contempt (the alleged contempt having been committed outside the four-walls of the House), or for the infringement of its privileges and immunities, or who passes any order on such petition, does not commit any contempt of the said Legislature, and the said Legislature is not competent to take proceedings against such a Judge in the exercise and enforcement of its powers, privileges and immunities - Order accordingly.

A.K. Sarkar, J. (contra)

(G) Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Advocates & Judges - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 13, 21, 32, 194, 226 - Contempt of State Legislature - Interference by HC - Scope - Whether the Assembly has the privilege of committing a person to prison for contempt by a general warrant, that is, without stating the facts which constituted the contempt, and if it does so, have the courts of law the power to examine the legality of such a committal - Held, Courts only has to interpret the Constitution and therefore, in the present case also, Court's duty is to interpret art. 194(3) of the Constitution - No scope for the Courts to eneter into the controversy with State Legislature as to what its privileges are - Language of art. 194(3) can only have one meaning and which is that it was intended to confer on the State Legislatures the powers, privileges and immunities which the House of Commons in England had - No occasion in the present case warranting for denying the plain meaning of the language in art. 194(3) by professing allegiance to a supposed theory of division of powers between Legislature and Judiciary - Power to commit by a general warrant with the consequent deprivation of the jurisdiction of the courts was, one of the privileges of the House of Commons and such privilege was possessed by the State Legislature in question also by virtue of art. 194(3) of the Constitution - No authority showed that the House of Commons possessed the powers to commit by a general warrant because it was a superior court of record - Courts only treated the House as entitled to the same respect as a superior court - It cannot be said that privilege in question can be exercised by the Indian State Legislatures subject to the fundamental rights of a citizen guaranteed by the Constitution - Majority in Sharma's case said without discussion that the law u/art. 194(3) would be subject to all fundamental rights, however, law made u/art. 194(3) is not to be read as is subject to fundamental rights, the position is that if the law is in conflict with any fundamental right, it is as good as not made at all - Art. 13 makes a law bad if it conflicts with fundamental rights, however, it cannot be said that since art. 13 might make laws made u/art. 194(3) would be void, the privileges conferred by the second part of art. 194(3) must also be void - Art. 13 has no application to the provisions of the Constitution itself and it governs only the laws made by a State Legislature - When there is a conflict between a privilege conferred on a House by the second part of art. 194(3) and a fundamental right that conflict has to be resolved as in Sharma's case by harmonizing the two provisions - However, harmonious construction means that both the provisions should be given maximum effect without one of them wiping out the other - In the instant case the conflict was between the privilege of the House to commit a person for contempt without that committal being liable to be examined by a court of law, and the personal liberty of a citizen guaranteed by art. 21 and the right to move the courts in enforcement of that right u/arts. 32or 226 - If the right to move the courts in enforcement of the fundamental right is given precedence, the privilege which provides that if a House commits a person by a general warrant that committal would not be reviewed by courts of law, would lose all its effect and it would be as if the privilege had not been granted to a House by the second part of art. 194(3) - Therefore, when a House commits a person for contempt by a general warrant that person would have no right to approach the courts nor can the courts sit in judgment over such order of committal - Order accordingly.

(H) Advocates & Judges - Constitution - Contempt of State Legislature - Summoning Judges and Advocate - Legality - Whether, on the facts and circumstances of the case, it was competent for the State Legislature to direct the production of the two HC Judges and Advocate, before it in custody or to call for their explanation for its contempt - Held, State Legislature would not be competent to find the HC Judges and Advocate to be guilty of contempt without giving them a hearing - Since HC Judges and Advocate were not guilty of contempt, State Legislature was not competent to direct their production in custody - Assembly directed the production of Judges not for the purpose of hearing them on the question of contempt but on the basis that they had committed a contempt, however, Assembly modified its resolution to have the Judges, Advocate and contemnor brought under custody and asked only for explanation from the Judges and Advocate for their conduct - Hence, question as to bringing judges and advocate in custody before the House does not arise on the facts of the case - Order accordingly.
