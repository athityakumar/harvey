 Corporation of Calcutta and Another v Liberty Cinema

Supreme Court of India

14 December 1964

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sarkar, K. Subba Rao, Raghubar Dayal, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, J.R. Madholkar

Where Reported

1964 Indlaw SC 364; AIR 1965 SC 1107; [1965] 2 S.C.R. 477

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Indirect Tax; Media & Entertainment

Keywords: Excise Regulation

Summary: A. K. Sarkar, J.

(A) Indirect Tax - Media & Entertainment - Constitution -Constitution of India, 1950, art.19(1) - Calcutta Municipal Act, 1951, ss. 443, 548(2) - Levy of licence fee - Quashing of resolution - Change in basis of assessment - Validity - Respondent was firm owning cinema house and carrying on business of public cinema shows - Respondent had u/ss. 443, 548(2) of the Act obtained licence for its cinema house and was paying licence fee as calculated - By resolution passed, Corporation changed basis of assessment of licence fee - Respondent then moved to HC for quashing of resolution - HC allowed same and was confirmed by appellate Bench - Hence instant Appeals - Whether levy of license fee amounted to expropriation and was invalid as violating art. 19(1)cls. (f) and (g) of the Constitution - Held, fee at rate of Rs. 5 per show in house with seating capacity of 551 could not in any sense be said to be unreasonably high - With that seating capacity respondent would at reasonable estimate be collecting about Rs. 1,000 per show and paying sum of Rs. 5 per show - There was no doubt increase in rate of fee from Rs. 400 to Rs. 6,000 per year was large - However, at same time circumstances obtaining in country had undergone immense change between 1948 when fee was earlier fixed and 1958 - Challenge to levy on ground that it amounted to expropriation was wholly unfounded and was rightly rejected in HC - It was said that Art. 19(1) cls. (f) and (g) of the Constitution were violated in any case as s. 548 of the Act gave arbitrary power of taxation - Therefore, there was no arbitrary power of taxation was conferred by s. 548 of Act - Appeals dismissed.

(B) Indirect Tax - Media & Entertainment - Nature of fee - Return for services - Whether levy authorised by ss. 443, 548 of the Act was fee in return for services to be rendered and not tax - Held, there was no reason for saying that levy was fee in return for services - Nature of services to be rendered in return for levy so as to make it fee was considered by Court in several cases and in all of them it was said that services could confer some benefit on person paying fee levy u/s. 548 of the Act was not fee as the Act did not provide for any services of special kind being rendered resulting in benefits to person on whom it was imposed - Work of inspection done by Corporation which was only to see that terms of licence were observed by licensee was not service to him - There was no question arose of correlating amount levy to costs of any service and levy was tax - Conferment of power did not require any guidance for its exercise to make it valid and it was pointed out that delegation of power necessarily meant delegation of power of delegator - On such delegation delegated power could only be exercised by delegates for use of delegator - That was not case of power conferred and in such case power of taxation conferred was for purpose of corporation itself and amount collected by taxation belonged to corporation - Appeals dismissed.

Ayyangar, J.

(C) Indirect Tax - Media & Entertainment - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, art.19(1) - Calcutta Municipal Act, 1951, ss. 443, 548(2) - Payment of licence fee - Change in basis of assessment - Validity - Delegation of power - Whether levy authorised by ss. 443, 548 of the Act was fee in return for services to be rendered and not tax and whether rule as to excessive delegation of legislative power was inapplicable in cases where devolution or conferment of power was on municipal corporation - Held, construction in list of entries was confirmed by fact that in Concurrent List which contained only entries in relation to legislative power as distinguished from entries conferring taxing power, last entry enabled fees to be levied as ancillary to legislative power conferred by other entries in that list - However, there was no discretion could be left to legislature to determine within permissible limits precise rate that would secure purposes which it sought to achieve but rather that no guidance was at all afforded and blank cheque given to subordinate authority - Where maxima was fixed and limit of discretion was thus controlled legislature had exercised its legislative power on that topic viz., particular tax - Whereas, where it merely authorised subordinate law making authority to levy tax without indicating essential legislative features of such tax it was not really legislation on taxation entry but was merely authorising subordinate legislature to enact law on that topic - Therefore, if said provisions did not afford any guidance to Municipal Corporation to fix rate of levy, it was not suggested that there were any others in the Act which performed that function - Ss. 443, 548(2) of the Act did not afford any help for that purpose - Therefore, it viewed as tax, delegation was unconstitutional as essential legislative functions were parted with to subordinate law making body and provision was unconstitutional - Appeals dismissed.
