State of Punjab v Modern Cultivators, Ladwa

Supreme Court of India

08 May 1964

Case Analysis

Bench

A.K. Sarkar, Mohammad Hidayatullah, J.R. Madholkar

Where Reported

1964 Indlaw SC 389; AIR 1965 SC 17; 1965 (1) ALT(SC) 36; 1964 (2) MLJ(SC) 185; 1965 (67) PLR 117; 1964 (2) SCJ 796; [1964] 8 S.C.R. 273

Case Digest

Subject: Water

Keywords: Northern India Canal and Drainage Act, 1873

Summary: Water - Northern India Canal and Drainage Act, 1873, ss. 6 and 15 - Indian Limitation Act, art. 2 Sch. I - (A) Whether art. 2 of Sch. I of Limitation Act is applicable in cases of omission? - Held, art. 2 cannot apply to omissions in following statutory duties because it cannot be suggested that they are 'in pursuance of any enactment' - Cases of malfeasance, misfeasance or non-feasance may or may not have statutory protection Act or omission which can claim statutory protection or is alleged to be in pursuance of a statutory command may attract art. 2 but act or omission must be one which can be said to be in pursuance of an enactment - (B) Whether Govt. can be held responsible for damage caused to plaintiff and, if so, what should be compensation? - Held, in presence of sufficient evidence to establish negligence and absence of reasonable explanation, Govt. becomes liable for payment of compensation for damage caused - Action on the part of the State is manifestly unreasonable and the legitimate inference that could be drawn from it is that if the documents had been produced they would have gone against the State and would establish its negligence - Though the plaintiffs have been unable to adduce positive evidence of negligence it could legitimately be presumed that the State was negligent inasmuch as it had deliberately suppressed evidence in its possession which could have established negligence - Appeal No. 416/1947 dismissed and Appeal No. 417/1947 allowed.
