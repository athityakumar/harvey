 R. Abdul Quader and Company v Sales Tax Officer, Hyderabad

Supreme Court of India

21 February 1964

Case Analysis

Bench

Kailas Nath Wanchoo, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, K.C. Das Gupta, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1964 Indlaw SC 328; AIR 1964 SC 922; 1964 (2) MLJ(SC) 54; 1964 (2) SCJ 229; [1964] 6 S.C.R. 867; 1964 (15) STC 403

Case Digest

Subject: Indirect Tax; Sales Tax

Keywords: Collected, Hyderabad General Sales Tax Act, 1950

Summary: Sales Tax - Indirect Tax - Hyderabad General Sales Tax Act, 1950, s. 11(2) - A/y 1953-1954 - Tax collected - Denied to pay - Challenged - Assessee/appellant acted as agent in State of X in regard to sale of betel leaves - Under the Act betel leaves were taxable at purchase point from 1-5-1953, by virtue of notification in that behalf - For A/y 1953-1954, assessee collected sales tax from purchasers in connection with sales made but did not paid amount realized to Government and kept it in suspense account of its principals (Purchasers) - When accounts were scrutinized by respondent/Sales Tax Department, that was discovered and assessee was called upon to pay amounts realized to Govt - Assessee objected to pay on ground that it was seller and relevant notification for relevant period imposed tax at purchase point, i.e. on purchaser which was over-ruled and assessee was directed to pay amount to Govt - Aggrieved assessee filed petition before HC, which was dismissed - Hence, instant appeal - Whether appeal was maintainable - Held, respondent relied on decision of HC in Indian Aluminium Co. v. State of Madras 1962 Indlaw MAD 29188, which was with respect to s. 8-B of the Madras General Sales Tax Act of 1939, as amended by Madras Act 1 of 1957 - Though words in s. 8-B(2) were not exactly same as words in s. 11(2), with which SC, was concerned there, provision in substance was to same effect as s. 11(2) - In view of what SC have said above, that decision might be held to be incorrect - Lastly, SC, come to contention of respondent that s. 11(2) of the Act was within legislative competence of State legislature in view of Entry 26 of List 11, such entry deals with 'trade and commerce within State subject to provisions of entry 33 of List III' - It was well settled that taxing entries in legislative Lists I and II of Seventh Schedule were entirely separate from other entries - Entry 26 of List 11 deals with trade and commerce and had nothing to do with taxing or recovering amounts realized wrongly as tax - It was said that s. 11(2) regulates trade and commerce and State legislature therefore was competent under Entry 26 of List II to enact it - SC, had not been able to understand what such provision had to do with regulation of trade and commerce, it could only be justified as provision ancillary to taxing statute - If it could not be so justified as SC, hold that it could not SC, were unable to uphold it as regulating trade and commerce under Entry 26 of List II, there was in SC, opinion no element of regulation of trade and commerce in provision like s. 11(2) - SC, therefore, of opinion that State legislature was incompetent to enact provision like s. 11(2) - SC, might also add that provision contained in s. 20(c), being consequential to s. 11(2) would fall along with it - In consequence it was not opened to respondent to ask assessee to made over what he had collected from purchasers ,wrongly as sales tax - It was not disputed, as appears from final assessment order of respondent, that assessee was not liable to pay amount as sales tax for relevant period - SC, allowed appeal and quashed assessment order which was based on s. 11(2) of the Act - Appeal allowed.
