Commissioner of Income Tax, Gujarat v Keshavlal Lallubhai Patel

Supreme Court of India

09 November 1964

Case Analysis

Bench

S.M. Sikri, K. Subba Rao, J.C. Shah, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar

Where Reported

1964 Indlaw SC 265; AIR 1965 SC 866; [1965] 55 ITR 637; [1965] 2 S.C.R. 100

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Indian Income Tax Act, 1922 , ss.16(3) (a) (Iii), 16(3) (a) (IV) - Hindu undivided family - Self acquired properties - Tax evasion - Transfer - Respondent A assessee was assessed till assessment year 1952-53 as an individual - Respondent before Deputy Nazir, District Court, Ahmadabad, throwing all his self acquired properties into common hotchpotch of Hindu undivided family, consisting of himself and his two sons - Assessee had a wife and two sons, one major and other a minor and no entries in books were passed - Oral partition was affected between several member of Hindu undivided family and consistent with partition entries in books were made - A joint declaration was made by assessee, his wife and major son before District Court - Properties were transferred in accordance with arrangement to names of several members of family - For year 1952-53 assessee claimed assessment should be made taking into consideration conversion of self-acquired into joint family property and subsequent partition - Appellate Tribunal confirmed orders of Income Tax Officer and Assistant Appellate Commissioner disallowing claim of assessee - Appellate Tribunal at instance of assessee referred question to HC - HC answered question in favour of assesse and granted a certificate under u/ s. 66A(2) - Whether there was any transfer of assets - Held, in computing total income of any individual for purpose of assessment there shall be included so much of income of a wife or minor child of such individual as arises directly or indirectly from assets transferred directly or indirectly to wife by husband otherwise than for adequate consideration or in connection with an agreement to live apart or from assets transferred directly or indirectly to minor child not being a married daughter by such individual otherwise than for adequate consideration - Partition was really a process in and by which a joint enjoyment was transformed into an enjoyment in severalty - Each one of sharers had an antecedent title and therefore no conveyance was involved in process as a conferment of a new title was not necessary - Partition between divided members of family does not amount either to a transfer of assets from body of tenants-in-common to each of such tenants-in-common - Appeal dismissed.
