 State of Gujarat v Vora Fiddali Badruddin Mithibarwala

Supreme Court of India

30 January 1964

Case Analysis

Bench

N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, K. Subba Rao, Mohammad Hidayatullah, Raghubar Dayal, Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, J.C. Shah, J.R. Madholkar

Where Reported

1964 Indlaw SC 357; AIR 1964 SC 1043; [1964] 6 S.C.R. 461

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Indian Independence Act, 1947, Titles, Government of India Act 1935, Foreign Jurisdiction Act, 1947

Summary: N. Rajagopala Ayyangar, J:

(A)Constitution - Land & Property - Constitution of India,1950, arts.19(1)(g), 31(1) - Succession of state - Competency of Govt. - Repudiation of title - Suits were filed for injunction and ancillary reliefs to restrain appellant and its officers from interfering with the plaintiff's alleged rights to cut and carry away timber from jungle, raising their rights under contract for period of disputed years on payment of consideration - Court took view that rights of plaintiffs, such as they were, could not be enforced by courts and dismissed suit - On appeals, First Appellate Court confirmed judgments of Trial Court - On second appeal, HC allowed same and restrained appellant by injunction from interfering with plaintiffs' enjoyment of rights in forests - Hence, instant Appeal - Whether Govt. which emerged as result of Constitution was competent to avoid or repudiate titles obtained by petitioners under previous ruler by 'Act of State.'

Held, Constitution emerged as result of conjoint action of subjects of former Indian rulers and people of former British India. When as result of this joint effort Constitution was brought into existence there was no question of conquest so as to attract those doctrines of Public International Law relating to effects of rights arising out of changes in sovereignty brought about by conquest, cession, treaty etc. Even if previous rulers had vested in them autocratic powers to revoke grants made by them in favour of their subjects, Govt. of Union and States which were functioning under Constitution which contained fundamental rights guaranteeing protection of property rights against arbitrary executive action could not claim to exercise those arbitrary powers which they might have inherited from the previous rulers. Court have no evidence as regards the creation of the several tenures referred to in the Tharav to base any conclusion as flowing from the original grant. No doubt, there is on record the translation of the grant of the village of Gothimada, but from this it does not follow that every one of the grants comprised in the' five tenures specified was of this pattern, Court consider that the 'Tharav' is more consistent with its being a law effecting an alteration in the tenures of the five classes of Jagirdars by expanding the range of the beneficial enjoyment to the forests lying within the boundaries of the villages which had already been granted to them. The 'Tharav' would not be an administrative order in any sense but would partake of the character of legislation by which an alteration was effected in the scope and content of the tenures referred to. This aspect is reinforced by the reference to the complaints of the tenure-holders whose grievance apparently was that though villages had been granted to them for their enjoyment under the several tenures, they were not permitted any rights in the forests within their villages. It was not thus a case of an individual grant but the yielding by the ruler to the claims of these large group of Jagirdars who requested that their rights should be extended. It is obvious that there must have been some rules which have the force of law as regards the administration of these forests and the enjoyment by the Jagirdars was made subject to the observance of these laws. 'Tharav' satisfies the requirement of a 'law' within art. 366(10) of Constitution, and in consequence, the executive orders of the Govt. of Bombay by which the forest rights of the plaintiffs were sought to be denied were illegal and void. Plaintiffs were entitled to succeed, though for different reasons.. Appeal disposed of.

(B)Constitution - Constitution of India,1949, art. 366(10) - Determination of agreement - Constitute as law - Whether Tharav constitutes law u/art.366(10) of the constitution and continued by art.372 of the Constitution or it is merely executive grant or administrative order.

Held, Tharav is not grant to any individual, treating him as individual or as one of number of individuals or to group them merely as separate individuals, but to holders of five specified tenures in State villages. No evidence as regards creation of several tenures referred to in Tharav to base any conclusion as flowing from original grant. There is on record translation of grant of village, but from this it does not follow that everyone of grants comprised in five tenures specified was of this pattern. However, 'Tharav' is more consistent with its being law effecting alteration in tenures of five classes of Jagirdars by expanding range of the beneficial enjoyment to forests lying within boundaries of villages which had already been granted to them. Further, there must be some rules which have force of law as regards administration of forests and enjoyment by Jagirdars was made subject to observance of these laws. 'Tharav' satisfies requirement of "law" u/art.366(10) of the constitution and executive orders of Govt. by which forest rights of plaintiffs were sought to be denied were illegal and void. Thus, 'Tharav' would not be administrative order but would partake of character of legislation by which alteration was effected in scope and content of tenures referred. Appeal disposed of.

Subba Rao J:

(C)Constitution - Land & Property - Constitution of India,1949, art.31(1) - Succession of states - Title of property - Recognized by constitution - Whether citizen of ceding State does lose his title to immovable property and on making of Constitution, his title thereto became indefeasible even against the absorbing State.

Held, doctrine of acquired rights, at any rate in regard to immovable property, has become crystallized in International Law. Under said law title of citizen of ceding State is preserved and not lost by cession. Change of sovereignty does not affect his title. Municipal law of different countries varies in matter of its enforceability against State. Before Constitution came into force State did not repudiate title. When Constitution of India came into force respondent and persons similarly situated who had title to immovable property in State had title to property and were in actual possession. They had title to property except against State and they had, at any rate, possessory title therein. However, art.31(1) of the Constitution declares that no person shall be deprived of his property save by authority of law. Constitution recognized title of citizens of erstwhile State of Sant, and issued injunction against sovereign created by it not to interfere with that right except in accordance with law. Further, recognition by supreme law of land must be in higher position than that of executive authority of conquering State. Thus, title to immovable property of respondent was recognized by Constitution and citizen of ceding State does lose his title to immovable property. Appeal disposed of.

(D)Constitution - Impugned letter - Merger agreement - Recognition of property - Whether letter formed part of merger agreement.

Held, recognition of title of respondent by Constitution, letter written by GOI, clearly recognized title of persons situated in position of respondent to their properties. In concluding portion of letter it is stated that contents of letter will be regarded as part of merger agreement. But merger had already taken place and letter was written after merger. It does not appear from that letter that Maharana of Sant State, who-ceased to be Ruler except in name for certain privileges, was party to it. However, letter can at best be treated as one of acts of the Govt. of India implementing terms of merger agreement. It cannot be said to be part of merger agreement. Further, if it was not, by calling it so it did not become one. At time letter was sent all citizens of erstwhile Sant State had become citizens of India. Letter contains clear statement that enjoyment of ownership of jagirs, grant etc. were guaranteed and that any order passed or action taken by Ruler before said date would not be questioned. This is clear recognition of property rights of respondent and similar others. Thus, letter cannot formed part of merger agreement. Appeal disposed of.

Hidayatullah J:

(E)Constitution - Existence of state succession - Act of State - Questioned in Municipal Court - Whether there took place in 1950 State succession and act of State can be questioned in Municipal Court.

Held, though people of India gave themselves Constitution, there was no State succession in so far as people of Sant State were concerned. For them State succession was over sometime before. There was no second succession in 1950. Whatever had happened had already happened in 1948 when Sant State merged with Dominion of India. Act of State which began in 1948 could continue uninterrupted even beyond 1950 and it did not lapse or get replaced by another Act of State. Constitution no doubt guaranteed rights of citizens after 1950 but these rights granted by Ruler were fort recognised even before 1950 and Constitution gave its support to those rights which were extant on dt.26-1-1950. However, right claimed is more of nature of gift by ruler at expense of State. There is no treaty involved and whatever guarantee there is, Constitution precludes municipal courts from considering. Thus, act of State can be questioned in Municipal Court has been adopted and it has been considered that it is matter for political departments of State. Appeal disposed of.

SHAH J:

(F)Constitution - Delegation of Dominion - Renunciation of right - Whether Govt. of Bombay as delegate of Dominion has renounced its right not to regard itself is bound by order made by Ruler of Sant State.

Held, pursuant to agreement State of Sant merged with Dominion of India. Sovereignty of Ruler was thereby, extinguished and subjects of Sant State became citizens of Dominion of India. However, sovereign is fountain head of all rights, all laws and all justice within State and only those rights which are recognised by sovereign are enforceable in his Courts. Final resolution cancelling agreement was passed and was recited in resolution that Tharav issued by Ruler in 1948 had been considered by Govt. to be mala fide and same had already been repudiated and it was not binding on Govt. of Bombay both by law and under agreement of integration, in spite of assurance contained in collateral letter. Since Tharav has not been recognised by Govt. but has been specifically repudiated, everything done in pursuance thereof including contracts entered into after passing of Tharav is not valid and binding on Govt. Thus, plea that Govt. of Bombay as delegate of Dominion had renounced its right not to regard itself as bound by order made by Ruler of Sant State cannot be sustained. Appeal disposed of.

(G)Constitution - Administration of Indian States Order,1948 - Determination of agreement - Constitution of law - Whether order can be regarded as 'law' u/cl.4 of the Orders.

Held, act or order of sovereign with absolute authority may be enforced and subjects have no opportunity of getting redress against infringement of their rights in Municipal Courts of State will not be decisive of true character of functions of sovereign in exercise of which act was done or order was made. If order is made during regime of sovereign who exercises absolute powers, and it is enforced or executed leaving nothing more to be done there under to effectuate it, any discussion of its true character would be idle exercise. Where however in set-up in which rule of law prevails, to support action taken pursuant to order you have to reach source of authority in power of previous autocratic sovereign, true nature of function exercised may become important, when laws of former State are by express enactment continued by new sovereign. Further, order of Ruler of Sant was not in form of legislative enactment.Thus, order merely granting forest rights not in pursuance of any legislative authority, but in exercise of power of sovereign in whom rights were vested, to jagirdars to whom villages were granted without forest rights, can be regarded as 'law' under cl. 4 of the Order. Appeal disposed of.

(H) Constitution - Constituion of India,1949, art.31(1) - Protection of provision - Entitlement - Whether respondents are entitled to protection u/art.31(1) of the Constitution.

Held, order which deprives them of right to cut forest trees which they claimed from jagirdar who derived them under grant from Ruler of Sant is executive order. Impugned orders cancelling grants in favour of respondents and taking of properties arose out of and during act of State and they could not be questioned before Municipal Tribunals, for orders of cancellation were passed before changeover of de jure sovereignty. However, by art.31(1) of the Constitution right to claim protection against deprivation of property otherwise than by authority of law is provided. It does not purport to invest person with right to property which has not been recognized by Dominion of India or Union. Further, if respondents under the Act after merger not acquired any right to forests by virtue of any recognition of Tharav, promulgation of Constitution did not invest them with any additional rights which would convert either their claims to forest rights into property or to enable them to enforce in Indian Courts such claims not recognized by State as fundamental right to property. Thus, respondents are not entitled to protection u/art.31(1) of the Constitution. Appeal disposed of.

Mudholkar, J:

(I)Constitution - Determination of agreement - Consideration as law - Whether Tharav relied upon by respondents can be considered as law.

Held, it was not open to officers of forest department to grant recognition to Jagirdars' rights for simple reason that right of granting recognition could be exercised only by Govt. acting through its appropriate agency. Permission which was accorded to respondents was only tentative and expressly subject to final decision of Govt. on question of their right under leases granted by Jagirdars. Rights derived by inhabitants of conquered and ceded territory from its former rulers cannot be enforced by them against new sovereign in courts of that sovereign unless they have been recognized by sovereign. However, if inhabitants of ceded territory have ceased to have right against new sovereign there is nothing for sovereign to waive. If letter of Secretary to States Department is regarded as part of agreement of merger municipal courts are precluded by art.363 of the Constitution from enforcing any rights arising thereunder. Further, new sovereign is expected to examine all grants and find out any of grants are vitiated by mala fides or were against his legitimate interests so that he can give recognition to those grants only which were not vitiated by mala fides or which were not against his interests. Thus, Tharao relied upon by respondents is not law. Appeal disposed of.
