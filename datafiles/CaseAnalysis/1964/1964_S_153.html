Masalti v State of Uttar Pradesh

Supreme Court of India

04 May 1964

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, K.C. Das Gupta, Raghubar Dayal

Where Reported

1964 Indlaw SC 393; AIR 1965 SC 202; 1965 CRLJ 226; [1964] 8 S.C.R. 133

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 149, 201, 302, 395, 396 and 511 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 374 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 136 - Unlawful assembly - Murder - Conviction - Death sentences - Challenged - Forty persons were charged u/ss. 302 r/w s. 149 and u/ss. 307/149, 201/149 and 511, 395, 396, 149 and 449 of IPC - Trial Court convicted 35 out of 40 accused persons and sentenced 10 accused persons to death and 25 others to imprisonment for life - On appeals, HC held that 7 out of the 35 appellants were not proved to have committed any of the offences, and so, they were ordered to be acquitted - In regard to the remaining 28 appellants, the HC confirmed the orders of conviction and sentence imposed on them by the Trial Court - Present appeals - (A) HC approach in a death reference - It was contended that HC failed in discharging its duty properly - Held, HC has to consider entire case and complete evidence for itself before deciding whether sentence of death should be confirmed or not - All arguments urged by the appellants and all material infirmities pressed before the HC on their behalf must be scrupulously examined and considered before a final decision is reached - In the present case, arguments urged on behalf of the appellants, have been carefully examined, the evidence given by the respective witnesses were accurately summarised and the infirmities in the said evidence closely scrutinised - After taking into account all the points which were urged before the HC, the HC adopted what it thought to be a safe test before acting on direct evidence - (B) Whether plea that was not urged before HC and lower court can be urged before SC u/art. 136? - Held, no hard and fast rule can be made prohibiting new pleas being raised before SC - It may be conceded that if a point of fact which plainly arises on the record, or a point of law which is relevant and material and can be argued without any further evidence being taken, was urged before the Trial Court and after it was rejected by it was not repeated before the HC, it may, in proper case, be permissible to the appellants to ask SC to consider that point in an appeal u/art. 136 of the Constitution - (C) Criminal Court's approach while dealing with evidence of partisan or interested witnesses - Held, a criminal court has to be very careful in weighing evidence who are partisan or interested - Evidence given by witnesses should not be discarded only on ground that it is evidence of partisan or interested witnesses - Judicial approach has to be cautious in dealing with such evidence but plea that such evidence should be rejected because it is partisan cannot be accepted as correct - (D) Whether members of 'unlawful assembly' can be assigned roles considering large number of members of such assembly? - Held, members of an unlawful assembly proceed to commit an offence in pursuance of common object of unlawful assembly - Task of court to appreciate evidence in respect of each member of unlawful assembly - Respective roles assigned on each offender - (E) Sentence of death imposed on 10 persons - HC held that the 10 persons who carried fire-arms should be ordered to be hanged, whereas others who were convicted u/s. 302/149, should be sentenced to imprisonment for life - Whether in confirming the sentences of death imposed by the Trial Court on 10 accused persons, the HC adopted a mechanical rule? - It was contended if a person was found guilty of murder u/s. 302/149 and it was not shown that he himself committed the murder in question, he was not liable to be sentenced to death - Held, whether or not sentences of death should be imposed on persons who are found to be guilt, not because they themselves committed the murder, but because they were members of an unlawful assembly and the offence of murder was committed by one or more of the members of such an assembly in pursuance of the common object of that assembly, is a matter which had to be decided on the facts and circumstances of each case - In the present case, 10 of these persons were armed with fire-arms and the others with several other deadly weapons, and evidence shows that five murders by shooting were committed by the members of this unlawful assembly - Conduct of the members of the unlawful assembly both before and after the commission of the offence has been considered by the courts below and it was held that in order to suppress such criminal conduct on the part of villagers it was necessary to impose the sentence of death on 10 members of the unlawful assembly who were armed with fire-arms - It cannot be said that discretion in the matter was improperly exercised either by the Trial Court or by the HC - Appeals dismissed.
