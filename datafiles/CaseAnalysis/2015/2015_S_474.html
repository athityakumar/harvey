Priyanka Srivastava and another v State of Uttar Pradesh and others

Supreme Court of India

19 March 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Prafulla C. Pant

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 226; (2015) 6 SCC 287; AIR 2015 SC 1758; 2015 CRLJ 2396; 2015 (3) CTC 103; 2015 (2) DRTC 1; 2015 (2) JCC 974; JT 2015 (5) SC 203; 2015 (2) KLT 451; 2015 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1622; 2015 (2) RCR(Criminal) 1034; 2015 (3) RLW 2404; 2015(4) SCALE 120; [2015] 4 S.C.R. 108

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 156(3) - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Recovery of Debts Due to Banks and Financial Institutions Act, 1993 - Registration of FIR - Respondent No.3 availed housing loan from financial institution - As there was default in consecutive payment of the installments, the loan account was treated as a Non- Performing Asset - Respondent No.3 preferred petition which was dismissed by HC - Respondent no.3 filed application u/s. 156(3) CrPC against appellants alleging criminal conspiracy and forging of documents referring to three post-dated cheques - HC while exercising the power u/s. 156(3) Cr.P.C. narrated the allegations and, thereafter, without any application of mind, passed an order to register an FIR for the offences mentioned in the application - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, 156(3) of Cr.P.C.warrants application of judicial mind. A court of law is involved. A litigant at his own whims cannot invoke the authority of the Magistrate. A stage has come in this country where s. 156(3) Cr.P.C. applications are to be supported by an affidavit duly sworn by the applicant who seeks the invocation of the jurisdiction of the Magistrate. That apart, in an appropriate case, Magistrate would be well advised to verify the truth and also can verify the veracity of the allegations. Enclosing affidavit can make the applicant more responsible. HC should have kept himself alive to the aforesaid provision before venturing into directing registration of the FIR u/s. 156(3) Cr.P.C. It is because the Parliament in its wisdom has made such a provision to protect the secured creditors or any of its officers. Order passed by HC was set aside and registration of FIR was quashed. Appeal allowed.
