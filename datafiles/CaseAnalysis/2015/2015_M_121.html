Zarif Ahmad (Dead) Through Lrs. and another v Mohd. Farooq

Supreme Court of India

27 January 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Prafulla C. Pant, Dipak Misra

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 415; AIR 2015 SC 1236; 2015 (3) ALD(SC) 13; 2015 (110) ALR 239; 2015 (2) AWC 2052; 2015 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1169; 2015 (1) RCR(Civil) 794; 2015 (128) RD 144; 2015(1) SCALE 690; [2015] 1 S.C.R. 779

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure

Summary: Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, O.7 r.3 - Suit for permanent injunction - Remand - Necessity - Plaintiff/respondents filed suit against defendants/appellants to restrain them from interfering with peaceful possession of plaintiff - Suit property described by plaintiff in plaint not only by boundaries but by municipal number, and by giving its description in plaint map - Plaintiff alleged that he was paying house tax for the suit property - Trial Court decreed suit - Defendants filed appeal - First Appellate Court reversed Trial Court order - However, on second appeal of plaintiff, HC set aside First Appellate Court order and restored Trial Court order - Hence, instant appeal by defendant - Defendant contended that no length and width of the land in question is mentioned in the plaint.

Held, since in the present case, the suit property has been described by the plaintiff in the plaint not only by the boundaries but also by the municipal number, and by giving its description in the plaint map, it cannot be said that the suit property was not identifiable in the present case. The HC has rightly held that the First Appellate Court has erred in law in dismissing the suit by holding that the land is not identifiable. It appears that the First Appellate Court has wrongly framed the additional issue as to whether the property in dispute is identifiable or not particularly when there was no such plea in the written statement. There was no need on the part of the First Appellate Court to remit the matter to the Trial Court as contended by the defendants before HC to allow the parties to adduce evidence on the additional issue, as neither issue on identifiability of land arises from the pleadings nor the evidence was lacking on record. It is not a healthy practice to remand a case to Trial Court unless it is necessary to do so as it makes the parties to wait for the final decision of a case for the period which is avoidable. Only in rare situations, a case should be remanded e.g. when the trial court has disposed of a suit on a preliminary issue without recording evidence and giving its decision on the rest of the issues, but it is not so in the present case. Therefore, the HC rightly rejected the contention of the defendants. Thus, no illegality is found with the impugned order whereby the HC has allowed second appeal, and restored the decree passed by the Trial Court. Appeal dismissed.
