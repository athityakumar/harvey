Union of India v Reliance Industries Limited and others

Supreme Court of India

22 September 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, A.K. Sikri

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 878; (2015) 10 SCC 213; 2015 (6) AWC 6111; 2015 (4) CLT(SC) 8; 2016 (1) CompLJ 377; 2015 (7) MLJ 360; 2016 (2) RCR(Civil) 19; 2015(10) SCALE 149; [2015] 10 S.C.R. 85

Case Digest

Subject: Arbitration & ADR

Keywords: Arbitration Agreement, Foreign Awards (Recognition And Enforcement) Act, 1961, Arbitration Proceedings, Arbitration (Protocol And Convention) Act, 1937, Solicitor, Arbitration Rules

Summary: Arbitration & ADR - Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1966, ss. 14, 34 - Foreign Awards (Recognition and Enforcement) Act, 1961, ss. 9(a), 9(b) - Partial Consent Award - Order Challenged - Appellant/Industries entered into two production sharing contracts with respondent/UOI, as well as other two corporation - Appellant appointed 'X' as arbitrator on erupt of dispute and differences, on other hand respondent appointed arbitrator as well as chairman of Tribunal - All the parties agreed to change seat of arbitration to London, and Tribunal passed final partial award - SC in appeal against order of HC, reversed judgment of HC and held petition as not maintainable - Further, HC dismissed application u/s. 14 of 1996, Act on ground that in view of SC judgment having held that part I of 1996 Act, was not applicable, such petition would not be maintainable - Hence, instant special leave petition - Whether applicability of Part I of 1996 Act has been excluded.

Held, provisions of Part I of 1996, Act would apply to all arbitrations and to all proceedings relating thereto. Where such arbitration is held in India the provisions of Part I would compulsorily apply and parties are free to deviate only to the extent permitted by the derogable provisions of Part I. However, in cases of international commercial arbitrations held out of India provisions of Part I would apply unless the parties by agreement express or implied, exclude all or any of its provisions. In that case the laws or rules chosen by the parties would prevail. However, any provision, in Part I, which is contrary to or excluded by that law or rules will not apply. Thus, Part-I of 1996 Act, is excluded by necessary implication if it is found that on the facts of a case either the juridical seat of the arbitration is outside India or the law governing the arbitration agreement is a law other than Indian law. This Court has already determined both that the juridical seat of the arbitration is at London and that the arbitration agreement is governed by English law. This being the case, it is not open to the appellant to argue that Part-I of 1996 Act, would be applicable. Therefore, both on grounds of res judicata as well as the law laid down in the judgment dtd. 28-5-2014, this application u/s. 14 of 1996, Act deserves to be dismissed. Hence, judgment passed by HC does not warrant any interference. Petition dismissed.
