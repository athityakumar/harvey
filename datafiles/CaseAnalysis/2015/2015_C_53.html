Radhey Shyam and another v Chhabi Nath and another

Supreme Court of India

26 February 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Adarsh Kumar Goel, H.L. Dattu, A.K. Sikri

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 157; (2015) 5 SCC 423; 2015 (3) ADJ 210; AIR 2015 SC 3269; 2015 (4) ALD(SC) 139; 2015 (110) ALR 4; 2015 (2) AWC 1716; 2015 (2) CTC 334; JT 2015 (3) SC 140; 2015 (1) KLT 1032; 2015 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 2114; 2015 (3) MahLJ 296; 2015 (2) MLJ 750; 2015(3) M.P.H.T. 178; 2015 (2) MPLJ 254; 2015 (2) RCR(Civil) 606; 2015(3) SCALE 88; [2015] 3 S.C.R. 197; 2015 (1) UPLBEC 646; 2015 (3) WLN(SC) 67

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Constitution

Keywords: English, Pronounced, Tribunals, Superintendence

Summary: (A) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. Interference with judicial orders of Civil Court in exercise of Writ Jurisdiction - Powers of Writ Court - HC in the instant matter in exercise of writ jurisdiction vacated the interim order passed by the Civil Court - Whether the law laid down in Surya Dev Rai v. Ram Chander Rai and Ors., 2003 Indlaw SC 598, making order of civil court amenable to writ jurisdiction u/art. 226 of the Constitution is correct.

Held, the judicial orders of civil court are not amenable to writ jurisdiction u/art. 226 of the Constitution. The view taken in Surya Dev Rai was overruled on the premise that the judgment passed therein did not correctly appreciated the existing settled ratio in the earlier Nine Judge judgment of the Supreme Court in Naresh Shridhar Mirajkar and Ors. v. State of Maharashtra, 1966 Indlaw SC 346 wherein it was concluded that Certiorari does not lie to quash the judgments of inferior courts of civil jurisdiction. The Mirajkar case makes a clear distinction between judicial orders of inferior courts of civil jurisdiction and those of inferior tribunals or court which are not civil courts and which cannot pass judicial orders. Judicial orders passed by civil courts of plenary jurisdiction stand on a different footing. Reference answered.

(B) Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950 - Cetiorari - Scope - Whether in exercise of writ jurisdiction a writ of Certiorari can be issued to interfere with the judgments of inferior courts of civil jurisdiction.

Held, Certiorari may lie and is generally granted when a court has acted without or in excess of its jurisdiction. The want of jurisdiction may arise from the nature of the subject-matter of the proceeding or from the absence of some preliminary proceeding or the court itself may not be legally constituted or suffer from certain disability by reason of extraneous circumstances. When the jurisdiction of the court depends upon the existence of some collateral fact, it is well settled that the court cannot by a wrong decision of the fact give it jurisdiction which it would not otherwise possess. A tribunal may be competent to enter upon an enquiry but in making the enquiry it may act in flagrant disregard of the rules of procedure or where no particular procedure is prescribed, it may violate the principles of natural justice. A writ of certiorari may be available in such cases. An error in the decision or determination itself may also be amenable to a writ of certiorari but it must be a manifest error apparent on the face of the proceedings, e.g. when it is based on clear ignorance or disregard of the provisions of law. In other words, it is a patent error which can be corrected by certiorari but not a mere wrong decision. Reference answered.

(C) Civil Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 226, 227, 32 - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, s. 115 - Challenge against Judicial Order - Whether a judicial order passed by Civil Court can said to be violating a fundamental right and can be challenged u/arts. 226 and 32 of the Constitution.

Held, challenge to judicial orders could lie by way of appeal or revision or u/art. 227 of the Constitution and not by way of a writ u/arts. 226 and 32 of the Constitution. Even if where a remedy by way of an appeal has not been provided for against the order and judgment of a District Judge, the remedy available to the aggrieved person is to file a revision before the HC u/s. 115 of the CPC. Where remedy for filing a revision before the HC u/s. 115 CPC has been expressly barred by a State enactment, only in such case a petition u/art. 227 of the Constitution would lie and not u/art. 226 of the Constitution. Writ jurisdiction is constitutionally conferred on all HCs. Broad principles of writ jurisdiction followed in England are applicable to India and a writ of certiorari lies against patently erroneous or without jurisdiction orders of Tribunals or authorities or courts other than judicial courts. There are no precedents in India for HCs to issue writs to subordinate courts. Control of working of subordinate courts in dealing with their judicial orders is exercised by way of appellate or revisional powers or power of superintendence u/art. 227. Orders of civil court stand on different footing from the orders of authorities or Tribunals or courts other than judicial/civil courts. While appellate or revisional jurisdiction is regulated by statutes, power of superintendence u/art. 227 is Constitutional. Further, a writ of mandamus does not lie against a private person not discharging any public duty. Scope of art. 227 is different from that of art. 226. Reference answered.
