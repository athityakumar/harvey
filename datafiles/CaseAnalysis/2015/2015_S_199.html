Yogendra Kumar Jaiswal v State of Bihar and others

Supreme Court of India

10 December 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, A.R. Dave

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 876; (2016) 3 SCC 183; AIR 2016 SC 1474; 2016 CRLJ 2129; 2016 (1) ILR(Cut) 804; JT 2015 (12) SC 501; 2015(13) SCALE 508

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Keywords: Commission Of An Offence, Fair Trial, Emergency, Wealth, Precedent, Confiscation, Reason To Believe, Forum, Assembly, Judicial Tribunal, Believe, Additional Sessions Judge, Wild Life (Protection) Act, 1972, Challenge, Corrupt, Criminal Trial, Proviso, Copies, Speedy Trial, Expiry, Justification, Dealing, Forfeiture, Irregularity, Illegality, Vague, Foreign Exchange Regulation Act, 1947, Explosive Substances Act, 1908, Prevention of Corruption Act, 1947, Judicial Officer, Criminal Law Amendment Ordinance, 1944, Employed, Consultation, Inconsistency, Pendency, Valuable Thing, Existing Law, Character, Attack, Delhi Special Police Establishment Act, 1946, Smugglers and Foreign Exchange Manipulators (Forfeiture of Property) Act, 1976, Repugnancy, Concurrent, Distinct, State Law, National Tax Tribunal Act, 2005, Maharashtra Rent Control Act, 1999, Ordinance, Stress, Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1961, Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act, 1967, Andhra Pradesh Forest Act, Prevention of Money Laundering Act, 2002, Orissa Special Courts Act, 2006, Bihar Special Courts Act, 2009, Orissa Special Courts Rules, 2007, Orissa Special Courts Act, 1990, Association, National Judicial Appointments Commission Act, 2014, Prevention of Money- Laundering (Amendment) Act, 2009, Bihar Special Courts Rules 2010

Summary: Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 13(1)(e), 11, 12, 13, 13(1)(a), 13(1)(b), 2, 3, 7, 8 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 143(1), 199, 20, 20(1), 20(2), 21, 212, 247, 254(2), 254(1), 300A, 316(10) - Orissa Special Courts Act, 2006, ss. 13(1)(e), 14, 15, 15(3), 16, 17, 18(1),19, 26, 27 - Confiscation of Accumulated Assets - Constitutionality Challenged - Appellants/public servants were facing criminal cases in various offences including offence u/s. 13(1)(e) of 1988, Act, against accumulating property disproportionate to source of income - Further, during pendency of proceedings, Orissa Special Courts Act was enacted and as consequence case was transferred and tried under it and interim punishment of confiscation of property was passed - HC upheld constitutional validity of the Act - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, art. 212 of Constitution, stipulates that the validity of any proceedings in the Legislature of a State shall not be called in question on the ground of any alleged irregularity of procedure. Thus, the Orissa Act, which was introduces as money bill is not hit by art. 199 of Constitution. Art. 247 of Constitution, empowers Parliament to establish additional courts for better administration in respect of laws passed under List I of the Seventh Schedule of the Constitution. The establishment of Special Courts under the Orissa Act as well as the Bihar Act is not violative of art. 247 of Constitution. The offence under the Orissa Act which has been carved out is the offence u/s. 13(1)(e) of 1988 Act and the Orissa Act provides for establishment of Special Courts and also provides for provisions pertaining to confiscation at an interim stage. The establishment of Special Courts under the Orissa Act as well as the Bihar Act is not violative of art. 247 of the Constitution.

Further, the provisions pertaining to declaration and effect of declaration as contained in ss. 5 and 6 of the Orissa Act and the Bihar Act are constitutionally valid as they do not suffer from any unreasonableness or vagueness. The Chapter III of the both the Acts providing for confiscation of property or money or both neither violates Article 14 nor Article 20(1) nor Article 21 of the Constitution. Furthermore, the procedure provided for confiscation and the proceedings before the Authorised Officer do not cause any discomfort either to Article 14 or to Article 20(3) of the Constitution. Also, the provision relating to appeal in both the Acts is treated as constitutional on the basis of reasoning that the power subsists with the High Court to extend the order of stay on being satisfied. The proviso to Section 18(1) of the Orissa Act does not fall foul of Article 21 of the Constitution. The provisions contained in Section 19 pertaining to refund of confiscated money or property does not suffer from any kind of unconstitutionality. Also, r. 12 (a) and (f) of the 2010 Rules being violative of the language employed in the Bihar Act are ultra vires or anything contained therein pertaining to the summary procedure is also declared as ultra vires the Bihar Act. Appeals disposed of.
