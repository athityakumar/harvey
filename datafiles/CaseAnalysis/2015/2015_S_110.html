Om Prakash (Dead) Through his Lrs. v Shanti Devi and others

Supreme Court of India

05 January 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Vikramajit Sen, M.Y. Eqbal, Anil R. Dave

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 1; (2015) 4 SCC 601; AIR 2015 SC 976; 2015(2) ALL MR 492; 2015 (109) ALR 219; 2015 (1) AWC 585; [2015] 189 Comp Cas 1; 2015 (1) CTC 337; JT 2015 (1) SC 105; 2015 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 227; 2016 (2) MahLJ 138; 2016 (1) MPLJ 617; 2015 (1) RCR(Civil) 432; 2015 (127) RD 284; 2015(1) SCALE 19; [2015] 1 S.C.R. 277; 2015 (1) WLN(SC) 40

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal; Land & Property

Summary: Family & Personal - Land & Property - Indian Evidence Act, 1872 - Transfer of Property Act, 1882 - Gift deed - Execution of document - 30 years old document - Respondent filed case before Trial Court submitting that he was the owner-allottee of the property and had parted with possession of the property to the Appellant on a nominal licence basis - It had been agreed between parties that as and when required by the respondent the appellant would vacate the property - However, despite the respondent's repeated requests the appellant does not accede thereto - Appellant contended that the respondent had executed a Gift Deed in his favour, thereby making him the owner of the property - Further contended that the Gift Deed had been registered in and by the Office of the Sub Registrar, Patiala - Trial Court and the Appellate Court held that the Gift Deed had not been proved u/ss. 68 and 69 of the 1872 Act - HC dismissed appeal filed against said order - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, the first and fatal stumbling block of the appellant's case, is that at the time of tendering of the Gift Deed before the Trial Court, the 30 maturation period provided by s. 90 of 1872 Act was not satisfied, the Gift Deed having been tendered in evidence after around 29 and one-half years, since he had alluded to it in the course of the appellant examining himself unlike the stage of pleadings this incontrovertibly partook the nature of tendering evidence. The time prerequisite to even essay availing of the Court's discretionary powers u/s. 90 of 1872 Act had not been met. Being a statutory requirement, Courts cannot alter the operation of the statute by reading into it as allowing a document aged 29 and one- half years to be open to the law's presumption. The Judgment of the HC below has considered the issue of this document's eligibility u/s. 90 of Act, and repudiated this submission, the document not even, echoing the words of s. 90 of 1872 Act, 'purporting' to be 30 years old at the time of tendering. Even if the document purported or proved to be 30 years old, the appellant would not axiomatically receive a favourable presumption, the s. 90 of 1872 Act presumption being a discretionary one. Since the Gift Deed in question was tendered in evidence five months prior to having become 30 old, the Appeal is devoid of merits. Appeal dismissed.
