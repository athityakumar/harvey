Sunil Kumar and others v Bihar Public Service Commission and others

Supreme Court of India

14 October 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Ranjan Gogoi, N.V. Ramana

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 731; (2016) 2 SCC 495; AIR 2016 SC 4385; JT 2015 (10) SC 670; 2015(10) SCALE 692; [2015] 12 S.C.R. 1070; 2016 (1) SLR 698

Case Digest

Subject: Service

Keywords: Examination, Examinations, Public Service Commission, Delhi Judicial Service Rules, 1970, Bihar Junior Civil Service (Recruitment) Rules, 1951

Summary: Service - Failure to follow method in examination - Abiding to ealier decision - Examination is conducted by Commission, however it is contended that results declared of examination is not in accordance with earlier decision of HC and as per principle laid down in Sanjay Singh and Another v U.P. Public Service Commission, Allahabad and Another, 2007 Indlaw SC 652, where it is was held system of moderation is applicable only to cases where candidates take common examination i.e. where there are no optional subjects and all papers in which candidates appear are same - Further, in situation where subjects are different, it is scaling method which has to be upheld and in such situations system of moderation would not be relevant - Hence, it is alleged that examination in question conducted by Commission involved taking of examination by candidates in different subjects/papers, results declared are vitiated as same has been finalized by following moderation method - HC failed to pass any order against Commission - Whether judgment in question laid down any binding and inflexible requirement of law with regard to adoption of scaling method to examination.

Held, having regard to context in which conclusions were reached and opinions were expressed by SC it is difficult to understand as to how SC in judgment in question could be understood to have laid down any binding principle of law or directions or even guidelines with regard to holding of examinations; evaluation of papers and declaration of results by Commission. Further, in judgment in question it was held that scaling is method which was generally unsuitable to be adopted for evaluation of answer papers of subjects common to all candidates and that application of said method to examination in question had resulted in unacceptable results. Further, SC held that judgment in question did not decide that to such examination i.e. where papers are common system of moderation must be applied and to examination where papers/subjects are different, scaling is only available option. Hence, SC failed to find any declaration of law or precedent or principle in judgment in question. Hence, decision, has to be understood to be confined to facts of case, rendered upon consideration of relevant Service Rules prescribing particular syllabus. Further, SC held that it cannot understand law to be imposing requirement of adoption of moderation to particular kind of examination and scaling to others. Both are, at best, opinions, exercise of which requires in depth consideration of questions that are more suitable for experts in field. Hence, SC held that action taken by Commission cannot be held to deviate either from directions passed by HC or from judgment in question. Appeals dismissed.

Ratio - Examination held by Commission cannot be challenged and set aside when it fails to adopt particular method suggested in earlier decision which is not binding.
