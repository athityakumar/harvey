Raja @ Rajinder v State of Haryana

Supreme Court of India

10 April 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, N.V. Ramana

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 247; 2015 ALL MR (Cri) 2055; JT 2015 (4) SC 57; 2015 (2) MLJ(Crl) 341; 2015 (2) RCR(Criminal) 1006; 2015(4) SCALE 498; [2015] 3 S.C.R. 947

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Summary: Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 34, 201, 302 - Circumstantial Evidence - Motive - Appellant was charged with offences punishable u/ss. 302 and 201 read with s. 34 of IPC and Trial Court convicted appellant and co-accused and passed sentence - Aggrieved, appellant and co-accused filed appeals before HC, which affirmed conviction and sentence of appellant but modified sentence imposed on co-accused - Hence, instant appeal by appellant challenging conviction and sentence imposed on him - Whether prosecution established chain in completeness against accused persons.

Held, circumstances that have been established by prosecution are that firstly, deceased was last seen with appellant at tea stall, secondly, knife, blood-stained clothes and ashes of the burnt blanket were recovered at instance of appellant, and also deposition of taxi driver, to effect that he saw bullock cart parked in front of house of accused and certain persons were tying bundle in a palli. Further, it was established that there was suspicion by accused that deceased was having relationship with his brother's wife and that had aroused his anger and said motive further strengthens the case of the prosecution. Hence, in view of circumstantial evidence, appeal could not be allowed. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - In cases based on circumstantial evidence, Court is required to evaluate the circumstantial evidence to see that the chain of events have been established clearly and completely to rule out any reasonable likelihood of the innocence of the accused. Further, in such cases based on circumstantial evidence, existence of motive is an enlightening factor in process of presumptive reasoning.
