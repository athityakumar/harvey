Surya Vadanan v State of Tamil Nadu

Supreme Court of India

27 February 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Madan B. Lokur, Uday Umesh Lalit

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 405; (2015) 5 SCC 450; AIR 2015 SC 2243; 2015 (1) CLT(SC) 259; 2015 (3) CTC 438; JT 2015 (3) SC 85; 2015 (3) Law Herald (P&H) 1920; 2015 (2) RCR(Civil) 183; 2015(3) SCALE 151; [2015] 4 S.C.R. 903

Case Digest

Subject: Family & Personal

Keywords: Writ Of Habeas Corpus

Summary: Family & Personal - Custody of children - Appellant husband and respondent no.3 wife married in India, however, appellant husband is resident of U.K. - Further, after marriage, appellant and respondent stayed and earned living in U.K. - From said wedlock of appellant and respondent, two daughters were born in U.K. - Further, due to some matrimonial problems, respondent wife brought her children to India and declined to return to U.K. along with appellant - Respondent no.3 filed divorce decree according custody of children before Indian courts - Further, appellant instituted legal action before U.K. Court for making children as wards of court - Foreign Courts passed order making children wards of court and requiring respondent to return children to jurisdiction of foreign court - Further, as respondent failed to comply with order passed by foreign court, appellant filed petition before HC i.e. Indian Courts for writ of habeas corpus on ground, inter alia, that respondent had illegal custody of two daughters of couple - HC held in favor of respondent no.3 that custody of children is legal and dismissed petition of appellant - Whether the custody is legal.

Held, respondent no.3 has not taken any steps to give up her foreign citizenship and to acquire Indian citizenship and he has taken no such steps even with respect to her children. Hence, she is desirous of retaining her foreign citizenship at cost of her Indian citizenship and would also like her children to continue with their foreign citizenship, rather than take Indian citizenship. Hence, there is no reason why courts in India should not encourage her and children to submit to jurisdiction of foreign court which has most intimate contact with them and closest concern apart from being located in country of their citizenship. Further, respondent no.3 filed proceedings for divorce in India way back in 2012, she made no serious effort to obtain any interim order in her favour regarding custody of children, nor did she persuade Trial Court for more than two years to pass interim order for custody of children. Further, since first effective order or direction was passed by foreign court, principle of comity of courts would tilt balance in favour of that court rather than Family Court. With respect to order passed by foreign court, SC held that there is no final determination on issue of custody and what foreign court has required is for respondent no.3 to present herself before it along with two children who are wards of foreign court and to make her submissions and foreign court has not taken any final decision on custody of children, hence, order passed by foreign Court is only interim and interlocutory order. Further, there is also nothing on record to indicate that any prejudice will be caused to children if they are taken to U.K. and subjected to jurisdiction of foreign court. Hence, SC directed that respondent no.3 to take children to U.K. and comply with foreign orders. Further, appellant will bear travelling cost of respondent and would pay maintenance to respondent no.3. Further, is respondent no.3 does not comply with decision of SC, appellant husband will be entitled to take children with him to U.K. for further proceedings. Appeal disposed of.

Ratio - Foreign order passed is binding upon its citizen and same has to be complied with.
