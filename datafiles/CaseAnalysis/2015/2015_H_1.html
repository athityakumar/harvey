L.C. Hanumanthappa (Since Dead), Represented by His Lrs. v H. B. Shivakumar

Supreme Court of India

26 August 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, A.K. Sikri

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 586; (2016) 1 SCC 332; AIR 2015 SC 3364; 2015 (6) ALD(SC) 14; 2015 (112) ALR 901; 2015 (5) AWC 5338; 2015 (3) CLT(SC) 390; 2015 (6) CTC 562; JT 2015 (8) SC 102; 2016 (4) MahLJ 762; 2016 (3) MPLJ 281; 2015 (4) RCR(Civil) 194; 2016 (130) RD 79; 2015(9) SCALE 277B; [2015] 9 S.C.R. 651; 2015 (3) WLN(SC) 103

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Land & Property

Keywords: Right To Sue, Refusing, Suit For Permanent Injunction, Maintainability, Damages For Breach Of, Boundaries, Punjab Pre-Emption Act, 1913

Summary: Land & Property - Civil Procedure - Suit for permanent injunction - Maintainability - Appellants filed suit against defendants for permanent injunction restraining defendants, his servants and agents from disturbing the peaceful possession and enjoyment of the suit schedule property - Further, defendant filed suit and claimed to be the owner in possession of the suit schedule property - Trial Court dismissed the suit of appellant and decreed defendants suit - Appeals were filed before HC - Appeals were disposed of and case was remanded back to Trial Court - Further plaintiff filed suit to amend the plaint - Suit was decreed, however turned down the plea of limitation - Appeal before HC - HC reversed the judgment on limitation - Hence instant appeal.

Held, it is clear that on the facts that amendment was allowed subject to the plea of limitation which could be taken up by the defendant when the trial in the case proceeds. It became clear that original written statement itself dt. 16-5-1990, the defendant had clearly put the plaintiff on notice that it had denied the plaintiff's title to the suit property. A reading in the written statement by Trial court on the facts of the case has been correctly commented upon adversely by the HC in the judgment under appeal. The original written statement read as whole unmistakably indicates that the defendant had not accepted the plaintiff's title. Secondly, while allowing the amendment, the HC in its earlier judgment dt. 28-3-2002 had expressly remanded the matter to Trial court, allowing the defendant to raise the plea of limitation. There can be no doubt that on an application of Khatri Hotels Private Limited and another v Union of India and another 2011 Indlaw SC 645, the right to sue for declaration of title first arose on the facts of instant case on 16-5-1990 when the original written statement clearly denied the plaintiff's title. By 16-5-1993, therefore a suit based on declaration of title would have become time-barred. It is clear that the doctrine of relation back would not apply to the facts of instant case for the reason that the court which allowed the amendment expressly allowed it subject to the plea of limitation, indicating thereby that there are no special or extraordinary circumstances in the instant case to warrant the doctrine of relation back applying so that a legal right that had accrued in favour of the defendant should be taken away. This being so, there is no infirmity in the impugned judgment of HC. Appeal dismissed.
