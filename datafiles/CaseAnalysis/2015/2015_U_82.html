Shreya Singhal v Union of India

Supreme Court of India

24 March 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, J. Chelameswar

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 211; (2015) 5 SCC 1; AIR 2015 SC 1523; 2015 (2) CompLJ 143; 2015 (218) DLT 370; JT 2015 (3) SC 225; 2015 (2) KLT 1; 2015 (3) MLJ 162; 2015 (2) RCR(Criminal) 403; 2015(4) SCALE 1; [2015] 5 S.C.R. 963

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; IT & E-Commerce

Keywords: Freedom Of Speech And Expression, Decency, Internet, Speaker, Freedom Of Speech, Public Order, Intermediary, Speech And Expression, Privacy, Electronic, Constitutionality, Severability, Water (Prevention And Control Of Pollution) Act, 1974, Madras Maintenance of Public Order Act, 1949, East Punjab Public Safety Act, 1949, Computer Resource, US Constitution, Bihar Government Servants Conduct Rules, 1956, Kerala Police Act, 2011, Information Technology (Intermediaries guidelines) Rules, 2011, National Security Ordinance, Central Provinces and Berar Goondas Act, 1946, Communications Decency Act, 1996, Information Technology (Procedure and Safeguards for Blocking for Access of Information by Public) Rules, 2009, Electronic communications, Punjab Maintenance of Public Order Act, U.K. Communications Act, 2003, U.P. Special Powers Act, Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines) Rules, 2011

Summary: (A) Constitution - IT & E-Commerce - Information Technology Act, 2000, ss. 66A - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19 - Constitutionality of s. 66A of the Act - Whether s. 66A of the Act infringes the fundamental right to free speech and expression and is not saved by any of the eight subjects covered in art. 19(2) of the Constitution.

Held, s. 66A of the Act arbitrarily, excessively and disproportionately invades the right of free speech and upsets the balance between such right and the reasonable restrictions that may be imposed on such right. S. 66A of the Act creates an offence which is vague and overbroad, and, therefore, unconstitutional u/art. 19(1)(a) of the Constitution and not saved by art. 19(2) of the Constitution. The wider range of circulation over the internet cannot restrict the content of the right u/art. 19(1)(a) of the Constitution nor can it justify its denial. The Section is unconstitutional also on the ground that it takes within its sweep protected speech and speech that is innocent in nature and is liable therefore to be used in such a way as to have a chilling effect on free speech and would, therefore, have to be struck down on the ground of overbreadth. Further, s. 66A of the Act purports to authorize the imposition of restrictions on the fundamental right contained in art.19(1)(a) of the Constitution in language wide enough to cover restrictions both within and without the limits of constitutionally permissible legislative action. S. 66A of the Act is struck down in its entirety being violative of art. 19(1)(a) of the Constitution and not saved u/art. 19(2) of the Constitution. Petitions disposed of.

(B) Constitution - IT & E-Commerce - Information Technology Act, 2000, ss. 66A - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 14 - Constitutionality of s. 66A of the Act - Whether there is no intelligible differentia between the medium of print, broadcast and real live speech as opposed to speech on the internet.

Held, the intelligible differentia is clear - the internet gives any individual a platform which requires very little or no payment through which to air his views. There is an intelligible differentia between speech on the internet and other mediums of communication for which separate offences can certainly be created by legislation. Challenge on the ground of art. 14 of the Constitution must fail. Petitions disposed of.

(C) Constitution - IT & E-Commerce - Information Technology Act, 2000, s. 69A - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19 - Information Technology (Procedure and Safeguards for Blocking for Access of Information by Public) Rules, 2009 - Constitutionality of s. 69A of the Act and the Rules.

Held, s. 69A of the Act deals with power to issue directions for blocking for public access of any information through any computer resource. First and foremost, blocking can only be resorted to where the Central Govt. is satisfied that it is necessary so to do. Secondly, such necessity is relatable only to some of the subjects set out in art. 19(2) of the Constitution. Thirdly, reasons have to be recorded in writing in such blocking order so that they may be assailed in a writ petition u/art. 226 of the Constitution. It is only after the procedural safeguards are met that blocking orders are made and in case there is a certified copy of a court order, only then can such blocking order also be made. The Rules are not constitutionally infirm in any manner. S. 69A of the Act and Rules are constitutionally valid. Petitions disposed of.

(D) Constitution - IT & E-Commerce - Information Technology Act, 2000, s. 79 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19 - Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines) Rules, 2011 - Constitutionality of s. 79 of the Act and the Rules.

Held, s. 79 belongs to Chap. 12 of the Act in which intermediaries are exempt from liability if they fulfill the conditions of the Section. S. 79(3)(b) of the Act has to be read down to mean that the intermediary upon receiving actual knowledge that a court order accordingly has been passed asking it to expeditiously remove or disable access to certain material must then fail to expeditiously remove or disable access to that material. Unlawful acts beyond what is laid down in art. 19(2) of the Constitution cannot form any part of s. 79 of the Act. Hence, s. 79 is valid subject to s. 79(3)(b) of the Act being read down to mean that an intermediary upon receiving actual knowledge from a court order or on being notified by the appropriate government or its agency that unlawful acts relatable to art. 19(2) of the Constitution are going to be committed then fails to expeditiously remove or disable access to such material. Rules, 2011 are valid subject to R.3(4) of the Rules being read down in the same manner as indicated in the judgment. Petitions disposed of.

(E) Constitution - IT & E-Commerce - Kerala Police Act, s. 118 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 19 - Constitutionality of s. 118.

Held, s. 118(d) of the Act is struck down being violative of art. 19(1)(a) of the Constitution and not saved by art. 19(2) of the Constitution. Petitions disposed of.
