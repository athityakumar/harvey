Mangu Singh v Dharmendra and another

Supreme Court of India

16 December 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Pinaki Chandra Ghose, R.K. Agrawal

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 872; 2016 (1) CGLJ 299; 2016 CRLJ 785; 2016 (2) JCC 821; JT 2015 (12) SC 296; 2016 (1) MLJ(Crl) 217; [2015] 10 S.C.R. 1035

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Bullet, Pistol

Summary: Criminal - Arms Act, 1959, s. 25 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Alleged Murder - Acquittal - Justification - Respondent accused came police Station and confessed vide written report that he had killed his wife and daughter - Accused's father in-law PW1 was informed and subsequently inquest proceedings were conducted to which PW1 is formal witness - FIR was lodged against respondent accused u/ss. 302, of IPC and s. 25 of Act - Trial Court convicted respondent accused and imposed life imprisonment - However, HC vide impugned order acquitted respondent accused on ground that prosecution failed to bring home guilt of accused beyond reasonable doubt - Further, acquittal was based on ground that both FIRs were ante-timed and eye witnesses who were relied upon by Trial Court were interested and unreliable witnesses - Whether impugned order passed by HC in favor of respondent accused is justified and in accordance with law.

Held, PW1 has deposed that accused respondent was in love with girl and this fact was told to him by his wife, who got this information from her daughter deceased. SC held that even if said fact is presumed to be true, still PW1's deposition to this fact is hearsay and in fact, his wife should have been examined to testify this fact. PW1 neither stated this fact in FIR nor in statement made before police, and it was only after two and half years later that this fact was stated in his deposition before Court. However, in investigation no such fact came to light, nor wife of PW1 was summoned for making statements before police or before Court. Further, with respect to testimony of eye-witnesses, SC held that police investigation itself disclosed that PW1 came to spot after information was sent to him by police. Intimation was him after accused is alleged to have made written FIR. In these circumstances, PW1 cannot be said to be eye-witness to offence. Further, with respect to deposition of PW4, SC held that it seems unnatural and not to be trustworthy. PW4 in his testimony firstly stated that victim was shot in his presence, but he did not know as to in which part of body she was shot. Then he corrected himself by deposing that bullet had pierced her abdomen and blood started oozing out from her body. This deposition is contradictory to medical evidence as cause of death of deceased was strangulation and she never received any gun-shot injury. Moreover, blood-stained clothes of victims or bullet ridden car parts were not recovered. It was not investigated at all as to in which portion of car victims were killed. Further, there appears material alteration in testimonies of witnesses. Both PW1 and PW4 deposed that they saw dead body in rear seat of car, whereas police investigation reveals that dead body was lying in front left side seat of car. Conduct of PW4 seemed unnatural that he did not sought for any help, nor did he inform anybody in village that daughter from his village was killed. PW4 also stated in his deposition that he did not remember as to whether victim was wearing saree or suit, yet he remembered registration number of car. Further, SC held that in FIR, informant PW1 failed to mention material facts, like he having been informed about incident by police, his participation in inquest, receiving ornaments seized by police from place of incident, two male wrist watches, he being accompanied by two other person to place of incident from his village. Even motive was not mentioned in FIR. This makes conduct of PW1 very unnatural and suspicious. Further, with respect to confessional FIR, SC held that accused respondent has taken defence that he was forced to scribe it at dictation of IO, after being assaulted at police station and it was registered ante-timed. Further, with respect to recovery of country-made pistol and empty cartridge, SC held that HC has rightly pointed out that during investigation, no statement disclosing fact/material to be discovered was proved before Court. SC held that prosecution has failed to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt against accused and HC was justified in doubting veracity of prosecution case and recording verdict of acquittal, which does not suffer from vice of perversity. Appeals dismissed.

Ratio - In criminal cases, it is settled law that defence needs to only establish its case based on probability, whereas prosecution has to prove guilt of accused beyond reasonable doubt.
