Keshavlal Khemchand and Sons Private Limited and others v Union of India and others

Supreme Court of India

28 January 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

J. Chelameswar, Sharad Arvind Bobde

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 58; (2015) 4 SCC 770; AIR 2015 SC 1168; 2015 (109) ALR 872; 2015 (2) AWC 1783; 2015 (1) BC 511; [2015] 190 Comp Cas 452; 2015 (1) CompLJ 536; 2015 (1) DRTC 305; JT 2015 (2) SC 1; 2015 (2) KLT 577; 2015 (1) MLJ 848; 2015 (4) RCR(Civil) 7; 2015(1) SCALE 709; [2015] 2 S.C.R. 51

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Constitution

Keywords: Pledge, Reserve Bank Of India, Constitutionality, Karnataka Excise Act, 1965, National Housing Bank, Enforcement of Security Interest and Recovery of Debts Laws (Amendment) Act, 2004, Deviation from original objects and reasons of the Act, Unreasonable classification of account of any borrower as a NPA

Summary: Banking & Finance - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 32 - Securitisation and Reconstruction of Financial Assets and Enforcement of Security Interest Act, 2002, ss. 2(1)(o), 13, 13(2), 13(3A), 13(4) - Recovery of Debts due to Banks and Financial Institutions Act, 1993 - Deviation from original objects and reasons of the Act - Unreasonable classification of account of any borrower as a NPA - Constitutionality - Appellants (petitioners) filed petitions HC challenging the amendment of s. 2(1)(o) of the Act, defines NPA by amended by Act 30 of 2004 - It was the amended definition which was subject matter of dispute in bunch of writ petitions before various HCs - Gujarat HC, by a common judgment in a batch of writ petitions, held that the amended s. 2(1)(o) of the Act was unconstitutional and ultra vires art. 14 of Constitution and object of the Act and restored the provisions which existed prior to amendment of 2004 and existed at the time of decision of SC in Mardia Chemicals Ltd. & Others v. Union of India & Others, 2004 Indlaw SC 258 - Thereafter, in another common judgment in a batch of writ petitions, Madras HC rejected the challenge - Hence, instant appeals and petitions, either the borrowers against whom proceedings u/s. 13 of the Act or 'SECURED CREDITORS' invoking art. 32 of Constitution - Petitioners contended that while the un-amended s. 2(1)(o) of the Act provided for a uniform standard by which an account of a borrower was to be classified as Non-Performing Asset (NPA) of the CREDITOR by applying the guidelines issued by Reserve Bank, the amended provision enables different CREDITORS to adopt different guidelines which prescribe different standards for arriving at a conclusion that the account of a borrower was NPA, such a provision was violative of art. 14 of Constitution as it amounts to a class legislation forbidden by art. 14 of Constitution.

Held, even prior to the coming into force of the Act, the CREDITORS were classifying the accounts of the borrowers as NPAs under the statutory guidelines issued by the RBI. Under the said guidelines FINANCIAL ASSETS are sub-divided into standard, sub-standard, doubtful, and loss. Depending upon the length of the period for which the installment of money is overdue, such assets are classified as NPA. As the length of the period of overdue increased, the account of the borrower is progressively classified from 'sub-standard' to 'loss'. The same classification is adopted by the Parliament while enacting the Act. Therefore, all NPAs do not belong to the same class. Such a classification is relevant and assumes importance in the decision making process of the SECURED CREDITOR u/s. 13(2) of the Act as to which one of the steps contemplated u/s. 13(4) of the Act should be resorted to in the case of a given defaulting borrower. if a statute does not contain the definition of a particular expression employed in it, it becomes the duty of the Courts to expound the meaning of the undefined expressions in accordance with the well established rules of statutory interpretation. Therefore, the function of prescribing the norms for classifying a borrower's account as a NPA is not an essential legislative function. The laying down of such norms requires a constant and close monitoring of the financial system demanding considerable amount of expertise in the areas of public finance, banking etc., and the norms may require a periodic revision. All that activity involves too much of detail and promptitude of action. The crux of the impugned Act is the prescription that a SECURED CREDITOR could take steps contemplated u/s. 13(4) of the Act on the 'default' of the borrower. The stipulation under the Act of classifying the account of the borrower as NPA as a condition precedent for enforcing the security interest is an additional obligation imposed by the Act on the CREDITOR. The borrower cannot be heard to complain that defining of the conditions subject to which the CREDITOR could classify the account as NPA, is part of the essential legislative function. If the Parliament did not choose to define the expression 'NPA' at all, Court would be bound to interpret that expression as long as that expression occurs in s. 13(2) of the Act. In such a situation, Courts would have resorted to the principles of interpretation. If the Parliament chose to define a particular expression by providing that the expression shall have the same meaning as is assigned to such an expression by a body which is an expert in the field covered by the statute and more familiar with the subject matter of the legislation, the same does not amount to any delegation of the legislative powers. Parliament is only stipulating that the expression 'NPA' must be understood by all the CREDITORS in the same sense in which such expression is understood by the expert body i.e., RBI or other REGULATORS which are in turn subject to the supervision of the RBI. Therefore, the submission that amendment of the definition of the expression 'non-performing asset' u/s. 2(1)(o) of the Act is bad on account of excessive delegation of essential legislative function, is untenable and is required to be rejected. Submission that by authorizing different REGULATORS to prescribe different norms for the identification of a NPA with reference to different CREDITORS amount to unreasonable classification is also required to be rejected for the reason that all the CREDITORS do not form a uniform/homogenous class. Therefore, to say that enabling them to follow different norms would be violative of art. 14 of Constitution, would be wholly untenable. S. 13(3A) of the Act obligates the SECURED CREDITORS to examine the representation/objection, if any, made by the borrower on the receipt of notice contemplated u/s. 13(2) of the Act and communicate the reasons to the borrower if such a representation is not accepted by the SECURED CREDITORS. Court fail to understand as to how inconsistency will render the Act unconstitutional. The objects and reasons are not voted upon by the legislature. If the enactment is otherwise within the Constitutionally permissible limits, the fact that there is a divergence between the objects appended to the Bill and the tenor of the Act, cannot be a ground for declaring the law unconstitutional. The amended definition of the expression 'NPA' u/s. 2(1)(o) of the Act is constitutionally valid. Appeals disposed of.
