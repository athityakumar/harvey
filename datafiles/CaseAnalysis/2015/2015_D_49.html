State of Haryana and another v Devander Sagar and another

Supreme Court of India

07 September 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Vikramajit Sen, A.M. Sapre

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 610; 2016 (114) ALR 259; 2015 (3) CLT(SC) 408; 2016 (1) RCR(Civil) 443; 2016 (130) RD 736; 2015(9) SCALE 590

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Land & Property

Keywords: Urgency, Constitution (Forty-Fourth Amendment) Act, 1978

Summary: Land & Property - Administrative - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, ss. 4, 6, 17(1), 17(3), 17(4) - Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013 - Acquisition of land - Permission to issue fresh declaration - Justification - Appellant State Govt. issued notification u/s. 4 of 1894 Act to acquire land in question - Further, appellant state invoked urgency provisions u/ss. 17(1) and 17(4) of Act thereby denying to respondent landowners opportunity to file objections u/s. 5A of Act - Further, declaration u/s. 6 was issued on next day - Respondent filed petition challenging notification issued under Act - DB noting that appellant State had not adhered to mandatory requirement of payment of 80 per cent compensation to landowners and that it did not qualify as a case of urgency since appellant State passed had failed to publish award within one year after s. 6 declaration, quashed latter - However, DB simultaneously permitted petitioners before it to file s. 5A of Act objections within thirty days and permitted appellant State to issue a fresh s. 6 declaration - Whether impugned order passed by DB is justified and in accordance with law.

Held, no compensation postulated by Statute u/s. 17(3) of Act, was given at the time that urgency provisions were invoked. This exercise ought to have been carried out by passing provisional or ad hoc award containing Collector's estimation of compensation to be paid to the landowners. It may be pointed out that stipulation regarding urgency in terms of s. 5-A of Act has no role to play when period of limitation u/s. 6 of Act is reckoned. Further, purpose for providing period of limitation seems to be avoidance of inconvenience to person whose land is sought to be acquired. Compensation gets attached from date of notification u/s. 4(1) of Act and s. 11 of Act provides that valuation of land has to be done on date of publication of notification u/s. 4(1) of Act. SC held that since compensation is calculated based on value of land on the date of s. 4 of notification, order of DB dt. 12-1-2004 resulted in landowners getting compensation at 2001 rates even though award was finally passed in 2006 and compensation is yet to be paid to respondents. Had DB struck down only declaration, which in turn would have resulted in entire acquisition lapsing, appellant state would have had to reinitiate acquisition proceedings, resulting in respondents receiving compensation at market rates current at time of the fresh notification. Hence, declaration dt. 30-12-2004 cannot be upheld merely by virtue of DB's erroneous and prejudicial Order. We are in agreement with the decision of the High Court in the impugned Judgment and consequently dismiss appeal. Further, in another appeal, SC held that contesting Respondents had not been parties before DB, SC held that respondents, too, initially consented to acquisition process and only challenged it belatedly by seeking to rely upon favourable Judgment that did not relate or pertain to them. Impugned order passed by DB is set aside and order passed by lower court is restored. Appeal disposed of.

Ratio - Acquisition of land without taking following procedure laid down in statute is not valid and is liable to be quashed.
