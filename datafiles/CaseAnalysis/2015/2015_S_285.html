B. S. Sheshagiri Setty and others v State of Karnataka and others

Supreme Court of India

15 October 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

V. Gopala Gowda, T.S. Thakur

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 887; (2016) 2 SCC 123; 2016 (1) KarLJ 1; 2016 (1) RCR(Civil) 239; 2015(10) SCALE 763

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Immoveable Property, Societies, Karnataka Co-operative Societies Act, 1959, Karnataka Co-operative Societies Rules, 1960, Mortgage Money

Summary: Banking & Finance - Practice & Procedure - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 21 - Karnataka Co-operative Societies Act, 1959, ss. 105, 106, 108, 82A, 89, 99 - Limitation Act, 1963, s. 29(2) - Karnataka Co-operative Societies Rules, 1960, rr. 38, 38(5)(a), 38(7) - Default of Payment of Loan - Confirmation of Sale Challenged - Arbitrator passed ex-parte order in favour of respondent no.2/bank on default of payment of loan by appellant/farmers - Tribunal in appeal set aside order of Arbitrator and HC in writ petition set aside order of Tribunal - Bank confirmed sale of land in favour of respondent no.6/auction purchaser - However, Minister allowed revision petition, set aside confirmation order and HC in writ petition by purchaser, set aside order passed by Minister - DB of HC dismissed writ appeals of appellants on ground of that revision filed by appellant before Minister was barred by limitation - Hence, instant appeals.

Held, Karnataka Co-operative Societies Act, is special legislation and s. 108 of the Act, confers upon the State Government the power of suo motu revising the order of the Tribunal. It is pertinent to note that no time limit has been set for the same. The provision confers the power upon the State Government in case the order of the Tribunal is contrary to law and has resulted in miscarriage of justice. Thus, by virtue of s 29(2) of 1961, Act, the power to condone delay is available with the state govt. In instant case, the sale of the property and the confirmation of the auction sale is contrary to the notification referred to supra and law and has resulted in a gross miscarriage of justice. The action of the sale officer has resulted in the deprivation of the right to livelihood of the appellants who are small landowners, guaranteed to them u/art. 21 of Constitution. The right to livelihood has been held to be an integral part of right to life. Further, confirmation of sale happened after payment of principal loan amount by appellants in compliance of notification issued by bank. Therefore, the rights of auction purchase cannot be protected as he cannot be said to be a bonafide purchaser. Hence, impugned judgment and order passed in writ petitions and writ appeals are set aside and order passed by Minister is restored. Further, respondent no.6 is directed to re-deliver the possession of the immoveable property to the appellants sold in auction by the bank and to receive sale consideration. Appeals allowed.
