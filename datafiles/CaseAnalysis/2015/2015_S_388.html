Santosh S/o Shankar Pawar v State of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

21 April 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

R. Banumathi, T.S. Thakur, Adarsh Kumar Goel

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 477; (2015) 7 SCC 641; AIR 2015 SC 3789; 2015 CRLJ 4880; 2016 (1) RCR(Criminal) 188; 2015(5) SCALE 424; [2015] 4 S.C.R. 962

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Kerosene, Concurrent

Summary: (A) Criminal - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Conviction - Appellant and deceased was married on 17-6-2005 - Further, on 4-9-2005 it was stated that deceased wife went to answer nature's call and on her return, appellant accused suspected her fidelity - Further, appellant accused started assaulting her with fists and kicks and poured kerosene and set her ablaze - Further, deceased ran towards accused in attempt to catch him, thereby burning hands of accused - Further, on way to hospital, deceased narrated incident to her mother PW2 and sister-in-law PW 3 and also to neighbour PW 1 - Further, dying declaration of deceased was recorded - Deceased succumbed to burn injuries - FIR was lodged - Trial Court convicted appellant accused u/s. 302 IPC and sentenced him to undergo life imprisonment and imposed fine - Order passed by Trial Court was confirmed by HC - Whether appellant is responsible for death of deceased.

Held, prosecution has adduced cogent evidence to prove that appellant has caused death of deceased. PW-6 Doctor certified that deceased was in fit mental condition to make statement and PW7-Executive Magistrate recorded dying declaration. SC held that in dying declaration, deceased had categorically stated that on date of incident, appellant poured kerosene over her person and set her on fire. Further, it is corroborated by oral testimony of PW3 sister in-law of deceased. SC held that prosecution has established guilt of accused by dying declaration and oral evidence of mother PW2 and sister-in-law PW3 and same cannot be doubted. Appeal dismissed.

(B) Criminal - Intention to Commit Murder - Conviction - Determination - Appellant contended that his conduct in trying to extinguish fire immediately after incident would shows that there was no intention on part of appellant to commit murder - Whether appellant had intention to commit murder of deceased wife.

Held, from facts and circumstances it is evident that there was homicide, namely death of wife, deceased was set ablaze by appellant and this act was not accidental or unintentional. Further, post mortem certificate revealed that deceased died due to shock and septicaemia caused by 60% burn injuries. SC held that when accused poured kerosene on deceased from kerosene lamp and also threw lighted matchstick on deceased to set her on fire, he must have intended to cause death of deceased. SC held that act of pouring kerosene, though in spur of moment, same was followed by lighting match stick and throwing it on deceased and thereby setting her ablaze. Both acts are intimately connected with each other and resulted in causing death of deceased and act of accused is punishable for murder. Further, SC held that any person of average intelligence would have knowledge that pouring of kerosene and setting her on fire by throwing lighted matchstick is so imminently dangerous that in all probability such act would cause injuries causing death. Hence, order passed by lower court convicting appellant for murder of deceased wife is justified. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - Conviction of accused is justified when prosecution has proved case against accused beyond reasonable doubt.
