Purolator India Limited v Commissioner of Central Excise, Delhi-III

Supreme Court of India

25 August 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, A.K. Sikri

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 578; (2015) 10 SCC 715; 2015 (323) E.L.T. 227; JT 2015 (7) SC 440; 2015(9) SCALE 257; [2015] 9 S.C.R. 812

Case Digest

Subject: Excise; Indirect Tax

Keywords: Assessable Value, Duty Of Excise, Central Excises And Salt Act, 1944, Excisable Goods, Normal Price, Place Of Removal, Trade Discount, Deduction, Central Excise Valuation (Determination of Price of Excisable Goods) Rules, 2000, Claim

Summary: Excise - Sales Tax - Central Excise and Salt Act, 1944.s. 4 - Central Excise Valuation (Determination of Price of Excisable Goods) Rules, 2000, r. 173C - Deduction - Claim - Appellant was engaged n the manufacture of excisable goods and filed declarations u/r. 173C of the Rules with the excise department - Appellant claimed deduction towards Sales Tax, Cash Discount and Volume Discount on excise duty payable to arrive at the assessable value u/s. 4 of the Act - Show Cause Notice was issued to appellant that he was not eligible for the various deductions claimed on account of volume discount, sales tax and cash discount - Further appellant allegedly removed new finished excisable goods instead of old/repaired goods - Commissioner confirmed duty demand of Rs. 44,66,247/- and also imposed penalty of Rs. 49,66,247/- - Tribunal upheld the finding of the Commissioner - Hence instant appeal - Whether s. 4 of the Act as amended in the year 2000 makes no change to the position.

Held, s. 4 of the Act as amended introduces the concept of 'transaction value so that on each removal of excisable goods, the 'transaction value" of such goods becomes determinable. Whereas previously, the value of such excisable goods was the price at which such goods were ordinarily sold in the course of wholesale trade, post amendment each transaction is looked at by itself. However, 'transaction value' as defined in s. 4 (3)(d) of the Act has to be read along with the expression 'for delivery at the time and place of removal'. It is clear, therefore, that what is paramount is that the value of the excisable goods even on the basis of 'transaction value' has only to be at the time of removal, that is, the time of clearance of the goods from the appellant's factory or depot as the case may be. The expression 'actually paid or payable for the goods, when sold' only means that whatever is agreed to as the price for the goods forms the basis of value, whether such price has been paid, has been paid in part, or has not been paid at all. The basis of 'transaction value' is therefore the agreed contractual price. Further, the expression 'When sold" is not meant to indicate the time at which such goods are sold, but is meant to indicate that goods are the subject matter of an agreement of sale. Once this becomes clear, what the learned counsel for the assessee has argued must necessarily be accepted inasmuch as cash discount is something which is "known" at or prior to the clearance of the goods, being contained in the agreement of sale between the assessee and its buyers, and must therefore be deducted from the sale price in order to arrive at the value of excisable goods 'at the time of removal. The basis of 'transaction value is therefore the agreed contractual price. Further, the expression 'when sold' is not meant to indicate the time at which such goods are sold, but is meant to indicate that goods are the subject matter of an agreement of sale. The amended s. 4 of the Act would require that such amount of 75% is not deductible as sales tax because, only sales tax that is 'actually paid' could be deducted post s. 4 of the Act as amended in 2000. The judgment does not in any manner deviate from the settled legal position so far as cash discounts are concerned as has been laid down Union of India and Others v Bombay Tyre International Limited and Others 1983 Indlaw SC 359 and Government of India and Others v Madras Rubber Factory Limited and Others 1995 Indlaw SC 937.The judgment did not concern itself with the 'price' of excisable goods that must be ascertained only at the time of removal from the factory gate. Since SC was only concerned with whether or not certain amounts by way of sales tax were or were not to be deducted from 'price', the said judgment has little application to the facts of the instant case. Thus, it is clear that 'cash discount' has therefore to be taken into account in arriving at 'price' even u/s. 4 of the Act as amended in 2000. Therefore, while affirming the Tribunal's order of remand, SC allows both parties to argue all points that may arise insofar as these issues are concerned. So far as the cash discount issue is concerned, Tribunal's order is set aside. Appeal disposed of.
