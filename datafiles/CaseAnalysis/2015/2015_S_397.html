State of Punjab and another v Bandeep Singh and others

Supreme Court of India

25 August 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Vikramajit Sen, Shiva Kirti Singh

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 583; (2016) 1 SCC 724; 2015 (5) AWC 5132; 2015 (4) BC 213; JT 2015 (7) SC 568; 2015 (5) Law Herald (P&H) 3985; 2015 (4) RCR(Civil) 303; 2015(9) SCALE 240; [2015] 10 S.C.R. 496

Case Digest

Subject: Practice & Procedure; Contract & Commercial

Keywords: Sustainability, Re-auction

Summary: Contact & Commercial - Practice & Procedure - Re-auction - Sustainability - Appellants filed instant appeal against the order of DB , Whereby DB concluded that no ustainable justification and rationalization was recorded in writing at the relevant time for ordering the re-auction of only the two subject properties.

Held, the presence of cartelization or 'pooling' could be a reason for the cancellation of an auction process. In addition, a challenge on the ground that the property has fetched too low a bid when compared to the prevailing market price, would also be valid and permissible provided this approach has been uniformly adhered to. In instant case however, while the latter was ostensibly the reason behind the decision for conducting a fresh auction, no evidence has been placed on the record to support this contention. The highest bids, marginally above the reserve price, have been accepted in the self-same auction. The factual scenario is clearly within the mischief which was frowned upon in Mohinder Singh Gill & Anr v The Chiief Election Commissioner, New Delhi & Ors 1977 Indlaw SC 53. Thus, SC upholds the impugned Judgment for all the reasons contained therein. The assailed action of the Appellant is not substantiated in the noting, which ought at least to have been conveyed to the respondents. The bid of the Respondents is already over a decade old, which is the period the instant appeal has been awaiting its turn in SC, Therefore, to balance the equities and interest of the adversaries as submitted by respondents that although the appellant had addressed a letter to the respondents purporting to return the sums received from them, the cheque for this amount was not enclosed with the letter. The fact remains that these sums continue to be in the coffers of the appellant. It is also submitted by that the balance sale consideration had been tendered by the Respondents to the Appellant, who declined to accept it on the premise that their appeal was pending in SC. Thus, SC directs the respondents to pay the price of the land at the prevailing Circle Rates, which suggestion has readily been accepted by appellant with alacrity. Since the Respondents have succeeded in the HC as well as in SC, they should not be deprived of the fruits of the litigation and suffer the disadvantage of losing the land for which they have successfully paid the earnest money and deposited more than twenty five per cent of the sale consideration and have tendered the entire remainder. Thus, SC holds, that if the respondents tender the price of the land equivalent to prevailing Circle Rate minus the sums already paid by them to appellant within ninety days and appellant shall take all necessary steps to convey the land to the Respondents within sixty days thereafter. Appeals disposed of.
