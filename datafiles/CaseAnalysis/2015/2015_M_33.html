Securities and Exchange Board of India v Magnum Equity Services Limited and others

Supreme Court of India

30 November 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Vikramajit Sen, Shiva Kirti Singh

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 781; [2016] 194 Comp Cas 83; 2016 (3) CompLJ 113; JT 2015 (11) SC 312; 2016 (1) MLJ 239; 2015(13) SCALE 12; [2015] 12 S.C.R. 102

Case Digest

Subject: Securities Exchange Board of India

Keywords: Partnership, Stock, Services, General Clauses Act, 1897, Membership, Brokers, Securities and Exchange Board of India (Stock-Brokers and Sub-Brokers) Regulations, 1992

Summary: Securities Exchange Board of India - Companies Act, 1956 - General Clauses Act, 1897 - Securities and Exchange Board of India (Stock-Brokers and Sub-Brokers) Regulations, 1992 - conversion of partnership firm to company - Failure to grant benefit of fee continuity - Determination - Respondent no.1 firm was registered partnership firm, comprising of seven partners, carrying on business as stock broker; and was member of NSE - Further, firm was converted to public limited company - Further, remaining erstwhile partners became Whole-time Directors of respondent no.1 - Respondent no.1 was registered as stock broker - Further, three Directors resigned from respondent no.1 and transferred their shares to remaining Directors and their family members - Respondent no.1 claimed benefit of fee which firm had paid earlier to SEBI on ground that earlier business carried on by firm had been transferred to respondent no.1 and as result there was continuity of that business - However, SEBI rejected claim of respondent no.1 predication that only three out of seven partners of firm continued as its Whole-time Directors for mandatory period of three years, which was in contravention of conditions laid down in Paragraph I(4) of Schedule III of Regulation - Similar stand was taken with respect to respondent no.2 and benefit of fee continuity was not granted to respondent no.2 - Tribunal vide impugned order quashed order passed by SEBI - Whether impugned order passed by Tribunal in favor of respondent company's is justified and in accordance with law.

Held, in light of provisions of 1992 Act under which Regulations have been issued, latter do not tantamount to Central Act as defined under sub-section (7) of definition clause of 1897 Act . Hence, 1897 Act is not applicable while interpreting language of Paragraph I(4) of Schedule III of Regulations. Further, it is prescribed that even if individual erstwhile partner holds 40 per cent of equity and remains Whole-time Director for stipulated period of three years, fee continuity would become available. SC held that to qualify for benefit of provision, there is two-fold requirement. First, corporate entity must earlier have been either sole proprietorship or partnership. Second, erstwhile partner should own at least 40 per cent of paid-up equity share capital and should also be Whole-time Director of company, for minimum period of three years. Alternatively, erstwhile partners who together hold at least 40 per cent equity must remain Whole-time Directors for minimum of three years. Hence, subsequent entry or exit of partners to and from original partnership firm would have no relevance on entitlement of newly formed corporate entity to take advantage of benefit not only of fee continuity under said provision but also fillip to growth of corporate sector and national economy. Further, same benefit would also be extended to erstwhile partners who after corporatization jointly retain at least 40 per cent of paid-up equity capital of corporate entity and were its Whole-time Directors. Hence, impugned order passed by Tribunal is justified and in accordance with law. Appeals dismissed.

Ratio - It is not justified to deny benefit under Act when conditions specified are fulfilled by party claiming benefit.
