State of Uttar Pradesh and others v United Bank of India and another

Supreme Court of India

26 November 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

M.Y. Eqbal, C. Nagappan

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 884; (2016) 2 SCC 757; 2016 (2) ADJ 32; 2016 (115) ALR 1; 2015 (6) AWC 6209; 2015 (4) CalLT 58; JT 2015 (11) SC 249; 2016 (1) MLJ 181; 2016 (131) RD 250; 2015(12) SCALE 704

Case Digest

Subject: Banking & Finance; Land & Property

Keywords: Government Grants Act, 1895, Leases, Mortgagee, Nazul Rules

Summary: Land & Property - Banking & Finance - Mortgage of Lease/suit Land - Direction Challenged - Lease hold interest of suit property was transferred in favour of appellant no.2/company and lease deed was executed by appellant no.1/state govt. of UP - Company obtained loan facility from respondent no.1/bank against mortgage of suit property - However, on default of payment of loan, bank obtained mortgaged decree, which was transferred to Tribunal (DRT) and suit property was purchased by respondent no.2/developer in auction sale - However, notice was issued by appellant no.1 directing resumption of property and DB of HC allowed writ petitions of respondent nos. 1-2, quashed notice and issued certain directions - All the parties to suit land, aggrieved by direction of DB filed instant appeals.

Held, a person can be said to have a 'legitimate expectation' of a particular treatment, if any representation or promise is made by an authority, either expressly or impliedly, or if the regular and consistent past practice of the authority gives room for such expectation in the normal course. As a ground for relief, the efficacy of the doctrine is rather weak as its slot is just above 'fairness in action' but far below 'promissory estoppel'. In appropriate cases, courts may grant a direction requiring the Authority to follow the promised procedure or established practice. A legitimate expectation, even when made out, does not always entitle the expectant to a relief. Public interest, change in policy, conduct of the expectant or any other valid or bonafide reason given by the decision-maker, may be sufficient to negative the 'legitimate expectation'. Thus, a person, who bases his claim on the doctrine of legitimate expectation has to satisfy that he has relied on the said representation and the denial of that expectation has worked to his detriment. In instant case, there was nothing on the record to show that the lessee/company has obtained any written sanction from the lessor i.e. Government before mortgaging his leasehold interest in the Nazul Land. Thus, the State never recognized the Bank as a mortgagee and never represented or promised either to the lessee/ company or to the Bank to give any benefit under the lease. In such circumstances, the High Court has committed grave error in applying the doctrine of legitimate expectation in favour of the bank. Therefore, the impugned notice issued by the State of U.P. directing resumption of the property is legal and valid and cannot be quashed at the instance of the Bank. Hence, impugned judgment and order passed by DB of HC is set aside, thereby appeal filed by state govt. is allowed. Further, appeals filed by other parties to the suit property are dismissed. Appeals disposed of.
