State of Punjab v Bawa Singh

Supreme Court of India

15 January 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

M.Y. Eqbal, Kurian Joseph

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 33; (2015) 3 SCC 441; 2015 CRLJ 1701; 2015 (2) JCC 843; JT 2015 (1) SC 241; 2015 (1) MLJ(Crl) 374; 2015 (1) RCR(Criminal) 757; 2015(1) SCALE 420; [2015] 1 S.C.R. 709

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Sustainability, Voluntarily causing grievous hurt, Remission in sentence

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 323, 326 r/w s. 34 - Voluntarily causing grievous hurt - Remission in sentence - Sustainability - Respondent (accused) and his wife (co-accused) allegedly hit the complainant with gandasa and thereby committed offence u/ss. 323, 326 r/w s. 34 of IPC - Trial Court convicted respondent and his wife and sentenced them to RI for 3 years - On appeal, Lower Appellate Court set aside the conviction of co-accused but upheld their conviction u/s. 323 of IPC upholding other findings of Trial Court - Aggrieved respondent filed revision petition before HC, which was disposed of reducing the sentence of respondent to the period already undergone - Hence, instant appeal filed by appellant (State).

Held, after proper appreciation of evidence the Trial Court as well as the Lower Appellate Court rightly came to the conclusion that the respondent is not entitled for benefit to probation since he caused injuries on the person of the complainant with Gandasa and dispute between the parties was already pending. However, without proper appreciation of the evidence and consideration of gravity of the offence, Single Judge has taken lenient stand, if not casual and shown undue sympathy by modifying the conviction to the period already undergone. HC while passing the impugned order has completely failed to follow the principles enunciated by SC in catena of decisions. Undue sympathy to impose inadequate sentence would do more harm to the justice system to undermine the public confidence in the efficacy of law. It is the duty of every Court to award proper sentence having regard to the nature of the offence and the manner in which it was executed or committed. The sentencing Courts are expected to consider all relevant facts and circumstances bearing on the question of sentence and proceed to impose a sentence commensurate with the gravity of the offence. The Court must not only keep in view the rights of the victim of the crime but also the society at large while considering the imposition of appropriate punishment. Meager sentence imposed solely on account of lapse of time without considering the degree of the offence will be counter-productive in the long run and against the interest of the society. Impugned order passed by the HC is set aside and the matter is remanded back to the HC to pass a fresh order in revision petition. Appeal allowed.
