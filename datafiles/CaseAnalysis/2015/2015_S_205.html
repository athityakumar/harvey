Dasin Bai@ Shanti Bai v State of Chhattisgarh

Supreme Court of India

11 February 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Pinaki Chandra Ghose, M.Y. Eqbal

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 90; (2015) 4 SCC 186; 2015 ALL MR (Cri) 1243; 2015 (2) CGLJ 107; 2015 (2) JCC 855; JT 2015 (2) SC 186; 2015 (2) Law Herald (P&H) 1426; 2015 (1) MLJ(Crl) 636; 2015 (2) RCR(Criminal) 29; 2015(2) SCALE 276; [2015] 1 S.C.R. 1060

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, s. 313 - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, s. 106 - Murder - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant lady allegedly murdered the deceased by setting him ablaze - Trial Court convicted and sentenced appellant u/s. 302 IPC - HC dismissed appellant's appeal thereafter - Hence, instant appeal.

Held, there is consistency between the statements of PW1 and PW3, who were present when deceased gave the oral dying declaration in the hospital, before he succumbed to the injuries. Merely because the deceased suffered 70 per cent burns, this does not raise an assumption that he could not have given the oral dying declaration. Thus, the HC was right in believing the oral dying declaration of the deceased as it did not suffer from any infirmity. Further, the appellant/accused in her statement, recorded u/s. 313 CrPC, has not given any explanation as to how the deceased was burnt and she even admits to be unaware of the name of the deceased. This is highly improbable and cast doubt on the innocence of the accused. She is unable to discharge the burden cast upon her by s. 106 of the Act, as it was within her special knowledge as to how the deceased came into the premises of her house. The ground of defense taken by the appellant, that she did not have any motive to kill the deceased, is ill founded and does not break the chain of circumstances. Further, with regard to the aspect of the witnesses, PW1 and PW3, who recorded the dying declaration, were neighbours of the accused and hence the Trial Court correctly held that they are not interested witnesses. The findings of the Trial Court also bring to light the fact that they had no animosity with the appellant, and were visiting her house only on the fateful night. Hence, the Trial Court and the HC have rightly analysed the evidence of these witnesses and the statements made in the dying declaration and held the accused guilty. Appeal dismissed.
