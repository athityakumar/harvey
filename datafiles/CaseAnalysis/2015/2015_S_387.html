Purushottam Dashrath Borate and another v State of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

08 May 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

H.L. Dattu, Sharad Arvind Bobde, Arun Mishra

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 338; (2015) 6 SCC 652; AIR 2015 SC 2170; 2015 ALL MR (Cri) 2421; 2015 CRLJ 2862; 2015 (2) MLJ(Crl) 747; 2015 (2) RCR(Criminal) 965; 2015 (3) RLW 2586; 2015(6) SCALE 204; [2015] 5 S.C.R. 1112

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Commutation, Subsequent Conduct, Antecedents, Aggravating

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, ss. 120B, 302, 376(2)(g), 364 and 404 - Gang Rape and Murder - Death Penalty - Justification - Deceased victim employee of company was using company cab for reaching office when gang rape was committed by accused appellant no.1 and 2 who were driver of can and employee of same company - Further, accused murdered her by means of strangulating her, slashing her wrist with a blade and smashing her head with stone. Further, accused-appellants stripped deceased of her possessions and money and then left her body in field - Trial Court convicted accused-appellants for offence u/ss. 302, 376(2)(g), 364 and 404 read with s. 120-B of IPC and consequently awarded death sentence which was confirmed by HC holding it to be 'rarest of rear case' - Hence, present appeal against death penalty is filed by appellant accused - Whether accused appellants were liable to death penalty taking into consideration mitigating circumstances.

Held, age alone cannot be paramount consideration as mitigating circumstance. Similarly, family background of accused also could not be said to be mitigating circumstance. Further, with respect to accused no.1 is concerned, it has been contended that he was happily married and his wife was pregnant at relevant time. However, accused No.1 did not take into consideration condition of his wife or his mother while committing said offence and, as result, his wife deserted him and his widowed mother is being looked after by his nephew and niece. Further, with respect to accused no.2, he has two sisters who are looking after his widowed mother. Lack of criminal antecedents also cannot be considered as mitigating circumstance, particularly taking into consideration, nature of heinous offence and cold and calculated manner in which it was committed by accused persons. SC held that accused no.1 abducted deceased with help of accused no.2, and subsequently they raped and murdered her. They did not show any regret, sorrow or repentance at any point of time during commission of heinous offence, nor thereafter, rather they acted in disturbingly normal manner after commission of crime. It has been established by strong and cogent evidence that after commission of gruesome crime, accused No.2 accompanied accused No.1 for second pick up and exited cab only prior to reaching gate of Company. Further, it has been brought on record that accused no.1 attempted to create false record of whereabouts of cab and cause of delay in arriving at workplace. In addition, it has been noticed that even though accused-appellants were seen by PW-12, that deceased repeatedly questioned them of unusual route, or that deceased was talking to friend on phone during journey, nothing deterred them from committing heinous offences. Hence, manner in which commission of offence was so meticulously and carefully planned coupled with sheer brutality and apathy for humanity in execution of offence, in every probability they have potency to commit similar offence in future. It is clear that both accused persons have been proved to be menace to society which strongly negates probability that they can be reformed or rehabilitated. SC opined that mitigating circumstances are wholly absent in present factual matrix and it is not case where offence was committed by accused persons under influence of extreme mental or emotional disorder, nor is it case where offence may be argued to be crime of passion or one committed at spur of moment. There is no question of accused persons believing that they were morally justified in committing offence on helpless and defenceless young woman. Hence, order of death penalty passed against appellant accused by lower Court is justified and in accordance with law. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - While awarding death penalty, Court should take in to consideration facts and given circumstances in each case, nature of crime, manner in which it was planned and committed, motive for commission of crime, conduct of accused and all other attending circumstances are relevant facts.
