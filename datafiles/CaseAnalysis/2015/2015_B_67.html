State of Maharashtra and others v Brijlal Sadasukh Modani

Supreme Court of India

15 December 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

Dipak Misra, Adarsh Kumar Goel

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 976; (2016) 4 SCC 417; AIR 2016 SC 1377; 2016 ALL MR (Cri) 1351; 2016 (2) JCC 799; 2016 (1) Law Herald (P&H) 238; 2016 (1) RCR(Criminal) 431; [2015] 10 S.C.R. 1158

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Companies Act, 1956, Dealing, Maharashtra Co-operative Societies Act, 1960, Madhya Pradesh Co-operative Societies Act, 1960, Multi-State Co-Operative Societies Act, 2002, Banking Regulation (Amendment) and Miscellaneous Provisions Act, 2004, Multi-State Cooperative Societies Act, 1984, Himachal Pradesh Cooperative Societies Act, 1968, Indian Penal Code, 1860, Constitution of India, 1950, Challenged, Quashing of notices

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, ss. 2(c) (ix), 2(c)(iii) - Banking Regulation Act, 1949 - Multi-State Co-operative Societies Act, 2002 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 12 - Quashing of notices - Challenged -Respondent was served with notices requiring him to give details of the property acquired by him - Respondent file petition before HC assailing the notice which were in the realm of investigation - High Court quashed the two notices issued by the Deputy Superintendent of Police, Anti-Corruption Bureau - Hence instant appeal by State - Whether a multi-State society which handles crores of rupees and persons who handle such huge amounts of money should be allowed to escape the rigour of corruption charges under the 1988 Act on the ground that they do not come under the ambit and sweep of art. 12 of the Constitution or solely because of construction placed u/s. 2(c) (ix) of the 1988 Act.

Held, State submitted in regard to the broad definitions of public servant u/s. 2(c)(iii) of the 1988 Act, the respondent should have been treated as a public servant under the purview of the 1988 Act and HC has fallen into grave error by treating him not to be one by misconstruing the provision. It is urged by him that if the statutory scheme of the 1949 Act and the 2002 Act is seen, it will be quite clear that he comes within the dictionary clause of the 1988 Act. On a perusal of various decisions of SC, it is manifest that stress has always been laid on s. 2(c)(ix) of the 1988 Act as a consequence of which the fall out is that the registered cooperative society must have received financial aid from the Central Government or the State Government or any other institution mentioned therein. HC has really been swayed by the concept of art. 12 of the Constitution, the provisions contained in the 1949 Act and in a mercurial manner taking note of the fact that the multi-state society is not controlled or aided by the Government has arrived at the conclusion. SC considered that, even any grant or any aid at the time of establishment of the society or in any construction or in any structural concept or any aspect would be an aid. SC is inclined to think so as the term 'aid' has not been defined. A sprinkle of aid to the society will also bring an employee within the definition of 'public servant'. The concept in entirety has to be understood in the backdrop of corruption. SC shares the said perception, and reiterate with agony. The ingemination has to be realized with sanctity. Therefore, SC is of the convinced opinion that it was entirely unnecessary on the part of the HC to enter into elaborate deliberation to arrive at the conclusion that the respondent was not a public servant. Regard being had to the facts of the case, it would be apposite that it is left to be dealt with in the course of Trial whether the society concerned has ever been granted any kind of aid or not. Hence, the judgment and order passed by HC is set aside and direct that the issue that whether the respondent is a public servant or not, shall be gone into during the trial. Appeal allowed.
