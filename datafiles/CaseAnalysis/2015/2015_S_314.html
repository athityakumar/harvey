S. T. Sadiq v State of Kerala and others

Supreme Court of India

04 February 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

R.F. Nariman, Ranjan Gogoi

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 77; (2015) 4 SCC 400; AIR 2015 SC 1306; JT 2015 (2) SC 161; 2015 (2) MLJ 92; 2015(2) SCALE 69; [2015] 3 S.C.R. 287

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Parliament & Legislature

Keywords: State Legislature, Legislative Power, Mysore Sales Tax (Amendment) Act, 1969, Constitutionality, Kerala Cashew Factories (Acquisition) Act, 1974, Kota Municipal Limits (Continued Existence) Validation Act, 1975, Kerala Cashew Factories Acquisition (Amendment) Act, 1995, Madras Entertainment Tax Act, 1939, Power to acquire any cashew factory

Summary: Constitution - Parliament & Legislature - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 32, 300A - Kerala Cashew Factories (Acquisition) Act, 1974, ss. 3A, 9, 3(1), 3A - Kerala Cashew Factories Acquisition (Amendment) Act, 1995, s. 6 - Land Acquisition Act, 1894, s. 5A - Power to acquire any cashew factory - Constitutionality - 1974 Act came into force on 19-11-1974 and s. 3 of 1974 Act enabled the State Govt. to acquire in public interest cashew factories under certain circumstances - 10 cashew factories were acquired under the Act and against that petitions filed before HC, which were dismissed by a common judgment and thereafter 36 factories approached SC u/art. 32 of Constitution which were disposed of by a judgment dt.12-5-1994 in Indian Nut Products v. Union of India, 1994 Indlaw SC 1820 - Petitioner filed instant petitions challenging the Constitutional validity of the 1974 Act, which was placed in the sch. 9 to the Constitution, being entry 148 thereof - Petitioner contended that s. 6 of the Amendment Act was bad as it seeks to directly nullify the SC judgments dt.12-5-1994 and 10-3-1995 without changing the basis of the law and that all the notices served were in identical terms, and considering that the objects and reasons of the 1995 Amendment Act placed all 46 factories at par, s. 6 of 1974 Act violated art. 14 of Constitution inasmuch as it discriminated between the 10 factories which were sought to be taken over and the 36 factories which were not sought to be taken over by the Amendment Act and that s. 9 of Amendment Act violated art. 300A of Constitution.

Held, State is correct in saying that no section of 1974 Act had been struck down and hence s. 6 of the Amendment Act did not need to remove the basis of any earlier decision striking down an Act. Court repeatedly asked State if action had been taken u/s. 3(1) or 3A of the Amendment Act to acquire any of the cashew factories before Court and answer was 'no'. The argument that s. 6 contains a third source of power to acquire cashew factories merely by putting them in a schedule has to be rejected as no notice or hearing is provided as in s. 3 of Amendment Act or s. 5A of 1894 Act or any other safeguard such as a resolution of the legislative assembly supporting such acquisition as in s. 3A of Amendment Act. If acquisition is to take place in conformity with law rules of natural justice cannot be bypassed. Further, s. 6 of Amendment Act is aimed only at directly upsetting a final judgment of a final Court. This is clear from non obstante clause wiping out 'any judgment' and the reference to the schedule of the Amendment Act which contains only the 10 cashew factories that were ordered to be handed back by a final judgment dt.10-3-1995 of SC. It is clear, therefore, that s. 6 of Amendment Act directly seeks to upset a final judgment inter-parties and is bad on this count and is thus declared unconstitutional. Statement of Objects of the Amendment Act shows that Kerala Legislature wished to interfere with two judgments of the Supreme Court making no distinction between factories that were managed by the Cashew Development Corporation (the 36 factories) and CAPEX (the 10 factories). It is interesting to note that apart from the Government suffering financially (if the factories are to be handed back), there will be large scale unemployment among workers in the cashew industry. It is clear that the objects and reasons for the Amendment Act makes no differentiation between the 36 factories handed back and the 10 factories taken over by the Amendment Act. The HC was in error in saying that there was an intelligible differentia between the two. Further, even otherwise, there is no difference between factories which post acquisition are run by the Cashew Development Corporation or CAPEX regard being had to the object sought to be achieved - namely to avoid unemployment of cashew workers. Whether 36 factories run by the Corporation are to be acquired or 10 factories run by CAPEX are to be acquired makes not the least difference to the object sought to be achieved. Large scale unemployment is there in both cases. And both the Corporation and CAPEX, along with the Govt., will suffer financially. In fact, the handing back of only 36 factories would be patently discriminatory as all 46 factories are similarly situate and have been treated as such by the State by issuing common notices to all of them u/s. 3 of 1974 Act. It is reliably informed that these 36 factories are functioning under their respective owners for the last 20 years. In the circumstances, there is no intelligible differentia between the 36 factories and the 10 factories taken over having any rational relation with the object sought to be achieved and on this ground also s. 6 of the Amendment Act deserves to be struck down as violating art. 14 of Constitution. Judgment of HC is set aside and it is ordered that the cashew factories and the land appurtenant thereto that have been taken over by the State under the Amending Act must be handed back within a period of 8 weeks. Appeals allowed.
