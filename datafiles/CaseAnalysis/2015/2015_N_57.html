Indian Oil Corporation Limited v Nilofer Siddiqui and others

Supreme Court of India

01 December 2015

Case Analysis

Bench

V. Gopala Gowda, Amitava Roy

Where Reported

2015 Indlaw SC 758; 2016 (1) ALD(SC) 87; 2015 (111) ALR 758; 2016 (115) ALR 512; 2016 (1) CLT(SC) 76; JT 2015 (11) SC 389; 2016 (1) MLJ 245; 2016 (1) RCR(Civil) 896; 2015(13) SCALE 313

Case Digest

Subject: Contract & Commercial

Summary: Contract & Commercial - Specific Relief Act, 1963, s. 14(1)(c) - Alleged violation of condition - Termination of Contract - Justification - Appellant Corporation allotted distributorship to respondent nos. 2 and 3 - Further, allotment letter was issued by appellant , - Respondent nos. 2 and 3 formed partnership to carry on business of distribution - Further, inspite of repeated reminders, appellant failed to provide standard agreement to respondent for signing - Further, after few months differences arose between partners i.e., respondent nos. 2 and 3 due to certain irregularities committed by respondent no.3, hence, partnership between them was dissolved - Further, respondent no.2 transferred his share in partnership in name of his wife which was not accepted by appellant Corporation - Appellant Corporation terminated distributorship granted to respondents - Trial court and appellant Court passed order against respondents on ground that respondent no.2 had no right to transfer his share in partnership in name of his wife i.e., respondent no.1 - However, HC vide impugned order reversed order passed by lower courts and held that termination of distributorship by appellant is illegal and gave direction for restoration of distributorship - Whether appellant Corporation had right to terminate distributorship of respondent nos. 2 and 3.

Held, it is undisputed fact that appellant had requested appellant Corporation twice to send Company's standard agreement for signature, but appellant Corporation failed to send it to them. Hence, Company's standard agreement was never executed by them and both respondent nos. 2 and 3 started their business without said standard agreement being signed by both of them. Partnership business continued to be regulated by terms and conditions of letter of allotment issued by Corporation Hence, claim of appellant that both respondent nos. 2 and 3 were aware of said standard agreement is unsusceptible in law. There is nothing on record to show that both respondent nos. 2 and 3 had any knowledge or had ever agreed to terms of said standard agreement. Hence, SC held that agreement which is not executed by parties cannot be legally made enforceable against them. Hence, HC has rightly held that standard agreement cannot be said to be legally binding upon respondent nos. 2 and 3 as same has never been executed between allottes and Corporation. Further, with respect to violation of clause of agreement by respondent, SC held that when standard agreement is not binding, then question of violation of terms and conditions does not arise. Further, SC held that HC has in impugned judgment and order has rightly held that provision u/s. 14(1)(c) of Act is not applicable to facts and circumstances of instant case. Hence, impugned order passed by HC is justified and in accordance with law. SC directed appellant to restore distributorship in favour of respondent nos. 1 or 2 and 3 and pay cost of Rs. 1 lakh be paid to respondent nos. 1 and 2. Appeal dismissed.

Ratio - Agreement which is not executed by parties cannot be legally made enforceable against them and same is not binding on parties.
