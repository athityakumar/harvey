Jethanand Betab v State of Delhi. (Now Delhi Administration)

Supreme Court of India

15 September 1959

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Subba Rao, Syed Jaffer Imam

Where Reported

1959 Indlaw SC 222; AIR 1960 SC 89; 1960 CRLJ 160; 1960 SCJ 68; [1960] 1 S.C.R. 755

Case Digest

Subject: Practice & Procedure; Telecom

Keywords: Repealing and Amending Act, 1952

Summary: Telecom - Practice & Procedure - Indian Wireless Telegraphy Act, 1933, ss. 6(1-A), 3, 6, 6-A - Repealing and Amending Act, 1952 - Indian Telegraph Act, 1885 - Indian Wireless Telegraphy (Amendment) Act, 1949 - Possession of wireless transmitter - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellant along with another, were prosecuted u/s. 6(1-A) of the 1933 Act for possessing a wireless transmitter in contravention of provisions of s. 3 of the Act, and was sentenced to 6 months RI - On appeal, Appellate Court upheld conviction but reduced sentence to period of imprisonment already undergone - On revision, HC confirmed both conviction and sentence passed by Trial Court - Hence, instant appeal - Whether order of Trial Court was sustainable - Held, the Act only gave power to control establishment, maintenance and working of wireless apparatus, in practice it was found that detection of unlicenced apparatus and successful prosecution of offenders were difficult, with result that State was losing revenue - To remove such defect, the 1933 Act was passed to prohibit possession without licence of a wireless apparatus - U/s. 6, of the 1933 Act penalty for such illegal possession of a wireless telegraphy apparatus was made an offence, but sentence prescribed was rather lenient - Subsequently, legislature thought that possession of a wireless transmitter was a graver offence, sometimes involving security of State, and so an amendment was introduced in 1949 constituting possession of such apparatus a graver offence and imposing a more severe punishment - Therefore, it could not be said that s. 6(1-A), inserted in the 1933 Act by amending the 1949 Act, was either covered by provisions of the 1885 Act, or a surplusage not serving any definite purpose - Even from history of legislation it was not possible to say that it disclosed an intention different from that envisaged in s. 6-A of the 1933 Act - Thus, s. 6 (1 -A) of the 1933 Act continued to be on statute book even after amending the 1949 Act was repealed by the 1952 Act, and that it was in force when offence was committed by appellant - Appeal dismissed.
