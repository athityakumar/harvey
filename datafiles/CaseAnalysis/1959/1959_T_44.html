 Prem Nath Kaul v State of Jammu and Kashmir

Supreme Court of India

02 March 1959

Case Analysis

Bench

Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Sudhi Ranjan Das, S.K. Das, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, Mohammad Hidayatullah

Where Reported

1959 Indlaw SC 135; AIR 1959 SC 749; [1959] 2 S.C.R. 270

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution

Keywords: Indian Independence Act, 1947, Delhi Laws Act, 1915

Summary: Land & Property - Indian Limitation Act, 1908 - Delhi Laws Act, 1915 - Constitution of India, 1950 - Govt. of India Act, 1935 - Indian Independence Act, 1947 - Jammu and Kashmir Big Landed Estate Abolition Act, XVII of 2007 - (A) Whether Yuvaraj Karan Singh had no authority to promulgate said Jammu and Kashmir Act as at time when Maharaja Hari Singh conveyed his powers to Yuvaraj Karan Singh by his proclamation of 20-06-1949, he was himself no more than a constitutional monarch and as such he could convey to Yuvaraj Karan Singh no higher powers? - Held, ss. 4 and 5 of this Act in terms continue to preserve all powers legislative, executive, judicial as well as right, prerogative of His Highnes just as much as s. 3 of Regn. 1 of 1991 - Powers conferred on executive and legislature, limited and qualified as they are, are made expressly subject to overriding powers of His Highness - Besides, there are specific provisions in Act which clearly emphasise preservation of said powers - It is true that by cl. 1 of Instrument of Accession His Highness conceded to authorities mentioned in said clause right to exercise in relation to his state such functions as may be vested in them by or under Govt. of India Act, 1935, as in force in said dominion on 15-08-1947, but this was subject to other terms of Instrument of Accession itself and cl. 6 of instrument clearly and expressly recognised continuance of sovereignty of His Highness in and over his state - (B) What was effect of proclamation issued by Maharaja Hari Singh in favour of Yuvaraj Karan Singh on 20-06-1949? - Further held, since Maharaja was himself an absolute monarch, there was no fetter or limitation on his power to appoint somebody else to exercise all or any of his powers - There was no authority or tribunal in State which could question his right or power to adopt such a course - Proclamation issued by Maharaja Hari Singh authorising Yuvaraj Karan Singh to exercise all his powers would clothe him with all such powers and he would be in same position as his father so long as proclamation stood - (C) Do provisions of art. 370(1) affect plenary powers of Maharaja in matter of governance of state? - Further held, it would not be permissible or legitimate to hold that, by implication, this article sought to impose limitations on plenary legislative powers of Maharaja - These powers had been recognised and specifically provided by Constitution Act of state itself and it was not, and could not have been, within contemplation, or competence of Constitution makers to impinge even indirectly on said powers - Appeal dismissed.
