Ram Dial v Sant Lal and Others

Supreme Court of India

23 April 1959

Case Analysis

Bench

Bhuvaneshwar Prasad Sinha, Mohammad Hidayatullah, J.L. Kapur

Where Reported

1959 Indlaw SC 74; AIR 1959 SC 855; 1959 (2) MLJ(SC) 99; [1959] 2 S.C.R. 748

Case Digest

Subject: Election; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Freedom Of Speech And Expression

Summary: Elections - Practice & Procedure - Representation of People Act, 1951, s. 123(2) - Corrupt Practices Prevention Act, 1854, s. 2 - (A) Whether difference lies in law of undue influence at elections between English law and Indian law? - Held, law in England, relating to undue influence at elections, is not same as law in India contained in s. 123, cl. 2, Representation of People Act, 1951 - Words of definition of English statute lay emphasis upon individual aspect of exercise of undue influence - Indian law, on other hand, does not emphasize individual aspect of exercise of such influence, but pays regard to use of such influence as has tendency to bring about result contemplated in clause - Material part under Indian law, is not actual effect produced, but doing of such acts as are calculated to interfere with free exercise of any electoral - Decisions of English Courts, based on words of English statute, which are not strictly in pari materia with words of Indian statute, cannot, therefore, be used as precedents in this country - (B) Whether s. 123, cl. 2, proviso (a)(ii) of Representation of People Act, 1951 attracts disobedience which carries divine displeasure on part of religious leader issuing hukam or farman and making speeches canvassing votes for candidate? - Further held, religious leader has a right to exercise his influence in favour of any particular candidate by voting for him and by canvassing votes of others for him, but such a course of conduct on his part, will only be a use of his great influence amongst a particular section of voters in constituency; but it will amount to an abuse of his great influence if words he uses in a document, or utters in his speeches, leave no choice to persons addressed by him, in exercise of their electoral right - Disobedience of his mandate would carry divine displeasure or spiritual censure, case is clearly brought within purview of second paragraph of proviso to s. 123, cl. 2 of Act - Appeal dismissed.
