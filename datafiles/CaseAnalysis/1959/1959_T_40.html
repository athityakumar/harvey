Anant Chintaman Lagu v State of Bombay

Supreme Court of India

14 December 1959

Case Analysis

Bench

Mohammad Hidayatullah, S.K. Das, A.K. Sarkar

Where Reported

1959 Indlaw SC 124; AIR 1960 SC 500; 1960 (62) BomLR 371; 1960 CRLJ 682; 1960 SCJ 779; [1960] 2 S.C.R. 460

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Subsequent Conduct, Poisoning, misa

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 302 - Murder - Conviction - Circumstantial Evidence - Sustainability - Appellant allegedly murdered X, by administering poison and charges were framed against him before Trial Court which convicted him u/s. 302 IPC - Aggrieved appellant filed appeal before HC which affirmed Trial Court's order - Hence, instant appeal - Whether lower Court's order was justifiable - Held, first, appellant had design during X's lifetime to misappropriate her properties which supplies motive for causing her death but did not prove that death which occurred, was unnatural death - Secondly, appellant did not give to hospital correct name of X name given however was not such as from it her identity could never have been discovered - Thirdly, appellant gave his own address instead of that X- It seems to Court that that was natural thing for him to have done in the circumstances of case for there would had been no one in X's flat to receive her letters and there was no other address which appellant could have given - Further, address given necessarily connected appellant with last hours of X's life conduct not very probable in person who had brought about her death - Therefore, that address was given only to ensure that communications from hospital concerning dead X should reach appellant was not very plausible - It was clear that if appellant had not given his own address, only other address he could possibly had given would have been X's address - Court was unable to appreciate what communication hospital could have sent to X at her address after her death or when she lay in hospital - In any event, appellant would have had no difficulty in getting hold of any such communication sent to X's own address - Fourthly, appellant told Doctor that X had hysterical fit- It was doubtful whether he said so, and also whether, if he did, it was purposefully false - What purpose it served was not clear - Appellant did not mention hysterical fit to doctor in charge of treatment nor did he do anything to induce her to take different line of treatment from that which she had adopted - Appellant did nothing to induce any idea in her mind as to cause of illness or disease - In these circumstances it did not seem possible to hold that hysterical fit had been mentioned by appellant to prevent detection of fact that X had been poisoned - Lastly, series of appellant's acts from immediately after X's death indicating his intention to acquire her properties and acquisition thereof by deception and forgery - These could not prove that X died unnatural death - Considering them all together - Hence, prosecution had failed to prove guilt of appellant, but accordance with opinion of majority - Appeal dismissed.
