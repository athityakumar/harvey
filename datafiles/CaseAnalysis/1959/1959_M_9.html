 Mahant Ram Saroop Dasji v S. P. Sahi, Special Officer-In-Charge of The Hindu

Supreme Court of India

15 April 1959

Case Analysis

Bench

S.K. Das, Sudhi Ranjan Das, Pralhad Balacharya Gajendragadkar, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, Mohammad Hidayatullah

Where Reported

1959 Indlaw SC 69; AIR 1959 SC 951; [1959] 2 S.C.R. 583

Case Digest

Subject: Trusts & Associations

Keywords: Charitable and Religious Trusts Act, 1920, Religious Endowments Act, 1863, Orissa Hindu Religious Endowments Act, 1939, Charitable Endowments Act, 1890, Orissa Hindu Religious Endowments Act

Summary: Civil Procedure - Family & Personal - Land & Property - Practice & Procedure - Trusts & Associations - Bihar Hindu Religious Trusts Act, 1951, ss. 2(1), 43 and 59 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 226 - (A) Whether Bihar Hindu Religious Trusts Act, s. 2, cl. 1 includes public as well as private trusts? - Held, definition of 'religious trust' in s. 2, cl. 1 does not include private trusts - Though definition in s.2 cl.1 of Bihar Hindu Religious Trusts Act, 1951, is expressed in somewhat wide language, other provisions of Act and particularly sub-section (5) of s. 4 make clear true scope and effect of definition clause namely, that definition clause does not include within its ambit private religious trusts - Thus Act and its provisions do not apply to private religious trusts - View that in as much as definition of 'religious trust' in s. 2, cl. 1 of Bihar Hindu Religious Trusts Act, 1951 mentions by way of an exception only one instance of a private endowment, all private endowments created otherwise than for worship of a family idol must be include within definition on maxim of expression unius exclusio alterius is not quite correct - If definition clause was intended to include within its ambit private trusts (other than those created for worship of a family idol), then it is difficult to understand why cl. 5 of s. 4 should be worded as it has been done - If private trusts created otherwise than for worship of a family idol were included in definition of religious trust, then cl. 5 was entirely otiose or redundant so far as those private trusts were concerned for earlier enactments never applied to them - Covers only public trusts - (B) What is distinction between public and private religious endowments under Hindu law? - Further held, there is distinction between both and essential is that in a public trust beneficial interest is vested in an uncertain and fluctuating body of persons, either public in a large or some considerable portion of it answering a particular description; in a private trust beneficiaries are definite and ascertained individuals or who within a definite time can be definitely ascertained - Appeal allowed.
