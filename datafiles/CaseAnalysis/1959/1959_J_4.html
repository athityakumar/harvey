Jnanendra Nath Ghose v State of West Bengal

Supreme Court of India

08 May 1959

Case Analysis

Bench

Syed Jaffer Imam, J.L. Kapur

Where Reported

1959 Indlaw SC 103; AIR 1959 SC 1199; 1959 CRLJ 1492; [1960] 1 S.C.R. 126

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal

Keywords: Approver, Misdirection

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Evidence Act, 1872, s. 133 - Criminal Procedure Code, 1898, ss. 297, 337 and 423 cl. 2 - (A) When can High Court interfere with verdict of jury? - Held, unless there was a misdirection or non-direction amounting to a misdirection in charge to jury which, in fact, had occasioned a failure of justice, jury's verdict must prevail and it cannot be interfered with - HC was of opinion that there was no misdirection in sessions judge's charge to jury and Supreme Court is in agreement with HC in present case - (B) Whether approver is a reliable witness considering his evidence? - Further held, approver's evidence must show that he is a reliable witness and that is test which is common to all witnesses - Where nothing is shown in case that apart from approver's testimony being regarded as tainted evidence his evidence as it stood was in any way unreliable charge to jury is not vitiated - (C) Whether approver's evidence alone can determine crime committed by accused? - Further held, there should be corroboration in material particulars not only concerning crime but corroboration of approver's story by evidence which connects or tends to connect an accused with crime - It is this corroborative evidence which determines mind of court or a jury that approver's evidence that accused committed crime is true as per CrPC, 1898, ss. 297 and 337 - Appeal dismissed.
