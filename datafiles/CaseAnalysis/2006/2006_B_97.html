Bombay Dyeing & Mfg. Co. Ltd v Bombay Environmental Action Group & Ors

Supreme Court of India

07 March 2006

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, P.P. Naolekar

Where Reported

2006 Indlaw SC 1611; (2006) 3 SCC 434; AIR 2006 SC 1489; 2006 (3) Bom.C.R. 260; JT 2006 (3) SC 235; 2006(3) SCALE 1; [2006] 2 S.C.R. 902

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Constitution; Environment, Wildlife & Animal; Land & Property

Keywords: Town Planning, Sick Textile Undertakings (Nationalisation) Act, 1974, Visva-Bharati Act, 1951, Bombay Industrial Relations act, Development Control Regulations, 1991, Development Control Rules, 1967, Textile Undertakings (Nationalisation) Act, 1995, Bombay Town Planning Act, 1915, Parliament Act, Legislative Act, BIFR Scheme, Integrated Development Scheme

Summary: (A) Environment, Wildlife & Animal - Textiles - Constitution - Maharashtra Regional and Town Planning Act, 1996, s. 37 - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 21 -Respondent trust filed Public Interest Litigation (PIL) before HC questioning the validity of Development Control Regulation No. 58 (DCR 58) framed by the State in terms of 1966 Act for the reconstruction of textile industries in the State - Respondent contended that the State should ensure open spaces for the city and to provide space for public housing - HC held that: (i) clarification dated 28-3-2003 issued by the State pursuant to DCR 58 seeking regularization of residential user was in violation of s. 37 of 1996 Act and art. 21 of the Constitution; (ii) various constructions carried out were in violation of Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) Notification as amended on 7-7-2004; (iii) sales of various textile mills carried out by National Textile Corporation (NTC) were contrary to various SC orders and sanctioned schemes of Board for Industrial and Financial Reconstruction (BIFR) - What was the scope of PIL in the instant matter - Held, while entertaining a PIL with several aspects of public interest being involved, the Court should find out as to how greater public interest should be subserved and for the said purpose a balance should be struck and harmony should be maintained between several interests such as (a) consideration of ecology; (b) interest of workers (c) interest of public sector institution, other financial institutions, priority claimed due to workers; (d) advancement of public interest in general and not only a particular aspect of public interest; (e) interest and rights of owners; (f) the interest of a sick and closed industry; and (g) schemes framed by BIFR for revival of the company - Courts in doing so would have to take into consideration a large number of factors, some of which may be found to be competing with each other - It would not be proper to give undue importance to one at the cost of the other which may ultimately be found to be vital and give effect to the intent and purport for which the legislation was made - PIL, thus, was entertained more frequently where a question of violation of the provisions of the statutes governing the environmental or ecology of the country had been brought to its notice in the matter of depletion of forest areas and/ or when the executive while exercising its administrative functions or making subordinate legislations had interfered with the ecological balance with impunity - HC, therefore, could not be faulted with for entertaining the writ petition as a PIL - Appeal allowed.

(B) Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Maharashtra Regional and Town Planning Act, 1996, ss. 22, 37 - What was the criteria for interpreting the DCR 58 - Held, a planning authority should take into consideration all the relevant factors, although in a given case, one got priority over the other - In the instant case, both open space as also the other factors relevant for making the regulation would be in public interest - DCR 58 should be interpreted having regard to the provisions of the 1996 Act which professed increase in the ecological interest by providing more open space and not decreasing the same - Amendments in the regulation should be construed in furtherance of the legislative policy and not in derogation thereof but, while doing so, the past experience of the State which paved the necessities for modifying the earlier regulation should not be forgotten - A statutory scheme also by way of s. 22 clearly spoken about open spaces - Legislative Act conferred guidelines which advocated the necessity of EIA - State, when it exercised its power u/s. 37 of the 1996 Act was required to act within the four-corners of the Act - Any modification or amendment should address the environmental consequences together with other relevant factors - However, literal interpretation of the Act and the Rules would give rise to many anomalies and would not advance the object and purport of the Act and would also create difficulties in implementing the statutory scheme - Hence, DCR 58 should be interpreted by taking recourse to the principles of purposive construction and interpret the same in accordance with the scope and object of the 1996 Act - Appeal allowed.

(C) Constitution - Administrative - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 14, 21 - Legislative policy - Scope of judicial review - Held, parameters of judicial review in relation to a policy decision would depend upon the nature as also the scope and object of the legislation - No hard and fast rule could be laid down thereof - Court normally would not, however, interfere with a policy decision which had been made by experts in view of the fact that it did not possess such expertise - However, scope and extent of judicial review of legislation would vary from case to case - Further, interpretation of a town planning statute which had an environmental aspect leading to application of arts. 14 and 21 of the Constitution of India should not be held to be within the exclusive domain of the executive - Hence, in cases where constitutionality and/ or interpretation of any legislation, be it made by the Parliament or an executive authority by way of delegated legislation, was in question, it would be idle to contend that a court of superior jurisdiction should not exercise the power of judicial review - Appeal allowed.

(D) Land & Property - Administrative - Practice & Procedure - DCR 58 - 'open land' - Meaning of - Held, both the phrases 'open lands' as also 'balance FSI' contained in DCR 58(1)(b) played significant role wherein the word 'balance' was crucial which would naturally mean FSI available to be utilized upon open land - Built-up area had consumed some FSI and, thus, when the expression 'balance FSI' was used, the same would mean additional built-up area - It was true that DCR 58(1) used the word entire land but the said expression was followed by the expression 'built-up area' - 'Balance FSI' in that situation would not mean the FSI which was involved for the purpose of construction of structures not only on the open land which had been existing but also the land which had become open by reason of the demolition of the existing structures - If open land was given its natural or dictionary meaning, no distinction could be made in between DCR 58(1)(a) and DCR 58(1)(b), which ex facie would lead to an anomaly - No necessity was there for the State to use two different words 'open land' and 'built-up area' separately and distinctly -Words 'built-up area' found its source from the definition of existing building - Existing built-up area was not to be shared and the same if read with the word 'existing', it should be contrasted with a built-up area additionally but separate and distinct from the old existing built-up area - Existing built-up area, thus, was sought to be protected which would mean that they were sought to be protected from non-shareable land component thereof - It was thus possible to come to the conclusion that the obligation to share was intended to be absent only so long as no additional built-up area was created - Appeal allowed.

(E) Land & Property - Administrative - Constitution - Constitution of India, 1950 - Maharashtra Regional and Town Planning Act, 1996, ss. 28(2), 37 - Whether DCR 58 was constitutionally valid - Held, it could not be said that before making DCR 58 in the year 2001, it was obligatory on the part of the State to accept in total the recommendations made by the Expert Committees who had undertaken certain exercises; the equities should have been adjusted and the provisions of the pollution laws including the provisions of s. 28(2) of the 1996 Act should have been considered - In the instant case, the State appointed two committees, however, both the committees did not consider the environmental aspect of the matter - Amendment to DCR 58 was carried out 10 years after the original DCR 58 was introduced - Before doing so, due consultative process as laid down in s. 37 of the 1996 Act which involved suggestions and objections from public and the concerned statutory authorities was taken recourse to - Consideration of the same by Dy. Director of Town Planning and thereafter promulgation of the same in the form of direct regulation established that the same was not ex facie arbitrary in nature, particularly when most of the suggestions of the said Committees were accepted - It should be borne in mind while interpreting DCR 58 that there existed a stark distinction between the interpretation of planning and zoning statutes enforcing ecology vis-a-vis industrial effluents and hazardous industries and those relating to concerted efforts at rehabilitating the industry - Environmental aspect considered in DCR 58 would not be to everybody's satisfaction but the regulation in question was required to be interpreted having regard to the purport and object for which the same was enacted, meaning thereby, a holistic approach to a large number of problems - DCR 58 was made in a special situation, sought to strike a balance between different public interests - DCR 58 should not be struck down solely on the ground that the interest of the common citizen (from the ecological point of view) had been affected, unless its actions were considered to be unfair - Sale of lands belonging to mills which were absolutely unviable and/or those which were lying closed for one reason or the other as also those who intended to modernize their mills and/or shifting the same and/ or part of it should be kept for consideration in the matter of interpretation of DCR 58 - Hence DCR 58 was constitutionally valid - Appeal allowed.

(F) Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Maharashtra Regional and Town Planning Act, 1996 - Whether clarification dated 28-3-2003 issued by the State was legal - Held, clarification was issued having regard to a letter of Municipal Corporation of Greater Mumbai (MCGM) dated 28-8-2001 to the Urban Development Department stating as to how it understood DCR 58 of 2001 which was confirmed by the Urban Development Department - Although at one point of time they interpreted DCR in the same manner as that of the State; only much later they raised a doubt which was bona fide - Only with a view to clear the air of doubt, the clarification was issued by the State in 2003 - If some mill owners claimed the right to change of user under sub-regulation (6) alone, the same would be in the teeth of the interpretation of DCR 58 - It could not be said that by taking recourse to the said power of clarification the State had improperly exercised its power - Reference to resolution dated 27-8-2003 passed by MCGM, did not have the effect of clarification being set at naught for DCR 58 - Letter dated 24-7-2003 issued by the Chief Executive Officer of Maharashtra Housing and Area Development Authority (MHADA) to the Housing Board or the State Government also did not talk about the incorrectness or otherwise of the clarification issued by the State but as regards the effect of DCR of 2001 - Thus clarification issued on 28-3-2003 was proper and legal - Appeal allowed.

(G) Administrative - Practice & Procedure - Maharashtra Regional and Town Planning Act, 1996 - Whether the sales of various textile mills carried out by National Textile Corporation (NTC) were contrary to various SC orders and sanctioned Board for Industrial and Financial Reconstruction (BIFR) schemes - Held, while passing the order dated 11-5-2005, SC merely noted the terms of the BIFR scheme, however, it did not issue any direction to the effect that the sale of the mill land should be effected strictly in terms thereof or in a particular manner - BIFR scheme evidently was referred to as SC noticed that even statutory authorities constituted under a Parliamentary Act found it necessary to direct sale of the mill lands in public interest - While considering a writ petition on an environmental issue, the focus of the court should have been confined thereto and it was impermissible for the HC to examine the BIFR scheme as if the environmental issues were considered therein - BIFR exercised its jurisdiction under a statute; the objects whereof were distinct and different from a town planning scheme - BIFR was not a town planner nor a development authority - BIFR had nothing to do with the town planning or development scheme or maintenance of ecological balance - BIFR was concerned only with the manner in which sick industrial undertaking should be made to revive - BIFR was not concerned with the interpretation of DCR 58 and, thus, only because SC in its orders dated 27-9-2002 and 11-5-2005 had referred thereto, the same would not mean that thereby any direction was issued either directly or indirectly that the sale of the lands pertaining to cotton textile mills should strictly be conducted in accordance with the said scheme - SC in its order dated 11-5-2005 categorically observed that if the transactions in respect of mills were not allowed to be completed, the scheme framed by the BIFR would come to a standstill resulting in accrual of liability of a huge amount by way of interest payable by NTC to the financial institutions besides other hardships which may be caused to various other persons including the workers - Scheme framed by the BIFR, therefore, was taken to be a relevant factor only for the purpose of determining the issues involved in the appeal which arose out of an interim order - Validity or otherwise of the transaction of sales of seven mills of NTC were, thus, not open to a further determination by the HC - BIFR scheme did not postulate that the surrender of lands to MCGM and MHADA should be out of the lands of each individual mill itself and not out of the lands of some other mills - BIFR had no occasion to say so nor could it do so having regard to the provisions contained in DCR 58 - Respondents had nowhere denied or disputed that the seven mills which were put up for sale were unviable ones - Lands pertaining to the mills were found to be surplus - For the purpose of giving effect to the scheme framed by BIFR, indisputably an Asset Sale Committee (ASC) was constituted to discharge the functions of overseeing the sale of surplus assets of the said mills - Further, an Integrated Development Scheme was framed by NTC with the assistance of the architects which was submitted to MCGM and the same was duly approved - Sanction of sale of two mills out of seven mills was not granted evidently in view of the pendency of the writ petition - BIFR scheme or the said Integrated Development Scheme framed by NTC was not in question in the writ petition - It was not in dispute that the writ petitioners merely filed an affidavit on 12-7-2005 before the HC alleging that the sale of surplus land by NTC was in violation of SC's order and/ or the scheme framed by the BIFR - If the prayer in the writ petition had not been amended, it was beyond comprehension on what premise the HC proceeded to consider the question as regards the alleged violation of the order of SC, as also the BIFR Scheme by NTC for the purpose of setting aside the sale - In a collateral proceeding, the HC, could not issue any direction which would not only be contrary to a statutory scheme but defensive of the purport and object for which Sick Industrial Companies (Special Provisions) Act, 1985 was enacted - Further, it was equally impermissible for the HC to consider as to whether despite there being a provision for multi-mill aggregation in terms of DCR 2001, the same had been taken into consideration under BIFR Scheme or not - For the purpose of considering the validity or otherwise of the sale in terms of BIFR Scheme itself, ASC was appointed wherein a member of the BIFR was also represented - Hence, impugned judgment of the HC in that behalf was not correct - Appeal allowed.
