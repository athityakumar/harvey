Ashok Lenka v Rishi Dikshit & Ors

Supreme Court of India

21 April 2006

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, P.K. Balasubramanyan

Where Reported

2006 Indlaw SC 1615; (2006) 9 SCC 90; AIR 2006 SC 2382; 2006(4) SCALE 519; [2006] Supp1 S.C.R. 142

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Excise

Keywords: Chhattisgarh Excise Settlement of Licences for Retail Sale of Country/ Foreign Liquor Rules, 2002

Summary: Administrative - Excise - Chhattisgarh Excise Act, 1915 - Chhattisgarh Excise Settlement of Licences for Retail Sale of Country/ Foreign Liquor Rules, 2002, rr. 9, 11 - State issued notices inviting tenders for grant of licenses under the Rules - Appellants filed applications and were granted licenses - Grant of licenses in favour of appellants were challenged before the HC - HC allowed the writ petitions and directed to cancel the licenses to appellants - Appellants filed appeal before SC wherein SC directed the State authorities to scrutinize the applications afresh - District Level Committee (DLC) , in terms of SC direction, scrutinized the applications of applicants and found that the respondents were not only eligible therefore but also fulfilled other conditions laid down in the Rules - Respondents filed objections and that was rejected by authorities - Respondents challenged the rejection order before the HC - HC held that official respondent nos. 1 to 12 had failed to carry out the directions issued by the SC in the manner expected of them and in conformity with the mandatory rr. 9 and 11 of the Rules - HC held that appellants were the beneficiaries of illegal grant of licenses to them by the authorities, hence their licenses came to an end - Whether respondent authorities did the scrutiny exercise in conformity with the Rules - Held, appellants produced a chart showing the mode and manner in which the directions of SC had been purportedly complied with - Chart revealed that there were several cases where only residence certificate of some incompetent person was produced in support of residence and certificate by a doctor in support of age were produced as proof without producing any other document to prove those two facts - Thus, there was no proper proof of residence or age produced by applicants - Record of scrutiny produced by the State showed that, those applicants who had submitted improper documents were not even asked by the State at the scrutiny, to submit proper documents such as residence certificate by Revenue Authority, etc. or proper proof of age such as School Certificate or Birth Certificate, etc. - Before the HC, it had been conceded that the residential certificates issued by the Councillors of Municipal Council or Sapranches or other persons who had no legal authority and/ or otherwise incompetent to issue the same, had been taken into consideration - Applicants, however, could procure such certificates from the competent authorities of the respective States where the applicant was a resident and who hold similar status in their State, particularly, when it was one of the eligible criteria - HC had also noticed that the certificates relating to age had been issued by Dental Surgeons and Orthopaedic Surgeons - HC furthermore noticed the report of the Station House Officer mentioning about one licensee that he was an employee of liquor contractor and financial condition of that licensee was not such that he could take the financial burden of liquor trade, however, in spite of it, liquor licence was granted to him - Further, HC in paragraph 22 of its judgment found that the official respondents had failed to carry out the directions issued by SC in the manner expected of them and in conformity with the mandatory rr. 9 and 11 of the Rules - HC in paragraph 23 also found that the burden was wrongly shifted on objectors to prove the negative facts by evidence which was a serious flaw in enquiry - In paragraph 24, it also took notice of a chart and found that vague and incomplete address such as assembly constituency, had been accepted by the authorities - Findings of the HC established that the licenses might have been issued in fake names and to the ineligible applicants - Some of the applicants were facing criminal trials, veracity whereof was not established - Character certificates were required to be issued by the respective Superintendents of Police in respect of the candidates concerned - If applicants had been residing at different places at different points of time, such character certificates were required to be issued by the Superintendent of Police of each such place - Terms and conditions of the advertisement did not mandate that a person residing outside the State of Chhattisgarh would be ineligible for filing an application - If that be so, then those who were residents of places other than the State of Chhattisgarh would also be eligible therefore - Such applicants may have a temporary address in Chhattisgarh or they may not have a permanent address within the said State but by way of temporary address, they should not give an address which was not their residence even for temporary purpose - Even if they had no temporary address but they had been able to file their character certificates and proof of permanent address, they should not be held to be ineligible for the grant of licenses - Further, there was nothing to indicate that the DLC had information as to whether the applicants were defaulters in respect of some other State - Such mechanism of scrutiny was not available in the statutory scheme and should be provided - Hence, appropriate steps should be taken to make the position clear that if the applicants were not residents of the State either on a temporary or permanent basis, they would not be shown to be residents of such places wherefrom their antecedents should not be verified - Such applicants, therefore, should not be allowed to furnish an address only for the purpose of communication and if so, the same should clearly be stated in the application - In the Act or the Rules, again no particular authority had been mentioned as having been authorized to issue certificate - No particular method of verification of proof of address and identity had also been shown to exist - It would be appropriate if a clarification was issued in that behalf - Scrutiny Committee appeared to have relied upon certificates issued by Tahsildars, Municipal Counsellors, etc. which had not been objected to by the respondents - It was stated that most of the applicants were income tax payees and they have been granted a PAN Card - In future, however, the authorities should insist that the applicants must enclose Xeroxed copies of their PAN Cards along with their applications - Direction issued to the State and its authorities would not commit the same mistakes in the coming years keeping in mind the mandate of art. 47 of the Constitution of India and scrupulously observe the rules for disposal of liquor shops - Appeals allowed.
