 Sunrise Associates v Government of Nct of Delhi and Others

Supreme Court of India

28 April 2006

Case Analysis

Bench

Ruma Pal, B.N. Srikrishna, S.H. Kapadia, Tarun Chatterjee, P.P. Naolekar

Where Reported

2006 Indlaw SC 613; (2006) 5 SCC 603; AIR 2006 SC 1908; 2006 (5) ALD(SC) 51; 2006 (129) DLT 719; 2007 (1) JhrCR(SC) 110; JT 2006 (5) SC 168; 2006 (3) RLW 2129; 2006(5) SCALE 1; 2006 (4) SCJ 341; 2006 (145) STC 576; [2006] Supp1 S.C.R. 421

Case Digest

Subject: Indirect Tax; Sales Tax

Keywords: Madras General Sales Tax Act, 1939, Transfer of Property (Amendment) Act, 2003

Summary: Sales Tax - Constitution of India, 1950, art. 366 (29 A)(a) - Delhi Sales Tax Act, 1975 - Madras General Sales Tax Act, 1939 - Indian Sale of Goods Act, 1930 - Tamil Nadu General Sales Tax Act, 1959 - Transfer of Property Act, 1882 - Karnataka Sales Tax Act, 1957 - Central Sales Tax Act, 1956 - Bombay Sales Tax Act, 1959 - Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881 - Whether lottery tickets are goods for purposes of art. 366 (29 A)(a) of Constitution and State Sales Tax Laws? - Held, a lottery ticket is a mere piece of paper - Its value lies in fact that it represents a chance or a right to a conditional benefit of winning a prize of a greater value than consideration paid for transfer of that chance - It is nothing more than a token or evidence of this right - In H Anraj v. Govt. of Tamil Nadu, held that a lottery ticket is a slip of paper or memorandum evidencing transfer of certain rights - The sale of a ticket does not necessarily involve sale of goods - Tickets are themselves, normally evidence of and in some cases contract between buyer of ticket and its seller - Therefore a lottery ticket can be held to be goods if at all only because it evidences transfer of a right - Decision in H Anraj incorrectly held that a sale of a lottery ticket involved a sale of goods - There was no sale of goods within meaning of Sales Tax Acts of different States but at highest a transfer of an actionable claim - Appeal disposed of.
