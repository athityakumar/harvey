West Bengal State Electricity Board v Dilip Kumar Ray

Supreme Court of India

24 November 2006

Case Analysis

Bench

Arijit Pasayat, Lokeshwar Singh Panta

Where Reported

2006 Indlaw SC 1454; (2007) 14 SCC 568; AIR 2007 SC 976; 2007 (7) AWC 6794; 2006(12) SCALE 559; 2007 (2) SLR 814; 2007 (2) SLR 815; [2006] Supp9 S.C.R. 554

Case Digest

Subject: Service; Tort

Keywords: Wrongful, Malice, Legal Process, Legal Practitioners Act, 1879, Malicious Prosecution

Summary: Service - Tort - Disciplinary proceedings - Malicious prosecution - Damages - Entitlement - Appellant/Board initiated disciplinary proceedings against 1st respondent/Superintending Engineer for misconduct - Enquiry Officer held that charges against the 1st respondent were partly proved - 1st respondent challenged the propriety of enquiry before the HC - HC held that enquiry proceedings were vitiated because the 1st respondent was not given reasonable opportunity of being heard and he was not allowed to inspect some of the vital documents which prejudiced his defence - HC, consequently, directed the appellant/Board to revoke the suspension of 1st respondent and to reckon his suspension period as that of his duty for the purpose of pension - 1st respondent superannuated - 1st respondent, subsequently, filed a civil suit against appellant/Board claiming damages for the institution of disciplinary proceedings with mala fide intention and publication of some news items defaming him - Trial Court decreed the suit in favour of 1st respondent and awarded damages to 1st respondent for harassment and for loss of reputation, however, Trial Court held that allegation of defamation against appellant/Board could not be sustained - Trial Court, further, observed that the truth could be revealed only through a high power enquiry, however, it was not competent to direct such enquiry - Appellant filed appeal - HC dismissed the appeal - Hence the present appeal challenging the Trial Court and HC orders - Whether the impugned orders were based on conjunctures and surmises - Held, averments in the plaint were extremely vague, lacking in details - Findings of the Trial Court, that the appellant/Board alone was responsible for the mala fide prosecution of 1st respondent because it was not established that any individual officer was responsible for it and dispute only had been revealed by the high power enquiry which the court was incompetent to direct, is clearly indefensible - HC took a confused view of the matter and failed to notice that the Trial Court itself had held 'it was highly probable' that the 1st respondent was suspended for extraneous reasons - Such conclusion of the Trial Court was based on surmises and conjectures - Further, HC noted that the Trial Court itself had held that the 1st respondent was not entitled to damages for defamation, however, while affirming the judgment and decree of the Trial Court, it held that the damages granted for harassment must be read as damages for malicious prosecution causing harassment - All conclusions of the HC, in short, are confusing, contradictory and do not convey any sense, therefore, impugned Trial Court and HC orders are set aside - Appeal allowed.
