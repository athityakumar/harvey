Lt. Governor, Nct & Ors v Ved Prakash @ Vedu

Supreme Court of India

05 May 2006

Case Analysis

Bench

S.B. Sinha, P.K. Balasubramanyan

Where Reported

2006 Indlaw SC 1511; (2006) 5 SCC 228; (2006) 2 SCC (Cr) 449; 2006 (2) JCC 758; JT 2006 (5) SC 425; 2006 (3) RCR(Criminal) 54; 2006(5) SCALE 250; [2006] Supp1 S.C.R. 927

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Delhi Police Act, 1978, s. 47 - Externment - Externment proceeding was initiated against the respondent in terms of s. 47 of the Act on the ground that his movements and acts had been causing alarm, danger and harm to person and property - Order of externment was passed against the respondent directing his removal beyond the limits of the National Capital Territory of Delhi for a period of two years - HC held that in view of the fact that in the show cause notice in regard to the criminal cases the names of the witnesses who were said to be reluctant to or did not come forward to depose against the respondent on account of fear, had not been disclosed, the principles of natural justice have been violated - Instant appeal - (A) Whether HC was correct in coming to the finding that the appellant No. 3 was bound to disclose the cases in which the witnesses had not deposed against the respondent out of fear or because of threat, etc? - Held, executive order must demonstrate due application of mind on the part of the statutory authority - When the validity of an order was questioned, what would be seen was the material on which the satisfaction of the authority was based - The satisfaction of the authority although primarily subjective, should be based on objectivity; but sufficiency of material as such may not be gone into by the writ court unless it was found that in passing the impugned order the authority failed to take into consideration the relevant facts or had based its decision on irrelevant factors not germane therefore - Mere possibility of another view may not be a ground for interference - In the instant case, HC itself held that the allegations made in the notice satisfy the statutory requirement but, the HC was not correct in coming to the finding that the appellant No. 3 was bound to disclose the cases in which the witnesses had not deposed against the respondent out of fear or because of threat, etc - If an attempt was made to communicate the cases in which witnesses were not forthcoming due to the activities of the proceedee, the same would violate the secrecy required to be maintained and would otherwise defeat the purpose for which s. 47 of the Act had been enacted - (B) Whether the principles of natural justice was violated? - Held, while interpreting the provisions of a statute like the instant one and in view of the precedents operating in the field, the court may examine the records itself so as to satisfy its conscience not only for the purpose that the procedural safeguards available to the proceedee have been provided but also for the purpose that the witnesses have disclosed their apprehension about deposing in court truthfully and fearlessly because of the activities of the proceedee - Once such a satisfaction was arrived at, the superior court would not interfere with an order of externment - The court would not direct the authorities to either disclose the names of the witnesses or the number of cases where such witnesses were examined for the simple reason that they may lead to causing of further harm to them - Thus, the purpose of maintaining the secrecy as regards identity of such persons may be defeated - Appeal allowed.
