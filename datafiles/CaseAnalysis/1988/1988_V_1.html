P. N. Duda v P. Shiv Shanker and Others

Supreme Court of India

15 April 1988

Case Analysis

Bench

S. Mukharji, S. Ranganathan

Where Reported

1988 Indlaw SC 68; (1988) 3 SCC 167; (1988) SCC (Cr) 589; AIR 1988 SC 1208; 1988 CRLJ 1745; 1988 CrLR(SC) 641; JT 1988 (2) SC 102; 1988(1) SCALE 728; [1988] 3 S.C.R. 547

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution; Criminal

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Constitution - Practice & Procedure - Minimum Wages Act 1948 - Constitution of India, 1950, arts. 23, 24 - Equal Remuneration Act 1976 - Contract Labour (Regulation and Abolition) Act 1970 - Inter State Migrant Workmen (Regulation of Employment and Conditions of Service) Act 1979 - Minimum wages - Violation of provisions and articles - Forced labour - PIL - Maintainability - Petition was filed in order to ensure observance of the provisions of various labour laws in relation to workmen employed in the construction work of various projects - Matter was brought to the attention of the Court by petitioner No. 1 which was an organisation formed for the purpose of protecting democratic rights by means of a letter addressed SC - Letter was based on a report made by a team of three social scientists who were commissioned by petitioner No. 1 for the purpose of investigating and inquiring into the conditions under which the workmen engaged in the various Asiad Projects were working - (A) Whether there was any breach of art. 23 when a person provides labour or service to the State or to any other person and was paid less than the minimum wage for it - Held, it was obvious that ordinarily no one would willingly supply labour or service to another for less than the minimum wager when he knows that under the law he was entitled to get minimum wage for the labour or service provided by him - It might therefore be legitimately presumed that when a person provided labour or service to another against receipt of remuneration which was less than the minimum wage, he was acting under the force of some compulsion which drove him to work though he was paid less than what he was entitled under law to receive - What art. 23 prohibits was 'forced labour' that was labour or service which a person was forced to provide and 'force' which would make such labour or service 'forced labour' might arise in several ways - It might be physical force which might compel a person to provide labour or service to another or it might be force exerted through a legal provision such as a provision for imprisonment or fine in case the employee failed to provide labour or service or it might even be compulsion arising from hunger and poverty, want and destitution - Where a person provided labour or service to another for remuneration which was less than the minimum wage, the labour or service provided by him clearly fell within the scope and ambit of the words 'forced labour' u/art. 23 of the Constitution - Such a person would be entitled to come to the court for enforcement of his fundamental right u/art. 23 of Constitution by asking the Court to direct payment of the minimum wage to him so that the labour or service provided by him ceases to be 'forced labour' and the breach of art. 23of Constitution was remedied - It was therefore clear that when petitioners alleged that minimum wage was not paid to the workmen employed by the contractors, the complaint was really in effect and substance a complaint against violation of the fundamental right of the workmen u/art. 23 of Constitution - (B) Whether there was any violation of the provisions of the 1948 Act, art. 24 of Constitution, the 1976 Act, the 1970 Act and the 1979 Act, by the contractors - Held, whenever any contracts were given by the Govt. or any other governmental authority including 2 public sector corporation, it should be ensured by intro ducing a suitable provision in the contracts that wage should be paid by the contractors to the workmen directly without intervention of any jamadars or thekadars and that the contractors should ensure that no amount by way of commission or otherwise was deducted or recovered by the Jamadars from the wage of the workmen - So far as observance of the other labour laws by contractors was concerned, respondents disputed the claim of petitioners that the provisions of these labour laws were not being implemented by contractors save in a few instances where prosecutions was launched against the contractors - Since it would not be possible for SC to take evidence for the purpose of deciding the factual dispute between the parties and SC also wanted to ensure that in any event the provisions of these various laws enacted for the benefit of the workmen were strictly observed and implemented by the contractors, we by our order dated 11th May 1982 appointed three ombudsmen and requested them to make periodical inspections of the sites of construction work for the purpose of ascertaining whether the provisions of these labour laws were being carried out and the workers were receiving the benefits and amenities provided for them under these beneficient statutes or whether there were any violations of these provisions being committed by the contractors so that on the basis of reports of the three ombudsmen, SC could give further direction in the matter if found necessary - Order accordingly.
