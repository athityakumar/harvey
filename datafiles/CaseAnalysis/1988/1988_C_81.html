Dipti Narayan Srimani and another v Controller of Estate Duty, West Bengal and another

Supreme Court of India

09 May 1988

Case Analysis

Bench

M.N. Venkatachaliah, R.S. Pathak

Where Reported

1988 Indlaw SC 605; (1988) 3 SCC 488; AIR 1988 SC 1511; 1988 (70) CTR 113; [1988] 172 ITR 476; JT 1988 (2) SC 559; 1988(1) SCALE 1140; [1988] Supp1 S.C.R. 269; 1988 TAXLR 1243; [1988] 38 TAXMAN 179; 1988 (2) UJ 437

Case Digest

Subject: Administrative; Land & Property

Summary: Land & Property - Administrative - Estate Duty Act, 1953, ss. 2(19), 12(1), 12 - Assessment - Estate duty - Settled property - Validity - Settler during his life-time had executed 3 trust deeds - Later, settler transferred trust to himself as trustee, 4 items of immovable property - On assessment to estate duty, question arose whether trust-deeds fell within s. 12(1) of the Act - Deputy Controller and Appellate Comptroller of Estate Duty held that entire subject-matter of two deeds should be deemed to pass on death - On appeal before Tribunal - Tribunal referred matter to HC u/s. 64 of the Act - DB held that so far as 4 properties comprised in deed were concerned, they were 'Settled-Property' within meaning of ss. 2(19) and 12(1) of the Act was attracted - Hence, instant appeals - Whether trust-deeds were dutiable u/s. 12(1) of the Act - Held, subject-matter of deeds were not respectively, 11/16 share and 1/2 share in properties - Thus, whole of properties were conveyed on trust - Two deeds clearly answer description of 'Settlement' u/s. 2(19) of the Act, viz. that there was a disposition including a dedication, whereby property was settled - However, under both deeds reservations of benefit of income from trust-properties were made in favour of settler - Hence, such reservations by themselves, bring properties within s. 12(1) of the Act - In instant case benefits reserved emanate from very properties constituting subject-matter of settlements and could not be said to be collateral in their nature - The distinction between a case of a benefit arising 'collaterally' and a case of benefit reserved by 'implication' would require to be kept clearly distinguished - The quantum of interest reserved did not determine extent of property passing u/s. 12(1) of the Act - It was not a case where several distinct properties or parcels were settled and a beneficial interest was reserved out of one alone when it might be possible to predicate that all properties comprised in such settlement, which should be held to be a composite deed dealing with several items did not attract s. 12(1) of the Act but only parcel out of which an interest was carved out and reserved for settler's benefit - Therefore, interest reserved might be very small indeed, however small interest, when by virtue of such a reservation a settlement falls within purview of s. 12 of the Act whole property would be deemed to pass - Appeals disposed of.
