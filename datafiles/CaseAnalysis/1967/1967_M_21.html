Ram Chandra Arya v Man Singh and Another

Supreme Court of India

08 December 1967

Case Analysis

Bench

Vishishtha Bhargava, Vaidynathier Ramaswami, J.C. Shah

Where Reported

1967 Indlaw SC 161; AIR 1968 SC 954; 1968 ALJ 545; 1968 BLJR 610; [1968] 2 S.C.R. 572

Case Digest

Subject: Civil Procedure; Practice & Procedure

Summary: Civil Procedure - Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 - Suit for possession - Maintainability - Suit was filed for recovery of sum - An ex parte decree in that suit was passed - In execution of that decree, the house was sold and the sale certificate was issued in favour of the father of appellant in instant appeal - Formal delivery of possession was taken and the certificate of delivery of sale - 'RL' who was in possession of the house in question continued to live in the house even thereafter, and later he died leaving no heir - RL was a subject of the Maharaja and on his death, servants of the Maharaja took possession of the house - Thereupon, suit was filed by father of appellant, for possession of the house - Suit was contested on the ground that RL was a lunatic and the earlier suit was instituted against RL without appointment of a guardian-ad-litem, so that the decree in the suit was a nullity - Sale in execution of that decree was also, therefore, challenged as void - Defence was accepted by the Trial Court and the suit was dismissed - First Appellate Court also upheld that decision - On Further appeal Single Judge referred it to DB as, in his opinion, the case involved an important question of law - DB confirmed the decisions of the lower Courts - Hence instant appeal - Whether Courts below were justified in affirming the judgment - Held, if the auction-purchaser was not a judgment-creditor, the sale could not be a nullity - SC was unable to read any such principle in that decision - In fact, the Privy Council, in very clear words, held that the sale was a nullity and only, at the end, took notice of fact that, in that particular case before it, the real purchaser happened to be the judgment creditor, so that the interest of a stranger-purchaser could not be defeated by him - SC was not prepared to read in that judgment any decision that, if the auction-purchaser was not the judgment creditor but a stranger, the sale would be a valid sale, even though it was held in execution of a decree which was void - A sale was void ab initio if it was held in execution of a decree which was a nullity and, consequently, to be treated as non-existent - In instant case therefore, no rights could be acquired by purchaser, father of appellant, when he purported to purchase the house in execution of decree against RL - RL having died without leaving any heir, the property naturally passed by escheat to the Maharaja of Jaipur - That principle was clearly recognised in Hindu law - Appeal dismissed.
