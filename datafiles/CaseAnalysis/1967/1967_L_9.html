Laxmipat Choraria and Others v State of Maharashtra

Supreme Court of India

14 December 1967

Case Analysis

Bench

Mohammad Hidayatullah, C.A. Vaidyialingam

Where Reported

1967 Indlaw SC 393; AIR 1968 SC 938; 1968 CRLJ 1134; [1968] 2 S.C.R. 624

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Customs; Practice & Procedure

Keywords: Code of Criminal Procedure 1898

Summary: Customs - Criminal - Practice & Procedure - Indian Evidence Act, 1872, ss. 132, 133 - Indian Penal Code, 1860, s. 120-B - Sea Customs Act, 1878, s. 167(81) - Indian Oaths Act, 1873, s. 5 - Criminal conspiracy - Conviction - Sustainability - Appellants/accused were convicted and sentenced by Trial Court for entering into criminal conspiracy to smuggle gold into India - On appeal, HC confirmed conviction and sentence and dismissed appeal - Appellants contended that co-accused could not be examined as witness - Hence, instant appeals - Whether impugned judgment and order of HC was justified - Held, co-accused evidence was not to be excluded from consideration for any reason, then SC saw no reason not to believe her - Apart from fact that HC and Court below had concurrently believed it already, SC find ample corroboration for it from her own previous statements made without warning - Evidence of co-accused could not, therefore, be ruled out as that of incompetent witness, since co-accused was self-confessed criminal, in conspiracy with others who were being tried, her evidence was accomplice evidence - Accomplice evidence denotes evidence of participant in crime with others, s. 133 of the 1872 Act made accomplice competent witness against accused person - Therefore, co-accused testimony was again that of competent witness, it was subjected to scrutiny and usual checks for corroboration and was, therefore, received with due caution - Further, prosecution was not bound to prosecute her, if they thought that her evidence was necessary to break smugglers' ring - Co-accused was protected by s. 132(proviso) of the 1872 Act even if she gave evidence incriminating herself, she was competent witness although her evidence could only be received with caution necessary in all accomplice evidence - Expression 'criminal proceeding' in exclusionary clause of s. 5 of the 1873 Act could not be used to widen meaning of word accused, same expression was used in proviso to s. 132 of the 1872 Act and there it meant criminal trial and not investigation - Further, if Court was satisfied that there was no trick photography and photograph was above suspicion, photograph could be received in evidence, but evidence of photographs to prove writing or handwriting could only be received if original could not be obtained and photographic reproduction was faithful and not faked or false - In instant case no such suggestion existed and originals having been suppressed by accused, were not available, evidence of photographs as to contents and as to handwriting was receivable - Therefore, SC saw no reason to interfere - Appeals dismissed.
