 Income Tax Officer, Tuticorin v T. S. Devinatha Nadar and Others

Supreme Court of India

25 October 1967

Case Analysis

Bench

G.K. Mitter, K.S. Hegde, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, R.S. Bachawat, Vaidynathier Ramaswami

Where Reported

1967 Indlaw SC 350; AIR 1968 SC 623; 1968 (1) ITJ 473; [1968] 68 ITR 252; 1968 (1) MLJ(SC) 135; [1968] 2 S.C.R. 33

Case Digest

Subject: Income Tax & Direct Taxes

Summary: Income Tax & Direct Taxes - Finance Act, 1956 - Indian Income Tax Act, 1922 - Partnership - Assessment - Rectification - Respondent along with his four brothers were partners of registered firm carrying on business - Assessment of firm was completed and share income of each partner was determined - Assessment of respondent as individual was completed and included in his income from partnership just noted - Assessment of firm was reopened and sum of Rs. 90, 000 was added to income of firm liable to be brought to tax - Notice was issued and reassessment of firm took place - Notice was served on respondent for rectification of his assessment as individual - Rectification was ultimately ordered to be made - Respondent applied to HC for quashing order - When matter came to be heard by HC, there were already three reported decisions - In last decision, doubt had been cast as to correctness of two earlier decisions but HC felt second decision in point of time was fully applicable and order of rectification was quashed - Hence instant appeal - Whether order for rectification of assessments u/s. 35(5) of Indian Income Tax Act, 1922, which was quashed by HC was justified - Held, s. 35(5) of Indian Income Tax Act, 1922, neither expressly nor by necessary implication empowered ITO to reopen assessments which had become final - When decisions were overruled, confidence of public in soundness of court decision was bound to be shaken - Reconsideration of decisions should be confined to questions of great public importance - In law finality was of utmost importance - Legal problems should not be treated as mere subjects for mental exercise - Question of law with which court were concerned in was of minor importance - It had become all more so because of passage of time, as it had relevance only to assessments of partners of firms made in cases where question of enhancing those assessments arises as a result of assessment or reassessment of concerned firms - Appeal allowed.
