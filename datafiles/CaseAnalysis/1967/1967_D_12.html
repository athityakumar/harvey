Munshi Ram and Others v Delhi Administration

Supreme Court of India

27 November 1967

Case Analysis

Bench

K.S. Hegde, S.M. Sikri, J.M. Shelat

Where Reported

1967 Indlaw SC 487; AIR 1968 SC 702; 1968 CRLJ 806; 1968 (2) SCJ 521; [1968] 2 S.C.R. 455

Case Digest

Subject: Criminal; Land & Property

Summary: Criminal - Land & Property - Displaced Persons (Compensation and Rehabilitation) Act, 1954 - Indian Penal Code, 1860 - Possession of property - Dispute - Criminal offence - Appellant was formerly the tenant in respect of that field under some Muslim landlords and after their migration to Pakistan, under the officer managing the evacuee property - Property was sold by Central Govt. in public auction and provisional delivery was given to respondent - Respondent claimed that when he entered in the land appellants attacked them - Appellant contended that he was tenant on the said land for 30years and their termination of tenancy by sale of land was illegal hence, they tried to throw off appellant from the land - Appellant was convicted u/ss. 447, 324 r/w ss. 149 and 148 of IPC - Appellant filed an appeal against the conviction order of HC - Whether, appellant had exceeded their right to private defence - Held, from the facts of the case it was obvious that appellant was tenant of land even after sale and there was no evidence to show that appellants were aware of delivery of land to respondents - Appellant was unwilling to deliver possession of the field hence, police assistance was applied for and obtained - No one, including the true owner, had a right to dispossess the trespasser by force if the trespasser was in settled possession of the land and in such a case unless he was evicted in due course of law, he was entitled to defend his possession even against the rightful owner - Where an individual citizen or his property faced a danger and immediate aid from the State machinery was not readily available, the individual citizen was entitled to protect himself and his property - That being so, it was a necessary corollary to the doctrine of private defence that the violence which the citizen defended himself or his property was entitled to use must not be unduly disproportionate to the injury which was to be averted or which was reasonably apprehended and should not exceed its legitimate purpose and in such a case right to private defence could not be vindicated - From the facts it could not be said that the appellants had exceeded their right of private defence - Appeal allowed.
