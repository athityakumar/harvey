 Mysore State Road Transport Corporation v Gopinath Gundachar Char

Supreme Court of India

06 October 1967

Case Analysis

Bench

R.S. Bachawat, Kailas Nath Wanchoo, Vaidynathier Ramaswami, G.K. Mitter, K.S. Hegde

Where Reported

1967 Indlaw SC 172; AIR 1968 SC 464; 1968 (2) LLJ 144; [1968] 1 S.C.R. 767; 1968 SLR 330

Case Digest

Subject: Labour & Industrial Law

Summary: Labour & Industrial Law - Road Transport Corporation Act, 1950, ss. 14(2)(3), 14(3)(b), 19(1)(c), 34 and 45(2)(c) - Application for appointment in Corporation - Prayer for quashing such appointment - Sustainability - Respondent was class III employee in the statistical dept. of State Road Transport Corporation/appellant - Respondent was temporarily promoted to act as statistical superintendent - General Manager of appellant/Corporation issued notice inviting applications for appointments to class 11 junior posts of (a) assistant/divisional statisticians and (b) labour welfare officers under the Rules - Respondent filed writ petition in the HC claiming that appellant had no power to issue the notice and praying for an order quashing it - HC allowed the petition and quashed the notice - Hence instant appeal - Whether appellant had the power to appoint officers - Held, appellant was autonomous Corporation incorporated under the Act for the purpose of operating road transport services in the State and extended areas - For the proper discharge of its functions, it was necessary for the appellant to appoint officers and servants - S. 14(2) of Act expressly confered upon appellant the incidental power to appoint such officers and servants as it considered necessary for the efficient performance of its functions - S. 19(1)(c) of Act empowered it to provide for its employees suitable conditions of service - S. 14(3) of Act provided that the conditions of appointment and service and the scales of pay of its officers and servants should be such as might subject to the provisions of s. 34 of Act to be determined by regulations made under the Act - S. 45(2)(c) of Act empowered appellant to frame regulations with the previous sanction of the State Govt. prescribing the conditions of appointment, service and scales of pay of the officers and servants - If the State Government issues any directions u/s. 34 of Act relating to the recruitment and conditions of service of the employees, appellant should obey those directions - Conjoint effect of ss.14(3)(b), 34 and 45(2)(c) of Act was that the appointment of officers and servants and their conditions of service should conform to the directions, if any, given by the State Govt. u/s.34 of Act and the regulations, if any, framed u/s.45(2)(c) of Act - But until such regulations were framed or directions were given appellant might appoint such officers or servants as should be necessary for the efficient performance of its duties on such terms and conditions as it thought fit - There was necessarily time- lag between the formation of appellant and the framing of regulations u/s. 45(2)(c) of Act - During the intervening period, appellant should carry on the administration of its affairs with the help of officers and servants - In the absence of clear words, it was difficult to impute to the legislature the intention that appellant would have no power to appoint officers and servants and fix the conditions of service unless the regulations u/s.45(2)(c) of Act were framed - There was no merit in the further contention that General Manager had no power to issue the notice in the absence of any resolution by appellant under s.12(c) of Act expressly authorising him to issue it - In the exercise of his general powers of management the General Manager had clearly the power to issue notice inviting applications from intending candidates - It was not alleged that he made any appointment pursuant to the notice - Respondent also contended that he had the right to be promoted to class II junior post - But there was nothing on the record to show that he had any vested right of promotion to the post - Appeal allowed.
