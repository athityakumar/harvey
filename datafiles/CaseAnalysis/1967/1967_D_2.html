 Satwant Singh Sawhney and Another v D. Ramarathnam, Assistant Passport Officer, New Delhi and Others

Supreme Court of India

10 April 1967

Case Analysis

Bench

K. Subba Rao, Mohammad Hidayatullah, C.A. Vaidyialingam, R.S. Bachawat, J.M. Shelat

Where Reported

1967 Indlaw SC 519; AIR 1967 SC 1836; 1968 (70) BomLR 1; 1968 (1) SCJ 178; [1967] 3 S.C.R. 525

Case Digest

Subject: Constitution

Keywords: Freedom Of Movement

Summary: Constitution - Indian Passport Act, 1920, s. 3 - Constitution of India, 1950,arts.19, 21 - Petitioner Sawhney, a citizen of India, who carries on business of Importer, Exporter and Manufacture of automobile parts - Frequent foreign trips - Regular passport from Govt. of India - Respondents vide their two letters called for returning and surrendering of passports issued to petitioner - (A) Whether a person living in India has a fundamental right to travel abroad? - Possession of passport is a necessary requisite for leaving India for traveling abroad - Passport, whether in England or in USA serves diverse purposes; it is a 'request for protection', it is a document of identity, it is a prima facie evidence of nationality, in modern times it not only controls exit from State to which one belongs, but without it, with a few exceptions, it is not possible to enter another State; It has become a condition for free travel - Held, 'Right to travel', 'Liberty/personal liberty', 'procedure established by law', 'due process of law' - Personal liberty u/art. 21 and liberty u/art. 19, decision of Court in 'A K Gopalan vs State of Madras, 1950' and observation of Fazl Ali, J - Drawing of distinction between freedom of movement, which is a part of personal liberty u/art 21 and limits of that liberty u/art. 19(1)(d) - A K Gopalan's case, as authority laid down that 'liberty' in Constitution bears same comprehensive meaning as is given to expression 'liberty' by 5 th and 14 th Amendments to U S Constitution and expression 'personal liberty' in art.21 only excludes ingredients of 'liberty' enshrined in art 19 of Constitution - Thus expression 'personal liberty' in art.21 takes in right of locomotion and to travel abroad, but right to move throughout territories of India is not covered by it inasmuch as it is specially provided in art 19 - Further held, u/art. 21 of Constitution no person can be deprived of his right to travel except according to procedure established by law and undisputed is position that no law was made by State regulating or depriving persons of such a right - (B) Whether act of respondents in refusing to issue passport infringes petitioner's fundamental right u/art. 14? - Doctrine of equality before law is a necessary corollary to high concept of rule of law accepted by Constitution - One of aspects of rule of law is that every executive action, if it is to operate to prejudice of any person, must be supported by some legislative authority - Second is, such a law would be void, if it discriminates or enables an authority to discriminate between persons without just classification - What a Legislature could not do, executive could not do - In instant case executive claims a right to issue a passport at its discretion i e, it can at its discretion prevent a person from leaving India on foreign travel - Irrespective of question as to whether right to travel abroad is part of personal liberty or not, such an arbitrary prevention of a person from traveling abroad will certainly affect him prejudicially - An executive arbitrariness can prevent one genuine person who has justified reasons to go abroad, and permit another to travel merely for pleasure - While in case of enacted law one knows where he stands, in case of unchannelled arbitrary discretion, discrimination is writ large on face of it - Such a discretion patently violates doctrine of equality, for difference in treatment of persons rests solely on arbitrary selection of executive - Further held, order refusing to issue passport to petitioner offends art 14 of Constitution - By Majority decision - Writ of mandamus issued - Petition allowed.
